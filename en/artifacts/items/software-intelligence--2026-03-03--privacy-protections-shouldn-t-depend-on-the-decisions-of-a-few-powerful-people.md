# Privacy Protections Shouldn't Depend on the Decisions of a Few Powerful People

## Summary
This article is not a technical paper, but a policy commentary: using the contract conflict between the U.S. Department of Defense and Anthropic, it argues that digital privacy should not depend on the ad hoc decisions of a small number of corporate executives, but should instead be stably constrained by law.

## Problem
- The core problem identified by the article is that public privacy protection may currently depend on contract negotiations between the government and large technology companies, rather than on clear, enforceable legal rules.
- This matters because the government is already able to purchase commercial data and, combined with AI analytical capabilities, carry out large-scale profiling and surveillance of the American public, while existing laws and judicial interpretations do not adequately restrict this behavior.
- If Congress and the courts do not impose limits in time, civil rights will rest on whether CEOs are “willing to do the right thing,” and that kind of protection is neither stable nor sustainable.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: the author uses as a case study the DoD demanding that Anthropic abandon its restrictions on use for “domestic mass surveillance” and “fully autonomous weapons,” and Anthropic’s refusal followed by contract termination, to show that private contractual limits cannot substitute for public rule of law.
- The article supports its risk assessment with existing government practices, including: CBP purchasing advertising data for surveillance, ICE using device-mapping tools based on purchased phone data, and ODNI proposing to establish a centralized data-broker market.
- As a normative claim, the author argues that the institutions that should truly bear responsibility for imposing constraints are Congress and the courts, not private companies; legislative and judicial updates are needed to close loopholes that let the government buy personal data and use AI to expand analysis.
- The article also cites statements from Anthropic’s CEO, emphasizing that even companies themselves acknowledge that whether to allow AI-driven domestic mass surveillance is, in essence, a decision that should be made by Congress and the judicial system.

## Results
- The article does not provide experiments, models, datasets, or benchmarks, so there are no quantitative research results in the traditional sense.
- The clearest factual outcome is that the U.S. military terminated its **$200 million** contract with Anthropic and ordered other defense contractors to stop using its products, because the two sides came into conflict over the boundaries of acceptable technological use.
- Public opinion figures cited in the article include: **71%** of American adults are concerned about how the government uses their data; among adults who have heard of AI, **70%** “hardly trust or do not trust at all” how companies use these products.
- The specific legislative development is that a bill intended to close the loophole allowing the government to purchase personal information passed the House in **2024**, but was shelved by the Senate, serving as evidence of “institutional failure outsourcing privacy protection to companies.”
- The strongest concluding claim is that the most needed “breakthrough” right now is not new AI technology, but enforceable legal restrictions so that privacy protection no longer depends on backroom dealings between corporate executives and the government.
