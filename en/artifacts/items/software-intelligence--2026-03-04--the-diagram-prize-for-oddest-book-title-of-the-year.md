# The Diagram Prize for Oddest Book Title of the Year

## Summary
This text introduces the history, rules, controversies, and influence of the British **Bookseller/Diagram Prize for Oddest Title of the Year**. It is not a research paper, but an informational entry about an annual literary novelty award for the “oddest book title.”

## Problem
- The “problem” it addresses is not a technical challenge, but rather providing entertainment for the 1978 Frankfurt Book Fair and continuing to recognize books with unusually strange titles.
- Its significance lies mainly in cultural dissemination and media attention: the prize amplifies the visibility of books with “odd titles,” generating public participation and publishing-industry discussion.
- The text also touches on governance issues around the award, such as how to define an “odd title,” how to avoid author self-promotion, and the controversies brought by public voting.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: each year, books with sufficiently “bizarre” English titles are solicited, shortlisted, and then a winner is selected.
- In the early years, the winner was decided by judges/organizers; **since 2000, it has been decided by online public vote**, while the organizers are responsible for producing the final shortlist.
- The rules prohibit nominating one’s own work or books one has published, and usually exclude titles that are “deliberately written to be funny,” in order to preserve a sense of “natural oddness.”
- Submission channels gradually expanded: initially limited to books at the Frankfurt Book Fair, later opened to readers, **Twitter nominations were accepted starting in 2009**, and self-published works became eligible from 2014.
- The prize focuses on “strange book titles” rather than book content, and even explicitly discourages judges and voters from reading the books.

## Results
- The text does not provide experimental, modeling, or benchmark results in the scientific sense, so there are **no academic quantitative metrics to report**.
- The strongest quantifiable result is growth in participation: **in 2009, 90 submissions were received, 50 of them from Twitter, nearly tripling the previous year’s 32 submissions**.
- **In 2008, the prize received 8,500 votes**, higher than **Best of Booker’s 7,800**, indicating strong public attention.
- The prize was **not awarded twice (1987 and 1991)** because the organizers judged that no title was “odd” enough to win.
- The most recent winning work in **2025** was *The Pornographic Delicatessen: Midcentury Montréal's Erotic Art, Media, and Spaces*.
- A specific claim related to automated generation is that the **2009 winning book**, *The 2009–2014 World Outlook for 60-milligram Containers of Fromage Frais*, was alleged not to have been written by the named author, but instead generated by **Philip M. Parker’s automated writing machine**, which became a notable controversy in the history of the prize.
