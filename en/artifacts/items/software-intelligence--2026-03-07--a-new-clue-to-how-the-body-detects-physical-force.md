# A new clue to how the body detects physical force

## Summary
This study explains why PIEZO2 is suited to sensing light touch while PIEZO1 is better suited to sensing broad stretching: the key lies not only in the protein itself, but also in PIEZO2 being linked to the actin cytoskeleton through filamin-B. This finding connects single-molecule structural dynamics with neuronal touch function and provides new clues for understanding sensory disorders.

## Problem
- The problem this work addresses is: **why PIEZO2 and the highly similar PIEZO1 respond differently to different types of mechanical force**, especially why PIEZO2 is specifically used for light-touch detection in sensory neurons.
- This matters because touch, proprioception, and some pain sensations all depend on this kind of mechanotransduction; without a clear mechanism, it is difficult to explain **sensory abnormalities associated with mutations in PIEZO2 or filamin-B**.
- Previous structural methods such as cryo-EM could reveal static shapes, but **could not directly explain in living cells how the protein moves or how it converts specific forces into channel opening**.

## Approach
- The researchers used **MINFLUX super-resolution microscopy** to track the position and conformational changes of PIEZO2 in living cells at nanometer scale, rather than only viewing frozen static structures.
- They combined this with **electrophysiological recordings**, directly linking “how PIEZO2 deforms when mechanical force is applied” with “whether the ion channel opens and whether ion flow increases.”
- They compared the mechanical properties of PIEZO2 and PIEZO1, finding that **PIEZO2 is intrinsically stiffer** and forms a **tether** with the actin cytoskeleton through **filamin-B**.
- They identified the **specific region where PIEZO2 connects to filamin-B**, then tested whether disrupting this connection altered the channel’s force selectivity in cells, mouse sensory neurons, and experiments closer to physiological conditions.

## Results
- The study reports that **PIEZO2’s force selectivity arises from a “rigidity + cytoskeletal tether” mechanism**, explaining why it prefers localized pressing/indentation rather than general membrane stretch.
- Mechanistically, **when a cell is locally “poked,”** filamin-B transmits force through the actin cytoskeleton to PIEZO2, making it easier to open; but **when the tether is intact, membrane stretch alone does not activate PIEZO2**.
- After disrupting the connection between PIEZO2 and filamin-B, in **mouse sensory neurons**, PIEZO2 showed **reduced sensitivity to indentation stimuli** and **unexpectedly gained responsiveness to membrane stretch**; this suggests the tether determines “what kind of force it should sense and should not sense.”
- The study also reports that **PIEZO1 expands and is activated under membrane stretch, whereas PIEZO2 shows the opposite response**, supporting the idea that the two follow different mechanosensing mechanisms.
