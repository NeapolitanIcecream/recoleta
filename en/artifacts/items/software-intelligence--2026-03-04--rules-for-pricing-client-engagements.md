# Rules for Pricing Client Engagements

## Summary
This article presents 3 rule-of-thumb pricing principles for freelancers and consulting service providers: clients usually are not “out of money,” but rather “do not treat your project as a priority.” The core argument is that resistance to pricing is mainly determined by client priorities, not just by whether the price is high or low.

## Problem
- The problem addressed: freelancers, independent consultants, and solo business owners often hesitate excessively when quoting clients because they fear either “losing the deal by quoting too high” or “getting burned by quoting too low.”
- Why it matters: incorrect pricing directly harms income, negotiating position, and business sustainability, while also creating unnecessary psychological stress.
- The author argues that many pricing problems are, at their core, not about pricing formulas but about misjudging buyer budgets, priorities, and purchasing behavior.

## Approach
- The core method is 3 simplified mental models: **Everybody has money**, **Everybody has $5,000 in their front pocket**, **Nobody cares what you cost**.
- Mechanism of the first rule: for real, serious buyers, the question usually is not whether there is absolutely no budget, but whether they are willing to reallocate resources to something of higher priority.
- Mechanism of the second rule: the author proposes an experiential threshold, arguing that for most mid-sized and large B2B buyers, around **$5,000** is often “walking-around money” that can be approved without much deliberation; this number can be adjusted up or down depending on industry and customer segment.
- Mechanism of the third rule: when a project is important enough, clients’ price sensitivity drops significantly; conversely, if a project is not a priority, it may be cut entirely no matter how cheap it is.
- Therefore, the simplest interpretation is: first determine whether the client truly cares about this issue, then decide how to price and how to justify value, instead of preemptively discounting on the client’s behalf.

## Results
- This is not empirical research; **the article does not provide systematic experiments, datasets, or formal quantitative metrics**.
- The most specific numerical claim is: for many mid-market and enterprise buyers, **$5,000** can often serve as a low-friction pricing benchmark; deals **below $5,000** are usually easier to move forward and involve less price haggling.
- The author also proposes an actionable rule-of-thumb range: if the quote is within about **2.5x** of that benchmark, you can usually expect the client to discuss reducing scope rather than rejecting it outright because of price.
- Anecdotal evidence 1: during a hiring freeze, a certain CMO, among multiple options presented by the author, **“almost immediately” chose the most expensive option**, suggesting that high-priority needs can override budget concerns.
- Anecdotal evidence 2: the author once lost an ongoing content project that had been “working pretty well,” but the reason was not failure to lower the price; rather, after an acquisition, the new parent company **no longer viewed the project as a priority**, and therefore canceled it entirely.
- The strongest conclusion of the article is: so-called “the price is too high” is often only a surface explanation; what really determines whether a deal closes is **priority, urgency, and how much the client cares about the outcome**.
