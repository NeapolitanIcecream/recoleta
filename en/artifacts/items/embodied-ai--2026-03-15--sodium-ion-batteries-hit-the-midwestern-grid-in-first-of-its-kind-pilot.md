# Sodium-ion batteries hit the Midwestern grid in first-of-its-kind pilot

## Summary
This is a pilot project for the first deployment of sodium-ion batteries on the US Midwestern grid. Its core selling point is reducing grid-scale energy storage costs through passive cooling and a more stable chemistry. The article’s focus is not algorithmic innovation, but rather validating the real-world feasibility and potential economic value of this approach on the MISO grid.

## Problem
- Demand for grid-side energy storage is rising, especially in regions like MISO, where load growth, rising energy prices, and increasing wind and solar integration are colliding with still-insufficient large-scale storage.
- Traditional lithium-ion storage systems typically require active cooling, maintenance, and reserved excess capacity to account for degradation, which raises the lifetime cost of stored energy.
- The problem, therefore, is how to provide a lower-cost, more stable energy storage solution suitable for grid-scale deployment, in order to reduce the need for new power plants and expensive power purchases.

## Approach
- The approach is to deploy Peak Energy’s passively cooled sodium-ion battery system on the MISO network in eastern Wisconsin, with RWE Americas conducting the grid-connected pilot operation.
- The core mechanism is simple: it leverages the higher inherent stability of sodium-ion chemistry so that the system does not need to rely on active cooling like many lithium battery systems do.
- By eliminating energy-intensive cooling systems, reducing routine maintenance, and lowering the degree of capacity “overbuilding” needed to account for battery degradation, the total system cost is expected to fall.
- In essence, the pilot is testing in a real grid environment whether this simpler system design can significantly reduce energy storage costs while maintaining performance.

## Results
- A clear quantitative claim: Peak Energy says its system can reduce the lifetime cost of stored energy by an average of **$70/kWh**.
- The article says this is roughly **half** of the “total cost of a typical battery system today.”
- Peak further claims that if **10 GWh** of storage is added in MISO over the next decade using its **GS1.1 sodium-ion** system, total storage system costs could be reduced by **more than 25%** compared with conventional lithium-ion solutions.
- Citing system-level modeling from Aurora Energy Research: if **10 GWh** of battery storage is deployed in the MISO region over the next decade, system costs could be reduced by as much as **$27 billion** versus a scenario with no storage deployment.
- RWE Americas currently operates about **13 GW** of energy assets in the US and will use this pilot to test the technology’s actual grid performance; however, the article **does not provide measured post-pilot performance data**, so the current results are mainly the fact of deployment plus cost and potential-benefit claims from the company and research firm.
