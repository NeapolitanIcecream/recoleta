# Ask HN: Most beautiful personal blog UI you have ever seen?

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a Hacker News discussion thread about what users consider the “most beautiful personal blog UI.” The content mainly consists of link sharing, design preferences, usability critiques, and personal aesthetic standards, without presenting any systematic research method or experimental conclusions.

## Problem
- The core question of the discussion is: what kind of personal blog UI is considered “beautiful,” distinctive, and worth revisiting.
- This matters because blog design needs to balance **aesthetic expression**, **information access efficiency**, and **usability**; pages that are beautiful but hard to use will harm the experience.
- The post also implies a product question: whether it would be possible to build a blog bookmark manager/classifier based on community aesthetic standards, dividing blogs into categories such as S-TIER, A-TIER, opinion, and so on.

## Approach
- There is no formal research method; the main mechanism is community-sourced examples, with users posting blog/UI cases they consider excellent.
- The original poster provides their own evaluation framework: blogs with high information density and educational value are prioritized, while purely opinion-based blogs rank lower.
- Replies further develop several informal evaluation dimensions: layout, typography, visual style, distinctive themes, whitespace, navigation efficiency, scrolling behavior, mobile experience, and readability.
- The discussion also emphasizes a simple principle: an excellent blog UI should not only be “stunning,” but should also avoid bad UX, such as requiring multiple clicks to navigate, scroll hijacking, or misleading heading styles.

## Results
- There are no quantitative experiments, datasets, metrics, or baseline comparisons, so there are **no reportable numerical results**.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is experiential: multiple users acknowledge that some sites have “famous and exceptional” design, such as `ciechanow.ski`, `lumafield.com`, `maggieappleton.com`, and `meyerweb.com`.
- The discussion also provides clear negative UX examples: some sites “require 3 clicks and 10 seconds to reach a subpage,” have underlined headings that are not links, or hijack scrolling / the back button / browsing history.
- One relatively stable consensus is that the most popular blogs usually either have a strong visual identity or do an excellent job with minimalist readability; flashy animations alone, if they sacrifice navigation experience, are not widely appreciated.
