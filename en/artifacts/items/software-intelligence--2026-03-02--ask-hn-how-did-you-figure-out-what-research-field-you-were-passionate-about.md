# Ask HN: How did you figure out what research field you were passionate about?

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but rather a request-for-advice post about how to discover a research direction one is truly passionate about. The core content is that the author, before graduating from high school, lacks a clear interest in a future research field and hopes to get advice on low-cost ways to explore different disciplines.

## Problem
- The author faces the problem that **their research interests have not yet taken shape**: although they are strong in physics, calculus, chemistry, and biology, no subject inspires sustained commitment.
- This matters because choosing a university major and a future research path usually requires a long-term commitment, and locking in too early may create path dependence.
- The author wants to know: how research passion usually forms, what signals indicate an interest can be sustained long-term, and how a freshman can explore different directions with low risk.

## Approach
- The text itself **does not propose a research method or technical solution**; instead, it asks for other people's experience-based advice.
- The author first provides personal background: they applied to electrical engineering because hardware and computing systems “seemed interesting and practical,” but they have not developed a true passion for it.
- Its core “mechanism” is to ask people with research experience and gather inspiration about paths such as projects, classes, papers, conversations, switching majors, joining labs, summer research, and self-study.
- The author also tries to define criteria for “long-term interest,” such as whether certain questions keep bothering them over time, and whether they can tolerate the daily work of experiments, debugging, and reading.

## Results
- There are **no quantitative experimental results, datasets, metrics, or baseline comparisons**; this text is not a research paper and does not report empirical conclusions.
- The strongest concrete information is the author's personal situation: they are about to graduate from high school, applied to **electrical engineering**, and earned **high nineties** grades in physics, calculus, chemistry, and biology.
- The text raises several clear exploratory directions: **summer programs, random labs, self-reading, odd electives, switching majors/labs**, but these are open questions rather than validated conclusions.
- Therefore, it is better suited as discussion material about research career choice and interest exploration, rather than as a reproducible academic research result.
