# Delay the Inference

## Summary
This is an opinion piece about how humans should preserve the capacity for independent thinking in the age of AI. Its central argument is to **"delay asking AI"** and let ideas naturally develop in the mind first. The author believes AI significantly boosts productivity, but handing half-formed thoughts to a model too early may weaken original thinking and the internal reasoning process.

## Problem
- The article focuses on this question: when AI is instantly available, will people outsource the early stages of thinking—stages they ought to do themselves—to a model too soon?
- This matters because the author worries that what gets weakened is not the “efficiency of getting answers,” but the internal phase of wandering, association, and incubation that comes before original ideas take shape.
- The piece compares this to search engines, but emphasizes an important difference: search mainly replaced information retrieval, whereas generative AI may intervene much earlier, in the generation of thought itself.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: **do not ask AI immediately when an idea has just appeared**; first delay invoking this “external reasoning system.”
- The author uses “delay the inference” for both AI and human cognition: for AI, it means delaying the path from prompt to output; for humans, it means delaying premature conclusions or premature reliance on external generation.
- The practical workflow is to first record raw ideas independently, then write a rough draft alone, then do research, and only afterward use AI for brainstorming and rewriting.
- Theoretically, the author draws on research perspectives about the default mode network and executive control systems in creative thinking, arguing that leaving room for “mental wandering” may help originality emerge.

## Results
- The text **does not provide quantitative experiments, datasets, baselines, or metric results**; this is not an experimental paper, but an opinion/essay piece.
- The strongest concrete claim is that over the past **6-12 months**, AI has enabled the author to do many things that previously felt “almost out of reach,” especially writing and brainstorming.
- The author’s reported personal method includes accumulating notes for several months, writing an initial draft independently, researching relevant neuroscience, then asking AI for several angles, and rewriting again after **1 day**.
- The main concluding claim is that seeking help from AI too early may compress the incubation period for original thought; a better usage habit is to form one’s own ideas first, and then use AI as a downstream supporting tool.
