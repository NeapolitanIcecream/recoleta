# Depleted weapons stockpiles could affect the Iran conflict

## Summary
This article analyzes the core constraint in the Iran conflict of whether weapons stockpiles can support sustained operations. It argues that although overall US military power is far stronger than Iran’s, a high-intensity war would rapidly consume both sides’ inventories of missiles, drones, and air-defense interceptors. The article’s key judgment is that the US is more likely to maintain a long-term advantage in air-to-ground strikes, but high-value munitions such as air-defense interceptors could become a more fragile bottleneck.

## Problem
- The article addresses the question of whether, in a high-intensity conflict between Iran and the US/Israel, both sides’ weapons stockpiles and replenishment capacity are sufficient to sustain ongoing operations, and how that would affect the course of the war.
- This matters because modern war depends not only on tactics and technology, but also on the ability to continuously supply missiles, bombs, drones, and interceptors; once stockpiles run low, military advantages can be rapidly weakened.
- Especially critical is that although the US has deeper overall stockpiles, the production of expensive air-defense interceptors such as Patriot is limited and demand is broad, which could constrain its long-term defensive capacity.

## Approach
- The article uses an **analysis of defense-industrial inventories and consumption rates**: by tracking the number of strikes carried out by both sides, missile/drone launch volumes, and the decline of these figures over time, it infers their ability to sustain combat.
- It synthesizes multiple public sources, including INSS, Centcom, US General Dan Caine, and estimates from CSIS expert Mark Cancian, to compare the inventory depth and replenishment capacity of Iran and the United States.
- The core mechanism can be understood simply as: **if the rate of consumption remains above the rate of production for a long time, even a major power will face bottlenecks in certain critical munitions**; and once air superiority is established, forces can shift from expensive long-range weapons to cheaper short-range munitions, easing pressure on stockpiles.
- The article also distinguishes between two types of wartime consumption: one is air-to-ground strike munitions, which the US can sustain more easily over the long term; the other is air-defense interceptor inventories, which are tighter and harder to replenish.

## Results
- INSS estimates that the US and Israel have already carried out **more than 2,000 strikes**, with each typically involving multiple munitions, indicating an extremely high tempo of consumption in the early stage of the war.
- INSS estimates that Iran has launched **571 missiles** and **1,391 drones**; a US general said its ballistic missile launches are down **86%** from the first day, and drone launches are down **73%**, while Centcom also said its missile activity fell another **23%** in the most recent **24 hours**.
- Before the war, Iran was estimated to have **more than 2,000 short-range ballistic missiles** and to have mass-produced **tens of thousands** of Shahed one-way attack drones, but the article argues that its ability to sustain a high tempo of strikes has already weakened significantly.
- On the US side, the article says it has shifted from expensive “stand-off weapons” such as Tomahawk to cheaper “stand-in” munitions such as JDAM; a CSIS expert says the US has **tens of thousands** of JDAMs, so air-to-ground operations could be sustained for “**almost indefinitely**” or “**a very long time**.”
- On air defense, Patriot interceptors cost **more than $4 million** each, and US annual production is about **700 rounds**; CSIS estimates US stockpiles at about **1,600 rounds**, and recent consumption has already occurred, so the article argues that the air-defense fight is “**more uncertain**” than the strike campaign against ground targets.
- The article does not present new experimental research findings; its strongest conclusion is: **the US is more likely overall to outlast Iran, but the real vulnerability lies in high-value air-defense interceptors rather than conventional ground-strike munitions.**
