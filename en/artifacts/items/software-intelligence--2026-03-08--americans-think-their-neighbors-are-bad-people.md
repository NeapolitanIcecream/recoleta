# Americans Think Their Neighbors Are Bad People

## Summary
This article draws on a Pew cross-national survey to argue that Americans suffer not only from low trust, but from a deeper form of “moral contempt” toward their fellow citizens: the United States is the only one of the 25 surveyed countries in which a majority believe their own people have “bad morals and ethics.” The author argues that this phenomenon is more dangerous than simple distrust because it erodes the foundations for cooperation, reform, and institutional repair in a democratic society.

## Problem
- The article tries to explain a more serious social problem: why Americans broadly believe that “the people around them are morally bad,” rather than merely believing that the government or institutions are untrustworthy.
- This matters because once the public escalates policy disagreements into judgments about others’ character, public debate shifts from “which solution works” to “is the other side made up of bad people,” thereby undermining cooperation and institutional repair.
- The author emphasizes that this is not simply a result of unusually high moral standards; on most specific moral issues, Americans are not exceptionally conservative, yet they are unusually inclined to render negative moral judgments about “people.”

## Approach
- The core method is **interpreting comparative survey data from the Pew Research Center across 25 countries**, with particular attention to the question, “Do the people in your country have good morals or bad morals?” and comparing the United States with other countries.
- The author further contrasts this overall judgment with attitudes on **specific moral issues** (extramarital affairs, marijuana, gambling, homosexuality, abortion, drinking alcohol, divorce, contraception, etc.), arguing that Americans are not especially harsh on behaviors, but especially harsh on people.
- The article uses **affective polarization** and **motivated reasoning** to explain the mechanism: people first label others as “good” or “bad” based on tribe/party, and then selectively interpret evidence.
- The author also analyzes **partisan differences and age differences**: those opposed to the governing party are more likely to evaluate fellow citizens negatively, while the American exceptionalism lies in the fact that both sides are high enough to push the national average above 50%; younger people are also more likely than older people to make negative moral judgments.

## Results
- The central finding is that **53% of American adults believe Americans’ morals and ethics are “bad”**; the author says the United States is the **only one of the 25 surveyed countries** where the overall national evaluation tips into the negative.
- By party, **Democrats/Democratic leaners: 60%** and **Republicans/Republican leaners: 46%** say their fellow citizens have bad morals; the author notes that other countries show similar tendencies, but **only the United States** is pushed into a national majority-negative view.
- By age, **18–39: 57%**, while **older adults: 50%**; the piece says Pew found this gap **still exists after controlling for party**.
- On specific moral issues, the United States does not appear exceptionally strict: for example, **only 23%** say **using marijuana** is morally wrong, and **only 29%** say **gambling** is morally wrong; both are around or below the global median, weakening the explanation that “Americans simply have higher moral standards.”
- One exception is **extramarital affairs**: **90%** of Americans say it is morally wrong, close to the highest level globally; but the author argues that **a single issue is insufficient to explain the broader nationwide moral pessimism of 53%**.
- The article does not present new experiments or causal identification results; its strongest substantive claim is that America’s social psychology has deteriorated from a “low-trust equilibrium” into a “low-respect/low-moral-evaluation equilibrium,” which will make institutional reform and collective action harder.
