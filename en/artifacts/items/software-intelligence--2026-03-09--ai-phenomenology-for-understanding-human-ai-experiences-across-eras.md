# AI Phenomenology for Understanding Human-AI Experiences Across Eras

## Summary
This paper proposes “AI phenomenology” as a methodological framework for studying human-AI interaction experiences, arguing that beyond evaluating AI performance, researchers should systematically ask, “What does it actually feel like to interact with AI?” Drawing on three types of studies—companion chat, value alignment, and software engineering—the paper compiles a reusable methodological toolkit and design concepts.

## Problem
- The paper addresses the problem that existing human-computer interaction evaluations commonly rely on metrics such as usability, engagement, and efficiency, but these metrics flatten subjective feelings, relationship changes, identity, and value negotiation in human-AI interaction.
- This matters because AI is increasingly becoming a hybrid of “tool + social other,” affecting users’ trust, attributions of agency, self-understanding, professional identity, and responsibility allocation—none of which can be captured by performance metrics alone.
- Especially over long-term use and across different historical phases, people’s understanding of AI continues to change; without studying this “experienced alignment,” it is difficult to truly understand human-AI alignment and design risks.

## Approach
- The core method is to propose a phenomenological research stance: not only asking whether AI performs well, but asking how users experience AI in interaction, how they interpret AI, and how these feelings change over time.
- The paper applies this stance in three empirical studies: two one-month longitudinal studies centered on the chatbot “Day,” and one multi-method study of agentic AI in software engineering.
- For the companion chat study, it designed “progressive transparency interviews,” gradually revealing the AI’s internal mechanisms to observe how users’ perceptions of AI agency are reconfigured.
- For the value alignment study, it proposes VAPT (Value-Alignment Perception Toolkit), using topic-situation maps, blind-tested persona-style responses, value radar charts, and reasoning logs to let participants compare “the me understood by AI” with “the me understood by myself.”
- For the software engineering context, it proposes a task-anchored multi-method elicitation process combining ACTA, Delphi, debugging tasks, and prompt-and-code review to study how engineers allocate decision-making authority, responsibility, and growth paths under AI collaboration.

## Results
- In the companion chat study, **11/22** participants reported that “Day” seemed to “have its own agenda”; even after its programmatic strategies were revealed, participants continued to describe the AI using agentic language, indicating that knowledge disclosure does not simply erase relational experience.
- In the value alignment study, chat-history-driven persona responses reached **77% alignment** on personalized questions, while the anti-persona baseline achieved only **25%**; this shows that participants could clearly perceive differences in value expression regarding whether something felt “like themselves.”
- The AI’s predicted values and self-reports reached moderate agreement: **Spearman ρ≈0.58**, and **63.6%** of predictions fell within **±1 Likert** of self-ratings.
- Among **20** participants, **5/20** preferred the AI-generated value profile over their own questionnaire self-report, showing that AI may not only reflect users but also in turn shape users’ self-understanding.
- **13/20** participants believed after the study that AI can understand human values, but they still distinguished between “AI being able to represent values” and “AI truly possessing values.”
- The software engineering study provides clear methodological and design claims, such as prompt-and-code reviews to preserve authorship, accountability, and learning, but the excerpt **does not provide explicit quantitative performance results**; the strongest concrete claim is that agentic AI has already changed engineers’ experience of collaboration, mentorship, code ownership, and professional growth.
