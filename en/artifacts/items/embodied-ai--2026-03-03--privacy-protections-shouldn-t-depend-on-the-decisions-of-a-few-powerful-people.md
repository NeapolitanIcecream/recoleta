# Privacy Protections Shouldn't Depend on the Decisions of a Few Powerful People

## Summary
This is a policy commentary article that uses the conflict between the U.S. Department of Defense and Anthropic over the boundaries of AI use as a case to argue that privacy protections should not depend on the ad hoc decisions of a small number of corporate executives, but should instead be stably guaranteed by law and institutions.

## Problem
- The problem the article addresses is that the privacy boundaries of U.S. citizens are currently being shaped by contract negotiations between the government and large technology companies, rather than being determined by clear legal constraints.
- This matters because the government is already able to conduct mass surveillance by purchasing commercial data and combining it with AI analysis; without legal limits, personal data can continue to be collected, profiled, and used for punitive purposes.
- Relying on individual CEOs to “do the right thing” is neither reliable nor sustainable; once a company’s position changes or the government applies pressure, civil rights lose their protection.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is not a new algorithm, but the use of a real-world case to illustrate an institutional gap: when Anthropic signed a contract with the Pentagon in 2025, it restricted its technology from being used for mass surveillance inside the United States or for fully autonomous weapons, but in early 2026 the Department of Defense demanded unrestricted use, and Anthropic was punished with contract termination after refusing.
- Put most simply, the article’s approach is to shift “privacy protection” from voluntary company terms to rules that are enforced through legislation by Congress and interpretation by the courts.
- The author further lists existing government data-purchasing and surveillance practices, such as Customs and Border Protection purchasing advertising data, ICE using device-mapping tools based on phone data, and intelligence systems promoting centralized data-broker markets, showing that the risk is not hypothetical but already occurring.
- The article also argues that the real solution is to close the legal loophole that allows the government to buy personal information and to establish more proactive, enforceable restrictions, rather than leaving responsibility to private companies.

## Results
- This is not a technical paper and does not provide experimental data, model metrics, or benchmark results.
- The most concrete event outcome described in the article is that the U.S. military terminated its **$200 million** contract with Anthropic and ordered other military contractors to stop using its products because the two sides clashed over what purposes the technology could be used for.
- The policy-progress data given in the article is that a bill intended to close the loophole allowing the government to buy personal information passed the U.S. House of Representatives in **2024**, but was stalled in the Senate, showing that legislative protections are still not in place.
- The public-opinion data cited in the article is that **71%** of U.S. adults are concerned about how the government uses their data; among adults who have heard of AI, **70%** “hardly trust or do not trust at all” how companies use these products.
- The article’s central concluding claim is that if privacy protection continues to depend on corporate contract terms and CEO decisions, it will not produce stable, fair, and sustainable protection of civil rights.
