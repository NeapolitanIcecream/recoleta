# Designing Secure Software

## Summary
This is an introductory text on software security for developers. Its central argument is that security should not be “added on” before release, but incorporated into software development during the design phase. It is more of a methodology and mindset training than a proposal of new technical algorithms or experimental systems.

## Problem
- Software security is too abstract, too broad in scope, and too complex for most developers, resulting in a high barrier to learning and practice.
- A common industry practice is to bring in experts to “harden” software late in development, creating a remedial, after-the-fact approach to security instead of building it in during early design.
- Outside large companies, many teams lack security design reviews and resident security experts, leading to fragmented knowledge and unsystematic practice.

## Approach
- Uses “secure design” as the main thread, emphasizing systematic consideration of security during the software **design phase** rather than last-minute fixes before release.
- Explains security concepts through stories, analogies, and simplified examples, deliberately lowering the barrier to understanding and helping readers build basic security intuition and mental models.
- Focuses on the “security mindset” and core concepts rather than trying to exhaustively cover every detail or provide universally applicable rules.
- Advocates gradually improving the security of real systems through continuous small improvements combined with specific design and implementation contexts.

## Results
- The provided excerpt **does not report any quantitative experimental results**; there are no datasets, metrics, baselines, or numerical comparisons.
- The strongest concrete claim is that shifting security from being “bolted on at the end of development” to being “built in during the design phase” can help developers identify risks earlier and establish more sustainable security practices.
- The author explicitly defines the goal as enabling more software practitioners to acquire a “good enough” foundational understanding of security, so they can continue learning independently and make incremental improvements.
- The text also claims that the book tries to address the widespread lack of security design reviews and systematic security education in the industry, but this is an experiential and educational claim rather than a breakthrough validated by quantitative evidence.
