# Satellite firm pauses imagery after revealing Iran's attacks on US bases

## Summary
This is a news report about the commercial satellite company Planet temporarily restricting the public release of remote sensing imagery during the conflict in the Middle East, rather than an academic research paper. Its core message is: to prevent belligerents from using near-real-time commercial satellite imagery for battle damage assessment, Planet has imposed a 96-hour delay on newly collected imagery from certain areas.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is that near-real-time commercial satellite imagery could be directly used by parties to the conflict for **Battle Damage Assessment (BDA)**, helping them determine whether strikes hit their targets, which targets were damaged, and how to adjust subsequent attacks.
- This matters because commercial remote sensing platforms are ordinarily open to a broad range of civilian and government users, and maintaining highly timely public access during wartime could inadvertently make them part of a military intelligence chain.
- The article also implies a governance question: how to balance commercial data openness and journalistic transparency against national security and operational security.

## Approach
- Planet’s mechanism is very direct: it imposes a **mandatory 96-hour delay** on **all newly collected imagery** from the “Gulf States, Iraq, Kuwait, and adjacent conflict zones” before the imagery enters the public archive.
- This restriction is **regional and temporary access control**, not a complete halt in collection; the company continues to collect imagery, but delays access for ordinary users.
- **Imagery over Iran** remains available as soon as possible after collection, indicating that the policy is not a uniform region-wide blackout but a geographically differentiated approach.
- **Authorized government users** retain immediate access for “mission-critical operations,” showing that Planet is using tiered access rather than a complete shutdown.

## Results
- The article does not provide academic-style experiments, datasets, or benchmark results, so there are **no quantifiable research metrics** to report.
- The clearest operational outcome is that Planet announced a **96-hour delay** for imagery from specific parts of the Middle East, covering new imagery from the “Gulf States, Iraq, Kuwait, and adjacent conflict zones.”
- An illustrative impact is that prior to this, Planet imagery had already publicly shown the aftermath of Iranian missile and drone strikes, including **damage to the US Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain** and **damage to a US-built early warning radar in Qatar valued at $1 billion**.
- Planet’s stated core rationale is to prevent “adversarial actors” from using its data for **Battle Damage Assessment**; this is the main effect the company says the restriction is intended to achieve, but the article **does not provide quantitative validation of the policy’s effectiveness after implementation**.
