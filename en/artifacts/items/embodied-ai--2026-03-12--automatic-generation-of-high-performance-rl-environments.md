# Automatic Generation of High-Performance RL Environments

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for using coding agents to automatically translate reinforcement learning environments into high-performance implementations, while using hierarchical verification to ensure the semantics are basically equivalent. Its significance is that environment acceleration work that originally required months of manual engineering can be reduced to an automated workflow costing less than $10.

## Problem
- In reinforcement learning training, environment simulation often accounts for **50–90%** of wall-clock time, and complex environments can become the main bottleneck.
- Existing high-performance environments usually rely on hand-written rewrites tailored to a single domain, making them costly and hard to reuse, which limits the range of environments researchers can train on.
- End-to-end rollout comparison alone makes it difficult to locate translation errors; if semantic deviations in the environment go unnoticed, they directly contaminate training signals and policy evaluation.

## Approach
- The core method is to have coding agents translate the reference environment module by module into **JAX** or **Rust**, where JAX is used for pure-function/GPU-parallel settings and Rust is used for state-heavy CPU-parallel environments.
- A four-level hierarchical verification loop is used: **L1 property tests** check single-module inputs and outputs, **L2 interaction tests** check cross-module state propagation, **L3 rollout comparison** compares full trajectories step by step, and **L4 cross-backend policy transfer** detects sim-to-sim gap.
- Once any level fails, the process rolls back and repairs the corresponding module before re-verifying; the authors emphasize that “iterative repair + hierarchical testing,” rather than one-shot generation, is the key to success.
- Humans mainly provide a general prompt template, module decomposition, and test structure; all target code is generated by the agent, and the total agent compute cost for a single environment is typically **<$10**.

## Results
- Validated on **5 environments**: EmuRust, PokeJAX, HalfCheetah, TCGJax, and Pong, covering discrete games, physics simulation, hardware emulation, and multi-agent systems.
- **PokeJAX**: random-action throughput increased from **21K SPS** to **500±9M SPS**, reaching **23,810×**; PPO training increased from **681 SPS** to **15.2±0.2M SPS**, reaching **22,320×**, relative to the TypeScript Showdown baseline.
- **Puffer Pong**: GRU rollout increased from **4.5M** to **140M SPS** (**31×**); GRU PPO increased from **854K** to **35.5M SPS** (**42×**), still showing a large improvement over an optimized C baseline.
- **HalfCheetah**: reached **1.66M SPS**, roughly on par with **MJX 1.6M SPS** (**1.04×**), and **5.0×** higher than **Brax 160K/4Kb**; **37×** higher than the Gymnasium CPU baseline of **45K**.
- **TCGJax**: random actions **140K → 717±0.6K SPS (5.1×)**, PPO **23K → 153±5K SPS (6.6×)**; claimed to be the first deployable JAX Pokémon card-game RL engine.
- All **5/5** environments passed L3 and L4 verification; for example, PokeJAX had identical cross-backend evaluation win rates (**0.313±0.007** and **0.313±0.007**, or **0.406±0.003** and **0.406±0.003**), supporting the claim of “zero sim-to-sim gap.” In terms of translation cost, the agent costs for the 5 environments were approximately **$0.43, $6, $3.26, $4.98, $0.05** respectively.
