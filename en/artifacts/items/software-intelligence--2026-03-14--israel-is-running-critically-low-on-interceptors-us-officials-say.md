# Israel is running critically low on interceptors, US officials say

## Summary
This report points out that Israel is facing a critical shortage of ballistic missile interceptors amid its ongoing conflict with Iran, exposing the immense pressure that high-intensity air defense operations place on stockpiles and production capacity. Its significance lies in the fact that this level of consumption could weaken Israel’s long-term defensive capabilities and affect US stockpiles, aid policy, and the regional military balance.

## Problem
- The issue highlighted in the report is that Israel is “critically low” on interceptors used to stop ballistic missiles, and these weapons are among the most effective defensive tools against long-range missile attacks.
- This matters because if interceptor stocks are depleted, Israel’s ability to defend against Iran’s long-range strikes will decline, forcing it to rely on alternatives such as fighter jets, which could raise both the cost and risk of defense.
- The problem also spills over to the US: if the US transfers or sells its own interceptors, it could further squeeze domestic stockpiles and affect longer-term readiness.

## Approach
- This is not a research paper and does not propose a technical algorithm; the core mechanism is an analysis, based on statements from US officials and public information, of the relationship between interceptor stockpile consumption, replenishment capacity, and aid options.
- The report follows a simple logic: frequent missile attacks -> sustained launches from advanced interceptor systems -> rapid stockpile decline -> constrained defense options, while also prompting discussion of US replenishment and transfer capacity.
- The article also adds factors that may affect the rate of depletion, such as reports that Iran has added cluster munitions to its missiles, which could further increase interception demand.
- At the same time, the report places the issue in the broader context of defense industrial production, noting that the US Department of Defense moved in January this year to significantly increase THAAD production to prepare for prolonged high-consumption scenarios.

## Results
- The most important quantitative detail is that in June of last year, during the **12-day** war with Iran, the US fired **more than 150 THAAD interceptors**, which CSIS estimated to be about **one-quarter** of the US inventory at the time.
- According to some reports, the US also used about **$2.4 billion** worth of Patriot interceptors in the first **5 days** of the current war.
- In describing battlefield outcomes, the White House said that after joint US-Israeli operations, Iranian **drone attacks fell by 95%** and **ballistic missile attacks fell by 90%**; however, these figures are official claims and not the same as independently verified results.
- As for Israel’s own inventory, the report’s conclusion is that it is in a “critical shortage” and that the US had known for months, but it **does not provide precise data such as Israel’s remaining interceptor count, consumption curve, or replenishment rate**.
- The report also mentions that the US announced last week the sale to Israel of **12,000** BLU-110A/B **1,000-pound** general-purpose bomb bodies, but this is offensive ammunition resupply and does not directly solve the shortage of ballistic missile interceptors.
