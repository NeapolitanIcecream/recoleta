# SuperSkillsStack: Agency, Domain Knowledge, Imagination, and Taste in Human-AI Design Education

## Summary
This paper studies how student teams in design courses incorporate generative AI into real design projects, and uses the SuperSkillsStack framework to explain which “human capabilities” remain irreplaceable. The core conclusion is that AI mainly accelerates early-stage ideation and organization, but what truly determines design quality is still human judgment, contextual understanding, and aesthetic discernment.

## Problem
- The paper addresses the question of how design education can use generative AI while continuing to cultivate students’ key human capabilities, rather than letting students outsource thinking to AI.
- This matters because AI can already rapidly generate ideas, summarize materials, and produce visual content, but it lacks genuine understanding of specific contexts, user experience, cultural settings, and real-world constraints.
- There is already much discussion of this issue, but there is a lack of empirical evidence on how students actually collaborate with AI in real course projects, especially a systematic analysis of how human judgment enters the process.

## Approach
- The authors propose and use **SuperSkillsStack** as an analytical framework, consisting of four capabilities: **Agency** (actively controlling how AI is used), **Domain Knowledge** (using field and domain knowledge to verify AI), **Imagination** (using AI to expand ideas), and **Taste** (judging which outputs are worth keeping and refining).
- The research data comes from reflective writing by **80 student teams** in a human-centered design course, with projects covering real settings such as public transportation, hawker centers, medical clinics, residences, gyms, and parks.
- The method is **qualitative thematic analysis**: first applying framework-based coding according to the four capabilities, then further identifying patterns in the design process such as observation, ideation, analysis, evaluation, and refinement.
- Students were free to choose whether and when to use ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude, Microsoft Transcribe, and image-generation tools, allowing the study to observe a more natural human-AI workflow.
- Put simply, the core mechanism of the paper is this: examine in students’ reflections where AI is most useful, where it is unreliable, and then use the “four-layer human capabilities” to explain why humans still need to steer the process in the end.

## Results
- The dataset includes **80 student teams**; this is the clearest sample size reported in the paper. The study is not a benchmark test, so it does not report machine learning metrics such as accuracy, F1, or win rate.
- The clearest frequency-related finding reported is that **Taste appears in every team’s reflection**; **Domain Knowledge appears in most reflections**; **Agency frequently appears in descriptions of tool choice**; and **Imagination mainly appears during early-stage brainstorming**.
- The study argues that AI’s primary role is concentrated in the early stages of design, such as **brainstorming, information synthesis, and problem framing**, rather than field observation, interview interpretation, or final design judgment.
- The paper summarizes a specific **four-step collaboration process**: **1) AI generates initial ideas and summaries; 2) students interpret and verify them using domain knowledge; 3) teams filter them through discussion; 4) students continue refining the design using human judgment**.
- The paper does not report numerical comparisons with other methods or course setups, nor does it provide percentage improvements; its strongest concrete conclusion is that in design education, generative AI is more like a **cognitive accelerator** than a replacement for human creativity or design judgment.
- In educational terms, the authors argue that the real capabilities worth cultivating are not simply “being better at using tools,” but the four higher-order capabilities of **Agency, Domain Knowledge, Imagination, and Taste** to support effective human-AI collaboration.
