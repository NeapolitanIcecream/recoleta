# Submit to an Appropriate Journal

## Summary
This article offers a set of practical recommendations for submitting mathematics papers: do not pursue prestigious journals for their own sake, but instead align the paper’s quality, scope fit, and journal tier. Its core value is helping authors reduce ineffective submissions, shorten the ordeal of peer review, and improve the probability of acceptance by an appropriate journal.

## Problem
- The problem it addresses is: **what tier, scope, and editorial structure of journal a research paper should be submitted to**, and how to avoid rejection after a long review process caused by a “submission mismatch.”
- This matters because inappropriate submissions can lead to **lengthy processes, harsh reviews, high publication standards, and ultimate rejection**, wasting the time of both authors and referees.
- The article also addresses a common uncertainty for authors: when one is unsure whether a paper meets the standards of a top journal, how should one systematically evaluate the paper itself?

## Approach
- The core method is simple: **first assess the paper honestly, then choose a journal that matches its quality and subject matter**, rather than fixating first on the most famous venues.
- The author proposes a multidimensional scoring framework that examines papers according to four criteria: **correctness, novelty, professionalism, presentation**—for example, whether the results are correct, whether there is substantial novelty, whether the writing is professional, and whether the structure is clear.
- The concrete journal-selection process includes: checking whether the journal covers the paper’s field; seeing whether the editorial board includes an editor who understands that area; looking at whether the journal has published similar work in the past; and browsing sample issues to judge whether the style and threshold are a good match.
- The author also gives procedural advice: in general, do not submit two unrelated papers to the same journal at the same time; if rejected, do not take it personally, but instead use the feedback to revise the paper and submit it to a more suitable or less selective journal.

## Results
- The text **is not an experimental paper, has no dataset, baseline model, or quantitative metrics**, and therefore **has no quantitative results to report**.
- One of the strongest concrete conclusions is that for top journals such as **JAMS**, a paper must make referees feel “**excited and enthusiastic**”; work that is merely a “**good, solid application of mostly standard techniques**” to solve a “**moderately interesting problem**” is explicitly judged by the author to have a **very low** probability of acceptance, even if it would be readily published elsewhere.
- The author argues that if a paper’s primary content falls outside the scope of “research mathematics” (for example, recreational mathematics, physics, philosophy, biology, computer science, etc.), then a research mathematics journal is **very unlikely to consider it in scope**.
- The article proposes an actionable submission strategy: if a paper scores highly in only a small subset of the four rubric categories, it is more suitable for a **lower-tier journal**; if additional effort improves the exposition and structure, then one can “**upscale**” and aim for a higher-tier journal.
- On rejection, the most specific claim the author gives is that a **positive rejection rate** at reputable journals is normal; after rejection, one should revise based on the feedback and usually submit to a **less selective** journal.
