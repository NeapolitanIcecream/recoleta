# A Collaborative and Pattern-Based Training Approach to Knowledge Acquisition and Decision-Making During the Design of Software Architectures Courses: A Case Study

## Summary
This paper studies a collaborative, pattern-based teaching approach for undergraduate software architecture courses: using the SAGITA guide and a catalog of training patterns to help instructors design courses more systematically and enable students to get closer to architecture decision-making practices in industry. The case took place in a joint course taught by two universities in Colombia and Argentina, with a focus on evaluating competency development, instructors' perceived usefulness, and students' course experience.

## Problem
- Software architecture education has long faced a **disconnect between academic training and industry needs**, leaving graduates insufficiently prepared for architecture design, trade-off analysis, and decision-making in business contexts.
- Software architecture itself is **abstract, depends on prerequisite knowledge, and is difficult to reproduce in realistic project environments in the classroom**, making it hard for students to develop transferable practical skills.
- Course designers lack a set of **reusable, practical teaching strategies** to continuously narrow the gap between course content, teaching methods, and rapidly changing industry demands.

## Approach
- The paper proposes a software architecture course design guide called **SAGITA** and compiles **7 training patterns**, mapping common teaching challenges to reusable instructional solutions such as mini-projects, large-project, cases-based, problem-solving, and games-based.
- A **collaborative case study** was conducted in 2023 courses at the two universities: they first aligned teaching objectives, course content, and pacing, and then taught and practiced around the same project, **OpenMarket**.
- The two universities used different main strategies: UNICAUCA mainly adopted **mini-projects-based training**, while UNPL mainly adopted **large-project-based training**; both also introduced **cases-based training**, inviting industry architects to explain real cases.
- In the later part of the course, **cross-national global collaborative development** was added, merging student teams from both universities for a joint iteration to simulate a distributed industrial collaboration scenario.
- Results were evaluated through **questionnaires, document analysis, and semi-structured interviews**, with particular attention to student competency attainment, instructors' perceptions of the guide's usefulness/ease of use, and student satisfaction; the framework for instructor perception referenced TAM.

## Results
- The case study covered **2 universities, 2 courses, and 40 students**: **14** at UNPL and **26** at UNICAUCA.
- The courses included **3** industry talks, with cases including Argentina's Ministry of Economy **E-sidif**, Mercado Libre's **migration from monolith to microservices**, and Banco Galicia's **large-scale architecture** practices.
- In the cross-university global collaboration phase, the authors explicitly report that because the joint iteration was short and required collaboration with **5 teams**, **only 1 team** achieved good collaboration and produced an integrated and executable software solution. This indicates that although the design is close to a real industrial environment, it is difficult to execute.
- Student self-assessments show that the course significantly improved several competencies, especially **C05** (independently evaluating whether an architecture satisfies functional/non-functional requirements), **C08** (conducting architectural trade-off analysis), and **C11** (maintaining existing systems and their architecture to support evolution); the paper says a “**large proportion**” of students felt the course “**helped a lot**.”
- Some competencies also showed limited improvement, such as **C22** (conducting regular code reviews), which the paper says a “**large proportion**” of students felt the course only “**helped a little**.”
- The paper **does not provide clear quantitative performance metrics** (such as means, variances, significance tests, or percentage-point comparisons with baseline courses); the strongest concrete conclusion is that this pattern-based and collaborative teaching approach is perceived to improve practical experience, teamwork, complex problem solving, architectural understanding, and industry relevance, at the cost of requiring additional planning and coordination effort from instructors.
