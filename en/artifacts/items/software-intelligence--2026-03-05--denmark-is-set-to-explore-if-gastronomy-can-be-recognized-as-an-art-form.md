# Denmark is set to explore if gastronomy can be recognized as an art form

## Summary
This article discusses whether Denmark should formally recognize fine dining/gastronomy as an art form. The core backdrop is that some Michelin-starred restaurants in Copenhagen are combining food, performance, music, and social issues into immersive experiences. It is not a technical paper, but a report about cultural policy, the definition of art, and the boundaries of the restaurant industry.

## Problem
- The issue to be addressed is: **should gastronomy, especially high-end, conceptual, immersive dining, be formally regarded by law and cultural institutions as “art” rather than “craft”**.
- This matters because once it is recognized as art, chefs and restaurant projects could gain access to **state subsidies, foundation funding, and higher cultural status**, changing how resources are distributed in the industry and how society perceives it.
- The point of controversy is that supporters argue food can carry expression just like painting or music; opponents argue that food’s core nature is to be consumed, and that this functional property makes it essentially different from art.

## Approach
- In January 2025, Denmark’s culture minister said the government would **explore** whether to formally recognize gastronomy as an art form; if advanced, it would ultimately require a vote in Denmark’s **179-seat parliament** to complete the reclassification from “craft” to “art”.
- The practical path taken by restaurants supporting this idea is to expand dining from merely serving dishes into an **immersive narrative medium**: for example, Alchemist uses projections, performance, music, spatial design, and food together to express issues such as marine plastic pollution, state surveillance, and animal welfare.
- The core mechanism can be simply understood as: **treating “food” as a medium for expressing ideas, not merely an edible commodity**; chefs design a complete experience like artists, guiding diners to reflect on social issues while they eat.
- The article also presents the opposing logic: critics argue that even if fine dining can create experiences, it still differs from art, because art can have expression as its sole purpose, whereas food must also fulfill the function of “being eaten”.

## Results
- The article **does not provide quantitative results in the sense of experiments, datasets, or academic benchmarks**, so there is no traditional research-style performance breakthrough.
- The strongest concrete development is at the policy level: in **January 2025**, the Danish government publicly said it would explore this issue; if implemented, Denmark could become the **first** country to place cooking/fine dining in a legal position close to that of traditional arts.
- As for supporting examples, the Alchemist restaurant opened in **2019**, was ranked the **world’s 5th-best restaurant in 2025**, and holds **2 Michelin stars**; its dining experience includes about **50 “impressions”**, serving as a representative example for the argument that “gastronomy can become art.”
- In terms of industry background, Denmark, a country of about **6 million people**, has **37 Michelin-starred restaurants**, showing that it has the restaurant ecosystem needed to support this kind of cultural recognition.
- The article also emphasizes the obstacles: the policy is still in the **exploratory phase（探索阶段）**, and may be affected by the **March 24 election**; the art world and some chefs worry that this could intensify **competition for arts funding** and create conceptual confusion.
