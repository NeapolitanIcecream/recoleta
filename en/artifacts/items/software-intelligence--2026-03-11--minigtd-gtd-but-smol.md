# MiniGTD: GTD but Smol

## Summary
miniGTD proposes a minimalist version of the GTD personal task management method: every morning, use a small notebook to rewrite that day’s goals and next actions from scratch, a process that takes only about 2 minutes. The author claims it delivers “most” of the benefits of traditional GTD with extremely low friction, and is easier to sustain over the long term.

## Problem
- Traditional GTD systems are too complex and heavy for some people, and can "collapse under their own weight in the middle of a crisis," making them hard to use consistently.
- Long task lists and continuously scrolling information increase cognitive load, making it harder to sort, decide, and start acting.
- If a task management system has high maintenance costs, then even if it is theoretically complete, it may lose practical value simply because people do not use it; this affects day-to-day productivity and management of mental burden.

## Approach
- Every morning, start from a blank page, write down the date, that day’s goals, and at least one "Next Action" for each goal.
- The entire system is deliberately limited to one page, one rewrite, and about 2 minutes, to minimize maintenance friction and increase actual follow-through.
- It relies on "rewriting from memory" as a natural filtering and prioritization mechanism: the things you can remember and are willing to move forward on that day get priority on the day’s page.
- To reduce omissions, after finishing, look back at the previous page and add anything that may have been missed; this substitutes for the heavyweight mechanisms in full GTD aimed at ensuring that nothing ever slips through the cracks.
- The author also mentions an optional "Waiting For" section, but the core method is essentially a minimalist daily plan plus next-action list.

## Results
- The article does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or benchmark tests, so there are no verifiable quantitative results.
- The author’s central quantitative claim is that rewriting the system each day "only takes 2 minutes" or "1 to 2 minutes."
- The author claims that in "literally 1–2 minutes," this method delivers "almost all the value" of the author’s full GTD system.
- The author further claims the effect is even greater: rewriting from memory naturally performs sorting and prioritization, while the one-page limit reduces decision paralysis caused by too much information.
- No paper-style comparisons, metrics, sample sizes, or long-term controlled results are provided; the evidence is mainly the author’s long-term personal practice and anecdotal statements.
