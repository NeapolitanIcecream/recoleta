# Hazardous substances found in all headphones tested by ToxFREE project

## Summary
This is a chemical safety investigation of headphone products on the Central European market, which found that all tested headphones contained hazardous chemicals, revealing a systemic gap in consumer electronics regulation. The article’s core contribution is to provide measured evidence across five countries and across price ranges, and to call on the EU to shift from regulating substances one by one to imposing restrictions on entire chemical classes.

## Problem
- The study aims to address whether headphones on the market commonly contain hazardous chemicals such as endocrine disruptors, plasticizers, and flame retardants, and whether current regulation is sufficient to protect consumers.
- This is important because headphones are products used frequently, for long periods, and in direct contact with the skin; heat and sweat may promote chemical migration, and long-term exposure may particularly affect vulnerable groups such as teenagers.
- The article also points out the problem of “regrettable substitution”: restricted hazardous chemicals are often replaced by structurally similar substitutes with similar toxicity, causing regulation to lag behind while risks persist.

## Approach
- As part of the ToxFree LIFE for All project, the study purchased and analysed headphone samples in five countries: the Czech Republic, Slovakia, Hungary, Slovenia, and Austria.
- A total of **81 headphone products** and **180 hard and soft plastic component samples** were tested, covering products marketed to children, teenagers, and adults, as well as both premium brands and cheap imported products.
- The analysis focused on hazardous chemicals such as bisphenols, phthalates, flame retardants, and chlorinated paraffins, comparing their frequency of occurrence and concentration levels.
- The study also interpreted the findings against regulatory thresholds, for example by comparing detected bisphenol concentrations with the **10 mg/kg** limit previously proposed by ECHA.

## Results
- Traces of hazardous chemicals were found in **100% (81/81)** of the tested headphones, including bisphenols, phthalates, and flame retardants, showing that contamination is not limited to individual brands or low-end products.
- **98%** of samples contained **BPA**, and **more than 75%** contained **BPS**, indicating that both restricted substances and their substitutes are already widespread in headphones.
- The highest bisphenol concentration reached **351 mg/kg**, about **35 times** higher than the **10 mg/kg** limit proposed by ECHA.
- The most toxic samples came from products purchased on international online marketplace platforms, but the study also makes clear that hazardous chemicals appear across the **entire price range**, and a higher price does not mean greater safety.
- The article does not provide model metrics or statistical significance results in the traditional academic sense; the strongest concrete conclusion is that in spot checks across **5 countries** and **81 products**, all samples contained hazardous chemicals, supporting its claim of a “systemic failure” in regulation.
