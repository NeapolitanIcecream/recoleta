# Delay the Inference

## Summary
This article proposes “Delay the Inference”: before immediately handing a half-formed idea to AI, preserve a period of independent thinking first. The core argument is that AI significantly boosts productivity, but outsourcing thought too early may weaken the internal incubation process required for forming original ideas.

## Problem
- The article tries to address this problem: when AI is always available, will people seek help too early, before their ideas have taken shape, and thereby treat their own thinking like a “slow API call” to be avoided?
- This matters because high-quality and original ideas often depend on uninterrupted internal wandering, association, and incubation, rather than merely getting answers faster.
- The author worries that if AI continually takes over the “first hop” of cognitive labor, what may be lost is not intelligence itself, but the ability, interest, and creative formative stage of independent thinking.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: **do not ask AI immediately when a thought first appears**; instead, consciously “delay the inference / delay calling the model.”
- The author connects the double meaning of “inference”: in AI, it refers to a model generating output from input; in human cognition, it refers to moving from an idea toward a conclusion. The suggestion is to “not run the model yet” in both senses.
- The practical workflow is to first record the raw idea, write a rough first draft yourself, then do relevant research, and only afterward use AI for brainstorming, leaving time to digest the feedback, and finally use AI to help revise.
- The theoretical intuition comes from creativity research: the interaction between the default mode network and the executive control system may support that original thinking phase of “drifting first, taking shape later.”

## Results
- The article **does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or quantitative metrics**, so there are no precise numerical results or statistical comparisons with baselines to report.
- The most specific evidence the author gives is personal experience: over the past **6–12 months**, with the help of Claude/ChatGPT, they have been able to do more and better writing work that previously felt “almost out of reach.”
- The article’s central claim is that AI does bring significant productivity gains, but if one seeks help immediately at the stage of a “half-formed idea,” it may compress the internal incubation process needed for generating original thought.
- Therefore, its “breakthrough” is not in an algorithm or experiment, but in proposing an actionable human-AI collaboration principle: think independently and draft first, then use AI as a later-stage tool for expansion, feedback, and polishing.
