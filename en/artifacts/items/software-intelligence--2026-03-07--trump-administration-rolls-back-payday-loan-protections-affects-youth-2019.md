# Trump administration rolls back payday loan protections, affects youth (2019)

## Summary
This article reports that in 2019 the U.S. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) proposed rescinding some payday loan protections, especially abandoning the requirement that lenders verify a borrower's "ability to repay." The article emphasizes that this move could make it easier for millions of young borrowers to fall into debt traps tied to high-interest, short-term loans.

## Problem
- The issue the article focuses on is whether borrowers will become more likely to obtain high-risk, hard-to-repay short-term loans after the government loosens federal regulation of payday loans.
- This matters because such loans are usually small in size but extremely costly, with an average annual percentage rate close to **400%**, making it easy for borrowers to repeatedly borrow new loans to repay old ones and fall into a long-term debt cycle.
- Young people are especially affected: about **10 million** millennials have used this type of loan in the past two years, and nearly **40%** of surveyed Gen Z respondents had seriously considered applying.

## Approach
- This is not an academic paper, but a policy and market impact analysis report. Its core mechanism is a comparison between the **Obama-era rule finalized in 2017** and the **2019 repeal proposal led by the Trump administration**.
- The key protection being removed is the "**ability-to-repay verification**": the original rule required lenders to verify information such as income, rent, and student loans before issuing a loan, confirming that borrowers could repay on time.
- After review, the CFPB argued that this requirement would limit credit availability and market competition, and therefore proposed removing it; at the same time, it retained a weaker restriction, namely prohibiting lenders from repeatedly debiting borrowers' bank accounts directly.
- By citing the CFPB, Pew, the National Consumer Law Center, and media survey data, the article explains how the policy change could affect borrowing behavior, default risk, and the exposure of young groups.

## Results
- The article does not present new experimental or causal identification findings; it mainly summarizes policy decisions and existing statistical evidence.
- The national average annual percentage rate for payday loans is about **400%**, more than **20 times** the average credit card interest rate.
- The CFPB found that nearly **1/4** of payday loans are reborrowed **9 times or more**.
- Pew research says borrowers typically need about **5 months** to repay the loan, paying an average of **$520** in additional finance charges, not including the original loan principal.
- There are about **23,000** payday lenders nationwide, close to **twice** the number of McDonald's locations.
- A CNBC/Morning Consult survey showed that **13%** of millennials (ages 22–37) had taken out a payday loan in the past two years; about **40%** of Gen Z (ages 18–21) had seriously considered applying. The article also says there are currently about **75.4 million** millennials in the United States.
