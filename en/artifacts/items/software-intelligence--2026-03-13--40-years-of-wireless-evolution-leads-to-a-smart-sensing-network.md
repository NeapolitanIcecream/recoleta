# 40 Years of Wireless Evolution Leads to a Smart, Sensing Network

## Summary
This is a review article focused on industry and technology trends. It looks back at how 1G through 5G gradually reshaped the relationship among “devices, networks, and applications,” and argues that 6G will push wireless networks into a new stage that is “AI-native, integrates communication and sensing, and supports large-scale collaboration among intelligent agents.”

## Problem
- The article tries to answer why 6G should not be understood merely as “a faster wireless network,” but rather as a fundamental shift in the wireless systems paradigm.
- Its central concern is that existing generational narratives overemphasize bandwidth, latency, and spectrum, while overlooking the system-level intelligence created by the co-evolution of devices, networks, and applications.
- This matters because future networks will need to support real-time collaboration among massive numbers of digital and physical AI agents across critical scenarios such as healthcare, transportation, and manufacturing; simply improving transmission performance will no longer be enough.

## Approach
- It uses a historical review framework, analyzing 1G through 6G within a unified three-part evolutionary model of “Devices / Networks / Applications.”
- It describes 1G-5G as a continuous progression from analog voice, to digital voice and SMS, to mobile data, to the mobile Internet, and then to “edge-connected intelligence.”
- For 6G, it proposes a conceptual mechanism: the network will become an **AI-native fabric** capable of dynamically predicting demand, allocating resources in real time, and integrating communication and sensing as ISAC (integrated sensing and communication).
- It further argues that 6G will combine terrestrial networks, satellite constellations, and high-altitude platforms to create more continuous wide-area coverage, supporting cross-domain collaboration between digital agents and physical agents.
- Put simply, the authors’ core view is that 6G is not just “a pipe for transmitting data,” but will become a network nervous system that can **see the environment, understand demand, and coordinate action**.

## Results
- The article does not provide experiments, benchmark datasets, or paper-style quantitative evaluation results; it mainly offers industry trend judgments and cross-generational capability comparisons.
- Its quantitative description of 4G is that LTE speeds can be **up to 100 times faster than 3G**, thereby enabling applications such as video streaming, maps, and video calls.
- Its quantitative description of 3G is that network speeds reached **hundreds of kilobits per second**, making mobile email and early web access usable for the first time.
- Its quantitative description of 5G is that on-device AI hardware can perform **trillions of operations per second**, while also supporting low latency, network slicing, and edge computing.
- Its strongest claim about 6G is that by **the end of this decade**, networks will evolve into a “universal data fabric” supporting **real-time collaboration among millions of agents**, and through integrated communication and sensing plus integrated terrestrial-space coverage, will serve scenarios such as healthcare, transportation, and factories.
- The article’s “breakthrough” conclusion is not that any experimental metric has been surpassed, but rather a directional judgment: 6G will blur the boundaries among devices, networks, and applications, transforming the network from connectivity infrastructure into an intelligent system that can sense, decide, and coordinate.
