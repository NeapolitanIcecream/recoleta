# One of Grammarly's 'experts' is suing the company over its AI feature

## Summary
This article reports that Grammarly / Superhuman’s “Expert Review” AI feature sparked legal disputes for using the identities of real journalists and authors without consent, and has since been urgently taken offline. Its core significance lies not in an algorithmic breakthrough, but in how generative products can legally and compliantly invoke real people’s identities and reputations.

## Problem
- The feature attached the names and identities of real people to AI-generated writing suggestions, but the relevant “experts” had not authorized it.
- This may infringe privacy and publicity rights, and may violate laws prohibiting the use of someone’s identity for commercial purposes without their consent.
- This matters because if consumer-facing AI products automatically embed “real-person endorsement” into generated content, they create legal, trust, and platform-governance risks.

## Approach
- At the system level, the feature cited or simulated “expert perspectives” in AI editing suggestions, and directly displayed identity labels of real journalists, editors, authors, and others.
- According to the CEO, the product goal was to help users discover “influential perspectives and scholarship,” while also enabling experts to build deeper relationships with their audiences.
- But based on the disclosures, the implementation did not obtain explicit authorization first; instead, it launched the feature first and only afterward provided an opt-out channel for writers and academics.
- After the controversy escalated, the company announced it was disabling the feature and said it would rethink its approach.

## Results
- Clear legal action has already emerged: journalist Julia Angwin filed a class-action lawsuit, accusing the company of using her identity for commercial purposes without consent.
- The Verge’s testing found that the identities of multiple real media figures were used in AI suggestions, including Casey Newton, as well as several current Verge employees and editor-in-chief Nilay Patel.
- Earlier this week, the company launched a related inbox allowing authors and academics to request opt-out; later the same day, it announced it was shutting the feature down entirely.
- The text provides no quantitative technical results such as model performance, accuracy, user growth, or baseline comparisons.
- The strongest verifiable conclusion is that this AI feature faced a class-action lawsuit over identity-use disputes and was taken offline, indicating major flaws in its product/compliance design.
