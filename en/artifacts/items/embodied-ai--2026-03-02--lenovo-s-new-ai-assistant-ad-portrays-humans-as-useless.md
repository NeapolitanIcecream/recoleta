# Lenovo's new AI assistant ad portrays humans as useless

## Summary
This article is not a research paper, but an opinion news piece criticizing Lenovo for portraying human employees as incompetent and redundant in an advertisement showcasing its AI assistant Qira. The core argument is that the ad both exaggerates the real-world capabilities of AI assistants and sends an unfriendly message about the future relationship between humans and work.

## Problem
- The article examines whether companies, when promoting AI assistants, depict humans as roles that “only watch from the sidelines and provide no real value,” and how that may shape public understanding of work.
- This matters because it concerns the real capability boundaries of AI products, how companies frame automation narratives, and the further amplification of worker anxiety about replacement.
- The article also points out that if the ad promises AI can flawlessly handle reports, budget explanations, and meeting support, that conflicts with the unreliability of real-world AI systems.

## Approach
- The article’s main “method” is not to propose a technical solution, but to conduct a scene-by-scene critical analysis of Lenovo’s Qira advertisement.
- Put simply, the author argues that in the ad, the employee Jeff can do almost nothing: the AI generates a report from a sketch, answers budget questions, and carries the meeting flow, leaving the human only to “take the credit.”
- Based on these scenes, the author makes a two-layer critique: first, that the ad conveys the value judgment that “human employees are completely useless”; second, that it implies AI assistants can already reliably replace knowledge workers, when reality is not like that.
- The article also situates this critique within the broader backdrop of the current AI industry narrative that “large numbers of jobs are about to be eliminated,” explaining why the ad feels especially jarring.

## Results
- There are no quantitative experiments, datasets, baseline models, or numerical metrics; this is not technical research, so there are no benchmark results to report.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that **100% of the key office output** in the ad is completed by Qira, while “not a single productive action is performed by a human” (the author’s direct critical summary).
- The article claims the ad shows the AI assistant completing **3 types of tasks**: generating a report from a sketch, accurately reciting Q1 budget details, and continuing to carry the main workload during a meeting.
- The author argues the ad fails in two ways: on one hand, it “promises capabilities that AI assistants currently cannot deliver reliably,” and on the other, it portrays the future employee as a “passive coffee-drinking observer.”
