# Preparing Students for AI-Driven Agile Development: A Project-Based AI Engineering Curriculum

## Summary
This paper proposes a project-based AI engineering curriculum that deeply integrates agile development with generative AI tools, aimed at cultivating students' ability to perform AI-driven agile development in realistic team environments. Using a second-semester undergraduate student project as a case study, the paper also provides an initial mixed-methods evaluation showing that this model helps improve practical skills and learning progress.

## Problem
- Existing engineering education often teaches agile methods and AI competencies separately, and mostly in a lecture-based format, making it difficult for students to understand how AI changes collaboration, quality assurance, and the division of responsibility in real projects.
- As LLMs and agentic tools enter requirements analysis, coding, testing, documentation, and other stages, curricula that still train students for "AI-free work settings" risk creating a skills mismatch.
- A key teaching challenge is how to allow AI-assisted development while ensuring that students genuinely master foundational knowledge rather than relying blindly on AI.

## Approach
- Build a project-based AI engineering training program: a shared core curriculum provides the foundation, followed by discipline-specific tracks, while consistently maintaining the theme of "AI as an engineering tool."
- About one-third of the curriculum workload is devoted to real team projects, where students use AI throughout an end-to-end agile process for requirements clarification, backlog refinement, architectural reasoning, coding, testing, documentation, and stakeholder simulation.
- In the case project, second-semester undergraduate students work in teams of 4–6 to develop a browser-based multiplayer board game over seven two-week Sprints, using Scrum events, cross-team demos, and continuous feedback.
- AI is not taught as a standalone course; instead, it is embedded into every teaching unit (such as React, TypeScript, and architecture courses) and daily assignments, while emphasizing human oversight of acceptance criteria, risk, ethical trade-offs, and final responsibility.
- To prevent "AI doing the learning for students," the curriculum includes individual 20-minute oral exams to verify students' understanding of their own code and their use of AI, combined with repository data, process documentation, and peer evaluation for assessment.

## Results
- The paper claims three main contributions: the curriculum concept and principles, a case study of AI-enhanced agile student projects, and an initial quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
- The clearest quantitative result concerns academic progress: the average share of missing ECTS dropped from 25% in WS 2021 to 12% in WS 2022, then to 4% in WS 2023 and 3% in WS 2024.
- In terms of absolute ECTS gap, the average credit shortfall improved from -45/180 in WS 2021 to -22/180 in WS 2022, -5/120 in WS 2023, and -2/60 in WS 2024.
- Qualitative findings include: teaching content must adapt quickly to new AI tools; individual oral exams are critical for ensuring foundational competence; and students began to "personify" ChatGPT as a team member.
- The paper does not report standard machine learning benchmarks, significance tests from controlled experiments, or more fine-grained software engineering output metrics; the strongest evidence is the early educational outcomes and lessons learned after three iterations of running the curriculum.
