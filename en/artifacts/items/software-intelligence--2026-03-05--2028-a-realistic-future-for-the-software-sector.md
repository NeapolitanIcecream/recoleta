# 2028: A Realistic Future for the Software Sector

## Summary
This is a rebuttal-style opinion piece aimed at software investing and enterprise technology decision-making. Its core argument is that AI will significantly reduce some code generation costs, but it will not cause most high-value software platforms to lose their moats. The author argues that software’s real defensibility mainly comes from data, workflow embedding, regulatory compliance, distributed systems capability, and market distribution, rather than the UI or visible features themselves.

## Problem
- The article responds to an investment narrative that “AI will destroy large amounts of SaaS/software platforms before 2028,” questioning its reduction of software value to “interfaces and code that can be quickly replicated.”
- The problem it seeks to address is: **As AI coding capabilities improve, will software platforms lose defensibility because development costs approach zero? Why does this matter for investing, procurement, and industry judgment?**
- This matters because if that assumption is wrong, enterprise software valuations, procurement strategies, platform replacement expectations, and judgments about disruption to payments/financial infrastructure will all systematically diverge from reality.

## Approach
- The author uses a **structured rebuttal** approach: first deconstructing the premise that “code costs fall to zero,” then analyzing the real moats of different software categories one by one.
- The core mechanism can be summarized simply: **AI is best at writing some portion of code, but the most expensive and hardest-to-replicate parts of software are usually not code-writing. They are domain knowledge, accumulated data, system scalability, compliance auditing, organizational processes, and long-term operational experience.**
- The article argues by category about where defensibility comes from: lightweight collaboration tools rely on distribution and inertia; vertical software relies on proprietary data; enterprise workflow software relies on years of configuration rules; systems of record rely on auditing and organizational context; infrastructure products rely on consistency/durability/fault tolerance; scientific software relies on physical and mathematical models; regulated software relies on validation and certification.
- In payments, the author further rebuts the claim that “AI will reroute payments from bank cards to stablecoins,” arguing that the real cost of payments includes dispute handling, consumer protection, KYC/AML, sanctions screening, FX, merchant acceptance networks, and regulatory capital requirements, not just on-chain transfer fees.

## Results
- The article **does not provide new experiments, benchmarks, or peer-reviewed quantitative results**; it mainly presents analytical claims based on industry experience.
- Contrary to the “development cost goes to zero” claim, the key figures cited by the author include: code generation accounts for about **25%** of a senior developer’s time, and even optimistic studies show only **20–55%** productivity gains in that area.
- The author says AI-generated code brings additional quality costs: vulnerability rates of about **40–45%**, **1.7×** as many issues per PR, and duplicated code reaching **8×** historical levels.
- Regarding enterprise procurement impact, the author notes that customer layoffs create mechanical seat contraction—for example, if a customer cuts staff by **15%**, related software licenses may also decline by **15%**—but describes this as seat and pricing pressure rather than evidence that software can be easily replaced wholesale.
- In high-throughput platform scenarios, the author uses specific engineering thresholds to emphasize irreplaceability: when loads spike by **50×** during extreme weather and affect **70%** of services, the system must still complete complex ticketing workflows within **500ms**; these figures are used to illustrate engineering complexity, not experimental results from a paper.
- In payment infrastructure, the author contrasts card-network **2–3%** interchange with stablecoins’ near-zero on-chain fees, arguing that the two are not directly comparable; the piece also cites facts such as Visa’s more than **100 million** merchant acceptance points globally and Wise’s **70+** global licenses to illustrate the compliance and distribution moats of payment networks.
