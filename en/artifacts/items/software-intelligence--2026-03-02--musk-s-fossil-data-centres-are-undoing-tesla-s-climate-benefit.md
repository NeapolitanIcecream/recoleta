# Musk's fossil data centres are undoing Tesla's climate benefit

## Summary
This article estimates the emissions from fossil gas units powering Musk’s generative AI data centres and compares them with the annual emissions reductions Tesla claims its products deliver. The core conclusion is that the direct emissions from these data centres may be significantly offsetting, and under some framings even exceeding, Tesla’s climate benefit.

## Problem
- The article seeks to answer: **how large is the true climate cost of Musk’s generative AI data centres**, and are they offsetting Tesla’s emissions-reduction contribution.
- This matters because data-centre emissions not only affect local air quality, but also create large-scale global greenhouse-gas emissions, while public understanding of their scale remains limited.
- The author also argues that AI infrastructure may be wrapped in narratives like “avoided emissions,” obscuring its real dependence on fossil energy and its climate harms.

## Approach
- Using **the number of gas turbines in permitting documents and associated emissions estimates**, the author first cites an existing figure: **51 gas turbines corresponding to 6.4 million tonnes of CO2e per year**.
- The author then worked with an anonymous analyst to estimate that **once all phases are completed, the buildout will include 86 fossil gas turbines emitting 11.3 million tonnes of CO2e annually**; the portion already in operation is about **5.1 million tonnes of CO2e per year**.
- These emissions are then compared with **Tesla’s reported annual “avoided emissions”**: Tesla says it avoided **32 million tonnes of CO2e in 2024** and **20 million tonnes of CO2e in 2023**; but third party Greenly argues the 2023 figure is more likely **10.2–13.4 million tonnes of CO2e**.
- Finally, the article expresses the impact through ratios and analogies, such as the share of Tesla’s global product climate benefit that is offset, and multiples relative to Tesla’s manufacturing emissions.

## Results
- The article says the AI data centre’s **currently operating portion** emits about **5.1 MtCO2e/year**, and **once fully built out** about **11.3 MtCO2e/year**, corresponding to **86** fossil gas turbines.
- As an intermediate basis, it notes that one phase described in permitting documents involves **51** gas turbines and about **6.4 MtCO2e/year**.
- Compared with Tesla’s claimed **32 MtCO2e** of annual avoided emissions in 2024, the author says the completed data centres would offset about **35%** of Tesla’s global product climate benefit.
- Compared with Tesla’s claimed **20 MtCO2e** in 2023, the author argues the not-yet-fully-built data-centre emissions are already enough to “exceed the entire benefit”; if Greenly’s **10.2–13.4 MtCO2e** estimate for 2023 is used, the comparison is even less favorable to Tesla.
- The article further says that, using Tesla’s 2024 Scope 1 and 2 manufacturing emissions as the benchmark, the **currently operating** data-centre emissions are about **5 times** Tesla’s manufacturing emissions, while the **fully built** data centres would be about **11 times** as much.
- More broadly, the article cites related analysis from Global Energy Monitor saying that if the US builds all planned on-site fossil plants for data centres, economy-wide emissions could rise by **27%**, involving about **100 GW** of on-site fossil gas capacity; however, this is background context, not the author’s direct estimate for a single project.
