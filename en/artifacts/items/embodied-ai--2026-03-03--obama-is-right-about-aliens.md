# Obama is right about aliens

## Summary
This is not an academic paper, but a popular science/opinion article arguing that, based on the Drake equation, exoplanet discoveries, and advances in astrochemistry, the statistical likelihood that extraterrestrial life exists in the universe is increasing. The article further emphasizes that the truly key question is not only whether alien life is common, but whether civilizations can endure over the long term.

## Problem
- The article examines the question: **whether extraterrestrial life may be widespread, and why humanity should use this question to reflect on the long-term survival of our own civilization.**
- Its importance lies in the fact that, as astronomy and astrochemistry advance rapidly, our prior judgment about the “commonness of life” is changing, which affects public understanding of life in the universe, civilizational survival, and technological risks (climate, nuclear war, AI).
- The core extended issue is **L (civilization lifetime)** in the Drake equation: even if life is abundant, if civilizations are short-lived, they may never be able to contact one another.

## Approach
- The article’s core mechanism is simple: **it reinterprets recent observational data through the intuitive framework of the Drake equation**—there are many stars, many planets, perhaps many habitable environments, and the precursor materials of life are also increasingly common, so the probability of extraterrestrial life becomes more statistically plausible.
- The author links together several recent scientific developments as supporting evidence, including: JWST’s discovery of many previously unknown galaxies, growth in the number of exoplanets recorded by NASA, potential biosignatures on K2-18b, RNA/life-precursor components in Bennu samples, increased detection of interstellar objects, and the discovery of organic molecules in the ocean of Enceladus.
- Methodologically, it does not propose a new model or experiment, but rather **synthesizes existing astronomical observations with popular-science reasoning** to argue that “the math is on the side of alien life.”
- On this basis, the article shifts the focus toward civilizational risk governance: if humanity is to have a future chance to discover or contact other life, it must increase the probability of the long-term survival of human civilization—that is, increase L in the Drake equation.

## Results
- This article **does not provide original experiments, formal methods, or peer-reviewed quantitative results**; its “results” are mainly synthetic claims based on publicly reported scientific findings.
- The article cites that NASA has confirmed **6,107** exoplanets around **4,554** stars, to support the claim that planets are common.
- The article cites that JWST discovered **94,000** previously unknown galaxies in a small patch of sky once thought to be dark; it also claims that one image of a small region could correspond to about **30 billion** stars or more, to support the view that the scale of the universe far exceeds intuition.
- The article cites that K2-18b is about **124 light-years** from Earth and about **8 times** Earth’s size; the research team claimed to have found the strongest evidence yet of possible biological activity, namely the chemical fingerprints of **DMS/DMDS**, but the article does not provide significance levels, error estimates, or quantitative comparisons with baseline methods.
- The article cites that Bennu samples contained the **5 canonical bases** of DNA/RNA and phosphate, and reported detection of **ribose**, using this to argue that “all components of RNA have now appeared.”
- The strongest overall conclusion is that, as observational evidence accumulates, the author sees the judgment that “the statistical probability of extraterrestrial life is rising” as increasingly credible; however, regarding “aliens visiting Earth” or “having already contacted humanity,” the article provides no supporting evidence and instead explicitly regards the probability as low.
