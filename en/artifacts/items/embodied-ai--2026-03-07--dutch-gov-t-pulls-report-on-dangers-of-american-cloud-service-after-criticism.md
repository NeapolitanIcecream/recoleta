# Dutch gov't pulls report on dangers of American cloud service after criticism

## Summary
This is a news article about the Dutch government withdrawing a report on the risks of Amazon's "European Sovereign Cloud," rather than a research paper. The core point is that there is clear controversy over whether U.S. cloud services truly satisfy European digital sovereignty, involving legal risk assessment, technical auditability, and policy judgment.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is: **whether the "European localization/sovereign cloud" offered by U.S. cloud providers still carries the risk of U.S. government access to data or service interruption**, and whether the government has underestimated that risk.
- This matters because the data sovereignty, service continuity, and national security of governments and critical infrastructure may all be affected when the parent company is subject to U.S. law and sanctions regimes.
- The controversy also touches on a deeper question: **whether making only a low-probability legal judgment ignores the systemic risks of black-box technology, unauditable source code, and path dependence on major U.S. tech firms**.

## Approach
- This is not a research paper proposing a new method; the article describes how the Dutch Ministry of Justice and Security commissioned the U.S. law firm **Greenberg Traurig** to conduct a **legal risk analysis** of AWS's "European Sovereign Cloud."
- The analysis focuses on two types of mechanisms: **whether the U.S. government could obtain data through law or informal pressure**, and **whether sanctions law could lead to service suspension**.
- The report's conclusion is not that there is "no risk," but that these events **could happen but are unlikely**; the government later clarified that this **was not a technical assessment, policy recommendation, compliance assessment, or complete risk assessment**.
- The core rebuttal from critics is that as long as the customer is dealing with a **U.S. parent company**, the location of the data center and the presence of European employees cannot eliminate the risk; at the same time, because the service is a **black box**, if backdoors cannot be examined at the **source code level**, it is difficult to truly verify security.

## Results
- The article **does not provide scientific experiments, datasets, benchmark models, or quantitative performance metrics**, so there are no comparable academic results.
- The most specific factual outcome is that the report was removed from the Dutch government's public website **3 days** after publication; it was then republished on **February 26** with an added note that it was only a **legal inquiry**.
- The law firm's conclusion was that the U.S. government **could** access data or interrupt services, but that the **likelihood is low / highly unlikely**; however, the article does not provide probability values, risk scores, or a formal quantitative framework.
- The strongest specific claim made by critics is that the report **underestimates the risk**, because issues such as control by a U.S. parent company, the enforceability of sanctions, and the unauditability of black-box technology do not disappear simply because of "European local deployment."
