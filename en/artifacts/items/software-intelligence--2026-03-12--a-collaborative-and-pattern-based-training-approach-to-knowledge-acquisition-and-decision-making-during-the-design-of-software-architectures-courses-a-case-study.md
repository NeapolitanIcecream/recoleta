# A Collaborative and Pattern-Based Training Approach to Knowledge Acquisition and Decision-Making During the Design of Software Architectures Courses: A Case Study

## Summary
This paper evaluates a course design method based on collaborative teaching and a catalog of "training patterns" (SAGITA) through a joint software architecture course case involving two universities in Colombia and Argentina. The core contribution is the proposal of reusable teaching patterns to more systematically develop software architecture knowledge acquisition and architectural decision-making abilities.

## Problem
- Software architecture education has long faced a gap between university training and industry needs, and students often lack experience in architectural design, trade-off analysis, and decision-making in real projects.
- Software architecture itself is abstract and depends on prerequisite knowledge, teamwork, realistic project contexts, and sustained practice, making it difficult to implement effectively at the undergraduate level.
- Course content and technical environments are updated more slowly than industry changes, so a replicable and organized course design method is needed to narrow the skills gap.

## Approach
- The authors propose the SAGITA guide and 7 types of reusable "training patterns," mapping common software architecture teaching problems to general instructional solutions, such as mini-projects, large-project, cases, problem-solving, and games.
- In the first semester of 2023, UNPL (14 students) and UNICAUCA (26 students) jointly planned the course, aligning learning objectives, course content, and teaching strategies.
- The two universities carried out practice around the same Open Market e-commerce project: from a monolithic architecture to microservices refactoring, with teams of 4-5 students completing requirements analysis, architecture design, and implementation.
- The course also introduced industry case talks (3 sessions) and one cross-university global development iteration, allowing students to learn architectural decision-making in a setting closer to an industrial environment.
- The evaluation used a case study method, combining questionnaires, document analysis, and semi-structured interviews, and examined teachers' perceived usefulness and ease of use of the guide from the TAM perspective.

## Results
- The case covered **2 universities and 40 students** (14+26), implemented in the **first semester of 2023**; team size was **4-5 people**, and **3** industry architecture talks were organized.
- The authors claim that large-project training and case-based training had clear positive effects on practical experience, teamwork, complex problem-solving, planning and management, architectural understanding, and industry readiness, but the excerpt does not provide precise statistical values or significance tests.
- For competency items, student self-assessments show that a relatively large proportion believed the course "**helped a lot**" with **C05** (architecture evaluation), **C08** (trade-off analysis), and **C11** (system evolution/maintenance); in contrast, improvement in **C22** (code review) was weaker, with many students believing it only "**helped a little**."
- In the cross-university global collaboration activity, the authors report that **5 teams** participated in collaboration, but because the iteration time was short, **only 1 team** achieved good collaboration and produced an integrated and runnable software solution.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that SAGITA and the training patterns can help teachers organize software architecture courses in a structured way and, to some extent, simulate industrial scenarios and promote students' architectural knowledge acquisition and decision-making training; however, implementation costs are high and require additional teacher time and coordination effort.
- The excerpt does not provide a comparison with a control group that did not use this method, nor does it give quantitative baseline improvement percentages, so the main innovation lies in the teaching framework and cross-university collaboration practice rather than in rigorous performance numbers.
