# Stablecoins effectively flood the US Treasury market

## Summary
This article analyzes how stablecoins are rapidly embedding themselves into the core of U.S. financial markets by using U.S. Treasuries as their primary reserve asset. It emphasizes that stablecoins could both strengthen the dollar and payment efficiency, while also transmitting crypto-market volatility into short-term Treasuries and the banking system.

## Problem
- The article focuses on the question: **after stablecoins expand in scale, will crypto-market runs and volatility be transmitted into the U.S. Treasury market and the broader financial system**.
- This matters because stablecoins have grown from about **$20 billion (2020)** to **$300 billion**, and the Federal Reserve estimates they could reach **$3 trillion within five years**, making them large enough to affect the roughly **$7 trillion** U.S. Treasury bill market.
- Another core issue is regulatory design: which collateral is allowed, who may issue, how redemption and compliance work—these determine whether stablecoins enhance dollar dominance or create new systemic risks.

## Approach
- The article’s core mechanism is straightforward: **stablecoins promise 1 dollar per token, and to maintain this “cash-like stability,” issuers hold reserve assets such as U.S. Treasuries as collateral**.
- As stablecoin issuance rises, issuers need to buy more short-term U.S. debt, so **crypto capital is effectively funneled into the U.S. Treasury market**.
- Conversely, if markets panic and users redeem en masse, issuers may need to **sell Treasuries quickly to raise cash**, thereby transmitting pressure from crypto markets into traditional financial markets.
- The article also discusses the policy-level “approach”: through the **GENIUS Act** and subsequent rules, the U.S. is bringing stablecoins more deeply into the traditional financial regulatory framework in hopes of reducing “Wild West”-style risks.

## Results
- Total stablecoin market capitalization has grown from **about $20 billion in 2020** to **$300 billion**; the Federal Reserve expects it could reach **$3 trillion within five years**.
- The article says stablecoin issuers now hold more U.S. Treasury debt than major overseas U.S. creditors such as **Saudi Arabia and South Korea**, showing they have become important participants in the Treasury market.
- In past stress events, stablecoins clearly depegged: **USD Coin fell below $0.87 at one point in 2023, and Tether USDT fell below $0.94 at one point in 2022**; however, both ultimately met redemption requests.
- The market currently receiving particular attention is the roughly **$7 trillion** U.S. **T-bill** market; the article argues that if stablecoins continue growing at the current pace, they could become “a fairly significant share” of that market.
- Specific risk signals include: **in November 2024, S&P downgraded Tether’s rating from “constrained” to “weak”**, partly because some of its reserves include assets riskier than cash and Treasuries, such as Bitcoin, gold, and corporate bonds.
- The article does not provide strict causal empirical evidence or controlled experimental results; its strongest concrete claim is that stablecoins may **increase demand for dollars and Treasuries, lowering Treasury yields**, while also **potentially triggering forced Treasury sales and liquidity-stress transmission during the next crypto crash**.
