# China Built an Underground Hypergravity Machine

## Summary
China has built the ultra-high-capacity hypergravity centrifuge CHIEF1900 underground in Hangzhou, using extreme centrifugal force to compress geological and engineering processes that would normally take hundreds to tens of thousands of years into observations that can be made within days. Its significance is that it provides civil engineering, geology, deep-sea, and environmental research with an accelerated physical experimentation platform that is more realistic than pure simulation.

## Problem
- Many critical geological and engineering processes, such as soil instability, pollutant migration, and structural degradation, evolve extremely slowly in the real world, often taking hundreds to tens of thousands of years, making direct experimental observation difficult.
- Large infrastructure systems, such as dams, deep-sea platforms, and high-speed rail foundations, face high failure risk under extreme operating conditions, but full-scale testing is extremely costly and too slow, while computer simulation alone may lack real physical data.
- A method is needed that can simultaneously reduce spatial scale and compress time scale in the laboratory, so that real-world failure and evolution can be predicted faster and more reliably.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: install a physical scale model on a large rotating arm with a radius of **6.4 meters** and spin it at high speed, using hypergravity to amplify the internal stresses in the small model to levels equivalent to those in a real large structure.
- The device, **CHIEF1900**, can generate a maximum load capacity of **1,900 g-tonnes**, allowing multi-tonne samples to experience forces close to **1900 times** Earth gravity, thereby accelerating natural processes.
- For example, a **3-meter** dam model under **100g** can simulate the internal stress of a real **300-meter** dam; similarly, slow geological migration, vibration-induced damage, and material degradation processes can also be accelerated.
- To address the heat and vibration caused by ultra-high-speed rotation, the system is housed in a circular chamber **15 meters** underground with an area of **230 square meters**, and uses vacuum cooling, forced ventilation, and cooling pipelines to maintain stable operation.
- The facility operates as a shared research platform, equipped with **6** specialized experimental cabins, **3** main units, and **18** in-flight devices for experiments involving slopes/dams, earthquake-resistant geotechnics, deep-sea engineering, and geological processes.

## Results
- The system is claimed to reach **1,900 g-tonnes**, surpassing the previous record machine rated at **1,200 g-tonnes** operated by the US Army Corps of Engineers in Mississippi, and also exceeding its predecessor **CHIEF1300**.
- A representative quantitative result given in the article: deep-soil pollutant infiltration and groundwater migration processes that would normally take **hundreds to tens of thousands of years** can be observed within **3.65 days**.
- A scale-equivalence result given in the article: a **3-meter** dam model under **100g** can produce internal stresses equivalent to those of an actual **300-meter** structure, helping identify potential failure points in advance.
- Previous experiments on CHIEF1300 had already successfully simulated **strong earthquakes** to verify the seismic resistance of hydropower dam foundations, and reproduced water pressure at **2,000 meters** depth to assess the safety of seabed combustible ice extraction.
- Earlier experiments also claimed to synthesize metal alloys with “fewer defects and higher strength,” but the article provides no specific material metrics, comparison baseline, or statistical significance.
- Overall, the article’s breakthrough claim is that the facility can create experimental environments spanning from **milliseconds to tens of thousands of years**, covering room temperature to extreme temperatures, in order to obtain real physical data that was previously difficult to acquire directly; however, aside from capacity figures and several examples, it lacks systematic paper-style benchmark data.
