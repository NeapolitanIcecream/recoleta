# With H-1B work visa lottery underway, experts worry new rules hurt business

## Summary
This is a news analysis about the impact of new U.S. H-1B visa rules on business hiring and innovation, rather than a technical research paper. The core argument is that higher fees and a lottery rule that prioritizes higher salaries may make it harder for startups to obtain high-skilled international talent, thereby weakening innovation and local economic vitality.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is whether, after the H-1B visa reform, the new **$100,000** fee and the weighted lottery mechanism will reduce companies’ — especially startups’ — ability to access high-skilled foreign talent.
- This matters because biotech and tech companies rely on scarce specialized talent to conduct R&D, product innovation, and entrepreneurial activity; limits on talent may slow drug development, technological innovation, and job creation.
- The article also points out that the policy may create structural unfairness: large companies are better able to absorb the costs, while small companies and early-stage startups face a greater impact.

## Approach
- This is not research proposing an algorithm or system, but a **policy impact analysis** based on news interviews and expert opinions.
- The article builds its argument through statements from multiple relevant stakeholders, including the head of a biotech incubator, the head of a tech industry association, and immigration lawyers.
- Its core mechanism is explained simply: the new rules **raise application costs** and **allocate slots with priority to higher salaries**, giving an advantage to larger companies with more resources and higher pay scales.
- The article further describes how companies are responding: reducing lottery applications, applying only for people already in the U.S., shifting to alternative paths such as O-1/F-1/green cards, or moving roles/offices overseas.

## Results
- The article provides several explicit figures: the annual H-1B cap is **85,000**; the new rules introduce a roughly **$100,000** fee; and companies will learn the results of this round of the lottery **by the end of this month**.
- It does not provide rigorous experiments, datasets, or statistical results, so there are **no verifiable quantitative performance metrics, baselines, or controlled comparisons**.
- The strongest specific claim is that startups face significant pressure from the additional **$100,000** cost, while large companies are more likely to absorb it.
- An immigration lawyer says the number of lottery applications this year is “lower,” but “not as obvious as he expected”; however, the article **does not provide a specific decline figure**.
- The article also claims that some employers are no longer willing to enter the lottery for candidates outside the United States and are increasingly turning to O-1, F-1, or green card pathways, but again **does not provide counts or proportions**.
- Regarding the weighted lottery, the article’s qualitative conclusion is that prioritizing higher salaries will place tech startups at a clear disadvantage, thereby suppressing innovation and local economic benefits.
