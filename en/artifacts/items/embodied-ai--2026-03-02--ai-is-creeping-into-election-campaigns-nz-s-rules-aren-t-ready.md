# AI is creeping into election campaigns. NZ's rules aren't ready

## Summary
This article discusses how generative AI has already begun to seep into New Zealand election campaigning, while current election regulations are unprepared for AI-generated misleading content, deepfakes, and targeted manipulation. The core argument is: unless disclosure and anti-falsehood rules are updated quickly, AI will further erode election transparency, trust, and fairness.

## Problem
- The problem to be addressed is **AI-driven election misinformation and manipulation**: the low-cost creation of fake images, videos, attack ads, and personalized political messaging that may mislead voters and influence voting.
- This matters because New Zealand's general election is approaching, while existing laws were shaped in the pre-AI era and lack adequate constraints on **whether AI use must be disclosed** and on **false campaign content itself**.
- The risk comes not only from political parties, but also from third-party lobbying groups and foreign actors; against a backdrop of already low political trust, this could deepen public alienation and damage democratic legitimacy.

## Approach
- This is not a technical paper, but a **policy/legal analysis**: it reviews New Zealand's current election advertising rules and examines where they fail in the AI era.
- The core mechanism is simple: first identify the new campaign risks created by AI (deepfakes, fake attack ads, profile-driven personalized message targeting), then compare them against existing legal provisions to see which behaviors are already covered and where gaps remain.
- The article notes that current rules mainly require ads to carry a **promoter's statement**, are subject to a three-month spending cap, and require written permission for ads that directly mention parties/candidates, but **place almost no limits on whether ad content is true or false**.
- The author's proposed direct improvements include: **mandatory disclosure of AI use in campaign advertising**, and expanding the ban on statements that are “knowingly false and intended to influence voting” from the final **3 days** to the entire advance voting period; it also references relevant frameworks in the EU, US state laws, and Australia.

## Results
- The article **does not provide experimental quantitative results or benchmark tests**; it is a normative analysis, not a model or dataset paper.
- The most specific factual point given is that New Zealand's current criminal rule on “knowingly false statements influencing voting” applies only to the final **3 days** of the election period, whereas in practice voting already lasts **12 days**, creating an obvious regulatory gap.
- The article claims that AI has reduced the production cost of political attack content to “**virtually zero cost**” and lowered the technical barrier to “**minimal skill**”, allowing smear propaganda that once required professional production to be replicated at scale.
- The article cites a research-style conclusion that even when people know content is fake, after seeing **AI-generated images** they are still more likely to believe someone committed a crime; however, the excerpt does not provide a specific paper title, dataset, or effect size.
- The strongest concluding claim is that unless the rules are updated, as the potentially fiercely contested **2026** election approaches, the threat AI poses to election integrity will continue to rise; and introducing AI disclosure obligations and expanding the anti-falsehood applicability period are low-threshold reforms that could be implemented immediately.
