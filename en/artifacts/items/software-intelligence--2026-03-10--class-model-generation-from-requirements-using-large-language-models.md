# Class Model Generation from Requirements using Large Language Models

## Summary
This paper investigates whether large language models can automatically generate UML class diagrams from natural-language requirements, and further examines whether LLMs acting as reviewers can reliably evaluate those diagrams. The core contribution is a “generation + dual validation” workflow: multiple models generate PlantUML, then two independent LLM judges and human reviewers are used for validation.

## Problem
- Converting software requirements into UML class diagrams usually relies on manual modeling, which is time-consuming, requires strong domain knowledge, and can easily introduce misunderstandings of requirements.
- Existing automation methods mostly depend on rules or traditional NLP, with limited cross-domain generalization ability, making them difficult to apply to real, heterogeneous requirements documents.
- In the absence of ground truth, how to evaluate the quality of LLM-generated class diagrams and determine whether LLM reviewers are trustworthy is a key question for deploying automated requirements engineering.

## Approach
- Compare 4 generation models: GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.0, Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking, and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, generating PlantUML class diagrams from natural-language requirements.
- Test on 8 heterogeneous real-world datasets covering domains such as data management, recycling, camping, healthcare, autonomous driving/embedded systems, inventory, and pacemakers; requirement set sizes range from 12 to 187 items.
- Use chain-of-thought prompting to break the task into the simplest possible steps: first extract entities/attributes/relationships, then add inheritance, multiplicity, and types, and finally output compilable PlantUML.
- Propose a dual-validation framework: two independent LLM judges (Grok, Mistral Small 3.1 24B) conduct pairwise comparisons and score along 5 dimensions: completeness, correctness, standards conformance, comprehensibility, and terminological alignment.
- Use Spearman correlation, Cohen’s Kappa, significance testing, and Cohen’s d to analyze agreement between LLM reviewers and between LLM reviewers and human experts.

## Results
- The study evaluates **8 datasets**, **4 generation models**, and **2 LLM judges**, with requirement document sizes ranging from **12–187 requirements**; these are the main experimental scale details provided in the paper.
- The paper explicitly claims that the UML class diagrams generated by LLMs are “**structurally coherent and semantically meaningful**,” indicating that they can already extract entities, attributes, and associations from requirements and form usable class diagrams.
- The paper also claims that LLM judges achieve “**substantial alignment**” with human reviewers, and that the judgments of Grok and Mistral show strong consistency with expert opinions, supporting the use of LLMs not only as modeling assistants but also as evaluators.
- The excerpt **does not provide specific numerical results**, such as average scores for each model on each dataset, Spearman/Kappa/Cohen’s d values, the best-performing model, or relative improvements over baselines, so precise metric/dataset/baseline comparison numbers cannot be reported.
- The strongest verifiable conclusion is that, compared with traditional manual UML modeling workflows, the authors believe this method can provide practical support for automated requirements engineering workflows, especially in **no-ground-truth scenarios**, by enabling scalable evaluation through “LLM-as-a-Judge + human review.”
