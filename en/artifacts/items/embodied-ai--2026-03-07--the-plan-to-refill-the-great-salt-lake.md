# The plan to refill the Great Salt Lake

## Summary
This article discusses Utah’s plan to restore the Great Salt Lake’s water level before the 2034 Winter Olympics. The core message is: the problem is extremely urgent, a cross-party coalition of government, philanthropy, and industry has formed to push it forward, but at the current pace the goal remains very daunting.

## Problem
- The Great Salt Lake has been shrinking for a long time, reaching a record low about four years ago. The exposed lake bed created **toxic dust**, heavy-metal health risks, and the potential for ecological collapse.
- The lake is crucial to the region’s ecology and economy: it provides stopover habitat for about **10 million birds and more than 330 species** each year, and it also affects snowfall in the Wasatch Mountains. Research says lake-effect snow can contribute **up to 10% of annual snowfall**; if the lake disappeared completely, a 2024 study estimated mountain precipitation would fall to **half**.
- The root cause is mainly human overuse of water: a 2022 study said about **75%** of the problem is caused by people; the lake’s volume has fallen about **67%** since early settlement, and in 2022 the water level was nearly **10 feet** below the healthy threshold.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: **reduce water use by people and businesses so more inflow can remain in the lake**, especially through agricultural water conservation and leasing, purchasing, or donating water rights to return water to the Great Salt Lake.
- On the policy side, Utah has revised its water laws over the past five years to define “letting water flow to the lake” as a beneficial use, reducing farmers’ concerns that not using water would cause them to lose their water rights, and it established a **$40 million** water trust.
- On the engineering and operations side, the state has raised the railroad causeway berm to regulate water and salt flows between the north and south arms of the lake; it is also controlling invasive phragmites, which use about **2 times** as much water as native plants; and it is evaluating diversions from the Newfoundland Evaporation Basin, potentially adding about **30,000 acre-feet per year**.
- On the capital side, Josh Romney launched a **$100 million** philanthropic fundraising campaign aimed at making it “financially more worthwhile” for farmers through voluntary transactions to lease agricultural water rights; Ducks Unlimited separately committed **$100 million** for wetland restoration; the state government also acquired U.S. Magnesium for **$30 million** to recover its water use.
- Politically, the plan depends on a cross-party, cross-interest coalition, including Republican lawmakers, environmental groups, industry, and efforts at the federal level to secure about **$1 billion** in support to improve the chances of execution.

## Results
- The article is not an academic paper and **does not report controlled experiments or model metrics on standard benchmarks**; instead, it provides policy progress, scale estimates, and interim ecological signals.
- To reach the target by 2034, the task force estimates the lake needs about **800,000 acre-feet** of additional water each year over the next **8 years**, roughly equivalent to **400,000 Olympic swimming pools per year**; last year only **163,468 acre-feet** were added, the highest in five years but still far below the target.
- The task force says the required annual replenishment is about **2 times** the amount of water the state has actually secured for the lake over the past **5 years**, showing that the current pace is clearly insufficient.
- After the state acquired U.S. Magnesium, it is expected to be able to redirect the **65,000–80,000 acre-feet per year** the company has used in recent years to the lake, but it will also have to bear roughly **$100 million to $200 million** in Superfund site cleanup costs.
- The earlier crisis was temporarily eased by two factors: improved causeway management improved salinity distribution, and one winter’s mountain snowfall reached **2 times** the seasonal norm; researchers said this “basically saved the lake,” and observed a recovery in brine fly populations, though overall water levels still have not returned to healthy levels.
- On funding, Romney said about **30%** of his **$100 million** goal has already been raised, and he believes stabilizing the lake may ultimately require about **$500 million**; in addition, the governor plans to seek about **$1 billion** in federal funding. Overall, the article’s strongest conclusion is that the restoration path is clearer and support has expanded significantly, but achieving the 2034 goal at the current pace remains “very daunting.”
