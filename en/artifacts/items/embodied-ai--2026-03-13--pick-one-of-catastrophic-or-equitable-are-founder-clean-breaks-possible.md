# Pick one of catastrophic or equitable. Are founder clean breaks possible?

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but rather a startup founder's advice-seeking post. The core question is whether a technical cofounder who wants to switch careers and attend law school can achieve a relatively "clean and fair" exit without harming the company. The content provides the company's current situation, the founder's personal motivations, and equity-related concerns, but it does not offer a systematic research method or empirical conclusions.

## Problem
- The issue under discussion is whether, in a startup that has raised a **$5M seed** round, is not yet profitable, and has only mediocre growth, a technical cofounder can leave early without causing a "catastrophic" impact on the company.
- Specific concerns include how to handle vested equity, whether it is possible for the company or investors to buy the founder out, whether part of the equity can be sold below its apparent fair value, and whether the founder's departure would be seen as a key company risk.
- This matters because a cofounder's exit directly affects company governance, investor confidence, team continuity, and the individual's financial and career-transition plans.

## Approach
- The text uses a **first-person case description** to explain the company background: the startup began in **late 2021**, has just completed a seed round, has a decent but not outstanding number of customers, has weak growth and profitability, and recently launched a major product and entered a new market.
- The author lays out their constraints: they are the **only technical cofounder**, there is no obvious successor on the team, they are not severely burnt out, but they have lost a sense of long-term career identification with the work and want to pivot to a legal career.
- The core mechanism is not to propose solutions, but to ask about several possible exit paths: staying on for a period of time, having investors/the company buy them out, selling part of their vested equity to pay tuition, or keeping these thoughts private for now to avoid disruption.
- Therefore, this is better understood as a request for real-world experience about whether a founder clean break is feasible, rather than a validated analytical framework.

## Results
- There are no **quantitative experimental results, datasets, baselines, or performance metrics**, so there is no paper-like comparison of results.
- The most concrete factual details given are: the company has completed a **$5M** seed round, the startup began in **late 2021**, the number of customers is "fair, but not great," and it remains "far from profitable."
- The author states that the company is currently in a state where "**Nothing's going wrong**" but also does "**not really**" match high-growth expectations, indicating that the issue is not business collapse, but the coexistence of insufficient growth and declining personal career motivation.
- The strongest practical claim is that, while the company still has runway, the product direction is still being tested, and the technical founder lacks a clear successor, the founder worries that exiting rashly could be seen as harmful to the company; however, the text itself does not provide a conclusion.
