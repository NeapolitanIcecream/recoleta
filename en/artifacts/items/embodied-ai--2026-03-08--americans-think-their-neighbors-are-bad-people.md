# Americans Think Their Neighbors Are Bad People

## Summary
This article interprets an anomalous result from Pew’s survey across 25 countries: the United States is the only country where a majority of respondents believe that “the people in their country have bad morals.” The author argues that this is more serious than “low trust,” suggesting that the U.S. has slid from a low-trust equilibrium into a “low moral evaluation” equilibrium.

## Problem
- The article seeks to explain why Americans not only distrust institutions, but also broadly believe their fellow citizens “have moral problems,” and why this is more dangerous than ordinary political distrust.
- This matters because once the public interprets policy disagreements as “the other side are bad people,” institutional repair, public cooperation, and democratic deliberation all become more difficult.
- The author distinguishes between a “trust problem” and a “contempt problem”: the former is skepticism about competence and motives, while the latter is a sweeping moral condemnation of one’s fellow citizens.

## Approach
- The core method is a comparative interpretation of **Pew Research Center** survey results from 25 countries, focusing on the United States’ relative position on the question, “Do the people in your country have good or bad morals and ethics?”
- The author further compares this result with Americans’ attitudes on specific moral issues, arguing that Americans are not unusually strict about standards of behavior, but are more inclined to make negative moral judgments about “people.”
- The article uses concepts from political psychology—**affective polarization** and **motivated reasoning**—to explain the mechanism: people first label others as “good” or “bad” based on tribe/party, then selectively gather evidence afterward to prove that conclusion.
- The author also draws on partisan and age-stratified data to show that this moral contempt is not confined to a single camp, but has spread across American society as a whole, and is more pronounced among younger groups.

## Results
- The central finding is that among the 25 surveyed countries, **the United States is the only one where a majority believe that the people in their country have “bad morals and ethics”**; specifically, **53%** of American adults hold this negative view.
- By partisan breakdown, **60% of Democrats and Democratic-leaning independents** say Americans have bad morals, while **46% of Republicans and Republican-leaning respondents** say the same. Based on this, the author argues that what makes the U.S. distinctive is not just polarization, but that both sides together push the national average into a negative majority.
- By age, **57% of those ages 18–39** hold this view, compared with **50%** of older adults; the article notes that Pew says this difference remains even after controlling for party.
- On specific moral issues, the U.S. is not notably more conservative: only **23%** say smoking marijuana is morally wrong, **29%** say gambling is wrong, and overall the U.S. sits near the international middle on many issues; the only standout exception is extramarital affairs, which **90%** of Americans consider morally wrong.
- Therefore, the article’s strong conclusion is not that Americans are more eager to condemn certain behaviors, but that they are **more likely to condemn their fellow citizens themselves**. The author summarizes this as: America’s problem is no longer simply a “low-trust equilibrium,” but a “low moral evaluation equilibrium.”
