# Altman admits OpenAI can't control Pentagon's use of AI

## Summary
This is a news report about OpenAI’s cooperation with the U.S. Department of Defense and the boundaries of its governance. The core point is that Sam Altman acknowledged OpenAI cannot control how the Pentagon uses its AI in military operations. The article emphasizes the ethical controversy surrounding military AI deployment, divisions within the industry, and the fragility of corporate “safety guardrails” in real-world procurement and combat scenarios.

## Problem
- The article examines this question: once AI companies provide models to the military, can they still constrain how those models are actually used in war, targeting decisions, or surveillance? This matters because it concerns responsibility, ethical boundaries, and public accountability in lethal contexts.
- The Pentagon reportedly asked AI companies to remove safety guardrails to expand military uses, raising concern among employees and the public over whether “safety commitments” are merely superficial.
- The different choices made by OpenAI and Anthropic over whether to accept military terms expose a conflict within the AI industry between national security cooperation and ethical red lines.

## Approach
- This is not a research paper and does not propose a technical method; its central “mechanism” is reporting the OpenAI CEO’s stated view on the boundaries of corporate governance: model suppliers do not participate in operational decisions for specific military actions.
- By citing Altman’s internal remarks, Pentagon procurement developments, Anthropic’s refusal of the deal, and statements from executives on both sides, the article illustrates how military procurement can bypass or weaken the original safety constraints imposed by model providers.
- Put simply: a company can sell AI tools, but once they are in the military’s hands, how they are actually used is often decided by the military, not by the model company.
- The report also links together “pressure to remove guardrails,” “rushed signing,” and “employee backlash” to show that the issue is not a single model’s capabilities, but deployment governance and power structure.

- One of the strongest concrete factual findings is that Altman explicitly said employees “do not get to weigh in” on operational decisions, directly acknowledging that OpenAI cannot control the Pentagon’s specific uses.
