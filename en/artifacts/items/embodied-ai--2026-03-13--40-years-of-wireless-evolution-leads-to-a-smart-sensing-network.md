# 40 Years of Wireless Evolution Leads to a Smart, Sensing Network

## Summary
This is a review article on industry and technology trends that looks back at how 1G through 5G gradually changed the relationship among “devices, networks, and applications,” and argues that 6G will evolve into an “intelligent sensing network” integrating communications, sensing, and agent collaboration. The significance of the article lies in positioning 6G as infrastructure that supports real-time coordination between the digital and physical worlds, rather than merely a faster wireless network.

## Problem
- Traditional understandings of wireless generational evolution focus too heavily on speed, latency, and spectrum, while overlooking the systemic changes in the relationship among devices, networks, and applications.
- Existing 1G-to-5G networks are still mainly “connectivity tools,” and cannot natively support real-time collaboration, reasoning, and action by large-scale digital/physical AI agents.
- If the future is to enable coordination among autonomous systems across industries, regions, and physical environments, networks will need to simultaneously provide communication, sensing, edge intelligence, and global coverage.

## Approach
- The article proposes using the “connectivity’s DNA” framework to understand wireless evolution: each generation rewrites the relationship among Devices, Networks, and Applications.
- It analyzes each generation from 1G to 5G: 1G enabled mobile voice, 2G brought digitization and SMS, 3G provided a mobile data platform, 4G created the full mobile Internet, and 5G brought programmable networks and edge intelligence to the forefront.
- In the simplest terms, the authors describe the core mechanism of 6G as making networks do more than transmit data: they can also “sense the environment, understand needs, coordinate agents, and allocate resources in real time.”
- 6G is described as an AI-native fabric: integrating sensing and communications (ISAC), combining terrestrial and non-terrestrial networks, dynamically predicting traffic, and supporting coordination among digital and physical AI agents.
- The article also maps future applications to scenarios such as intelligent transportation, smart healthcare, smart factories, robotics, and autonomous driving, emphasizing that networks will evolve from “pipelines” into a “nervous system.”

## Results
- This is not an experimental paper and **does not provide new datasets, benchmarks, or reproducible experimental results**.
- Historical quantitative comparisons given in the article include: 4G/LTE speeds reached **up to about 100 times those of 3G**, making video streaming, maps, and video conferencing practical.
- 3G network speeds are described as being on the order of **hundreds of kbps**, enough to support early mobile email and browsing, but not yet enough to truly transform industries.
- As an example of device-side AI capability in the 5G era, the Apple Neural Engine is said to perform **trillions of operations per second**, supporting on-device translation, computational photography, and AR.
- The article’s key breakthrough claim for 5G is not simply higher peak speed, but **programmable / sliceable infrastructure** and edge computing, allowing networks to be customized to application needs.
- The article’s strongest claim about 6G is that, by the end of this decade, networks will support **millions of agents collaborating in real time**, and through **integrated communication + sensing, unified terrestrial + satellite coverage, and AI-native resource scheduling**, will upgrade networks from connectivity infrastructure into a “universal data fabric” for coordination across the digital and physical worlds.
