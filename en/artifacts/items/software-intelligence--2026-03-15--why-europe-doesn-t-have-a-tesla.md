# Why Europe doesn't have a Tesla

## Summary
This article argues that the core reason Europe lacks frontier firms like Tesla is not a shortage of capital, research, or talent, but rather an expensive and complex layoff regime that raises the cost of failure and suppresses firms’ willingness to pursue high-risk innovation. The author argues that if Europe wants to once again produce world-class innovative companies, it needs to shift from “protecting jobs” to “protecting incomes.”

## Problem
- The article seeks to explain why Europe struggles to produce frontier companies like Tesla, Google, and Waymo in key industries such as automobiles and technology.
- Its importance lies in the fact that this concerns not just the success or failure of individual firms, but also Europe’s income growth, industrial competitiveness, and long-term economic stagnation; the article notes that incomes in the EU’s original six member states were only **10%** below those of the United States in 2000, but are now **20%** lower.
- The author argues that common explanations such as high taxes, expensive housing, high energy costs, and insufficient R&D are not sufficient; the real key is that European labor law makes it very difficult and expensive for firms to scale down operations and reallocate labor after failure.

## Approach
- The article uses **institutional economics / comparative institutional analysis**: comparing layoff costs, procedures, and differences in firm behavior between the United States and countries such as Germany, France, Italy, and Spain.
- The core mechanism is simple: **innovation requires repeated trial and error, and trial and error sometimes means shutting down projects, closing factories, and laying off teams; if layoffs are especially costly, firms will make fewer high-risk attempts and focus instead on safer, incremental improvements.**
- The author supports this mechanism with multiple corporate case studies, including Google, Audi, Volkswagen, Nokia, Continental, Amazon, Bayer, and Opel, showing that European firms face especially high organizational adjustment costs after failure.
- The article further proposes a growth-path explanation: even if small firms are exempt from some labor laws, as they grow and cross employee thresholds they become constrained by stricter rules, so European startups find it harder to scale, harder to be acquired by domestic firms, and more likely to relocate to places like the United States.
- As an alternative, the author recommends a “**flexicurity**” system: easing constraints on dismissals while protecting workers’ income security through unemployment insurance, retraining, portable compensation accounts, and similar mechanisms.

## Results
- The article’s most central quantitative conclusion is that, according to rough estimates by Coste and Coatanlem, the unit layoff cost of corporate restructuring is **31 months of wages in Germany, 38 months in France, 52 months in Italy, 62 months in Spain, and only 7 months in the United States**; on this basis, the author argues that the cost of failure for European firms is far higher than for American firms.
- The article says that, relative to income levels, **the cost for large firms to lay off workers in Germany and France is about 4 times that in the United States**; and that **American workers are about 10 times more likely than German workers to be fired in any given year**, showing that European firms are less willing to create jobs that might later need to be eliminated.
- Specific cases include: **Google spent $2.1 billion to lay off 12,000 workers in 2023**, or about **$175,000 per person**; **Opel** paid **€552 million for 3,300 workers** when closing a German factory; after negotiations over its closure, **Audi Brussels** paid **€610 million**, equivalent to **more than €200,000 per employee**; **Nokia** spent **€1.8 billion** on restructuring in 2012, and laying off **2,000 workers** at a German plant in 2008 cost **€200 million**.
- On innovation failures, the author lists examples such as: **Apple canceled its self-driving car project after spending $10 billion**; **GM Cruise was acquired in 2016 for $1 billion, generated only $102 million in revenue in 2023, lost $3.4 billion, and was shut down in 2024**; the author uses these examples to show that American firms can absorb such failures and keep experimenting, whereas European firms would bear greater additional costs from labor institutions in similar situations.
- On markets and the startup ecosystem, the article says that **79% of startup acquisitions tracked by Crunchbase in 2012–2016 occurred in the United States**; among acquisitions that took place in Europe, **44% of the buyers were American companies**; meanwhile, **European companies accounted for only 7% of acquisitions of American startups**. On this basis, the author argues that Europe’s institutional environment weakens firms’ ability to scale and make acquisitions.
- The article does not provide a strict causal identification strategy or regression estimates to “prove” that labor law is the only main cause; its strongest conclusion is instead based on cross-country comparisons and case evidence: **high layoff costs systematically push European firms toward low-risk, incremental innovation, causing them to fall behind in technological transitions such as electric vehicles, autonomous driving, and smartphones.**
