# Is College Over?

## Summary
This is a review-style article about whether college is still worth pursuing as the default path in the AI era, rather than a strict academic research paper. The article argues that the employment-return logic of college is being weakened by generative AI and skills-based hiring, but that college's value in social networks, judgment formation, and civic education has still not been replaced.

## Problem
- The article attempts to answer: against the backdrop of generative AI weakening entry-level knowledge work, companies removing degree requirements, and tuition and debt continuing to rise, is college still worth attending, and is its value structurally declining?
- This matters because college has long been viewed as the default route into high-income careers, especially in computing and knowledge work; if that premise fails, students, families, employers, and educational institutions all need to reallocate investment and pathway choices.
- The impact is especially significant for computer science: the major once seen as the safest is now being directly hit by AI automation and the contraction of junior roles.

## Approach
- The article uses a news analysis / opinion review approach, aggregating data, surveys, historical views, and cases from multiple sources, rather than proposing a new model or conducting original experiments.
- The core mechanism can be understood simply as: **AI is automating the portion of "codifiable knowledge work" that colleges used to sell**, such as writing, research, basic programming, debugging, and analysis, thereby reducing the signaling value of degrees for entry-level jobs.
- At the same time, the author proposes a counterbalancing logic: the remaining value of college lies not mainly in course content, but in **peer effects, mentor relationships, in-person interaction, social networks, and cross-disciplinary judgment**, which are harder for AI tools or bootcamps to replicate.
- The final conclusion is not that "the degree is dead," but that "the degree alone is not enough"; what will be more valuable in the future is **degree-level thinking ability + demonstrable AI collaboration skills**.

## Results
- Degree requirements are declining: the article says that **45% of companies planned to eliminate degree requirements in 2024, and 25% planned to do so by the end of 2025**, indicating that skills-based hiring is expanding.
- Entry-level tech jobs have declined sharply: the article says **down 46% in the UK and 67% in the US**; it also cites Harvard research saying that after companies adopted generative AI, **junior hiring fell 7.7% within 6 quarters**, and that **each quarter they hired 3.7 fewer junior employees than companies that had not adopted AI**.
- Higher education and CS demand are weakening: total enrollment has **fallen 15% since 2010**; a CRA survey says **62% of institutions saw CS enrollment decline in fall 2025**, including **a 15% decline in graduate enrollment** and **a 6% decline in undergraduate enrollment**.
- Employment pressure for CS majors is rising: the article says **the unemployment rate for CS graduates is 6.1%**; meanwhile students are shifting toward AI and cybersecurity, which grew at **56%** and **58%** of institutions, respectively.
- The economic return to college has not completely disappeared: the article says **70% of students recoup their educational investment within ten years**, showing that "college is worthless" is not the article's conclusion.
- Cost pressure and cognitive split coexist: **83% of Gen Z say college is important, but only 53% think they can afford it**; **average student debt is $39,375**; **2024-25 borrowers pay $136 more per month than 2020-21 borrowers, and $16,266 more over the full lifecycle**; **only 33% of the class of 2025 think their education is worth the cost**. These are the article's strongest real-world claims, but it does not provide a unified experimental benchmark or a single verifiable breakthrough research metric."
