# The Death of the Cheap Laptop Is Coming

## Summary
This is not an academic paper, but a consumer-facing industry analysis article. It argues that AI data centers' competition for memory, storage, and chip manufacturing capacity is driving up prices for PCs, laptops, phones, and gaming devices, while squeezing the supply of low-cost products.

## Problem
- The article seeks to explain: **why consumer electronics, especially cheap laptops and PC components, are getting more expensive and may continue to get more expensive**.
- This matters because it directly affects ordinary consumers' purchase costs, available configurations, and the availability of entry-level devices, rather than only affecting AI companies.
- The author also emphasizes that this is not just a matter of a single tariff or short-term inventory fluctuations, but rather a **global upstream supply-chain squeeze** triggered by the expansion of AI infrastructure.

## Approach
- The article’s core mechanism is simple: **AI data centers and model companies are locking up large amounts of DRAM, NAND, foundry capacity, and other server-related production capacity, making key components shared with consumer hardware more expensive and scarcer**.
- It explains this through supply-chain structure: DRAM is mainly supplied by Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron; advanced chip manufacturing is highly dependent on TSMC and a small number of vendors, so capacity bottlenecks spill over into consumer markets.
- The key causal chain presented is: AI companies lock in supply at high prices → manufacturers prioritize resources for AI/server products → spot and contract prices for components rise → OEMs raise system prices, cut lower-spec models, and reduce low-cost product lines.
- The article also adds that companies are responding by stockpiling in advance, shifting production locations, lowering memory configurations, and raising prices; however, the author believes these methods will become increasingly unable to offset rising costs after 2026.

## Results
- The article says desktop memory prices have risen by **300%** since “last summer”; SSD prices have **doubled or more** in some cases since **December 2025**.
- The author gives a personal example: a similar motherboard + processor + DDR5 memory combination cost about **$800 in January 2025**, but when purchased again a few weeks ago it had become **$1,200**, an increase of about **50%**.
- For complete laptops, the article says **comparable models have risen by as much as about 20% year over year**; it also advises consumers to prepare for a **20%–30%** higher budget for new laptops later this year.
- Case studies: Dell’s new **XPS 14** starts at **more than $2,000**, while the comparable 2025 **Dell Premium 14** had an initial price of **$1,550**; the Asus **Zenbook Duo** went from **$1,700/1TB** to **$2,400/2TB**.
- On the supply-chain side, the article says OpenAI locked in large amounts of DRAM output from Samsung and SK Hynix, potentially reaching **900,000 wafers per month**; analysts estimate this could correspond to **40% of global RAM supply**. The two companies together account for about **67% of the global DRAM market**.
- The strongest quantitative forward-looking claim is that analysts expect **memory prices to keep rising through 2027**, and that some component vendors have already notified customers that **2026 inventory is sold out**. These are not experimental benchmark results, but industry observations and forecasts.
