# How Fat Is Schrödinger's Cat?

## Summary
This is a popular-science overview of quantum physics centered on the “Schrödinger’s cat” paradox, explaining quantum superposition, entanglement, and decoherence, and reviewing how quantum “cat states” have expanded from microscopic particles to experimentally observable objects on visible scales. The core message of the article is: quantum superposition did not stop at a thought experiment, but has been experimentally pushed toward ever larger objects.

## Problem
- The question it discusses is: **Can quantum superposition extend to macroscopic objects, and where is the boundary between the quantum world and the classical world?** This matters because it touches the central tension in interpretations of quantum mechanics and is also a foundational question for the scalability of quantum technologies.
- Using the Schrödinger’s cat paradox as the entry point, the article points out that the Copenhagen interpretation extends microscopic uncertainty into the macroscopic superposition of “the cat is both dead and alive,” which appears counterintuitive and even absurd.
- A more practical research question becomes: **How large and how “macroscopic” can a quantum state be made, and for how long can it remain observable before environmental decoherence sets in?**

## Approach
- Rather than proposing a new algorithm or theory, the article **uses a historical narrative to connect key experiments**, showing the scale-up of “cat states” from thought experiment to experimental realization.
- The core mechanism can be simplified as: putting a particle, atom, molecule, or mechanical oscillator **into two different path/position/vibrational states at the same time**, and then using **interference fringes or state detection** to prove that the superposition is physically real.
- Put as simply as possible, the method is: first place the system into “two possible states existing simultaneously,” then measure the coherence between those states before the environment disrupts it.
- The article emphasizes that **decoherence** is the dominant factor at the quantum/classical boundary: the larger the system and the more channels it has for coupling to the environment, the faster the superposition turns into a classical mixed state.

## Results
- In 1935, Schrödinger proposed the cat paradox, in which radioactive decay on a **1-hour half-life** timescale causes the cat to be “dead/alive” with **50/50** probability, to attack the absurdity of macroscopic superposition; this was a thought experiment, not an experimental result.
- In 1974, an Italian team first sent **single electrons one by one through a double slit/double prism** and observed interference, proving that a single electron can exist in a superposition of two spatial paths; in 1989, a Hitachi team further observed electrons arriving one by one in real time and gradually building up an interference pattern.
- In 1996, Monroe/Wineland at JILA created a “cat state” with a **single beryllium ion** by coupling its internal state to its motional state, extending superposition from electrons to atomic-scale objects.
- In 1999, Zeilinger’s team demonstrated matter-wave interference with **C60 buckyballs (60 atoms)**; subsequent work extended this line to the **thousands-of-atoms** scale.
- In 2023, ETH Zurich realized a cat state in a **16-microgram mechanical oscillator**, corresponding to about **10^17 atoms**, with a device size of roughly **1 millimeter in diameter and 100 microns thick**, large enough to be seen with the naked eye; its decoherence time was about **40 microseconds**.
- In 2026, Nature reported matter-wave interferometry detecting metallic clusters consisting of about **7000 sodium atoms** and measuring about **10 nanometers** in size; the authors said that by some measures of “macroscopicity,” this may be the **largest spatial quantum cat state to date**. The article does not provide a SOTA table under a single unified benchmark, but its strongest quantitative claim is that quantum superposition has advanced along two frontiers: **visible macroscopic devices (16 micrograms)** and **spatial interference of nanoscale clusters containing thousands of atoms**.
