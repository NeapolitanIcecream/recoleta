# Is it still worth learning to code?

## Summary
This article argues that in an era when AI can generate code, learning programming is still worthwhile, especially for career development. The core reason is not that “humans replace machines in writing code,” but that people with programming ability can use AI to achieve orders-of-magnitude higher output and stronger judgment.

## Problem
- The article discusses the question: when people can use natural language to have machines generate software, **is it still worth learning programming**, especially whether it still pays off professionally.
- Its importance is that this question affects not only programmers, but also signals that knowledge work in writing, design, legal work, medicine, and more will face the same challenge of “is it still worth learning.”
- The author argues that if learning is understood only as “personally producing results by hand,” then we underestimate the value of learning in cultivating judgment, understanding, and taste.

## Approach
- The core point is simple: **the value of learning programming shifts from “manual execution” to “the advantage gained when collaborating with AI.”**
- The author broadens programming ability to mean not just writing syntax, but also skills such as system design, architecture, and trade-offs; these abilities determine whether a person can effectively guide, evaluate, and correct AI outputs.
- The article proposes “discernment” as the key mechanism: when the cost of execution approaches zero, what is truly scarce is knowing what to do, what is better, and what is usable.
- Therefore, the focus of learning should shift from pure implementation details toward system design, architectural thinking, trade-offs, and using these abilities as soon as possible through practical projects.

## Results
- This is not an empirical paper and **does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or reproducible experimental metrics**.
- The strongest quantitative claim is that **a strong programmer using AI may be about 10–20x more efficient than a beginner**; however, the article does not provide an evaluation method, samples, or a baseline definition.
- The article also offers an anecdotal observation that in large companies, **a single engineer** using AI may outpace some dev/data teams, but again without specific numbers or controlled experiments.
- The article’s core conclusion is that AI lowers barriers to entry but does not eliminate the value of high-level ability; on the contrary, **judgment, understanding, and taste** become more important in the age of automation.
