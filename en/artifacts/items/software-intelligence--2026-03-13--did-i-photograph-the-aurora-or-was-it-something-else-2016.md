# Did I photograph the Aurora or was it something else? (2016)

## Summary
This is a public-facing popular science guide that uses a set of simple, actionable questions to help determine whether a photographed luminous phenomenon is really an aurora. Its importance lies in reducing cases where phenomena such as cloud reflections and light pollution are mistaken for auroras.

## Problem
- The problem it addresses is: after ordinary people photograph a luminous phenomenon in the night sky, how can they quickly judge whether it is an aurora.
- This matters because auroras are not common in lower-latitude regions such as the UK, and many photos are actually light pollution, clouds, fog, or other atmospheric phenomena.
- Without clear criteria, false reports can frequently appear on social media, affecting public understanding and the quality of observation information.

## Approach
- The core method is a **rule-based manual checklist**: sequentially check aurora alerts, whether it is dark enough, whether stars are visible, whether light pollution is present, whether the phenomenon is moving, whether the colors match auroral characteristics, and whether the viewing direction is north.
- First, use AuroraWatch UK alert levels as a prior filter: if an aurora is nearly impossible at the observer’s location, then the photo is probably not an aurora.
- Then combine this with observing conditions: in daylight, around dawn or dusk, under thick cloud or fog, or when stars are not visible, the chance of seeing an aurora is very low.
- Finally, use visual characteristics to rule out false positives: auroras commonly appear as green near the horizon with red/purple bands above; orange and yellow are more likely to be streetlight-related light pollution; in the UK they should usually appear near the northern horizon.

## Results
- The article **does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or quantitative metrics**, so there is no reportable accuracy, recall, or numerical comparison with baseline methods.
- It provides **7 specific diagnostic questions** as a practical process for identifying aurora photos.
- It gives a clear rule of thumb about timing: in the UK, auroras are most commonly seen around **9 pm to 12 pm**; daytime and periods near dawn/dusk are usually not dark enough.
- It gives a directional rule: when observing from the UK, an aurora should appear only in the **north**; if the glowing phenomenon appears in another direction, then it is “definitely not” an aurora.
- It gives a color rule: common aurora colors are **green and red/purple**; **orange and yellow** most likely come from light pollution such as streetlights.
- The strongest practical claim is that, through this set of simple questions, users can significantly improve their ability to judge for themselves whether they photographed an aurora, and can then seek additional confirmation through community manual review.
