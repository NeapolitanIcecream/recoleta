# A new clue to how the body detects physical force

## Summary
This study explains why the touch channel PIEZO2 is especially good at detecting localized light touch on the skin, while its close relative PIEZO1 is more tuned to sensing whole-cell stretching. The core finding is that PIEZO2’s force selectivity comes not only from the protein itself, but also from its physical connection to the cytoskeleton.

## Problem
- The question the study aims to solve is: **PIEZO2 and PIEZO1 have similar structures, so why do they respond so differently to different types of mechanical force**, especially why PIEZO2 is suited to sensing light touch and localized indentation.
- This matters because **touch, body position sense, and some forms of pain** depend on this class of mechanotransduction channels; without a clear mechanism, it is difficult to understand the molecular basis of related sensory disorders.
- The paper also examines how **PIEZO2 mutations and filamin-B-related abnormalities** affect sensory function, providing mechanistic clues for explaining sensory disorders.

## Approach
- The authors combined **MINFLUX super-resolution microscopy** with **electrophysiological ion current recordings** to observe both force-induced deformation of PIEZO2 and channel opening behavior in the native cellular environment.
- They compared **PIEZO2 and PIEZO1** responses to different mechanical stimuli, distinguishing between **local poking/indentation** and **membrane stretch**.
- The study found that **PIEZO2 is intrinsically “stiffer” than PIEZO1**, and that it is tethered to the **actin cytoskeleton** through **filamin-B**; this connection can transmit local external forces to PIEZO2.
- The authors further mapped the **specific region where PIEZO2 binds filamin-B** and tested how force selectivity changes when this connection is disrupted.
- They also carried out related ex vivo / in vivo validation in **mouse sensory neurons**, linking single-molecule-scale findings to physiological function.

## Results
- The central breakthrough of the study is the proposal that **PIEZO2 force selectivity is jointly determined by “channel stiffness + a filamin-B tether to the actin cytoskeleton”**, rather than by static protein structure alone.
- When cells undergo **local poking/indentation**, the filamin-B-mediated internal connection transmits force to PIEZO2, making it easier to open; in contrast, **when the tether is intact, membrane stretch alone does not activate PIEZO2**.
- **After disrupting the connection between PIEZO2 and filamin-B**, PIEZO2 in mouse sensory neurons showed **reduced sensitivity to indentation** and **unexpectedly gained responsiveness to membrane stretch**, indicating that this connection determines its characteristic of “preferring light touch while ignoring stretch.”
- The authors also report that **PIEZO1 is expanded and activated by membrane stretch, whereas PIEZO2 shows the opposite response to the same type of force**, supporting the idea that the two rely on different mechanotransduction mechanisms.
- The text **does not provide specific quantitative metrics** (such as percent current change, statistical significance values, sample size, or relative improvement over baseline), so precise performance figures cannot be reported; the strongest specific conclusion is that removing the tether shifts PIEZO2 from an “indentation-preferring” mode to one that can be activated by **membrane stretch**.
