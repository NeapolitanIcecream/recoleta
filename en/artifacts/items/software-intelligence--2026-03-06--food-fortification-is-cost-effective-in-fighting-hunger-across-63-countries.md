# Food fortification is cost-effective in fighting hunger across 63 countries

## Summary
This is a systematic review on the economics of large-scale food fortification, synthesizing 56 studies and more than 200 economic analyses from 63 countries. The conclusion is that food fortification is generally highly cost-effective in reducing “hidden hunger,” especially in resource-constrained countries.

## Problem
- The study addresses the question of whether food fortification is worth the investment and whether it can effectively reduce the disease burden and mortality caused by vitamin and mineral deficiencies at relatively low cost.
- This matters because the global burden of “hidden hunger” is enormous, affecting about 56% of children aged 6-59 months and 69% of non-pregnant women aged 15-49 years, and worsening further during food insecurity and rising prices.
- Against the backdrop of major cuts to global nutrition assistance funding, governments need evidence-based, high-value interventions to prioritize limited budgets.

## Approach
- The authors conducted a systematic review synthesizing more than 200 economic analyses from 56 studies, covering 63 countries, more than 40 of which are low- and middle-income economies.
- The core mechanism is simple: compare the costs and health gains of “implementing food fortification” versus “not implementing fortification,” assess how much it costs to avert one DALY, and whether monetized benefits exceed costs.
- Key metrics include the incremental cost-effectiveness ratio (ICER), benefit-cost ratio, and cost-utility analysis results.
- The study covers a variety of nutrient-food combinations, most commonly adding vitamin A, folic acid, iron, and iodine to staple foods and condiments, especially wheat flour, edible oil, sugar, and salt.
- The authors also interpreted the results against country-specific GDP per capita thresholds to determine whether interventions could be considered cost-effective at different income levels.

## Results
- Among 232 main cost-effectiveness analyses, 58% had ICERs below **$150/DALY averted**, and 84% were below **$1000/DALY averted** (2022 US dollars, compared with no fortification).
- All **47** benefit-cost ratio analyses were positive, indicating that in monetary terms, benefits consistently exceeded costs.
- Of **8** cost-utility analyses, **6** showed that food fortification was both “less costly and more effective” than no fortification.
- Using national GDP per capita as a benchmark, overall **87%** of interventions were below the country-specific **50% GDP per capita** threshold; in low- and middle-income countries, **84%** were below **35% GDP per capita**; in low-income countries, **71%** were below **20% GDP per capita**.
- The paper claims this is the **most comprehensive** synthesis of economic evidence on food fortification to date, and that despite limitations in the reporting quality of the original studies, results across countries and methods consistently support its high cost-effectiveness.
