# Anti-ICE protesters accused of being part of Antifa found guilty of terrorism

## Summary
This report discusses a precedent-setting criminal case in Texas: prosecutors charged and secured convictions against anti-ICE protesters for “providing support for terrorism” while repeatedly describing them as an “Antifa cell.” Its core significance is not technological innovation, but that it tests whether broad anti-terrorism laws can be used to address political protest activity and the boundaries of the First Amendment.

## Problem
- The “problem” the case addresses is whether the government can treat a protest that ultimately turned violent as “providing support to terrorism,” even if the defendants are not members of a formal terrorist organization.
- This matters because it could expand the use of anti-terrorism law in domestic political protest and affect freedom of speech, association, and protest.
- The case also raises a key dispute: prosecutors made the “antifa” label central to their narrative, but the relevant statute itself does not require the defendants to have a legal affiliation with any particular terrorist organization.

## Approach
- The prosecution’s core mechanism was straightforward: it did not need to prove the existence of a formal “Antifa organization,” only that the defendants provided material support to “terrorists” by assisting specific federal felonies.
- Concretely, prosecutors portrayed the protest as a “coordinated ambush”: fireworks were used to draw out guards and police, after which some participants damaged property and gunman Benjamin Song shot and wounded an officer.
- To prove “coordination” and “foreseeable violence,” prosecutors relied on substantial circumstantial evidence, such as the use of Signal, aliases, auto-deleting messages, phone counter-surveillance measures, black clothing, and the carrying of weapons and body armor.
- The defense, by contrast, argued that the original plan was a peaceful demonstration, that the firearms were legally purchased and intended for self-defense, and that many participants did not know each other well before the incident; some cooperating witnesses who pleaded guilty also said they were surprised by the outbreak of violence.

## Results
- Nine defendants were tried together and, with limited exceptions, **were convicted on all charges they faced**, including counts such as “providing support for terrorism.”
- Of the five defendants facing **attempted murder** and firearms charges, **four (Evetts, Hill, Morris, Rueda) were acquitted**; **Song was acquitted on 2 of 3 attempted murder counts and convicted on 1**, and the firearms charges against him were upheld.
- The sentencing exposure highlights the severity of the case: **Song faces a minimum of 20 years and a maximum of life imprisonment**; many of the other defendants **face 10 to 60 years**; **Sanchez-Estrada faces up to 40 years**.
- The report says this is **the first time the government has alleged in a criminal prosecution that defendants belonged to an “Antifa terrorist cell,”** giving the case clear precedent-setting significance.
- The article does not provide traditional research-style experimental metrics, datasets, or baseline comparisons; the strongest quantifiable results are the number of convictions, the number of partial acquittals, and the sentencing ranges faced by each defendant.
