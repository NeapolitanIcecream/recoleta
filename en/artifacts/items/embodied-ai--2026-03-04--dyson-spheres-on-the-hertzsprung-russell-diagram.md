# Dyson Spheres on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram

## Summary
This paper analyzes how the equilibrium temperature, radius, and radiative characteristics of Dyson spheres built around white dwarfs and red dwarfs (M-dwarfs) would appear on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram. The core conclusion is that low-luminosity stellar hosts would make Dyson spheres appear as cooler, fainter, but predictable technosignature targets in the infrared.

## Problem
- The paper aims to address the question: **if Dyson spheres are built around different types of low-luminosity stars, what observable radiative features would they exhibit, and where would they lie on the H-R diagram**.
- This matters because Dyson spheres are a classic candidate for extraterrestrial technological-civilization "technosignatures"; if their infrared colors, temperatures, and luminosities can be predicted, sky surveys can be designed more effectively.
- The excerpt emphasizes that white dwarfs and M-dwarfs are "promising" hosts, but a unified radiative-equilibrium framework is needed to produce testable observational constraints.

## Approach
- The authors use **radiative equilibrium**, the simplest physical model: assuming the Dyson sphere completely intercepts stellar radiation and reradiates the absorbed energy as a blackbody.
- Using representative parameters for white dwarfs and M-dwarfs, they compute the **temperature-radius relationship** of Dyson spheres, finding that the temperature decreases with Dyson sphere radius as **R_D^-1/2**.
- They place these hypothetical structures on the **Hertzsprung-Russell diagram (H-R diagram)** and compare their apparent locations and peak emission bands for different host types.
- The paper also compares the total luminosity and observed bolometric flux for the two host classes, noting that these are determined by the host star's output rather than by the Dyson sphere radius alone.

## Results
- The paper explicitly gives a scaling law: **the equilibrium temperature decreases with Dyson sphere radius as R_D^-1/2**.
- For **white dwarf hosts**, Dyson spheres produce **cooler, fainter blackbody radiation**, with emission peaks in the **near-infrared to mid-infrared**.
- For **M-dwarf hosts**, Dyson spheres **radiate more strongly**, but their peaks occur at **longer wavelengths**.
- For both host types, the **total luminosity and observed bolometric flux remain fixed by the stellar output**; that is, changing the Dyson sphere radius mainly changes the temperature and spectral shape, not the total energy budget.
- The paper excerpt **does not provide specific numerical tables, sample sizes, error bars, or quantitative improvement metrics relative to existing methods**, so it is not possible to report numerical results such as dataset size, accuracy, or statistical significance.
- The strongest concrete claim is: **low-luminosity stars (especially white dwarfs and M-dwarfs) are astrophysically suitable as Dyson sphere hosts and can provide practical filtering constraints for future technosignature searches in infrared surveys.**
