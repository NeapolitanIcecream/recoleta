# Military AI Policy by Contract: The Limits of Procurement as Governance

## Summary
This article criticizes the United States for governing military AI through procurement contracts rather than public law, arguing that this model of “regulation by contract” is flexible but fundamentally inadequate. Using the Pentagon disputes involving Anthropic and OpenAI, the article shows that when key constraints depend on bilateral agreements, technical guardrails, and after-the-fact dispute procedures, major issues such as domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons, and intelligence oversight cannot be governed robustly.

## Problem
- The problem the article addresses is that the boundaries on military AI use are being outsourced to procurement contracts and vendor terms, rather than being defined by laws, regulations, and democratic accountability mechanisms; this matters because it concerns high-risk state powers such as **domestic surveillance, lethal strikes, autonomous weapons, and intelligence oversight**.
- Contracts bind only the parties that sign them, and enforceability depends on the contractual vehicle (such as a FAR contract or OT agreement), the integrator layer, and whether the vendor can still control the model after deployment, making it difficult to provide unified, durable, and public governance.
- Under an “any lawful use” policy, the government often becomes the immediate interpreter of what counts as lawful; if a vendor objects, the remedy is usually ex post, monetary, and delayed, unable to stop a disputed use in the moment.

## Approach
- The article’s core method is **legal-institutional analysis**: through the cases of Anthropic being designated a supply chain risk and OpenAI rapidly reaching an agreement with the Pentagon and then revising terms on social media, it analyzes the structural defects of “procurement as governance.”
- The author breaks the issue down into its simplest mechanism: **first write red lines into the contract, then try to enforce them through technical guardrails, but once the system enters military operations, the party that usually holds interpretive and operational power is the government, not the contract text itself.**
- The article focuses on comparing two governance paths: Anthropic sought explicit contractual prohibitions while retaining vendor enforcement authority; OpenAI instead anchored restrictions to existing law and defense policy, with the government interpreting how those laws apply.
- The author further analyzes key contractual details, such as wording like “consistent with applicable laws” and “intentionally,” showing that although these appear to impose limits, they may in fact return interpretive discretion to the government and narrow the scope of the prohibitions.
- At the same time, the article discusses the technical “safety stack” as a more real-time constraint than contracts, but points out that it may also directly conflict with the Defense Department’s requirement that “models must not include use restrictions that impede lawful military uses.”

## Results
- This is not an experimental paper and **does not provide quantitative results such as model performance, benchmark datasets, or statistical significance**.
- One of the most specific facts presented is that in July 2025, the Pentagon’s CDAO awarded Anthropic a **2-year prototype OT agreement capped at $200 million**, showing that military AI procurement is already advancing through high-value, fast-track channels.
- The article says Anthropic was the **first frontier AI company to enter U.S. government classified networks**, and was then designated by the Defense Department as a national security supply chain risk; this reversal is used to show that contract-based governance is highly unstable under political and operational pressure.
- The article says Claude was deployed to classified networks through the Palantir Maven Smart System and, according to *The Washington Post*, used for **target proposal generation in the Iran campaign**; the author uses this to show that high-risk applications are already occurring while constraint mechanisms still rely mainly on contracts and vendor technical controls.
- OpenAI’s agreement with the Pentagon was reportedly reached in **just a few days**, and the CEO acknowledged the process was “rushed”; then on **March 2**, additional terms were announced publicly through social media, which the author cites to argue that key military AI oversight terms are being rewritten through informal, public-opinion-driven means.
- The article’s central concluding claim is that a procurement framework cannot substitute for congressional legislation, judicial review, and public policymaking; even when contracts include restrictions, the government can still evade real-time constraints through immediate interpretive authority, termination rights, or national security powers, so this governance model is structurally inadequate for handling the public-law issues raised by military AI.
