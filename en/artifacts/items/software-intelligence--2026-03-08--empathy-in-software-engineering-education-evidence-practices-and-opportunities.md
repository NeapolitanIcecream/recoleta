# Empathy in Software Engineering Education: Evidence, Practices, and Opportunities

## Summary
This paper is a systematic review of empathy teaching in engineering education and software engineering education, attempting to integrate scattered research evidence into a baseline that can be used for curriculum design. The core conclusion is that empathy is shifting from a marginalized “soft skill” to an educational construct in software engineering that can be taught, practiced, and partially assessed.

## Problem
- The problem the paper addresses is: how empathy is defined, taught, applied, and assessed in software engineering education. Current research is scattered and lacks systematic integration, leaving curriculum design without a clear foundation.
- This matters because modern software engineering depends not only on technical ability, but also on collaboration, ethical judgment, inclusive design, and understanding users’ contexts; a lack of empathy can lead to poor requirements understanding, team conflict, user alienation, and unfair design.
- The authors also point out that there is relatively little direct research in software engineering, so broader engineering education evidence is needed to identify transferable practices.

## Approach
- The authors use a systematic literature review method, following established review guidelines in software engineering research, and search **6 databases**: Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore, ACM DL, Compendex, Science Direct, and Scopus.
- The search string centers on combinations of keywords such as empathy / perspective taking / compassion and engineering education, covering studies from **2001–2025**.
- The literature screening process was: an initial **374** papers, **336** after deduplication, **220** excluded by title and abstract, and after full-text review, an additional **33** excluded for not being full papers, **21** unavailable, and **1** non-English paper, resulting in **43** included studies.
- The study combines descriptive analysis and thematic analysis to compare how empathy is conceptualized, taught, its benefits, and its barriers in “engineering education” versus “software engineering education.”
- The core mechanism can be understood simply as: rather than proposing a new algorithm, the authors systematically synthesize existing teaching evidence and distill an operational teaching framework and set of practice patterns for embedding empathy into software courses.

## Results
- The most direct result is an evidence map covering **43 studies**, showing that engineering education typically frames empathy as an **ethical and reflective capacity**, while software engineering tends to translate it into **structured, design-oriented, and measurable practices**.
- The paper argues that in both domains, empathy teaching is generally associated with better **collaboration, ethical reasoning, bias awareness, and learning motivation**; in the software engineering context, it is also associated with better user understanding and support for inclusive design and requirements communication.
- The common software engineering practices summarized by the authors include **empathy maps, personas, user scenarios, design thinking, service learning, reflection, role-play, video-based training, accessibility interventions** and others, though the excerpt does not provide a unified effect size for each method.
- Key limitations are also clearly identified: empathy still has low curricular priority, assessment standards are inconsistent, measurement is difficult, and implementation is constrained by time, teaching staff, and resources.
- At the quantitative level, the clearest numbers provided in the excerpt mainly come from the review process itself: the screening results of **6 databases, 374→336→116→43 papers**; it **does not provide** standardized cross-study performance metrics, significance statistics, or percentage improvements over baseline.
- Therefore, the paper’s “breakthrough” lies more in **evidence integration and curricular positioning** than in a single experiment setting a new benchmark: it proposes that empathy can serve as a **continuous, assessable component** of software engineering courses, rather than merely an auxiliary soft skill.
