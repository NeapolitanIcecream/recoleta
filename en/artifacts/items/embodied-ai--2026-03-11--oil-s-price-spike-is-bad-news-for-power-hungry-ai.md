# Oil's price spike is bad news for power-hungry AI

## Summary
This article analyzes how the oil and gas price increases triggered by the Middle East conflict could raise the costs of AI data centers and semiconductor manufacturing through electricity, liquefied natural gas, and critical material supply chains, while also disrupting the pace of AI expansion. It is not a technical paper, but rather a supply-chain assessment of AI infrastructure costs and supply risks.

## Problem
- The article examines whether fluctuations in oil prices, LNG prices, and the supply of critical industrial gases could increase the costs of AI data centers and chip manufacturing, and slow the expansion of AI infrastructure.
- This matters because AI data centers consume massive amounts of electricity, and advanced chip manufacturing also depends on stable, low-cost energy and materials; once costs rise, cloud providers, chipmakers, and end customers all come under pressure.
- The article also notes that, beyond energy, key semiconductor inputs such as helium and bromine also face supply disruption risks caused by geopolitical conflict, which could further affect chip yields and production capacity.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is straightforward: **war disrupts energy and logistics → oil/LNG prices rise → operating costs for data centers and fabs increase → AI server deployment and chip profit margins come under pressure**.
- The article uses supply-chain transmission logic to analyze the risks: the US hosts most AI data centers, so higher energy prices would first affect compute operating costs; fabs in Taiwan and South Korea are more directly exposed to rising LNG prices.
- At the same time, it adds the upstream materials angle: Qatar's LNG shutdown would not only affect natural gas, but also tighten the supply of helium as a byproduct, and helium is critical to semiconductor manufacturing.
- Its judgment on the chip side is that if high costs persist, some of the pressure may be passed on to customers, but with AI chip supply and demand already tight, the industry as a whole would still face higher volatility and a slower capacity expansion pace.

## Results
- Chip stocks have already shown clear volatility: since the conflict began, shares of TSMC, Samsung Electronics, and SK Hynix at one point fell **9% to 22%**.
- Oil prices have risen significantly: Brent at one point **broke above $100 per barrel**, then later traded around **$87 per barrel**; WTI was around **$83 per barrel**. The US Energy Information Administration expects Brent to average **above $95 per barrel** over the next two months, before potentially falling back to around **$70 per barrel** by year-end.
- The article says oil prices have risen **more than 40%** so far this year, and notes that oil accounts for **38%** of total US energy consumption, so even though oil is not the main source of electricity generation, its price increases still transmit pressure across the broader energy market.
- Morningstar estimates that energy spending by TSMC, Samsung, and SK Hynix accounts for about **3% to 6% of their 2025 revenue**; if the war continues, this portion of costs could "rise significantly."
- On the materials side, Qatar supplies nearly **one-third** of the world's helium; if its LNG shutdown continues, it could tighten the helium market, reduce chip yields, and in the worst case even cause temporary fab shutdowns.
- On bromine supply, about **98%** of South Korea's bromine comes from Israel; although flows are currently relatively stable, if the conflict escalates, memory chip supply could also be affected.
