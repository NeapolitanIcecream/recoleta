# With H-1B work visa lottery underway, experts worry new rules hurt business

## Summary
This is a news report about the impact of changes to U.S. H-1B visa policy on business hiring and innovation, rather than a research paper. The article’s core argument is that the new high fees and weighted lottery rules could significantly raise the barriers for small and medium-sized businesses to access highly skilled international talent, while weakening local innovation and employment.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is whether the Trump administration’s new H-1B rules will harm business hiring, scientific innovation, and local economies, especially for startups and tech/biopharma companies that rely on highly skilled foreign talent.
- This matters because H-1B is an important channel for U.S. companies to access scarce specialized talent; if costs and uncertainty rise, businesses may delay hiring, move positions overseas, or reduce R&D investment.
- The report also highlights the tension between policy goals and reality: the government says the reform is meant to prevent companies from replacing Americans with cheaper foreign workers, but industry interviewees say the real issue is a “talent shortage,” not “intentional replacement.”

## Approach
- This is not a research work proposing a new algorithm or system; its “method” is journalistic interviews and fact synthesis, bringing together observations from business leaders, industry associations, and immigration lawyers on the effects of the new rules.
- The article focuses on two policy mechanisms: first, a new **$100,000** H-1B fee, and second, replacing the previous random lottery with a weighted lottery that prioritizes higher salaries.
- Through interviewee examples, the report explains how these rules are changing business behavior: smaller companies have more difficulty bearing the cost, and some businesses are considering overseas partnerships, overseas offices, or alternative pathways such as O-1, F-1 training programs, and green cards.
- The article also provides institutional background: the annual H-1B cap is **85,000**, registration for this round is taking place this month, and employers will learn by the end of the month whether they were selected.

## Results
- The clearest numerical policy changes are: a new **$100,000** fee for each new H-1B petition; and an annual total of **85,000** available H-1B slots.
- The report says the fee may be manageable for large corporations but is “a major hit” to early-stage startups; however, this is the view of interviewees, and the article **does not provide systematic quantitative research findings** to measure innovation losses or employment effects.
- One specific trend mentioned is that an interviewed immigration lawyer said the number of lottery applications this year is “lower,” but “not as significant as he originally expected”; at the same time, employers he works with **will not** submit lottery registrations for candidates who are not already in the United States, precisely because of the **$100,000** cost.
- Another specific conclusion is that the new weighted lottery will prioritize higher-paid applicants, and is therefore seen as more disadvantageous to tech startups, whose salary competitiveness is usually weaker than that of large companies; however, the article **does not provide selection rates, salary thresholds, or historical comparison data**.
- The report also claims that businesses are already showing behavioral substitution: more companies are seeking channels such as O-1, F-1, and green cards, and some are considering overseas offices or filling roles through overseas partnerships/consulting arrangements; but these too are mainly interview-based observations, with **no formal dataset, experiment, or baseline comparison**.
