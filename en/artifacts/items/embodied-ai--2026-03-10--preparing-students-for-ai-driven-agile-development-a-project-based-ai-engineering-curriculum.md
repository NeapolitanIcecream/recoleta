# Preparing Students for AI-Driven Agile Development: A Project-Based AI Engineering Curriculum

## Summary
This paper proposes a project-based AI Engineering curriculum that deeply integrates agile development with generative AI tools to cultivate students’ AI-assisted engineering capabilities in realistic team environments. The paper also presents an undergraduate case and an initial evaluation, suggesting that this integrated teaching approach may improve learning outcomes and AI literacy.

## Problem
- Existing engineering education often teaches agile methods and AI capabilities separately and with a lecture-heavy approach, making it difficult for students to understand how AI changes teamwork, quality assurance, and the distribution of responsibilities in real projects.
- Learning only Scrum theory or AI tools in isolation cannot adequately train students to carry out AI-driven development under changing requirements, continuous feedback, and multi-person collaboration.
- This matters because software and engineering development in industry are being rapidly reshaped by generative AI and agentic tools, and if curricula are not updated, a skills mismatch will result.

## Approach
- The authors designed a project-based AI Engineering curriculum: a shared core curriculum builds the foundation, then specialized tracks for different engineering directions are layered on top, while “AI as an engineering tool” is continuously integrated throughout teaching.
- About **one-third** of the learning workload is placed into project-based teaching, allowing students to practice agile processes and AI-assisted development end-to-end under realistic constraints.
- In the case study, early-stage undergraduate students work in **teams of 4–6** on a browser-based multiplayer board game project, following a Scrum process of **7 two-week sprints**.
- AI is not treated as a standalone module, but embedded into everyday tasks such as requirements clarification, backlog refinement, architectural reasoning, coding support, testing, and documentation, while emphasizing human responsibility for acceptance criteria, risk trade-offs, and quality.
- To prevent students from blindly relying on AI code generation, the course includes a **20-minute individual oral exam** to verify whether their code understanding and AI usage are appropriate.

## Results
- The paper claims an “initial quantitative evaluation” and qualitative observations, but the overall evidence is still an early case study rather than a rigorous controlled experiment.
- The clearest quantitative result is improved student credit progress: the average missing ECTS proportion fell from **25% in WS 2021** to **12% in WS 2022**, then to **4% in WS 2023** and **3% in WS 2024**.
- In absolute terms, the average ECTS gap improved from **-45/180** (WS 2021) to **-22/180** (WS 2022), **-5/120** (WS 2023), and **-2/60** (WS 2024).
- Another main concrete conclusion is that teaching content must be updated in a more agile way to keep pace with the rapid evolution of tools such as Copilot and agentic frameworks.
- The authors emphasize that the **individual oral exam** is crucial for ensuring mastery of foundational abilities; this is presented as a key practical lesson from AI-assisted programming instruction.
- Another observation is that students tend to anthropomorphize chatbots, even treating ChatGPT as an additional team member; this is an interesting finding about team dynamics and perceptions of human-AI collaboration, though no quantitative metric is provided.
