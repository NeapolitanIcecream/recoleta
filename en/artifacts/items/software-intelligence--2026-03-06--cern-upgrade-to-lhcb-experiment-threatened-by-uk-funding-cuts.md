# CERN upgrade to LHCb experiment threatened by UK funding cuts

## Summary
This is a news report about the UK's withdrawal of funding for CERN's LHCb upgrade II, thereby threatening the experiment's future operation and scientific output, rather than a technical paper. The core message is: unless the decision is overturned, LHCb will likely stop operating in 2033 and be unable to take advantage of the subsequent physics opportunities of the High-Luminosity LHC.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is that UK Research and Innovation (UKRI) has canceled support for the remaining funding of **LHCb upgrade II**, meaning the upgrade project may not be implemented.
- This matters because LHCb mainly studies key questions such as **CP violation** and exotic hadrons (tetraquarks/pentaquarks), which are related to why there is more matter than antimatter in the universe and whether the Standard Model is complete.
- If the upgrade fails, LHCb is expected to end operations in **2033** due to detector lifetime limits and radiation damage, making it unable to continue collecting data during the **HL-LHC** phase and unable to fully leverage CERN's accelerator investment.

## Approach
- This is not research proposing a new scientific method; the “core mechanism” described is actually a **large experimental upgrade plan**: building **LHCb upgrade II** so that the detector can adapt to the higher collision intensity of the **High-Luminosity Large Hadron Collider (HL-LHC)**.
- Put simply, the detector would be made more capable of withstanding higher luminosity and higher data rates, so that it can record more and more precise particle-collision data in the coming years.
- The article says this upgrade would, on top of **upgrade I**, further enable about **an order-of-magnitude (roughly 10x)** increase in luminosity, improving the experiment's ability to measure CP violation and the binding mechanisms of the strong interaction.
- The UK team submitted the proposal in **2021** and received **£49.4m** in initial support in **2022**; about **£5m** of this has already been used for the pre-construction phase, including defining the scope and design of the improved detector with international partners.

## Results
- The text does not report new quantitative experimental-physics results; it mainly provides an assessment of policy- and project-level impacts.
- The LHCb collaboration includes about **1700** scientists and technicians from **22** countries and **100+** institutions, and has produced **800+** papers so far, showing that the experiment has very high scientific output.
- After the current upgrade (shutdown in 2018, completed in **2022**), the article says data-taking capability increased “substantially” and has already led to significant improvements in the precision of many measurements, but it does not provide specific metrics or baseline comparisons.
- The total cost of upgrade II is about **£150m**; if it proceeds as planned, construction would begin in **2027**, components would be installed in **2035**, and the experiment would run for another **5–6 years** before the HL-LHC ends in **2040**.
- If the UK's defunding decision is not overturned, the strongest concrete conclusion is that LHCb will likely end operations in **2033** and **will be unable to exploit the HL-LHC**; the article also says upgrade II could have delivered **about a 10x luminosity increase** relative to upgrade I.
- Economically, the most specific figure given is that an earlier LHCb upgrade brought about **£15m** in contracts to **80+** UK companies; this cut therefore means similar industrial opportunities may be lost.
