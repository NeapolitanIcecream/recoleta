# Show HN: Crazly – structured AI workflows instead of random prompts

## Summary
Crazly is an AI workflow product for users across different professions, advocating the use of structured processes, tool combinations, and preset prompts instead of random trial-and-error prompting. It addresses the execution gap of “knowing AI is important but not knowing how to apply it efficiently,” rather than proposing a new foundation model or agent method.

## Problem
- Although many users have access to AI tools such as ChatGPT, they lack systematic workflows, so they can only keep trying random prompts and end up with generic, low-value results.
- AI tools, models, and “best practices” evolve too quickly, and users do not have time to continuously research, filter, and learn them, leading to high adoption barriers and low execution efficiency.
- This matters because the gap in AI usage effectiveness is widening: people who are truly efficient rely on well-structured workflows, while ordinary users lack systems they can directly execute.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: first segment users by profession, then provide a structured AI workflow for the corresponding role, instead of asking users to figure out prompts on their own.
- Each workflow includes three parts: which AI tools to use, in what order to use them, and what exact prompts to input at each step.
- The product claims these workflows come from methods used by the “top 1% of professionals,” and are designed with the goal of “producing real output immediately,” rather than serving as instructional content.
- The interaction flow has three steps: choose a profession → get the complete playbook → execute step by step, reducing the cost of configuration, research, and trial and error.

## Results
- The text **does not provide rigorous experiments, datasets, or baseline comparisons**, so there are no verifiable academic quantitative results.
- The most specific efficiency claim is that users can complete in **20 minutes** work that previously required **half a day**, but it does not specify the task type, sample size, or evaluation method.
- It claims coverage of workflows for **5 professional roles**, but does not list the full set of roles or differences in effectiveness.
- On the content supply side, it claims workflows are “**updated weekly**” and that tool mappings are “**curated by humans rather than scraped**,” but provides no quality evaluation metrics.
- The claimed user groups are mainly early adopters such as freelancer, creator, and developer, but there are no retention, conversion, or satisfaction figures to support this.
