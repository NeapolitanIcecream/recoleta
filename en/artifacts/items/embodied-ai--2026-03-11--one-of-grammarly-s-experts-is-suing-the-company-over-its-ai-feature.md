# One of Grammarly's 'experts' is suing the company over its AI feature

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a news report about a legal dispute involving Grammarly / Superhuman for using the identities of real people without consent in its AI "Expert Review" feature. The core information is: the feature has been shut down following public pressure, and a class-action lawsuit has been filed.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the article is that the company used the names, identities, and reputations of journalists and experts in an AI feature to add "expert endorsement" to product suggestions, without obtaining permission from the individuals involved.
- This matters because it involves privacy rights, publicity rights, the legality of using someone's identity for commercial purposes, and how AI products handle real people's data and reputations.
- More broadly, it reflects the compliance and ethical risks that generative AI products may create when they "borrow" real people's identities, content, or style.

## Approach
- The article does not propose a research method; instead, it describes a product mechanism: the AI editing feature labeled generated suggestions as coming from certain "expert perspectives" or "Expert Review."

- The report notes that multiple real media figures and authors were included in the feature, including Julia Angwin, Casey Newton, and current employees of The Verge.
- After the controversy emerged, the company first provided an email address allowing writers and scholars to request removal, and then announced that it would disable the feature entirely and rethink the approach.
