# London Man wore smart glasses for High Court 'coaching'

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a news report about a witness in the UK High Court whom a judge found was suspected of receiving external “prompts” or “coaching” through smart glasses and a mobile phone while giving evidence. Its core significance is that it highlights the procedural risks that wearable devices and generative AI may pose to the credibility of judicial testimony.

## Problem
- The report focuses on the question of whether a witness secretly received external answers or prompts through smart glasses, a mobile phone, or a voice connection while testifying in court.
- This matters because once testimony is externally influenced during cross-examination, the credibility of the evidence, the fairness of the hearing, and the integrity of the judicial process are all undermined.
- The article also points out that the combination of generative AI (which the witness blamed on ChatGPT) and wearable devices could make real-time “remote assistance” harder to detect.

## Approach
- The article does not propose a research method; instead, it reports how the judge made factual findings based on courtroom observations and events that occurred during the hearing.
- The judge noticed that the witness “seemed to pause quite a bit” before answering and heard “interference” coming from around him, so she ordered him to remove the smart glasses.
- After the smart glasses were removed, the mobile phone in the witness’s pocket began broadcasting a voice; based on this, together with call records, device connections, and his conduct in court, the judge concluded that he had been “assisted or coached” during cross-examination.
- Put as simply as possible, the core mechanism is: the witness was suspected of transmitting audio from a phone call to the smart glasses and receiving external prompts in real time while answering questions.

## Results
- There were no research experiments, datasets, models, baselines, or quantitative metrics, so there are no paper-style quantitative results to report.
- The judge’s key conclusion was that the witness was “assisted or coached” in answering during cross-examination at the January hearing.
- The report says his phone made multiple calls to a contact named “abra kadabra”; the witness said the person was a taxi driver, but the judge did not accept that explanation.
- The judge further found that the call had connected to the smart glasses and continued while he was giving evidence, until the phone was taken away.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that the judge believed the witness was “untruthful” in denying the use of the smart glasses and the related calls, and therefore his evidence was treated as “unreliable and untruthful”.
