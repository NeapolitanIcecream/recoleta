# The Future of Software Engineering Conferences: A New Zealand Perspective

## Summary
This article discusses the systemic participation barriers that software engineering conferences pose for researchers in geographically remote regions such as New Zealand, and proposes recommendations for more inclusive conference organization reforms. The core argument is that hybrid participation, geographic diversification, cost control, and governance improvements can enhance equitable global participation.

## Problem
- Flagship software engineering conferences have long been concentrated in North America and Europe, leaving researchers in remote regions such as New Zealand facing **high travel costs, extremely long flight times, visa barriers**, and fewer informal collaboration opportunities.
- Academic calendars in the Southern Hemisphere are misaligned with mainstream conference schedules and submission cycles, and this, combined with weaker regional representation, reduces conference accessibility for faculty, caregivers, students, and early-career researchers.
- These barriers not only affect individual career development, but also skew conference governance, the origins of research participants, and research conclusions toward a small number of regions, undermining the global representativeness and fairness of the software engineering community.

## Approach
- The authors analyze the sources of these problems from a New Zealand perspective: conference location distribution, travel and registration costs, visas, academic calendar misalignment, insufficient representation on steering committees, and geographic bias in research samples.
- They propose **hybrid/virtual conferences** and regional satellite-event models, aiming to reduce costs, lower carbon emissions, and provide remote participants with a more complete interactive and social experience.
- They recommend expanding **geographic rotation and hosting diversity** for conferences, and helping lower-resourced regions host conferences by decoupling general chair appointments from local hosting capacity and providing financial and organizational support.
- They suggest **flexible pricing and cost control**, such as charging based on purchasing power or regional differences, prioritizing travel support for disadvantaged regions, and using university venues instead of expensive hotel conference spaces.
- They recommend reforming **governance and participation mechanisms**, including increasing geographic and career-stage diversity on steering committees, improving cross-time-zone meeting arrangements, and providing more leadership and service opportunities for early-career researchers.

## Results
- The paper is a **position/policy recommendation** article. It does not report experiments, models, or benchmark evaluation results, nor does it provide standard quantitative performance metrics.
- The article presents several key facts to support its argument: in the **2023–2026** rotation of flagship conferences, there is only **1** major conference per year in regions outside North America/Europe, for a total of **4**; among them, **2 are in Brazil, 1 in South Korea, and 1 in Australia**, indicating that most regions of the world still have few opportunities to host.
- As an example of cost, attending **ICSE 2026** plus one **2-day** co-located event would cost ACM/IEEE member early-bird registrants about **1280 USD ≈ 2220 NZD**; airfare from New Zealand typically exceeds **3000 NZD (to the U.S.)** or **5000 NZD (to Europe)**.
- Long-haul travel typically involves more than **20 hours** of flight time, further increasing time-zone burden and time costs and reducing participation feasibility for researchers with teaching or caregiving responsibilities.
- The strongest empirical example comes from increased participation following improved accessibility: when **ICSME 2025** was held in Auckland, attendance from New Zealand reached **26 people (13%)**. Based on this, the authors argue that closer or more accessible locations can significantly increase local/regional participation.
