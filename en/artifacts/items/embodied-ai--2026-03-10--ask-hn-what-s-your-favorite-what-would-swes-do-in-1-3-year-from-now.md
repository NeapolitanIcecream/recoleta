# Ask HN: What's your favorite "what would SWEs do in 1-3 year from now?"

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a Hacker News discussion thread about how software engineers' work may be reorganized by LLM-driven tools over the next 1–3 years. The content mainly consists of the author's personal speculation about the labor market, compensation structures, corporate M&A, and immigration trends, without any systematic methodology or experimental validation.

## Problem
- The question under discussion is: as AI coding tools such as Claude Code and Codex develop rapidly, what will software engineers do in the next 1–3 years, and how will roles change?
- This matters because if AI significantly changes the software development process, it could affect employment, wages, company organizational structures, and the direction of technology investment.
- But the text is essentially a solicitation of opinions and market forecasts, not a formal definition or solution of a verifiable research problem.

## Approach
- The core approach is not to propose an algorithm, but to make scenario-based predictions grounded in personal judgment: assuming that LLM coding stacks will continue to improve and drive “labor replacement” and job restructuring.
- The author uses a very simple economic narrative to explain the future: traditional SWE bargaining power declines, and non-tech companies may shift toward something more like pay-for-results, even including bidding or bounty-style models for “fixing bugs / prompting AI.”
- For large companies, the author believes engineers will not disappear immediately, but will instead shift toward more frequent “guiding/supervising AI” to develop new features.
- The author further speculates that capital markets will respond to expectations of efficiency gains, for example with private equity more aggressively acquiring companies and using AI to reduce costs and improve efficiency.

## Results
- No quantitative experimental results, datasets, metrics, or comparisons with baselines are provided.
- One of the clearest concrete claims is the **1–3 year** time window, meaning the author believes the division of software engineering labor will change significantly in the relatively short term.
- The author claims that traditional SWE roles will “lose pricing power,” while technical work in non-tech companies may become closer to a market competing on delivered outcomes, but there is **no numerical evidence** supporting this.
- The author predicts that engineers at large technology companies will take on more responsibility for **steering AI**, but provides **no cases, samples, or measurement results**.
- The author also predicts that **STEM immigration to the US/UK will drop sharply**, and that the IT industry will undergo more **M&A** and margin compression, but these are all unverified macro-level judgments.
