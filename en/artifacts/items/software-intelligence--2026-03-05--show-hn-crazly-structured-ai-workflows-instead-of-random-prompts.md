# Show HN: Crazly – structured AI workflows instead of random prompts

## Summary
Crazly is not an academic paper, but a product introduction for an AI workflow tool aimed at professional use cases. Its core claim is to replace fragmented prompt trial-and-error with structured processes. It attempts to productize AI usage methods across different professions to help users obtain executable outputs more quickly.

## Problem
- Although many users know AI is powerful, in practice they rely on random prompts and often get generic, low-quality results.
- AI tools, models, and usage techniques evolve too quickly, and ordinary users do not have time to continuously research, test, and learn them, resulting in high adoption barriers and low efficiency.
- The gap between professional users and ordinary users comes from “whether they have mastered systematic workflows,” which affects work efficiency and the effectiveness of AI adoption.

## Approach
- Match users with pre-designed AI workflows by professional role (such as developer, marketer, student, founder), rather than asking them to explore on their own.
- Each workflow provides a structured playbook: including which AI tools to use, in what order, for which tasks, and the “exact prompts.”
- Workflows are curated by “people who have already figured out effective methods,” emphasizing curated rather than scraped, positioning the product as encapsulated experience and an execution system.
- The usage process is simplified into three steps: choose a profession, get an AI workflow, and execute step by step, reducing setup and learning costs.
- The product emphasizes direct output rather than theoretical instruction, with the goal of enabling users to quickly reuse best practices.

## Results
- The text **does not provide a rigorous experimental design, dataset, baseline comparison, or reproducible quantitative evaluation**, so there are no quantified results in the academic sense.
- The most specific efficiency claim is that, by following its workflow, users can “**do in 20 minutes what used to take half a day**,” but it does not specify the task type, sample size, or comparison baseline.
- The product covers “**5 professional roles**,” indicating that workflows are currently provided for 5 categories of professional roles.
- The stated update frequency is “**new workflows monthly**” and “**added every week**,” indicating that its knowledge base/workflows are continuously expanding, though the two phrasings are slightly inconsistent.
- The target users are described as early adopters such as “**freelancers, creators and developers**,” indicating that its initial audience leans toward knowledge workers and execution-oriented individual users.
- Its strongest concrete claim is not a performance metric, but the product positioning: replacing random prompt experimentation with structured, profession-tailored AI workflow to improve practical execution efficiency.
