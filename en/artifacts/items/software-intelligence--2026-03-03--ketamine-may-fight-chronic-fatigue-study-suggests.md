# Ketamine may fight chronic fatigue, study suggests

## Summary
This was a small proof-of-concept randomized crossover trial for chronic fatigue, evaluating whether low-dose ketamine can rapidly reduce fatigue. The study found that it showed some short-term antifatigue signal after administration, but its advantage over the active placebo was limited, and validation in a larger sample is still needed.

## Problem
- The study aims to address the lack of **effective, fast-acting treatments for chronic fatigue**; this symptom affects work, family life, and basic functioning, and rest is usually ineffective.
- This is important because the U.S. CDC estimates that about **3.3 million** adults are affected by chronic fatigue, and it is especially common among cancer-treatment survivors and patients with fibromyalgia, lupus, and chronic fatigue syndrome.
- Existing standard management relies mainly on exercise, but many patients **lack the energy or motivation to start and stick with it**, so interventions that can provide a quick “boost” are needed.

## Approach
- The core method was simple: researchers tested whether a **single low-dose ketamine infusion** could improve patients' energy and reduce fatigue over a short period.
- The mechanistic hypothesis came from prior findings that the glutamate receptor **mGluR5** is associated with chronic fatigue, and ketamine can block glutamate receptors, so it may act on the relevant neural pathways.
- The study used a **randomized crossover trial**: **10** participants with chronic fatigue each received one ketamine dose and one midazolam dose (active control/active placebo), with **2 weeks** between administrations.
- Before the trial, the study prespecified a success criterion: if real-time fatigue scores **3 days** after ketamine administration fell by **20%** from baseline, it would be considered worth pursuing in a larger trial.
- The authors proposed that its potential clinical role would not be as a long-term medication, but as a short-term **“bridging” intervention** to help patients regain enough energy to engage in long-term strategies already known to be effective but difficult to initiate, such as exercise.

## Results
- **3 days** after ketamine administration, real-time fatigue scores were on average **21%** lower than before infusion, **meeting and slightly exceeding** the prespecified **20%** positive threshold.
- The largest effect appeared **24 hours** after administration, with real-time fatigue scores down by nearly **39%**.
- However, ketamine was **not significantly superior to** the active control midazolam; the latter also unexpectedly showed an antifatigue signal, making causal interpretation more complicated.
- The study originally planned to recruit **59 people**, but actually enrolled only **10**; the authors explicitly noted that this was mainly due to **COVID-19** and strict eligibility criteria, so statistical power was limited.
- The strongest conclusion is that, for chronic fatigue that has long lacked interventions, ketamine showed a **rapid, short-term, clinically meaningful signal worthy of further study**, but the current evidence remains preliminary.
