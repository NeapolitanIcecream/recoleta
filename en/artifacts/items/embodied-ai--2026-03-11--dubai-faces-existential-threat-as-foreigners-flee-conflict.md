# Dubai faces existential threat as foreigners flee conflict

## Summary
This is not a research paper but a news report describing how the US-Israeli conflict with Iran has shaken the foundations of Dubai as a haven for tourism, finance, and expatriates. The article’s central point is that the war has undermined Dubai’s sense of security, international image, and economic model that depends on a foreign population.

## Problem
- The article examines how the spillover of regional war poses a “existential threat” to Dubai in terms of security, the economy, and society, especially given its heavy dependence on tourism, aviation, finance, and foreign labor.
- This matters because **more than 90% of Dubai’s residents are foreigners**, and tourism generates **about $30 billion** annually; once expatriate residents and tourists leave, the city’s operations and growth model are weakened.
- The report also emphasizes that the conflict’s effects are uneven: higher-income foreigners are more able to leave, while many economic migrants from South Asia and elsewhere lack the ability to depart and face greater physical and informational risks.

## Approach
- The article does not present a scientific method; instead, it organizes evidence through journalistic investigation and on-the-ground interviews.
- Mechanistically, the author uses interviews with residents, teachers, drivers, scholars, banks, and laborers’ families to show how the war is gradually eroding the “Dubai dream” through **missile threats, infrastructure damage, departures, declining tourism, and information control**.
- The article supports its argument with several specific incidents, such as the attack on Dubai airport, attacks on data centers that disrupted digital payments, a hotel being hit, and the evacuation of staff from foreign financial institutions.
- At the same time, the report contrasts the surface-level appearance of people still being able to “live as usual” with the reality that labor groups struggle to speak out and to leave, illustrating the asymmetry in how risk is distributed.

## Results
- The article does not provide research experiments, models, or benchmark results, so there are **no quantitative academic metrics** to report.
- The strongest concrete factual conclusions include: the UAE absorbed **more than two-thirds** of Iran’s strikes; of about **1,700 Iranian projectiles**, **more than 90%** were intercepted, but some still hit significant targets.
- Dubai airport was attacked and shut down, and after two data centers were hit, residents were temporarily **unable to use their phones for digital payments**.
- Tourism is described as contributing **about $30 billion** annually; **more than 90% of Dubai’s residents are foreigners**, making it highly sensitive to population outflows.
- The article says that **tens of thousands** of residents and tourists have already fled Dubai; Citibank and Standard Chartered have evacuated staff from Dubai over security concerns.
- Since the conflict began, there have been **4 deaths** in the UAE, of whom **3 were South Asian migrant workers**; a drone strike near Dubai airport also injured **4 people** (two Ghanaians, one Indian, and one Bangladeshi).
