# From Education to Evidence: A Collaborative Practice Research Platform for AI-Integrated Agile Development

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework that transforms a project-based, AI-integrated agile education environment into a collaborative practice research platform, aimed at generating evidence relevant to software engineering practice more quickly. Its core value is to establish a repeatable, scalable intermediate layer with real stakeholder participation between laboratory research and real industrial settings.

## Problem
- Agile software development and generative AI/agentic tools are changing too quickly, and traditional research often becomes inapplicable by the time results are published due to **time lag**.
- Many studies also face **theory and transfer gaps**: they lack clear applicability conditions, context descriptions, and reusable evidence, making adoption in practice difficult.
- AI has entered development, testing, and decision-making processes, but issues of quality, traceability, accountability, and trust require more timely research and governance mechanisms.

## Approach
- The authors design a **project-based AI engineering education program** as a collaborative research platform: researchers pose questions, external stakeholders bring real needs, and student teams implement and evaluate solutions in agile sprints.
- The platform uses a fixed **Scrum cadence**: 7 two-week sprints, including Dailies, midterm demos, Sprint Review, and cross-team Review Parties after sprints 3, 5, and 7.
- Research materials are accumulated through **standardized evidence carriers**, such as backlog/user stories, code repositories, architecture and test documentation, review records, and retrospectives, to support cross-semester comparison and knowledge accumulation.
- **Quality gates** are used to ensure accountability in AI use, including a 20-minute individual oral exam, evaluation based on repositories and living documentation, and peer assessment; the focus is not on “whether AI was used,” but on “whether AI outputs and decisions can be explained and validated.”
- The course includes instructional units called “Schools,” which teach technical content such as React, TypeScript, and client-server architecture together with AI capabilities, limitations, validation, and refactoring practices.

## Results
- The paper’s results are mainly **evidence of platform feasibility and scalability**, rather than strict performance improvements compared with baseline methods; the authors also explicitly acknowledge that the current findings are primarily early observations and lack more systematic quantitative evaluation.
- From the **summer semester of 2023 to the winter semester of 2025**, the number of projects executed per semester grew from **7 to 18**, indicating that the platform can maintain a fixed process and repeatability while scaling.
- Over the same period, the number of participating students grew from **26 to 111**; the typical team size was **4–6 people**, indicating that the platform can support larger cohorts and strengthen the potential for cross-team triangulation.
- In the **winter semester of 2025**, **20 projects were proposed and 18 were executed**. The authors view this “oversupply” as a built-in topic selection mechanism that can help research remain relevant to emerging practice problems.
- Each semester consistently included **1–3 real industry projects**, covering domains such as mobility, smart city, and AI-enabled systems; based on this, the authors claim the platform can steadily introduce real constraints, success criteria, and context, improving evidence transferability.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that a fixed sprint cadence, real stakeholders, unified artifacts, and AI accountability quality gates together enable the platform to generate **timely, context-rich, reusable** practice evidence more efficiently; however, the paper **does not provide quantitative comparative metrics against other research paradigms**.
