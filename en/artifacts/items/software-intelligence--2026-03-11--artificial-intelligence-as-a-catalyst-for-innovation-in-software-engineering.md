# Artificial Intelligence as a Catalyst for Innovation in Software Engineering

## Summary
This paper explores how artificial intelligence acts as a catalyst for “agility and innovation” in software engineering, combining a literature review with an exploratory survey of practitioners to analyze the role of AI in requirements, coding, testing, and project management. The core conclusion is that respondents generally view AI as a holistic driver of improved productivity, agile responsiveness, and innovation capability.

## Problem
- Software engineering faces long-standing problems such as frequently changing requirements, tight delivery cycles, and difficulty ensuring quality, and traditional rigid processes are hard to adapt.
- Even when using Agile methods, teams are still often constrained by complex requirements management, difficulties in cross-team collaboration, and inconsistent governance in large-scale projects.
- The paper focuses on the question of whether and how AI can improve agility, efficiency, and innovation across the full software engineering lifecycle, which is important for modern high-frequency iterative development.

## Approach
- It first conducts a literature review to map the main application areas of AI in software engineering: requirements engineering, design and planning, coding, testing and quality assurance, maintenance and operations, and project management and collaboration.
- The core mechanism is summarized as using ML/NLP/LLM to automate repetitive work, make predictions from historical data, and provide developers with code/documentation/decision support, thereby enabling teams to respond to change more quickly.
- An online survey was conducted and distributed to more than 1,000 software-engineering-related email addresses, ultimately collecting 64 complete responses; the analysis covered AI adoption, impacts on agility/productivity/innovation, challenges, and future expectations.
- Exploratory factor analysis and reliability testing were applied to the scale to verify whether “AI impact and adoption readiness” can be treated as a single latent construct.

## Results
- The survey obtained **64 complete responses** in total, and the authors explicitly position the results as **descriptive and exploratory**, without claiming statistical generalizability.
- The scale was suitable for factor analysis: **KMO=0.870**; Bartlett’s test of sphericity was significant, **χ²=197.802, df=21, p<.001**.
- Exploratory factor analysis produced a **single-factor solution**, with acceptable model fit: **χ²=18.688, df=14, p=0.177**.
- Item loadings on this factor ranged from **0.58–0.86**; the highest were **innovation (Q10)=0.855**, **productivity (Q8)=0.800**, and **agility (Q6)=0.774**, indicating that respondents most strongly associated AI’s value with these three outcomes.
- The scale showed strong internal consistency: **McDonald’s ω=0.882** (95% CI **0.837–0.926**), **Cronbach’s α=0.872** (95% CI **0.807–0.937**).
- The paper also cites specific claims from related studies, for example that AI-driven automated code review and defect detection can achieve accuracy rates of up to **95%**; however, in this paper’s own empirical section, **it does not provide direct comparative effect-size figures between AI tools and manual/traditional methods on real development tasks**.
