# Is it still worth learning to code?

## Summary
This article argues that in an era when AI can generate code, learning programming is still worthwhile, and is especially more important for career development. The core reason is not that humans should replace machines in writing code, but that people with programming ability can use AI to achieve orders-of-magnitude higher output and better judgment.

## Problem
- The article discusses the question: **after AI can generate code from natural language, is it still necessary for people to learn programming**, especially whether it is still worth investing time in for career returns.
- This matters because the same doubt will extend to writing, design, law, medicine, and other fields: if execution is automated, should people still learn skills at all.
- The author believes it is a mistake to understand learning merely as “personally producing results”; what will truly be scarce is judgment, understanding, and taste — that is, “discernment.”

## Approach
- This is not an experimental paper, but an opinion analysis article; its core mechanism is to shift the value of “learning programming” from **manual execution** to **high-quality decision-making and control with the help of AI**.
- The author’s simple argument is that people who can program and use AI are not just slightly better than beginners, but significantly stronger because they understand system design, architecture, trade-offs, and implementation constraints.
- Therefore, the focus of learning should also change: place less emphasis on mechanically writing code, and more emphasis on **system design, architecture, trade-offs**, while building real judgment as quickly as possible through hands-on projects.
- The article further generalizes this mechanism: when “making something” becomes cheap, the real bottleneck becomes “knowing what to make, how to evaluate quality, and how to choose among options.”

## Results
- The strongest quantitative claim the author gives is that **strong programmer + AI** compared with **beginner + AI / no relevant ability** may reach **10–20x better** productivity, but the article **does not provide experimental design, datasets, evaluation metrics, or a formal baseline**.
- The article also offers an empirical observation: in large companies, **single engineers** may leave entire **dev / data teams** “left in the dust,” but likewise **does not provide specific numbers, sample sizes, or reproducible evidence**.
- It does not report standard machine learning/robotics experimental results, public benchmarks, success rates, error rates, throughput, or statistical significance.
- The most concrete concluding claim is: **learning programming is still worthwhile, and more worthwhile than before**; the reason is not to execute instead of the machine, but to improve judgment and leverage when collaborating with AI.
