# The plan to refill the Great Salt Lake

## Summary
This article describes Utah’s large-scale restoration plan to “refill and raise” the Great Salt Lake by 2034. The core message is: the problem has become extremely serious, but current progress, funding, and water-conservation efforts still fall clearly short of the goal.

## Problem
- The Great Salt Lake has been shrinking for decades and in 2022 approached a catastrophic low; the exposed lake bed created a risk of toxic dust containing heavy metals and threatened the regional ecosystem.
- This is not only an environmental issue, but also affects public health, the local economy, and winter sports: research says lake-effect snow may contribute **up to 10%** of annual snowfall in the Wasatch Mountains, and another 2024 study says that if the lake disappeared completely, mountain precipitation could be cut in half.
- The root cause is mainly human overallocation of water rather than drought alone: a 2022 study said about **75%** of the problem was human-caused, and agriculture accounted for **65% of the lake’s water losses between 2020 and 2024**.

## Approach
- The overall goal is to keep more water in the lake by **2034**, mainly by reducing water use in agriculture, mining, households, and businesses, rather than relying on a single engineering solution.
- On the policy side, Utah has amended its water laws over the past five years to define “letting water flow to the lake” as a beneficial use, supporting **donation, purchase, and leasing** of water rights so water can voluntarily be redirected to the lake.
- Implementation uses a “puzzle-piece” combination: subsidizing farmers to improve irrigation efficiency, strengthening water measurement, controlling the water-intensive invasive plant **phragmites**, and evaluating water transfers from the **Newfoundland Evaporation Basin**.
- The state government is also directly reclaiming water through asset acquisition: it bought the bankrupt **U.S. Magnesium** for **$30 million** to obtain its recently used **65,000-80,000 acre-feet** of water; meanwhile, philanthropic funding plans to expand inflows to the lake by leasing agricultural water rights.
- In addition, raising the berm that separates the lake’s north and south arms is being used to regulate water and salt movement, helping in the short term to reduce salinity and ease the risk of ecological collapse.

## Results
- To reach the target by 2034, a task force report says the lake needs about **800,000 acre-feet** of additional water each year for the next **8 years**, which the article describes as roughly **400,000 Olympic swimming pools** of water annually.
- Compared with actual progress: Utah sent only **163,468 acre-feet** of additional water to the Great Salt Lake last year. Although that was the highest level in nearly **5 years**, it remains far below what is needed; the report also says the required annual volume is about **twice** the amount of water the state has secured for the lake over the past **5 years**.
- The historical scale of decline is very large: in 2022 the lake’s surface elevation was nearly **10 feet** below the healthy threshold, and the lake’s volume was down about **67%** from the period when pioneers first settled there.
- Ecological impacts have already been quantified: the Great Salt Lake provides critical stopover habitat each year for about **10 million birds** and more than **330 species**; a 2022 study observed major disappearances of brine flies and brine shrimp, followed by a “return of species” after heavy snow years and improved salinity.
- On funding, the state has established a **$40 million** water trust; Josh Romney has raised about **30%** of his **$100 million** target and estimates that stabilizing the lake may ultimately require **$500 million**; Ducks Unlimited has separately pledged **$100 million** for wetland restoration; and the state may also seek about **$1 billion** in federal support.
- The article does not provide strict experimental results in terms of “percentage improvement in effect”; the strongest concrete conclusion is that current policy and funding mobilization are expanding, but at the current pace they are still clearly insufficient to meet the 2034 restoration target, and there is no historical precedent of any terminal saline lake being fully restored.
