# How to Prepare for AGI for Dummies

## Summary
This is an AGI preparedness guide for ordinary readers, not a technical paper. It tries to answer how AI will affect work and personal preparation, and offers a set of practical action recommendations.

## Problem
- The problem the article aims to solve is: how people without a technical background can assess risks and make realistic preparations amid rapid progress in AGI/AI.
- This matters because the author believes the diffusion of AI capabilities is accelerating, and the impact on jobs is uneven, with roles centered on information transfer, organization, and rewriting being especially vulnerable.
- The core pain point is not understanding how models work, but that ordinary people lack a clear sense of AI’s capability boundaries, their own irreplaceable skills, and financial/career buffers.

## Approach
- The method is essentially a **personal adaptation strategy checklist**: first, use tools like ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini frequently to build intuition for what AI is good at and where it fails.
- Readers are encouraged to identify the truly hard-to-replace part of their work: not their job title, but the tacit judgment and situational awareness compressed from long experience.
- It recommends avoiding career paths that are purely about moving information from one format to another, and instead shifting toward work that defines problems, judges the value of information, and sets constraints and success criteria.
- It emphasizes that giving good prompts actually corresponds to clear thinking and the ability to break down problems; therefore, improving expression, writing, and explanatory ability is a foundational skill for using AI.
- It also proposes risk hedging: learn a skill in the physical world, and make your finances more resilient (low spending, savings, emergency fund) to withstand future career volatility.

## Results
- The article **does not provide experiments, datasets, benchmarks, or quantitative evaluation results**, so there are no verifiable academic metric improvements.
- One of the strongest concrete claims is a time recommendation: use one mainstream AI tool every day for **1 month** to develop a feel for its capability boundaries.
- The author explicitly names high-risk job categories such as **junior analyst, copywriter, first-year associate, research coordinator, content manager**, arguing that these jobs involve a great deal of summarizing, drafting, compiling, and rearranging information.
- The article’s key conclusion is that the people with more of an advantage in the future will not be those who move information around, but those who can judge **what information matters, and why it matters**.
- Another core conclusion is that, faced with potentially greater economic and career turbulence over the next **10 years**, the most effective preparation is not precise prediction, but building buffers such as skills, cash flow, and emergency reserves.
