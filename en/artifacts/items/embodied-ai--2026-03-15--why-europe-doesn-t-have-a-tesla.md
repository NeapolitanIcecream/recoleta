# Why Europe doesn't have a Tesla

## Summary
This article argues that the core reason Europe lacks “Tesla-scale” companies is not capital, research, or culture, but rather the high and rigid costs of layoffs, which suppress firms’ willingness to pursue high-risk innovation. The author contends that Europe remains strong at incremental improvement, but is institutionally constrained and slowed in fields like electric vehicles and autonomous driving that require repeated trial and error.

## Problem
- The article seeks to explain why Europe has produced neither frontier firms like Tesla, Google, and Waymo, nor kept pace with the United States even in the auto industry, where it has traditionally held an advantage.
- Its importance lies in the fact that when the technological paradigm shifts from internal-combustion-style incremental improvement to high-uncertainty innovation such as electrification, autonomous driving, and software-defined vehicles, economies that cannot absorb the cost of failure will steadily lose competitiveness.
- The author rejects common explanations such as insufficient R&D, expensive energy, high taxes, and cumbersome procedures, arguing that these are not enough to explain why California can still produce Tesla and Waymo while Europe cannot.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: **if firing employees is extremely costly, companies will avoid pursuing high-risk projects that may later require layoffs**, and will instead allocate resources to businesses that are more stable, predictable, and subject to less change.
- The article relies mainly on institutional comparison, systematically contrasting the United States with Germany, France, Italy, Spain, and others in terms of layoff-related rules, including statutory severance, union/works council consultation, social selection tests, regulatory approval, waiting periods, and additional compensation plans.
- Using examples from automobiles, electric vehicles, autonomous driving, and smartphones, the author shows how the “cost of failure” changes corporate strategy: American firms can iterate through repeated trial and error, while European firms, even when they have the technology and capital, are more inclined to delay transformation, take fewer risks, or place high-risk projects outside Europe.
- The article also points to a growth-related side effect: small firms may enjoy exemptions, but once they grow larger they enter the range where rigid labor laws apply, making it harder for European startups to scale, reducing the likelihood of domestic acquisitions, and even pushing them to relocate to the United States.
- On policy, the author proposes a “flexicurity” approach: rather than placing all employment protection on firms, worker income should be protected through mechanisms like portable severance accounts, unemployment insurance, and retraining subsidies, while lowering firms’ costs of experimentation.

## Results
- This is not an experimental paper and does not provide formal causal identification or a unified benchmark test; its “results” consist mainly of cross-country comparisons of institutional costs and case evidence.
- Per capita income in the EU’s original six member states: **in 2000 it was only 10% below the United States; today it is 20% lower**, used as macro background for Europe’s long-term relative decline.
- Estimated layoff/restructuring costs: the total cost per dismissed worker is about **31 months of wages** in Germany and **38 months** in France; about **52 months** in Italy and **62 months** in Spain; versus only about **7 months** in the United States. Based on this, the author argues that European firms face much greater downside risk when projects fail.
- Comparison of labor-market rigidity: **the probability that an American worker is fired in a given year is about 10 times that of a German worker**; in terms of monthly layoff rates, Germany is about **0.1%** and the United States about **1%**.
- Firm cases: Google laid off **12,000** workers in 2023 and spent **$2.1 billion**, or about **$175,000 per worker**; when Opel closed its Bochum plant in Germany, it paid **€552 million** to **3,300** employees; the Audi Brussels closure cost **€610 million**, or about **€200,000 per worker**; Nokia’s 2012 restructuring cost **€1.8 billion**, and laying off **2,000** workers at a German plant in 2008 cost **€200 million**.
- Cases of innovation failure and experimentation: Apple abandoned its autonomous driving project after spending **$10 billion**; GM Cruise was acquired in 2016 for **$1 billion**, generated only **$102 million** in revenue in 2023, lost **$3.4 billion**, and shut down in 2024. The author argues that the United States can absorb these failures, making it more likely to produce successes like Waymo.
- European startup and capital outcomes: **79%** of startup acquisitions tracked by Crunchbase in **2012–2016** took place in the United States; among acquisitions occurring in Europe, **44%** were completed by American companies; European companies accounted for only **7%** of acquisitions of American startups. The article also says that the median internal rate of return for European venture capital in **1998–2022** was about **5 percentage points** lower than in the United States, supporting the argument that the problem is insufficient returns to opportunity rather than insufficient capital.
