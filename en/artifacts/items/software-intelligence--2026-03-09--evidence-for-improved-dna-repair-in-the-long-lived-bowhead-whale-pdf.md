# Evidence for improved DNA repair in the long-lived bowhead whale [pdf]

## Summary
This study investigates why the bowhead whale, which lives for more than 200 years, remains resistant to cancer despite its enormous body size and extremely large number of cells. The authors propose that, rather than relying primarily on additional tumor-suppressive “barriers,” the bowhead whale depends on stronger and more accurate DNA repair to maintain genome stability.

## Problem
- The study aims to solve the question of how the bowhead whale avoids high cancer risk under conditions of **extreme longevity (>200 years)** and **very large body size (>80,000 kg)**, that is, to explain one mechanism underlying Peto’s paradox.
- This matters because large body size and long lifespan usually imply more cell divisions and greater accumulation of DNA damage; understanding its cancer resistance and longevity mechanisms could provide new ideas for anticancer and anti-aging interventions.
- One prevailing explanation is that large animals prevent cancer through increased copies of tumor suppressor genes or stronger apoptosis; this paper tests whether the bowhead whale uses a different strategy.

## Approach
- The authors compared primary fibroblasts from bowhead whales, humans, and other mammals, testing the number of oncogenic hits required for malignant transformation, apoptosis/senescence features, mutation rates, and the capacities of multiple DNA repair pathways.
- They found that bowhead whale cells **do not require more oncogenic hits**: whereas human fibroblasts typically require hTERT + HRAS(G12V) + SV40 LT + SV40 ST, bowhead whale cells could be transformed with only **HRAS(G12V) + SV40 LT** in an hTERT background; CRISPR disruption of **TP53 and RB1** plus HRAS was also sufficient.
- The authors then used whole-genome sequencing, SMM-seq, HPRT mutagenesis assays, DSB repair reporter systems, γH2AX/53BP1 foci, micronucleus assays, and chromosomal abnormality analyses to show that bowhead whale cells have lower mutation rates, stronger double-strand break repair (both NHEJ and HR), and higher NHEJ fidelity.
- Mechanistically, the authors identified **CIRBP** as highly expressed in bowhead whale cells and tissues; introducing bowhead whale CIRBP into human cells enhanced NHEJ and HR, reduced indels and micronuclei, promoted DNA end protection and in vitro end joining, whereas CIRBP knockdown in bowhead whale cells produced the opposite effects.
- To test in vivo relevance, the authors overexpressed human or bowhead whale CIRBP in Drosophila and observed changes in lifespan and radiation resistance.

## Results
- The bowhead whale lives **more than 200 years** and can weigh **>80,000 kg**; although it would be expected to accumulate oncogenic mutations more readily, it does not show high cancer susceptibility.
- In cell transformation experiments, bowhead whale fibroblasts required **fewer oncogenic hits than humans**: humans needed a **4-factor combination (hTERT + HRAS + LT + ST)**, while bowhead whales required only **2 factors (HRAS + LT)** in an hTERT background; genetically, **TP53/RB1 inactivation + HRAS** was also sufficient for transformation, and mouse xenograft and soft agar results were consistent.
- Despite being more easily transformed, bowhead whale cells carried a lower mutational burden: in tumor samples, **de novo somatic SNVs were significantly lower than in humans and mice**, and there were also **fewer indels and structural variants (SVs)**, especially a marked reduction in **large SVs >500 kb**.
- In mutagenesis assays, after ENU treatment, bowhead whale cells showed the **smallest** increase in SNVs, whereas mice showed the **largest**; HPRT assays showed that after **ENU, MNNG, EMS, or γ-radiation**, mutation rates in bowhead whale fibroblasts were all lower than in human cells.
- In terms of DNA repair, bowhead whale cells showed **significantly higher NHEJ and HR frequencies than other species**; they had fewer endogenous **γH2AX/53BP1** foci, cleared foci faster after bleomycin, were **more resistant to bleomycin and etoposide** in colony formation assays, and formed **fewer micronuclei** after irradiation.
- In repair quality, bowhead whale NHEJ was more accurate: in reporter systems and sequencing of CRISPR repair at the endogenous PTEN locus, bowhead whales produced **fewer deletions**, had the **highest proportion of unmodified alleles**, and showed the **lowest frequency of large deletions**. Functionally, overexpression of bowhead whale CIRBP in human cells increased NHEJ/HR, reduced indels, accelerated the disappearance of damage foci, and reduced chromosomal abnormalities; in Drosophila, overexpression of either human or bowhead whale CIRBP could **extend lifespan** and **improve survival after ionizing radiation**, although this excerpt does not provide specific percentages for lifespan extension.
