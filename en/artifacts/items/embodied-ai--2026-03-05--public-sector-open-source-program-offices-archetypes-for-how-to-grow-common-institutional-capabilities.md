# Public Sector Open Source Program Offices - Archetypes for how to Grow (Common) Institutional Capabilities

## Summary
This paper examines how the European public sector can use Open Source Program Offices (OSPOs) to improve its capacity to adopt, develop, and collaborate on open source software, and proposes six OSPO archetypes for institutions to choose from based on their context. Its value lies in providing experience-based guidance for building replicable organizational capabilities in the public sector.

## Problem
- The public sector relies heavily on open source software, but often struggles to use it systematically due to dependence on outsourcing, complex procurement rules, insufficient internal technical capacity, and a conservative culture.
- Existing research on OSPOs mostly comes from corporate settings and lacks design guidance tailored to the public sector that accounts for government layers, resource constraints, and policy goals.
- This matters because open source is seen as underpinning **90%+** of digital infrastructure and is tied to digital sovereignty, interoperability, service quality, transparency, and economic growth.

## Approach
- Uses **qualitative research**: interviews with **18** OSPO representatives covering **16** public-sector OSPO cases across the EU, Norway, Liechtenstein, and Iceland.
- Cross-analyzes the cases to derive **6 OSPO archetypes**, comparing their organizational structures, scopes of responsibility, and how they support open source adoption.
- Builds an analytical framework from existing industry OSPO literature, focusing on organizational placement, role design, and responsibilities such as strategy, compliance, policy processes, community collaboration, and training.
- Validates and supplements the findings through **2** follow-up focus groups, and develops policy recommendations accordingly.

## Results
- The paper’s central output is the identification of **6** public-sector OSPO archetypes to guide public bodies in designing OSPOs according to their own context, resources, and policy objectives.
- The empirical basis includes **16 cases**, **18 interviewees**, and **2 focus groups**, which are the clearest indicators of study scale provided in the paper.
- The paper claims these archetypes can help the public sector better promote open source adoption, sharing and reuse, interoperability, digital sovereignty, and policy implementation, but **does not provide quantitative performance metrics, benchmark models, or statistically significant results**.
- The paper also makes several strong policy-level claims: OSPOs should be regarded as a strategic capability-building mechanism in the public sector rather than merely a technical governance unit; their design needs to align with institutional level, resources, governance goals, and the local regulatory environment.
