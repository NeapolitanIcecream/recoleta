# Harnessing eDNA to help conserve Australia's oceans

## Summary
This article explains how environmental DNA (eDNA) can be used for marine conservation in Australia, especially to monitor marine biodiversity in a less invasive and more scalable way. Its key focus is on pushing eDNA beyond simply asking “is a species present?” toward higher-resolution applications such as identifying individuals and estimating population genetic diversity and abundance.

## Problem
- Traditional marine biological surveys rely on visual observation, capture, or tagging, which are costly, limited in coverage, and more invasive to animals.
- Existing eDNA methods can usually answer only whether a species is present with reasonable reliability, but struggle to estimate population size, individual identity, and genetic diversity—information that is critical for conserving endangered species.
- eDNA is also constrained by incomplete reference barcode databases, rapid DNA degradation, and the difficulty of inferring true abundance from samples, all of which can affect species identification and management decisions.

## Approach
- Extract trace DNA left by organisms from environmental samples such as seawater or soil, compare short DNA barcodes against reference databases, and use metabarcoding to identify multiple species at once.
- In the context of Australian marine parks, research teams are building a more complete local fish genetic database to improve metabarcoding coverage and accuracy.
- For whale sharks, researchers adopt a forensic-style approach by designing a whale shark–specific panel of highly polymorphic genetic markers, using short but highly variable DNA fragments to distinguish individuals.
- By collecting eDNA from seawater behind whale sharks, they aim to directly identify individuals and further estimate the genetic diversity and potential abundance of local populations.

## Results
- The article says eDNA can detect organisms without directly seeing them and can cover a wide range of life, from microbes to whales; this is a concrete advantage over traditional surveys, though no single benchmark figure is provided.
- In Australia, eDNA is seen as having the potential to support species and ecosystem health monitoring across the country’s **60 marine parks**, enabling more frequent, lower-cost sampling across very large spatial scales.
- A **2022** study used eDNA to confirm the presence of Irwin's turtle (Elseya irwini) in Queensland’s Lower Burdekin River, marking the first local confirmation of the species in **about 20 years**.
- For whale sharks, the research team reports that they can already identify individuals from seawater eDNA collected “directly behind the animal,” avoiding invasive sampling or tagging, though this requires being very close to the animal.
- In controlled experiments, the research showed that when samples contained DNA from **10 or fewer whale sharks**, it was possible to **accurately identify the number of individuals**; separating larger populations remains difficult.
- The article also provides conservation context: whale sharks are an endangered species, and the global population has declined by **about 60%** over the past **120 years**, so improving eDNA to the population level has practical conservation significance."
