# State of AI 2026: The $600B inference subsidy, energy bottlenecks, and labor

## Summary
This is a macro research report on the interlocking risks across AI industry economics, energy, semiconductors, and labor, with the central argument that the current AI boom is built on subsidies, compute bottlenecks, and electricity constraints, and is therefore unsustainable both physically and financially. The report seeks to explain who will benefit and who will be harmed in the coming years by competition over AI infrastructure.

## Problem
- The report aims to address whether the current AI industry is built on a sustainable economic and infrastructure foundation, and how these hidden constraints will reshape employment, software business models, chip supply chains, and national competitiveness.
- This matters because the author argues that the main risks in AI are no longer insufficient model capability, but rather the systemic imbalances caused by **excessive inference costs, inadequate energy supply, overconcentration of advanced chips, and accelerating labor substitution**.
- If these constraints continue to worsen, enterprise software pricing, the affordability of AI services, national industrial security, and white-collar career paths will all undergo structural change.

## Approach
- The core method is simple: treat AI as an **industrial system constrained by physical resources**, rather than merely a software or algorithm problem; then place labor, inference economics, chip manufacturing, power generation capacity, and geopolitics within the same analytical framework.
- The report synthesizes data and cases from **more than 200 public sources**, organized around seven themes: employment shocks, AI subsidy pricing, Taiwan chip bottlenecks, the power crisis, systemic convergence, software industry repricing, and a final assessment of winners and losers.
- At the mechanism level, the author’s central argument is that AI companies are using capital to subsidize low-cost inference services in order to capture market share, but as models become more “agentic” and per-task electricity and compute demands rise, cost pressure will shift from training to ongoing inference spending.
- Further, the author argues that advanced AI expansion is constrained by the two hardest bottlenecks: **Taiwan-dominated advanced chip/packaging capacity** and **stable baseload electricity**; competitive advantage will therefore shift from pure software capability to “chips + power + sovereign infrastructure.”

## Results
- The report claims that current AI service prices are heavily subsidized: users pay only **1/10 to 1/50** of the true cost, meaning providers may absorb an additional **$9 to $49** in cost for every **$1** collected.
- Regarding OpenAI, the report provides several figures: it reached a **$25B annualized revenue run rate** in **February 2026**, had **910 million weekly active users** and **9 million paying enterprise users**; but it was still unprofitable, and the report claims the company will burn through **$665B** cumulatively before reaching profitability. At the same time, for every **$1.00** paid by enterprise customers, about **$0.37** of OpenAI’s spending allegedly goes directly to electricity and hardware inference costs.
- On labor, the report argues that the “real unemployment/underemployment rate” in the United States is **23.8%**, far above the headline **4.4%**; the wage premium for AI skills is **56%**; **42.4%** of recent graduates are underemployed; and firms can use AI to replace traditional entry-level knowledge work.
- In the software industry, the report says the “SaaSocalypse” of **February 2026** wiped out about **$285B** in market value from traditional software companies (later in the text also described as nearly **$300B**), because AI agents can complete work directly via APIs, undermining the seat-based SaaS model.
- In the chip supply chain, the report says Taiwan produces **92%** of the world’s most advanced semiconductors; the global semiconductor market in **2026** is about **$975B**, of which AI chips contribute about **50%** of revenue but less than **0.2%** of shipment volume; and TSMC N2’s initial yield is about **65%**, higher than N3’s **55%**.
- On energy, the report says global data center electricity consumption will grow from **460 TWh in 2024** to **1000+ TWh by 2030**; China has **37** nuclear reactors under construction while the United States has **0**; and capacity prices in Northern Virginia’s PJM have risen **833% in two years**. These figures are used to support its conclusion that AI expansion will hit the power wall first.
