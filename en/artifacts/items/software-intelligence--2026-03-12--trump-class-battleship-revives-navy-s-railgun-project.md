# Trump class 'battleship' revives Navy's railgun project

## Summary
This article reports that the U.S. Navy resumed live-fire testing of its prototype electromagnetic railgun in February 2025, indicating that although the project had once been shelved, it was never fully terminated. Its significance lies in the possibility that the railgun could once again become a key weapon for future “Trump class” large surface combatants, while also offering value for hypersonic testing support.

## Problem
- The railgun project had previously been publicly shelved around 2021 due to engineering challenges such as **power supply, cooling, sustained rate of fire, and barrel wear**, and it had long failed to develop into a sea-deployable combat capability.
- The Navy still needs a weapon with **long range, low ammunition cost, and deep magazine capacity** to engage maritime, land, and air targets, and potentially even intercept incoming hypersonic threats.
- As the concept for the future 35,000-ton “Trump class/BBG(X)” advances, the Navy needs to reassess whether the railgun can transition from an experimental system into a practical shipboard weapon, a question that affects the firepower architecture of next-generation warships.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: **a railgun does not use gunpowder, but instead uses electromagnetic force to accelerate a projectile to extremely high speed before firing it**.
- The article reveals that the Navy conducted a **three-day high-velocity firing test at White Sands Missile Range in New Mexico in February 2025**, carried out jointly by the White Sands Detachment and NSWC Dahlgren.
- The test was conducted for **NAVSEA’s Joint Hypersonics Transition Office (JHTO)**, indicating that the railgun may serve not only as a weapons prototype, but also as a hypersonics-related test platform or launch asset.
- The article also links this renewed testing to the future “Trump class” warship program, suggesting that the Navy may use it to reassess the existing BAE Systems prototype or consider new design options.

## Results
- The clearest result is that the Navy **completed a three-day live-fire test campaign in February 2025**, proving that after years in storage, the prototype railgun **still retains at least some level of operability**.
- The article notes that future “Trump class” large surface combatants are expected to have a **displacement of about 35,000 tons** and are described as platforms that could carry railguns, missiles, 5-inch naval guns, and laser weapons.
- The article does not provide key quantitative metrics for this round of testing, such as **muzzle velocity, range, accuracy, number of shots fired, power efficiency, or barrel life**, nor does it offer formal comparative data versus missiles or traditional naval guns.
- Therefore, the article’s strongest conclusion is not a numerical performance breakthrough, but rather a **signal that the project has officially been “revived”**: after near-silence following 2021, the railgun was fired again in 2025 and has now been folded into discussions of future combat ships.
- The article also provides a timeline comparison: the railgun was moved to White Sands in **2019**, the Navy announced in **2021** that it would wrap up the effort and store the hardware, and it has now been tested again in **2025**, showing a clear resurgence of strategic interest."
