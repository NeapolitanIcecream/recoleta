# My spicy take on vibe coding for PMs

## Summary
This article is not an academic paper, but an opinion piece aimed at product managers, arguing that in a large-company environment, PMs should not treat "personally shipping production code" as high-leverage work. The author believes PMs are better suited to using coding for prototyping, experimentation, and understanding systems, rather than bypassing normal prioritization processes to launch features.

## Problem
- The article discusses the question: **Should PMs personally submit production code**, especially in large-scale organizations like Meta?
- The author argues this matters because if PMs spend their time shipping code, they may **drift away from their real responsibilities**: fixing prioritization and collaboration mechanisms, rather than relying on individual efforts to bypass the system and push features forward.
- It also relates to **technical debt, organizational efficiency, and opportunity cost**: highly paid PMs doing low-leverage engineering work, while "temporary PM pet features" may introduce risk to production systems.

## Approach
- The core argument is simple: **distinguish between "PMs writing code" and "PMs shipping production code."** The former can be valuable; the latter is usually not worth encouraging.
- The author’s structural recommendation is that if a feature is truly important, PMs should **push the team toward proper prioritization and resource allocation**, rather than implementing it themselves by bypassing process.
- PM coding is better suited for **prototyping and demos**, because "showing is usually better than telling," and can help engineers and stakeholders understand requirements more quickly.
- PM coding is also appropriate for **understanding systems and running more realistic experiments**, such as building interactive prototypes or conducting user experiments, rather than relying only on static mocks.
- In a few cases, PMs can use their own **unique domain knowledge or prior experience** to validate ideas quickly, but this is still different from taking on formal long-term production development.

## Results
- The article **does not provide quantitative experiments, datasets, metrics, or baseline comparisons**, so there are no academic-style result numbers to report.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that at Meta scale, PMs personally submitting production diffs is usually **low-leverage behavior**, because the truly high-value work should be correcting prioritization and driving organizational coordination.
- The author explicitly claims that PMs writing production code can lead to **accumulated technical debt**, and notes that "even changes to intern tools can affect production."
- The author also makes an opportunity-cost argument: when PMs write code, their output may resemble a "**slow E3**," while the cost is closer to an "**IC7 salary**," meaning the input-output ratio is not worthwhile.
- The positive case is that reasonable uses of PM coding include **better expressing ideas, understanding systems, running more realistic human experiments, temporarily leveraging unique resources, and having fun**.
