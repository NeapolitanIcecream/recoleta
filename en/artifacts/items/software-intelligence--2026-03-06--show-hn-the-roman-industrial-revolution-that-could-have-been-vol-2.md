# Show HN: The Roman Industrial Revolution that could have been (Vol 2)

## Summary
This content is more like a historical setting/narrative work than a research paper. It describes a story in which, after the Pompeii disaster, the first machines are built through “new designs,” using mechanization to improve the efficiency of the wool industry and trigger larger-scale economic changes.

## Problem
- The problem to be solved is: after the destruction of Pompeii, a large number of wool workers are dead or injured, the wool in warehouses is rotting, and the Campania region is facing economic collapse and famine.
- This matters because the sharp reduction in labor makes a key industry unable to operate, directly threatening community survival and regional stability.
- The text also implies a second-layer problem: even if production is restored, the old social and market structures may not be able to absorb the surplus capacity brought by mechanization.

## Approach
- The core approach is to use “new designs” to build the earliest batch of machines, using mechanization to replace part of the damaged human labor force.
- Put simply: enable a small number of survivors to complete more textile/wool-processing work with the help of machines, thereby significantly increasing output per unit of labor.
- Mechanistically, it first alleviates the post-disaster labor shortage, then saves the community economy through higher productivity.
- But the narrative also points out that after industrial scale expands, it produces a surplus, exposing problems of insufficient distribution, demand, and institutional capacity.

## Results
- The text claims that the machines “allow[ed] the survivors to work much faster than ever before,” but **does not provide any quantitative metrics**, such as percentage productivity gains, output, cost, mortality, or famine-relief data.
- The strongest concrete concluding claim is that mechanization helped them “save the community,” meaning it prevented the area from sliding further into ruin and starvation.
- Another clear results claim is that the “massive scale” output of the new industry created a surplus, and that “the old world wasn't built to handle” this surplus, indicating that technological progress produced economic consequences beyond the capacity of existing institutions.
- There is no dataset, baseline model, experimental setup, or numerical comparison, so no standard research-style performance results can be extracted.
