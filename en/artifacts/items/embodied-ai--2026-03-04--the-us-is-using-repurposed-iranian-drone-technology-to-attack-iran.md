# The US is using repurposed Iranian drone technology to attack Iran

## Summary
This is not a robotics or AI research paper, but a military analysis article discussing how the United States reverse-engineered and adapted Iran’s low-cost Shahed kamikaze drone technology to develop Lucas and use it in operations against Iran. Its core argument is that cheap, expendable drone swarms are changing the cost structure and operational logic of American air power.

## Problem
- The article examines the problem that traditional high-end US air force platforms are highly capable, but extremely expensive, limited in number, slow to replace, and politically costly to lose.
- In modern warfare, low-cost drones can overwhelm air defense systems through a “quantity advantage”; if the US continues to rely only on expensive platforms, it may be at a disadvantage in wars of attrition.
- This matters because battlefields in the Middle East and Ukraine show that small, cheap drones have become key weapons reshaping battlefield economics and the way air defense contests are fought.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: the US first obtained and reverse-engineered the Iranian Shahed-136, then evolved training replicas into the combat-capable Lucas low-cost one-way attack drone.
- Lucas inherits the Shahed-style concept of being “cheap, mass-producible, and expendable,” using large numbers of low-cost drones to saturate enemy radar and air defense resources.
- Unlike most pre-programmed Shaheds, Lucas has reportedly been observed with a nose gimbal camera and satellite communications module, theoretically allowing in-flight retasking, target updates, and more dynamic coordinated salvos.
- The article further notes that a potential upgrade path is AI-driven “swarm” tactics, enabling multiple drones to operate as a coordinated attack team.

## Results
- The article does not provide standard academic experiments, benchmark datasets, or reproducible performance evaluations, so there are no strictly defined quantitative research results.
- It states that the Lucas program advanced quickly: **within 5 months of the program’s launch**, the Pentagon had equipped US forces in the Middle East with Lucas.
- It says Lucas had already undergone **sea-launch testing** and was confirmed by US Central Command to have been used in combat for the first time on **February 28, 2026**.
- In terms of cost, Lucas is priced at about **US$35,000** per unit; by comparison, the Shahed-136 costs about **more than US$20,000** per unit.
- In terms of performance comparison, the article says the lighter US replica has a range of about **400 miles** and a warhead of about **18 kg**; the Shahed-136 can strike targets **more than 1,000 miles** away, carries a warhead of about **40–50 kg**, can loiter for up to **6 hours**, and flies at about **115 miles per hour**.
- More broadly, the operational argument includes that about **75%** of battlefield casualties in Ukraine are attributed to small drones; the US “drone dominance” plan aims to stockpile **340,000** similar drones by **early 2028** to enable warfare at “affordable mass”.
