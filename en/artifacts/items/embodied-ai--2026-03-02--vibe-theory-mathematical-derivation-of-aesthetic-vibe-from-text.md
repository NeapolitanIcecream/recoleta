# Vibe Theory: Mathematical Derivation of Aesthetic Vibe from Text

## Summary
This work proposes a tool that formalizes “vibe” (atmosphere/aesthetic unity) in literary criticism into computable metrics, using both VAE and diffusion/manifold models to extract a text’s “shared generative structure.” It is better understood as proof-of-concept research plus a software implementation, rather than a machine learning paper validated on standard benchmarks.

## Problem
- It attempts to answer whether the “aesthetic atmosphere” in a text or an author’s body of work can be **mathematized and operationalized**, rather than remaining at the level of subjective literary description.
- This matters because the author argues that stylistic unities people perceive, such as the “Kafkaesque,” are fundamentally cases where **diverse phenomena can be approximately generated by a small amount of shared structure**.
- Existing single global latent-space methods (such as VAE) may fail to capture data that are “very different on the surface yet still vibe-consistent,” so a local manifold perspective is needed.

## Approach
- The raw text is split into overlapping sliding windows (**phenomena**), then represented as vectors using **TF-IDF + standardization**.
- **VAE '17 model**: trains an autoencoder that compresses text windows into a low-dimensional latent space; uses reconstruction error to define the **canon** (the core phenomena most aligned with the vibe), and computes metrics such as vibe density, aesthetic unity, vibe strength, and excess of reality.
- **Diffusion '25 model**: trains a small score network based on denoising score matching, treating vibe as an implicit low-dimensional manifold; uses **score norm** to determine whether points are on-manifold.
- In the diffusion version, it further performs **SVD on the score Jacobian** to extract local tangent spaces, and defines **local comparability** via cosine similarity after neighborhood projection, shifting “meaning” from global coordinates to local neighborhood structure.
- The author explicitly states that most of the mathematical definitions in the 2025 version are the author’s own **operationalization** of Grietzer’s conceptual text, not theorems or standard formulas already present in the original.

## Results
- For the **VAE '17** example on Kafka’s **The Trial**: 23 text windows, 69-dimensional TF-IDF, training early-stopped at **epoch 330**, **final loss=0.085626**, effective latent dimensionality **5**, compression ratio **0.0725**.
- In the same example, the VAE metrics are: **Vibe Density=0.261**, **Aesthetic Unity=-0.058**, **Full-Text Comparability=-0.027**, **Vibe Strength=-0.031**, **Mean Reconstruction Error=1.9768**, **Excess of Reality=2.1996**; canon size **6/23**.
- For the **Diffusion '25** example on the same text: training early-stopped at **epoch 141**, **final loss=0.927987**; **on-manifold 17/23**, i.e. **0.739**, **Mean anomaly score=1.4527**.
- The diffusion version also reports: **Mean local comparability=0.414**, **Local comparability variability=0.126**, **Global comparability=-0.044**; the mean of the first 8 tangent-space singular values decreases from **0.8814** to **0.4045**.
- In the two-text comparison example (Kafka vs. Stein): Kafka has **Aesthetic Unity=0.061**, Stein has **-0.026**; the cross-reconstruction errors are **1.4727** and **1.3953**, and the system outputs **Vibe Similarity=1.000**, claiming the two have “strong vibe affinity.”
- However, the full text provides **no standard dataset, ablation study, statistical significance, or external baseline comparison**; the strongest conclusion is that the author demonstrates a working framework and uses several single-text analyses to show that these metrics can produce interpretable style/atmosphere reports.
