# Off-Grid Electricity and Hot Water from Scrap Wood

## Summary
This article describes a DIY off-grid system that uses scrap wood to produce charcoal, then further recovers heat and enables subsequent power generation/heating. The key idea is to make as much use as possible of both the combustible volatiles and waste heat produced during wood pyrolysis, in order to obtain electricity and hot water.

## Problem
- The article addresses the question of how to use readily available scrap wood to obtain usable fuel, hot water, and a limited duration of electricity in an off-grid setting.
- This matters because remote or off-grid environments often lack stable energy supplies, while wood is easier to obtain and renewable compared with fossil fuels, and charcoal is better suited than raw wood for stable combustion and energy supply.
- Traditional direct wood burning has efficiency limitations and ongoing maintenance burdens; if the combustible gases and waste heat from charcoal production can also be utilized, overall energy efficiency can be significantly improved.

## Approach
- The core method is producing charcoal through wood pyrolysis: wood is placed in an oxygen-poor sealed metal container (retort) and heated, driving off moisture and volatile organic compounds and leaving behind a combustible carbonaceous residue.
- The setup uses a metal retort converted from a metal ammo box, with a pipe extending from the side and holes drilled underneath; it is initially heated by a small fire, after which the combustible gases released by the wood jet from the pipe and burn, in turn continuing to heat the retort and creating a roughly self-sustaining reaction.
- To recover waste heat, the builder adds a double-walled metal cylinder around the retort and places a sand-packed copper tube heat-exchange structure inside it, used to transfer waste heat from the charcoal-making process to water and produce hot water.
- The resulting charcoal is then used in another downstream process for electricity generation and heating, forming a relatively complete off-grid energy system.

## Results
- The article explicitly claims that the downstream process using the produced charcoal can provide “about a day” of electricity and hot water, but it does not provide detailed quantitative metrics such as energy output, power, efficiency, or load conditions.
- In the hot-water recovery test, the water was heated to the point that the tubing used in the test “began to melt,” indicating very strong heat-exchange performance, but no specific temperature value was given.
- The charcoal-making process is described as being sustained after startup by the volatile gases released from the wood, meaning the initial small fire eventually goes out while the retort continues operating by heating itself with those gases.
- No standard dataset, experimental baseline, efficiency percentage, cost analysis, or quantitative comparison with other off-grid electricity/hot-water solutions is provided.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that the system successfully converts scrap wood into charcoal, simultaneously recovers charcoal-production waste heat to make hot water, and can support about a day of off-grid electricity and hot-water supply in a later stage.
