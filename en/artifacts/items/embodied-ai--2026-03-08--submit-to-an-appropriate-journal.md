# Submit to an Appropriate Journal

## Summary
This article is not a technical research paper, but a submission-advice piece. Its core point is that one should choose an appropriate journal based on the paper's quality, originality, scope fit, and writing maturity, in order to reduce unnecessary rejections and submission delays. It provides a relatively systematic self-evaluation framework to help authors judge what tier of journal their paper is suitable for.

## Problem
- The problem it addresses is: how researchers should choose an **appropriate journal for submission** rather than blindly aiming for highly prestigious journals.
- This matters because choosing the wrong journal can lead to **lengthy review, excessively high standards, and eventual rejection**, wasting the time of both authors and reviewers.
- The article also discusses common submission risks such as scope mismatch, mismatch with the editors' expertise, and submitting multiple papers to the same journal at the same time.

## Approach
- The author proposes a simple mechanism: first determine whether the paper matches the journal in terms of **tier, topical scope, and editorial expertise**, and then decide where to submit.
- It gives four categories of self-evaluation dimensions: **Correctness, Novelty, Professionalism, Presentation**, and lists many actionable questions as a scoring rubric.
- Authors are advised to examine **sample issues, historically similar papers, and the editorial board's background** to judge whether the journal is truly suitable for their work.
- It offers heuristic advice on submission strategy: do not lightly submit two unrelated papers to the same journal at the same time; if rejected, absorb the feedback and resubmit to a more appropriate or less selective journal.

## Results
- The article **does not provide quantitative experiments, datasets, baselines, or numerical metrics**; it is experiential/normative advice rather than empirical research.
- Its strongest concrete claim is that if a paper is only a “borderline case” for a top journal, the result is often **a longer review process + harsher reviews + ultimate rejection**.
- Using **JAMS** as an example, the author explicitly states that only papers that make referees feel “**excited and enthusiastic**” are more likely to be accepted; papers that are merely “solid but use fairly standard methods to solve a moderately interesting problem” have a **lower probability of acceptance**.
- The author proposes a relatively systematic framework for judging submissions: if a paper scores highly across multiple dimensions—**correctness, originality, professionalism, and quality of presentation**—then a higher-tier journal may be worth considering; if only a few dimensions stand out, a lower-tier journal is more appropriate.
- The core conclusion regarding rejection is that a **healthy rejection rate is the norm at reputable journals**; authors should not take rejection personally, but should instead use the feedback to improve the paper and submit again.
