# MiniGTD: GTD but Smol

## Summary
miniGTD proposes a minimalist version of the GTD personal task management method: every morning, you rewrite that day's goals and next actions from scratch in a small notebook, and the whole process takes only about 2 minutes. The author claims it achieves most of the benefits of traditional GTD with extremely low friction, and is especially suitable for maintaining execution when complex systems are prone to collapse.

## Problem
- Traditional GTD systems are too complex and cumbersome for some people, and can easily "collapse under their own weight in the middle of a crisis."
- Long task lists and endless scrolling increase cognitive burden, making decisions and action harder rather than easier.
- If the task management process itself is time-consuming or troublesome, people are more likely not to do it, causing the system to fail.

## Approach
- Every morning, rewrite the day's list from scratch in a small notebook, which takes about 1–2 minutes.
- First write the date, then write the day's goals, and for each goal write at least one "next action."
- Use "rewriting from memory" to naturally perform filtering and prioritization, keeping only the items you truly want to move forward on today.
- After writing, look back at the previous page to check whether anything important was missed, to compensate for the risk of information loss from relying purely on memory.
- The method intentionally stays analog, minimalist, and low-friction to increase the likelihood of actually sticking with it.

## Results
- The article does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or reproducible quantitative evaluation results.
- The author's core quantitative claim is that daily maintenance takes only about **1–2 minutes**, yet delivers "**almost all**" of the value of a traditional full GTD system.
- The author further claims that, compared with Full GTD, miniGTD "**gets more**" in some respects: for example, rewriting from memory produces more natural prioritization, and shorter pages reduce decision burden.
- The article describes it as "**battle tested**" and says it replaced the author's nth failed GTD system during a personal crisis, but this is anecdotal evidence rather than a controlled experiment.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that a minimalist process + pen-and-paper rewriting + reviewing the previous page can improve sustained execution at extremely low usage cost; however, no numerical comparison baseline against standard GTD is provided.
