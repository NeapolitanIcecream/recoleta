# Off-Grid Electricity and Hot Water from Scrap Wood

## Summary
This piece describes a DIY system that uses scrap wood to produce charcoal and then, in an off-grid setting, co-generates hot water and electricity. Its core value is that it makes use of the combustible gases and waste heat that would otherwise be wasted during the wood pyrolysis process, improving energy self-sufficiency.

## Problem
- The goal is to solve how to stably produce **storable fuel (charcoal)**, **hot water**, and **electricity** from cheap, readily available scrap wood in an off-grid environment.
- This matters because burning wood directly is less efficient and less controllable, while charcoal burns hotter and more consistently, making it better suited for later cooking or power-generation uses.
- The article also implicitly focuses on energy independence and relatively low carbon impact: compared with fossil fuels, wood is closer to a renewable resource when managed appropriately.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is **oxygen-free pyrolysis**: heating wood under oxygen-limited conditions to drive off moisture and volatile organic compounds, leaving behind a combustible carbon-rich residue, namely charcoal.
- The system uses a **metal retort/charcoal kiln (retort)** converted from a metal ammo box, with a pipe coming out the side and holes opened below so that the released volatile gases can be ignited.
- These volatile gases in turn continue heating the retort, allowing the process to start with a small initial fire and gradually become a **self-sustaining reaction**, reducing the need for external fuel.
- To recover waste heat, the author places the retort inside a **double-walled metal cylinder** and installs a sand-packed copper tube within it to transfer heat from the charring process to water, producing hot water.
- The cooled charcoal is then used in another stage to provide about a day’s worth of electricity and hot water, forming a complete off-grid energy chain.

## Results
- The article provides very limited quantitative results and **does not report standardized experimental metrics, efficiency, power, energy consumption, or rigorous comparisons with baseline systems**.
- One explicit performance claim is that the later process using the charcoal can provide **about 1 day (a day’s worth)** of electricity and hot water.
- In the hot-water recovery test, the water in a large drum was heated to the point that the test tubing **began to melt**, indicating that the waste-heat recovery temperature/heat flux was significantly higher than expected, but **no specific temperature values were provided**.
- Another specific claim is that the charring process transitions from “heating with a small fire underneath” to being sustained mainly by the combustible gases released from the wood, implying that the system is capable of **self-sustaining operation**, though again **no quantified duration or fuel-savings ratio was provided**.
