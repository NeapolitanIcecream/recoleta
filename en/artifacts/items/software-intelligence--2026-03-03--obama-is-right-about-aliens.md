# Obama is right about aliens

## Summary
This article is not a strict scientific paper, but rather a popular-science/commentary piece based on astrobiology and the Drake equation. Its core argument is: based on current observational data, the probability that extraterrestrial life exists in the universe is high, but whether civilizations can endure over the long term determines whether humanity will ever have a real chance to discover it.

## Problem
- The article attempts to answer two questions: **whether extraterrestrial life very likely exists**, and **why we still have not made contact with it**.
- This matters because it connects new discoveries in astronomy, clues about the origin of life, and the long-term survival of human civilization, explaining why “the universe may be full of life, yet we are still alone.”
- The author especially emphasizes that the final term in the Drake equation, **L** (the duration of a civilization), may be the decisive bottleneck: even if life is common, if civilizations are short-lived, they are unlikely to encounter one another.

## Approach
- Put simply, the article’s method is to plug recent discoveries in astronomy, planetary science, and astrobiology into the various factors of the Drake equation and reassess whether life is common in the universe.
- The specific mechanism is to synthesize multiple observational clues: more galaxies have been discovered, the number of exoplanets has risen rapidly, habitable planets may be more common than previously thought, and chemical precursors to life have been found on asteroids and in solar system bodies.
- The article also cites atmospheric chemistry observations of the exoplanet K2-18b as an example of a “possible biosignature,” showing that we have begun to detect potential signs of life at great distances.
- In explaining “why we still haven’t seen aliens,” the author does not appeal to conspiracy theories, but instead uses two factors—vast distances and the limited survival time of civilizations—to explain a Fermi-paradox-like phenomenon.

## Results
- The article does not present new experiments, models, or peer-reviewed results; its “results” are mainly a synthesis and interpretation of existing public scientific findings.
- The author cites that NASA has confirmed **6,107** exoplanets across **4,554** star systems, to show that planets are extremely common in the universe.
- The author says JWST identified **94,000** previously unknown galaxies in a small patch of sky once thought to be “dark,” and notes that ordinary galaxies may contain about **100 million** stars, with the largest reaching **100 trillion** stars, to emphasize the enormous scale of the universe.
- The author cites an estimate from his book that there may be **1 sextillion (10^21)** habitable planets in the universe; this is one of the strongest order-of-magnitude claims in the article, but it is not independently derived here.
- Regarding potential evidence of life, the article mentions that a Cambridge University team found chemical fingerprints of **DMS/DMDS** on the exoplanet **K2-18b** (about **124 light-years** from Earth), calling it one of the “strongest possible pieces of evidence yet” for biological activity.
- The article’s central concluding claim is not a new number, but that, in light of this progress, the judgment that “extraterrestrial life very likely exists” has become stronger; however, whether humanity will live to witness such a discovery depends on whether we can extend civilization’s duration **L** as much as possible.
