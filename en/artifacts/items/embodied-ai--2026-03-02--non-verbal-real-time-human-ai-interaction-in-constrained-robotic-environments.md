# Non-verbal Real-time Human-AI Interaction in Constrained Robotic Environments

## Summary
This paper proposes a dual-task framework based on 2D human keypoints, enabling AI to understand and respond to human non-verbal body movements in real time in constrained robotic environments. The core finding is that pretraining on synthetic motion improves motion prediction on real data, but a measurable statistical gap still exists between human motion and AI-generated motion.

## Problem
- Problem addressed: in robotic/edge environments constrained by compute, energy, privacy, and connectivity, how can real-time human-AI interaction be achieved using only **non-verbal full-body motion**, including **short-term future motion prediction** and **emotion recognition**.
- Why it matters: this capability can support **proactive collision avoidance, handoff timing decisions, camera/robot motion adaptation**, and make human-robot collaboration more natural, without relying on speech or text.
- The study also examines a deeper question: whether **AI-generated motion data is close enough to real human motion** to support reliable training and generalization.

## Approach
- The input consists of the past **60 frames (2 seconds)** of **17 COCO 2D keypoints**; the output includes both prediction of the next **30 frames (1 second)** of keypoints and **3-class emotion** classification ("enthusiastic", "laughing", "happy to see you").
- A joint learning framework with a **shared encoder + two task heads** is used: one head performs future motion regression, and the other performs emotion classification; the training loss is **MSE + 0.3 × Cross-Entropy**.
- Four lightweight models are compared: **MLP, LSTM, CNN-LSTM, Transformer**, with the goal of running in real-time closed loop on edge devices.
- Baselines are first trained on real data, then pretrained on synthetic skeletal sequences generated by **MotionLCM** (**9k / 45k / 90k**), and finally fine-tuned on real data.
- Videos generated by **SORA** and **VEO3** are additionally used to extract keypoints and test model generalization to “purely synthetic video motion,” thereby measuring the synthetic-to-real gap.

## Results
- Real-time performance: all four models run faster than real time on **NVIDIA Orin Nano**; inference latency is about **1.643ms (MLP)**, **3.371ms (LSTM)**, **4.844ms (CNN-LSTM)**, and **9.294ms (Transformer)**, with even the slowest model reaching about **100 FPS**.
- Data scale: the real dataset contains only **437 video clips**, expanded via sliding windows into **6992 samples**; synthetic pretraining data comes from MotionLCM, totaling **90k samples** (**30k per class**).
- Motion prediction: the paper explicitly claims that **sequence models** (LSTM / CNN-LSTM / Transformer), after synthetic pretraining, show a **continued decrease in MAE** on the real test set, with further improvement as pretraining data grows from **9k → 45k → 90k**; however, the excerpt **does not provide a complete MAE table**.
- Emotion recognition is near ceiling on the human test set: baseline accuracy is **100 / 100 / 91.71 / 100** (CNN-LSTM / LSTM / MLP / Transformer), and after pretraining it becomes **100 / 100 / 94.78 / 100**, with **MLP improving by +3.07 points**.
- On **SORA_Human**, after pretraining accuracy changes from **45.76→38.28 (CNN-LSTM, -7.48)**, **45.54→44.31 (LSTM, -1.23)**, **47.99→36.72 (MLP, -11.27)**, and **46.65→45.98 (Transformer, -0.67)**, indicating that some SORA re-rendered human motions can hurt generalization.
- On **SORA_AI**, pretraining helps some models substantially: **LSTM 32.66→50.00 (+17.34)**, **Transformer 35.74→48.15 (+12.41)**, while **CNN-LSTM 31.25→28.35 (-2.90)**; on **VEO**, results are closer to the real distribution, such as **CNN-LSTM 39.27→51.67 (+12.40)** and **Transformer 53.12→53.65 (+0.53)**, supporting the authors’ conclusion that **temporal consistency matters more than image realism**.
