# Altman admits OpenAI can't control Pentagon's use of AI

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a news report about the relationship between OpenAI and the Pentagon regarding military uses of AI. The core point is that Sam Altman acknowledges OpenAI cannot control how the US Department of Defense uses its AI in military operations, raising disputes over ethics, governance, and safety boundaries.

## Problem
- The issue discussed in the report is: once AI companies provide models to the military, can they still control the specific military uses, and who should be responsible for the resulting ethical consequences.
- This matters because the text explicitly mentions that AI has already been used for wartime targeting, external military operations, and amid policy pressure to remove safety guardrails.
- It also involves a crisis of trust among internal employees, competitors, and the government: one side worries that “safety guardrails” are being weakened, while the other worries that refusing cooperation will trigger political and commercial retaliation.

## Approach
- The text does not propose a technical method; it mainly defines OpenAI’s position through Altman’s remarks to employees: the company provides AI products, but does “not participate in operational decisions.”
- The report uses the contrast between OpenAI and Anthropic to illustrate two governance paths: OpenAI continues cooperating with the Pentagon, while Anthropic refuses cooperation due to concerns about domestic mass surveillance and fully autonomous weapons.
- The article also describes an institutional mechanism: the Pentagon is demanding looser model safety guardrails to expand the scope of military applications, which forms the core background of the controversy.
- From the simplest perspective, the so-called “mechanism” is not an algorithmic breakthrough, but a division of responsibility in the supplier–military relationship: the model company delivers capability, and the military decides how to use it.

## Results
- It provides no research experiments, datasets, model metrics, or quantitative performance results.
- The most concrete factual outcome is that the Pentagon announced a partnership agreement with OpenAI, which has been interpreted in timing as replacing Anthropic’s Claude in military applications.
- The text claims AI systems have “already” been used in US military operations, including the operation targeting Nicolás Maduro and targeting decisions in the war against Iran, but provides no verifiable technical details or numbers.
- Altman admitted the announcement of the partnership was too rushed, making the company appear “opportunistic and sloppy,” indicating that public and internal backlash has already had real effects, though the scale of the impact is not quantified.
- Anthropic was labeled a “supply-chain risk” by the US defense secretary, which the article says could cause significant financial harm, but again gives no specific amount or probability.
