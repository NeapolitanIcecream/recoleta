# Interface Hall of Shame (2000)

## Summary
This is not a research paper, but a webpage collecting examples of poor interface design, intended to help developers avoid common mistakes through negative examples. Its value lies primarily in design education and accumulated experience, rather than in proposing new algorithms or experimental results.

## Problem
- The problem it aims to address is that recurring interface design mistakes in software and websites harm usability and user experience.
- This matters because these mistakes are widespread, and developers who lack systematic reflection are likely to repeat them.
- The text also implicitly indicates the need to identify both “practices that should be eliminated” and “practices worth emulating.”

## Approach
- The core mechanism is very simple: create an “Interface Hall of Shame” to continuously collect and showcase common cases of poor interface design.
- By publicly calling out these negative examples, it encourages developers to recognize bad patterns and avoid reproducing them in future products.
- At the same time, a companion “Hall of Fame” serves as a positive reference, creating a contrastive learning setup between bad and good examples.
- Part of the content comes from community submissions, after which editors add nominated cases to the collection.

## Results
- The provided excerpt contains **no quantitative experimental results**, and includes no dataset, metrics, baselines, or numerical comparisons.
- The strongest specific claim is that highlighting common design mistakes can help developers avoid similar errors.
- Another specific claim is that the collection will continue searching for “design practices worthy of extinction” and “practices worthy of emulation.”
- The text also provides an actionable mechanism: users may submit candidate cases by email to expand the case library.
