# From Education to Evidence: A Collaborative Practice Research Platform for AI-Integrated Agile Development

## Summary
This paper proposes a collaborative practice research platform centered on project-based, AI-integrated agile education to generate useful and transferable evidence for AI-integrated agile development more quickly. Its core value lies in designing the educational setting as a high-frequency research environment positioned between the laboratory and real industry.

## Problem
- The paper addresses the following issue: AI and agile development are changing so quickly that traditional software engineering research often cannot produce results **in time**, and the results lack reusable context and transfer conditions.
- This matters because generative AI and agentic tools have already entered everyday development workflows, affecting coding, testing, design, and team collaboration, while also introducing issues of quality, accountability, and trust.
- Without a faster, more practice-grounded research mechanism, academic conclusions will struggle to guide real enterprise decisions in AI-integrated development.

## Approach
- The core method is to turn **project-based AI agile education** into a “collaborative practice research platform”: student teams build real projects, instructors and researchers organize the research, and external stakeholders bring real needs and constraints.
- The platform uses a fixed structure of **7 two-week sprints**, Dailies, Sprint Reviews, and cross-team Review Parties in sprints **3/5/7**, thereby creating rapid, comparable feedback loops.
- Evidence is accumulated through a unified artifact backbone, including backlog/user stories, code repositories, architecture and testing artifacts, review materials, retrospective records, and more, making later reuse and research analysis easier.
- Explicit quality gates are used to ensure accountability for AI use, such as a **20-minute individual oral exam** requiring students to explain code, implementation decisions, and how AI was used; grades also incorporate repositories, documentation, and peer evaluation.
- Put simply, the work does not invent a new AI model; it designs an **operating mechanism that continuously collects evidence on AI agile development through educational projects**.

## Results
- The paper’s main results are **early evidence of platform feasibility and scalability**, not model-performance SOTA; the authors also explicitly state that there is currently a **lack of rigorous, general quantitative evaluation of effects**.
- From the **2023 summer semester to the 2025 winter semester**, the number of projects conducted each semester grew from **7** to **18**, showing that the platform can maintain stable output while scaling.
- Over the same period, the number of participating students grew from **26** to **111**, with a typical team size of **4–6 people**, indicating that the platform supports repeatable observation and cross-team comparison in larger cohorts.
- In the **2025 winter semester**, a total of **20** projects were proposed and **18** were ultimately executed; the authors interpret this “oversupply” as a built-in mechanism for topic selection and practical relevance.
- Real industry/stakeholder projects continued to appear each semester, typically **1–3**, covering areas such as mobility, smart city, and AI-enabled systems, supporting the platform’s claims of external realism and transferability.
- The strongest concrete conclusion is that an educational platform built on a fixed sprint rhythm, unified artifacts, and quality gates can generate practice evidence related to AI-integrated agile development at relatively high frequency and with clearer contextual grounding; however, the paper does not yet provide quantitative comparisons of benefits versus other research methods.
