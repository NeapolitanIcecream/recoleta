# Musk's fossil data centres are undoing Tesla's climate benefit

## Summary
This article estimates emissions from Musk’s fossil-fuel-powered data centers used for generative AI/X chatbots, arguing that their scale is significantly offsetting the claimed climate benefits of Tesla products. The core conclusion is: the direct fossil emissions from AI data centers could consume a substantial share of Tesla’s annual “avoided emissions” contribution, or even exceed it.

## Problem
- The article seeks to answer: **how much greenhouse gas are these fossil-fueled data centers actually emitting, and what does that imply for Tesla’s climate narrative**.
- This matters because the public usually sees Tesla/electric vehicles as reducing emissions, while underestimating the costs of data centers supporting generative AI in terms of air pollution, compliance, and global climate impacts.
- The author also emphasizes that if this kind of “petrotech” expansion is overlooked, it could help the fossil fuel industry continue expanding gas infrastructure under the banner of AI demand.

## Approach
- The core method is simple: **use permitting documents and on-site deployment information to estimate the annual CO2-equivalent emissions of the data centers’ gas turbines, then compare them with Tesla’s reported or third-party-estimated “avoided emissions”**.
- The author first cites one public estimate: the proposed **51 gas turbines correspond to about 6.4 MtCO2-e/year**.
- Then, working with an anonymous analyst, the author expands this to the scale of “all phases” once built out, arriving at **about 11.3 MtCO2-e/year for 86 gas turbines**, of which **the currently operating portion is about 5.1 MtCO2-e/year**.
- Finally, these emissions are compared proportionally with Tesla’s claimed **32 MtCO2-e of avoided emissions in 2024**, its claimed **20 MtCO2-e in 2023**, and Greenly’s third-party **10.2–13.4 MtCO2-e** estimate for 2023.

## Results
- The article claims that **once fully built, the data centers will emit about 11.3 MtCO2-e annually (86 gas turbines)**; the **currently operating portion emits about 5.1 MtCO2-e/year**.
- Compared with Tesla’s claimed **32 MtCO2-e** climate benefit in **2024**, the author says these data centers **would offset about 35%** of Tesla’s annual global product climate benefit.
- Compared with Tesla’s official **20 MtCO2-e** figure for **2023**, the author says the data center emissions would amount to **more than half of its full-year climate benefit**; using Greenly’s independent estimate of **10.2–13.4 MtCO2-e**, the article claims the data center emissions could **exceed Tesla’s entire 2023 climate benefit**.
- The article gives a vehicle-scale framing: **among Teslas sold in the U.S. in 2025, the “avoided emissions” of about 2 out of every 3 vehicles would be offset by the operation of these data centers**.
- Using manufacturing emissions as a comparison, the author says that based only on the **currently operating 5.1 MtCO2-e**, the emissions from generating chatbot responses are about **5 times** Tesla’s **2024 Scope 1+2 manufacturing emissions**; using the full-build **11.3 MtCO2-e** figure, that rises to about **11 times**.
- This is not an experimental result from a peer-reviewed paper, but an analytical claim based on permitting/deployment information; the article does not provide more formal methodological uncertainty ranges or independent verification.
