# AI Is Being Misunderstood as a Breakthrough in Planning. It's Not

## Summary
This article argues that AI’s real value in military campaign planning is not to “replace planning judgment” or “optimize for the right answer,” but to generate multiple plausible-looking options more quickly, thereby exposing where commanders must make tradeoffs. Drawing on actual experience using AI at U.S. Forces Japan headquarters, the author emphasizes that AI improves the efficiency of drafting and structure generation, but also makes it easier for unresolved priority choices to be concealed by tidy structure.

## Problem
- The article addresses the problem of how to correctly understand AI’s role in military planning and avoid mistaking it for a “planning breakthrough” that can automatically make priority judgments and resource tradeoffs.
- This matters because failure at the campaign level usually does not result from insufficient information, but from not clearly deciding “what matters more, what can be deferred, and what risk is acceptable” under conditions of conflicting objectives, limited resources, overlapping authorities and responsibilities, and adaptive adversaries.
- If organizations treat AI-generated plans that are complete, balanced, and clearly organized as good enough answers, they will mistake “structural completeness” for “correct judgment,” thereby obscuring the responsibility and tradeoffs that should rest with command leadership.

## Approach
- The core mechanism is simple: do not treat AI as an optimizer that produces a single best plan, but as a tool for rapidly generating multiple “internally coherent planning frameworks.”
- In practice, this means having AI quickly generate multiple defensible campaign frameworks, task organizations, and narrative versions, and then having planners compare where each framework fails, distorts reality, or downplays the importance of certain responsibilities.
- Based on embedded use in real planning workflows at U.S. Forces Japan, the author explains that AI was used for synthesizing material, reframing problems, drafting text, and rapid iteration, rather than for offline demonstrations or laboratory testing.
- In the U.S. Forces Japan case, AI repeatedly generated plans that were “structurally valid but conceptually fragile”; these failures were not prompt problems, but diagnostic signals showing that the mission itself could not be coherently explained through a single doctrinal frame.
- Accordingly, the better use is not to pursue one perfectly unified framework, but to use AI’s cheap and fast reframing ability to clearly expose points of conflict, forcing commanders to make human judgments about priorities, relationships of subordination, and acceptable risk.

## Results
- The article provides no formal experiments, benchmark datasets, or statistical metrics, so **there are no quantitative results to report**.
- One of the strongest concrete conclusions is that, in campaign design at U.S. Forces Japan, when using Maven Smart Systems (which provides Anthropic Claude Sonnet) and Ask Sage (which can access models such as OpenAI’s), large language models **consistently** produced frameworks that were “analytically defensible but conceptually fragile”; each framework could organize tasks and objectives, but each failed in different places.
- The author claims that AI’s direct effect is “raising the floor, collapsing the median”: it significantly compresses the time and effort required to generate and revise internally consistent plans, while making genuine insight and priority judgment relatively scarcer and more expensive.
- In the U.S. Forces Japan case, the team ultimately did not adopt a single unified framework, but instead recommended that the commander accept **three coexisting roles** and their distinct tasks and functions; this decision is described as more realistic than choosing some AI-generated “best unified plan.”
- The article’s key benefit is not “finding the right answer,” but using the cheap generation of multiple plausible frames to more quickly identify which theories “cannot withstand collision with requirements and constraints,” thereby making explicit the points where command judgment must intervene.
- The author’s strongest claim is that the way AI in planning may truly “raise the ceiling” is not by replacing decision-making, but by stripping away false distinctions between plans so that teams can see more clearly where they must impose asymmetric priorities and assume risk through human choice.
