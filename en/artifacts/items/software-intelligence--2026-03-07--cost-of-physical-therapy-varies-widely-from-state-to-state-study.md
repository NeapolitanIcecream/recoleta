# Cost of physical therapy varies widely from state to state: study

## Summary
This study analyzed public prices for outpatient physical therapy (PT) services at U.S. hospitals and found substantial price differences across states, regions, and payment methods. Its significance is that price exposure may affect whether patients initiate or continue PT, thereby influencing rehabilitation outcomes and subsequent health care use.

## Problem
- The study aims to address how large the price variation is for outpatient PT services in the United States, and whether that variation may cause patients to reduce or discontinue treatment because of high costs.
- This matters because PT is widely used for postoperative rehabilitation, musculoskeletal injuries, and chronic disease management. If patients use less PT because of cost, it may lead to worse functional recovery and greater downstream use of health care services.
- Previously, there has been a lack of systematic description of how PT coverage restrictions and price variation affect patient costs, utilization, and outcomes.

## Approach
- The researchers used hospital price transparency data compiled by Turquoise Health, based on medical prices that U.S. federal regulations require hospitals to disclose publicly.
- They analyzed outpatient PT pricing data from **1,666** U.S. hospitals.
- The study focused on **10 categories** of hospital outpatient PT services, including common evaluation/management services and supervised exercise interventions that support functional rehabilitation.
- The study compared three types of prices: **list price, cash price, commercial negotiated price**, and also compared prices between rural and urban facilities, adjusting for geographic price differences in the analysis.

## Results
- The study found **substantial variation** in commercial negotiated prices for outpatient PT in the United States.
- For evaluation services as an example, the **median price ranged from $151 to $215**, showing clear differences in price distributions across services and regions.
- Price variation occurred **more across hospitals than within the same hospital**, indicating that location/institution is a more important determinant of price.
- **Commercial insurance prices were typically lower than cash prices**, meaning uninsured or self-pay patients may face a higher financial burden.
- **Prices in rural areas were typically lower than in urban areas**, and this conclusion was reached after adjusting for geographic differences in medical prices.
- The article **does not provide more detailed statistical significance, effect sizes, or quantified comparisons with prior baseline methods**; the strongest concrete conclusion is that PT prices vary substantially by location, insurance status, and whether payment is made in cash, which may affect patient utilization and downstream health outcomes.
