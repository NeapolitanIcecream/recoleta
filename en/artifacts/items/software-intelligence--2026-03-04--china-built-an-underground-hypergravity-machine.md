# China Built an Underground Hypergravity Machine

## Summary
China has built the underground hypergravity centrifuge CHIEF1900 in Hangzhou, using extreme centrifugal force to compress the observation of geological and engineering evolution processes from hundreds to tens of thousands of years into just a few days. Its significance lies in providing a more direct physical experimental platform than purely computational simulation for large-scale infrastructure, geological processes, and materials research.

## Problem
- Many geological, civil engineering, and deep-sea engineering processes evolve very slowly and at enormous scales; direct observation often requires hundreds to tens of thousands of years, making the experimental cost and timeline unacceptable.
- Relying only on computer simulation may miss critical physical data, making it difficult to identify failure mechanisms early in major structures such as dams, rail systems, and offshore platforms.
- Traditional hypergravity equipment has limited capacity, making it difficult to apply higher loads to multi-tonne samples while maintaining stable operation.

## Approach
- Build the large underground hypergravity centrifuge CHIEF1900, which spins heavy physical models at high speed on a rotating arm with a 6.4-meter radius, applying hypergravity loads of up to 1,900 g-tonnes to samples.
- Use scale models and the principle of hypergravity equivalence: for example, a 3-meter model under 100g can reproduce the internal stress state of a real 300-meter structure.
- Reduce the impact of strong vibrations on the surrounding environment by placing the facility 15 meters underground in a 230-square-meter circular chamber.
- Use vacuum cooling, forced ventilation, and oversized flanges combined with cooling pipelines to control the heat generated by high-speed rotation and maintain system stability.
- Position the facility as a shared research platform, equipped with multiple experimental chambers to support many types of experiments including dam engineering, seismic geotechnics, deep-sea engineering, and geological processes.

## Results
- The equipment can generate a load of **1,900 g-tonnes**, exceeding the previous record machine of the US Army Corps of Engineers at **1,200 g-tonnes**, and also surpassing its predecessor **CHIEF1300**.
- In terms of time compression, the article states that natural processes that originally required **hundreds to tens of thousands of years** can be shortened to **a few days**; the process of deep-soil contaminants migrating into groundwater can be compressed to **3.65 days**.
- In terms of scale equivalence, the article gives the example that a **3-meter** dam model under **100g** can simulate the stress level of a real **300-meter** dam.
- Previous CHIEF1300 experiments had already successfully simulated **strong earthquakes** to verify the seismic performance of hydropower dam foundations, and reproduced **2,000-meter deep-sea pressure** to evaluate the safety of seafloor methane hydrate extraction.
- The article also claims that the platform has synthesized metal alloys with **fewer defects and higher strength**, but provides no specific material metrics, comparison baseline, or error range.
- It does not provide formal paper-style benchmark data, statistical significance, or systematic error analysis; the strongest conclusion is that it has achieved an engineering-grade breakthrough in capacity, time-compression capability, and the range of multi-scenario physical simulation.
