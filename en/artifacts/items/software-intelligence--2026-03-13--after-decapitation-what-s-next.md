# After Decapitation, What's Next?

## Summary
This is a geopolitical commentary whose core argument is: airstrikes alone cannot achieve regime change in Iran, and if the United States wants durable leverage, it might consider seizing Kharg Island, Iran’s oil lifeline — but this would be a high-risk gamble. The author argues that the Iranian regime’s institutional resilience and high tolerance for pain mean this idea of “limited occupation in exchange for long-term leverage” may not work.

## Problem
- The article examines the question: **if airstrikes cannot bring about regime change in Iran, what strategy can the United States pursue next to gain lasting coercive pressure and negotiating leverage over Iran?**
- This matters because the US has publicly stated goals that include weakening Iran’s nuclear program, missile capabilities, and regional proxy network, but the author argues: **as long as the current regime remains in power, these goals will be difficult to achieve over the long term; yet the United States is unwilling to commit to a ground occupation to overthrow that regime.**
- The author stresses that mistaking **military vulnerability** for **political pliability** leads to strategic misjudgment; Iran may be weakened, but its internal repression apparatus and institutional structure remain sufficiently solid.

## Approach
- The author first explains why “decapitation” or high-altitude strikes cannot produce regime change: there are no ground forces, and there is no organized opposition capable of taking over the regime; the Iranian regime is an **institutionalized structure**, not the rule of a single strongman.
- On that basis, the author proposes an alternative mechanism: **seizing and controlling Kharg Island**, because the island accounts for **80%-90%** of Iran’s oil exports and is the fiscal lifeline of the regime.
- The simplest version of this idea is: **rather than directly controlling Tehran, control its most critical cash-flow export point**, thereby affecting Iran’s ability to rebuild military power, revive its nuclear program, and fund proxies.
- The author further analyzes why this option may appear feasible: the island is small and relatively isolated, Iran’s air and coastal defenses have been weakened, and the US already has naval assets in the Gulf; at the same time, this plan may be easier to frame domestically in the US as a “limited operation” than a long-term occupation.
- But the author also systematically assesses the risks of countermeasures: Iran could **destroy the terminal itself** before a US landing, or use missiles, drones, mines, sabotage, and prolonged harassment to turn the seizure and garrisoning of the island into a costly war of attrition.

## Results
- This is not an academic paper and has no experiments or benchmark tests, so there are **no formal quantitative evaluation results**.
- The most important quantitative fact in the article is that **Kharg Island handles 80%-90% of Iran’s oil exports**, while **China buys about 80% of Iran’s crude exports**; the author uses this to illustrate its strategic and market leverage.
- The author cites the intensity of the conflict to support the judgment that **Iran will not yield easily**: in the first **48 hours**, Iran reportedly launched **more than 500 ballistic missiles** and **1,000 drones** in retaliation, and the attacks exceeded US expectations.
- The author also gives an oil-price risk assessment: if Iran chooses to destroy Kharg Island rather than accept US control, **Brent crude could rise above $100 per barrel**, with the article specifically mentioning the possibility of reaching **$120**.
- The article’s main **conclusion-like claim** is: **seizing Kharg Island may be one of the few options that is “strategically coherent” for the United States if it wants long-term leverage without pursuing regime change, but success is far from guaranteed and could very likely fail because of Iran’s ideological rigidity and retaliatory capacity.**
- Compared with the original idea that “airstrikes could produce regime change,” the author believes this alternative is more realistic, but it still rests on a key untested assumption: **that Iran will value economic revenue more than refusing to submit to the United States**; and the author makes clear that, based on the evidence presented, he **does not favor this bet**.
