# Proprietary EDA Software Is Dead, Long Live Proprietary EDA Software!

## Summary
This is an opinion piece aimed at the semiconductor/EDA industry, rather than a strict experimental paper. The author argues that proprietary EDA tools will continue to exist for a long time at advanced process nodes, but that AI and open-source software engineering methods are weakening their moat and driving the modernization of hardware development workflows.

## Problem
- Chip development in large-scale industrial environments still depends heavily on proprietary, highly specialized EDA/CAD toolchains, with development experience, collaboration methods, and infrastructure that clearly lag behind modern software engineering practices.
- This matters because it affects hardware R&D efficiency, tool accessibility, methodology portability, and whether the industry can use the open-source ecosystem and AI to lower barriers to entry.
- The root causes of the current situation include the extremely high cost of advanced-node R&D, export controls and supply-chain constraints, the high concentration of the EDA market, and a blurred boundary between "methodology vs. trade secrets."

## Approach
- The article uses a historical and industry-analysis approach, reviewing the background of how EDA tools emerged in the semiconductor industry and explaining why proprietary tools have long dominated high-end chip design.
- The core mechanism can be understood simply as: **in the short term, extreme optimization at advanced process nodes still requires expensive and closed proprietary tools; in the medium to long term, AI is gradually turning "design intelligence" into software and making it more general-purpose, thereby weakening the exclusive advantage of proprietary tools.**
- The author offers two core judgments: first, proprietary EDA will continue to exist at leading-edge nodes, but AI is reshaping its competitive landscape; second, as hardware companies scale up, they will increasingly map their methodologies onto open-source software toolchains such as build systems, CI/CD, version control, and cloud compute infrastructure.
- The article also points out that AI will not only be used by major EDA vendors to enhance proprietary tools, but will also reduce the cost for companies to migrate workflows, integrate OSS, and build internal hardware infrastructure, thereby accelerating the "software-ization of hardware workflows."

## Results
- The article **does not provide experiments, benchmarks, or quantitative metrics**, so there are no precise numerical results, datasets, or performance comparisons against baselines to report.
- One of the strongest concrete conclusions is that at the "most advanced nodes/bleeding edge," proprietary EDA may continue to dominate, because behind it lies extremely costly tool development and supply-chain coordination, making it difficult for purely OSS solutions to catch up in the short term.
- Another core claim is that AI has already been applied to "some of the hardest problems in chip design," and that major EDA vendors such as Cadence, Synopsys, and Siemens are accelerating the integration of AI into their toolchains; the author sees this as a signal of both enhanced proprietary capabilities and democratized access to capabilities.
- The author also argues that more and more hardware R&D workflows will draw on mature software engineering infrastructure, such as large-scale build systems, CI/CD, storage systems, and cloud compute farms, to improve scalability and long-term maintainability.
- In terms of industry trends, the article's "breakthrough result" is more of a forward-looking conclusion: AI will significantly reduce the cost of hardware workflow migration and OSS integration, and the industry is at an inflection point for innovation in EDA and hardware infrastructure.
