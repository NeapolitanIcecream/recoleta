# Tehran Hijacked AI

## Summary
This article argues that terrorist organizations and regimes such as Iran are manipulating Wikipedia to "launder" propaganda into mainstream AI systems, thereby shaping public perception. Its core claim is that when large models rely on contaminated open knowledge sources, AI may repeat biased or even dangerous narratives.

## Problem
- The problem the article seeks to address is: **after Wikipedia is infiltrated by propaganda machines, AI may inherit and amplify these distorted narratives**, portraying terrorist organizations as ordinary political or "resistance" forces.
- This matters because Wikipedia is widely used by students, journalists, policymakers, and AI platforms; once the upstream knowledge source is polluted, false narratives can be repeatedly propagated downstream.
- The article emphasizes that this kind of "information laundering" hides the original propaganda source within the dissemination chain, making distorted content appear to be credible, neutral knowledge.

## Approach
- This is not a formal academic paper, but an **investigative commentary**; the method mainly consists of the author's case-by-case checks of ChatGPT responses, Wikipedia entries, and their cited sources.
- The core mechanism is simple: propaganda organizations first influence the content and citations of Wikipedia entries, and then AI repeats those claims because its training or retrieval depends on Wikipedia.
- Through comparisons across multiple people/organization cases, the author argues that the wording of Wikipedia entries closely overlaps with source materials from Islamist jihadist groups, Hamas, Iranian state media, and others, while key violent context is omitted.
- The author also provides large-scale counts in an attempt to show that Wikipedia's citations to Iranian state media, proxy media, and jihadist media are not isolated cases but a systemic phenomenon.

## Results
- The article's example claims that when asking ChatGPT "**What is Hezbollah**," the system answered "**a Lebanese political party**" rather than "a U.S.-designated terrorist organization," and **provided only 1 citation: Wikipedia**.
- For Palestinian Islamic Jihad commander **Abu al-Walid al-Dahdouh**, the author says ChatGPT described him as a "**prominent commander**" without highlighting his terrorist background; meanwhile, in his Wikipedia entry, **3 of 4 sources came directly from the Palestinian Islamic Jihad website**.
- The author claims the research found **29,000+** Wikipedia citations pointing to **Iranian state media**; among them, **Tasnim News** is described as one of the most common sources.
- The article also claims that Wikipedia contains **8,400+** citations from media associated with **Iranian proxy organizations (including Hamas and Hezbollah)**, **1,000+** from **Muslim Brotherhood-affiliated media**, and **100+** from **al-Qaeda-affiliated media**.
- Regarding the **2025 Shabelle offensive** entry, the article says it cites **Radio Furqaan** (al-Shabaab's official media) **nearly 50 times**, and cites **Shahada News Agency** more than a dozen times.
- The article does not provide reproducible experiments, control baselines, or standard academic evaluation metrics; its strongest concrete claim is: **AI's reliance on Wikipedia may enable manipulated narratives to spread at scale.**
