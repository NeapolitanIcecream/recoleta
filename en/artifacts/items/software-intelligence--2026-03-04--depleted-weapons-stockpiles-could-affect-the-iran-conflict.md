# Depleted weapons stockpiles could affect the Iran conflict

## Summary
This article analyzes how both sides’ weapons stockpiles and replenishment capacity affect the sustainability of the Iran conflict. Its core conclusion is that Iran’s ability to sustain high-intensity strikes is declining more quickly, while the US, though dominant overall, also faces pressure in replenishing high-end air-defense interceptors.
It is not an academic paper, but a situation analysis based on public military estimates and expert judgment, focused on the “stockpile constraints in a war of attrition.”

## Problem
- The article examines whether **weapons stockpiles can support sustained operations** during high-intensity airstrikes and missile/drone exchanges, and how this could shape the trajectory of the Iran-US/Israel conflict.
- This matters because modern war depends not only on battlefield performance, but also on **whether ammunition consumption outpaces production**; once stockpiles run low, even a strong military will be constrained.
- The article also specifically notes that **expensive, production-limited precision-guided weapons and air-defense interceptors** may become more critical bottlenecks than ordinary ground-attack munitions.

## Approach
- The article uses an analytical approach based on **open-source intelligence + expert estimates + military statements**, rather than experiments or a formal model.
- It infers each side’s stockpile condition and ability to sustain combat by comparing the number of strikes already conducted, missile/drone launch totals, and the extent of decline in those numbers.
- For Iran, the focus is on whether its missile and drone launch tempo has clearly declined, and whether its launchers, stockpiles, and production facilities are being systematically destroyed.
- For the US, the focus is on distinguishing between stockpiles of **ground-attack munitions** and **air-defense interceptors**: the former can shift to cheaper weapons after air superiority is achieved, while the latter are more vulnerable because Patriot missiles are expensive and annual output is limited.

## Results
- INSS estimates that the US and Israel have already carried out **more than 2,000 strikes**, each involving multiple munitions; Iran has launched **571 missiles** and **1,391 drones**, showing extremely high consumption intensity in the early stage of the conflict.
- US commander Gen Dan Caine said Iran’s ballistic missile launch volume had fallen **86%** compared with the first day of the war; Centcom said it fell another **23%** in just the past **24 hours**, which is used as evidence that Iran’s ability to sustain high-intensity strikes is weakening.
- The same source said Iran’s drone launch volume had fallen **73%** from the first day, reinforcing the judgment that **Iran is struggling to maintain its initial operational tempo**.
- Before the war, Iran was estimated to possess **more than 2,000 short-range ballistic missiles** and “**tens of thousands**” of Shahed one-way attack drones; however, the article argues that replenishment will become more difficult after the loss of air superiority and strikes on production facilities.
- On the US side, the article says it has “**tens of thousands**” of JDAM bombs and, after gaining the conditions for close-range strikes, can shift from more expensive **Tomahawk and other stand-off weapons** to cheaper **stand-in weapons**. Therefore, ground-attack operations can be sustained at high intensity for “**almost indefinitely**” or a “**long time**” (expert judgment, not a strict measurement result).
- The most critical constraint is air defense: each **Patriot interceptor** costs more than **$4 million**, current US annual production is about **700**, and a CSIS expert estimates the stockpile at around **1,600**. Based on this, the article argues that although the US can outlast Iran overall, **the sustainability of the air-defense battle is more uncertain than that of ground-attack airstrikes**.
