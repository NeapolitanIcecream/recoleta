# Regression Testing in Remote and Hybrid Software Teams: An Exploratory Study of Processes, Tools, and Practices

## Summary
This paper explores how remote and hybrid work changes regression testing processes, tool usage, and collaboration methods through qualitative interviews with 20 software testing practitioners. The core conclusion is that the basic stages of regression testing have not fundamentally changed, but effective execution depends more heavily on documentation, automation, and tool integration to support asynchronous collaboration.

## Problem
- The problem the paper addresses is: **how regression testing is organized, executed, and coordinated under remote/hybrid work conditions**, and why these changes affect software quality assurance.
- This matters because regression testing is used to prevent code changes from breaking existing functionality, and it is itself costly; the paper cites prior research indicating it can account for **50%–80%** of the verification or maintenance budget.
- When teams shift from co-located work to distributed collaboration, informal communication decreases, cross-time-zone collaboration increases, and infrastructure and tool interoperability become more complex, which may slow defect feedback and affect test traceability and stability.

## Approach
- The study uses an **exploratory qualitative research** design: structured interviews were conducted with **20** software professionals with relevant experience, centered on three dimensions: processes, tools, and remote/hybrid context.
- Participants covered different seniority levels, client regions, testing platforms (web, mobile, desktop, VR/AR), and manual/automated testing approaches, to increase diversity of experience.
- Data were collected from **January 2024 to April 2025**. Each interview lasted about **30–50 minutes**, was conducted via Google Meet, and was manually checked after recording and transcription.
- The analysis method was **thematic analysis**: manual coding, categorization, and theme synthesis; two researchers reviewed the coding, and interpretation was validated through member checking with **17/20** participants to confirm alignment with real experience.
- Put most simply, this paper does not propose a new algorithm. Instead, it **systematically interviews practitioners to summarize which regression testing practices remain unchanged in remote/hybrid teams and which must rely on stronger documentation, automation, and shared tools to compensate for the lack of face-to-face collaboration**.

## Results
- The research sample included **20** participants, with testing experience ranging from **3–15 years**; theoretical saturation appeared around the **14th** interview and was confirmed by the **17th**, but the study continued to 20 interviews to broaden coverage.
- Data collection ran from **January 2024 to April 2025**, with each interview lasting **30–50 minutes**; this indicates the conclusions are based on relatively systematic, long-term interview material rather than a one-off questionnaire.
- A total of **17** responses were received during the member-checking phase. The authors state that participants generally agreed with the synthesized conclusions, with only minor wording and precision adjustments.
- In terms of substantive findings, the paper explicitly claims that the **core stages of regression testing remain stable**, but execution in remote/hybrid environments depends more on **documentation, automation, tool integration, standardized reporting, shared code/test repositories, and traceability mechanisms** to support asynchronous collaboration.
- The paper also claims that communication and coordination problems under remote conditions are partially mitigated through more formal written communication and shared infrastructure; regression testing should be viewed as a **socio-technical practice** shaped jointly by human collaboration patterns and digital infrastructure.
- The provided excerpt **does not report quantitative comparative results such as performance metrics, changes in defect rates, or percentage efficiency improvements**; the most specific numbers mainly come from the research design and sample size rather than from experimental benchmarks.
