Summary

This Reuters graphic analysis assesses the impact of a closure of the Strait of Hormuz on global oil and LNG supply chains, prices, and regional economies. The core conclusion is that because the strait carries about one-fifth of the world’s oil and gas transport, a prolonged closure would cause a severe imbalance in the global energy system, with limited substitute capacity.

Problem

  • The article examines the question: after the Strait of Hormuz is closed, how much global oil and gas supply would be lost, which regions are most vulnerable, and whether alternative transport and buffer capacity are sufficient.
  • This matters because the strait is one of the world’s most critical energy shipping chokepoints, accounting for about 20% of global daily oil and LNG supply, and any disruption would directly drive up energy, food, and industrial costs while increasing inflation and recession risks.
  • It also involves secondary problems: oilfield output cuts, refinery shutdowns, inventory depletion, infrastructure damage, and elevated shipping risks would all significantly lengthen the recovery timeline.

Approach

  • The article uses journalistic data aggregation and visualization analysis, combining shipping volumes, prices, import dependence, infrastructure, and attack-related impacts from multiple sources to map the chain of disruption.
  • By comparing the daily oil and gas transport volumes through major shipping chokepoints, it illustrates Hormuz’s systemic importance in global energy flows.
  • By showing price trends for Brent crude, Dutch natural gas, and Asia LNG, as well as changes in U.S. retail fuel prices, it explains how supply disruptions are transmitted to end markets.
  • By listing the bypass capacity of the Saudi East-West Pipeline and UAE ADCOP, it evaluates why alternative export routes cannot fully close the gap.
  • Combining Asia’s share of Middle Eastern crude and LNG imports with cases of refinery/oilfield shutdowns and damage, it analyzes the hardest-hit regions and the main recovery bottlenecks.

Results

  • The article says the Strait of Hormuz carries about one-fifth of global daily oil and LNG supply shipments, so its closure constitutes a “nightmare scenario for the global energy system.”
  • The disruption has already lasted 10 days. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, and Kuwait have cut oilfield output because they cannot load cargoes for export and storage is nearing capacity.
  • Brent crude rose to $119 per barrel on Monday, the highest since 2022; the article also says prices for natural gas, Asia LNG, gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals, and fertilizer have all risen significantly, though it does not provide all specific increases.
  • The International Energy Agency plans to recommend releasing 400 million barrels of oil reserves, which the article says would be the largest such action in IEA history.
  • Alternative pipeline capacity is clearly insufficient: Saudi Arabia normally exports about 6 million barrels per day via Hormuz; its East-West Pipeline has a design capacity of 5 million barrels per day, but loadings at Yanbu have “rarely exceeded” 2.5 million barrels per day; the UAE’s ADCOP pipeline has capacity of 1.5 million barrels per day.
  • Downstream facilities have also been hit hard: Kuwait’s Al Zour refinery has capacity of 615,000 barrels per day, Bahrain’s Sitra refinery has capacity of 380,000 barrels per day; Qatar’s LNG plants would need weeks to recover from a full shutdown. The overall conclusion is that even if the conflict ends quickly, supply recovery would not be immediate.
