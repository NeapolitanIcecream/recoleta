Summary

This is not a technical paper, but a systematic rebuttal of the claim that "AI will destroy most of the software industry by 2028." The author argues that software’s real moat lies primarily not in code generation, but in data, workflows, compliance, distribution, scaled engineering, and institutional trust.

Problem

  • The article addresses the question: As AI code generation improves, will enterprise software/SaaS rapidly lose its defensibility and be cheaply replicated? This matters because the answer affects software investing, enterprise procurement, platform valuations, and long-term industry expectations.
  • The author directly challenges a popular assumption: if AI can replicate the surface features of mid-market SaaS in a matter of weeks, then software development costs will approach zero, triggering SaaS pricing collapse and an industry-wide re-rating.
  • The core dispute is whether software value comes mainly from "writing code," or from harder-to-replicate intangible assets such as proprietary data, complex business rules, regulatory certifications, infrastructure capabilities, and customer/channel networks.

Approach

  • The author uses a layered rebuttal approach: first deconstructing the premise that "code costs go to zero," then analyzing software moats category by category, including collaboration tools, vertical data platforms, deeply embedded enterprise workflows, systems of record, infrastructure/data tools, high-throughput transaction platforms, scientific computing software, heavily regulated software, and payment platforms.
  • The central mechanism can be summarized simply as: AI can more easily copy the "visible interface," but struggles to copy the "invisible system"—for example, data assets accumulated over many years, 4,000 customized workflow rules, global payments compliance licenses, distributed-system reliability, and audit trails.
  • The author further argues that code generation is only one part of the development process; the truly expensive stages are requirements understanding, architecture design, security review, testing, deployment, incident response, governance, and regulatory compliance, and these do not simultaneously fall to zero just because code generation does.
  • In the payments example, the author uses the idea that "low stablecoin transfer cost ≠ low compliance payment cost" to show that institutional-layer costs such as consumer protection, chargebacks, KYC/AML, foreign exchange, and merchant acceptance networks are an important part of platform value.

Results

  • The article does not provide formal quantitative results in the form of experimental design or academic benchmarks; it mainly offers industry reasoning and several empirical figures to support the conclusion that "AI will not invalidate the moats of most high-value software."
  • On development efficiency, the author claims that code generation accounts for only about 25% of a senior developer’s time, while independent studies typically show AI speedups of only 20–55% in that stage; therefore, total development cost will not linearly approach zero.
  • On quality and governance costs, the author states that AI-generated code has a vulnerability rate of 40–45%, the number of issues in PRs is 1.7×, and duplicated code reaches 8× historical levels; based on this, the author argues that AI may shift the burden downstream into review, testing, and operations.
  • On organizational embeddedness, the author gives the example that a Fortune 500 ITSM platform can accumulate 4,000 customized workflow rules, illustrating that "building a replacement UI over a weekend" and truly replacing a mission-critical system are not problems of the same difficulty.
  • On payments infrastructure, the author notes that Visa already has more than 100 million merchant acceptance points globally, and Wise holds 70+ global licenses; by contrast, stablecoin merchant acceptance remains weak, so "low on-chain fees" cannot directly replace existing payment networks.
  • The strongest overall conclusion is that the most threatened software is lightweight software with thin functional moats that relies mainly on surface-level features; by contrast, in areas such as proprietary data, complex workflows, infrastructure, high-throughput systems, scientific software, and heavily regulated domains, the author believes AI is more likely to strengthen incumbents than destroy them.
