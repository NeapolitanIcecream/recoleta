Summary

This work demonstrates quantum teleportation for the first time between two different, mutually independent quantum dots, showing the feasibility of semiconductor quantum dots as future quantum internet nodes. This result advances demonstrations from within “the same emitter” to a network-level scenario “between separated independent emitters.”

Problem

  • The quantum internet requires reliable transmission of quantum states between independent nodes at separated locations, but previous quantum teleportation usually relied on the same emitter, making it difficult to scale to real networks.
  • Without establishing high-fidelity teleportation and relays between different quantum emitters, it is difficult to realize scalable quantum communication, quantum repeaters, and distributed quantum computing.
  • The key challenge is enabling different quantum dots, long-distance optical links, single-photon detection, and timing synchronization to simultaneously meet strict quantum coherence requirements.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is to transfer the single-photon polarization state produced by one quantum dot, via a quantum teleportation protocol, into the corresponding system of another quantum dot located at a different physical site.
  • The researchers used two independent semiconductor quantum dots as quantum light sources, rather than the same emitter, making the setup closer to communication scenarios between real quantum network nodes.
  • The experiment connected the two systems through a 270-meter free-space optical link and used GPS-assisted synchronization, ultrafast single-photon detectors, and an atmospheric-turbulence-resistant stabilization system to ensure operation of the protocol.
  • Overall, this combines high-quality quantum dot materials, nanofabricated resonant structures, and optical quantum experimental systems to verify the feasibility of using independent deterministic sources for quantum repeaters.

Results

  • It demonstrated quantum teleportation between two different quantum dots for the first time: the single-photon polarization state generated by one quantum dot was successfully transmitted to another independent quantum dot system.
  • The experiment was completed over a 270-meter free-space link, showing that this is not merely an on-chip or same-source demonstration, but a prototype validation for distributed quantum networks.
  • The reported teleportation state fidelity reached up to 82 ± 1%.
  • This fidelity exceeds the classical limit by more than 10 standard deviations, indicating that the quantum teleportation was not a classical pseudo-effect but a reliable realization of genuine quantum transmission.
  • The paper positions this as an important step toward scalable quantum repeaters and a practical quantum internet; the next goal is to achieve entanglement swapping between two quantum dots and build the first quantum repeater based on two deterministic sources.
