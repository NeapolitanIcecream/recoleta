Summary

This article describes how Saharan dust crossed the Mediterranean and broadly affected Europe in March 2026, and explains how researchers combined reanalysis, satellite observations, and atmospheric models to analyze its impacts on weather and energy. Its core value lies in showing that dust affects not only air quality and precipitation, but can also significantly reduce solar power generation through cloud-radiation effects.

Problem

  • The article focuses on the question of how Saharan dust spreads across Europe, settles out, and in turn affects weather phenomena, climate processes, and solar power generation efficiency.
  • This matters because transregional dust events can bring so-called "blood rain," reduced visibility, changes in cloud microphysics, and real losses to photovoltaic power generation.
  • The research also points to a broader issue: winter dust events in Europe may have become more frequent and more intense in recent years, requiring better understanding of their drivers and impact mechanisms.

Approach

  • NASA's GEOS/GEOS-FP atmospheric model was used to generate an animation of dust column mass density from March 1 to March 9, 2026, tracking the transport of dust from northwestern Africa toward the Mediterranean and Western Europe.
  • The model combines satellite data with mathematical equations describing atmospheric physical processes; in the simplest terms, it stitches together observations and weather physics to estimate how much dust is in an air column and where it is moving.
  • Combining NASA MERRA-2, MODIS, and other satellite products, researchers further disentangled the effects of airborne Saharan dust on solar power generation in Hungary.
  • The key mechanism is that dust not only directly blocks solar radiation, but also promotes the formation of high-altitude “dusty cirrus,” changing cloud reflectivity and reducing the solar radiation reaching photovoltaic panels.

Results

  • In March 2026, dust was lofted from the Sahara by winter winds and spread into Western Europe during March 1 to 9, affecting areas including Spain, France, the United Kingdom, Switzerland, and Italy.
  • After combining with moisture-laden weather systems, the dust produced so-called “blood rain” in southern and eastern Spain, parts of France, and southern UK, showing that large amounts of particles ultimately settled to the surface with rainfall.
  • The new analysis cited in the article shows that in Hungary, photovoltaic system performance dropped to 46% on high-dust days, while on low-dust days it was typically 75% or higher.
  • This means that under high-dust conditions, photovoltaic performance fell by at least 29 percentage points compared with low-dust conditions (46% vs. 75%+).
  • The research argues that the largest power-generation losses did not come solely from dust in the air itself, but because dust increased the presence and reflectivity of cirrus clouds, further reducing the radiation reaching solar panels.
  • For the claim that winter dust in Europe has become more frequent and more intense in recent years, the article does not provide new quantitative statistics, but the specific explanations offered include drier conditions in northwestern Africa and weather patterns that more often blow winds northward from the Sahara.
