Summary

This report discusses Israel’s severe shortage of ballistic missile interceptors amid its ongoing conflict with Iran, and the resulting pressure on Israeli and US stockpiles and air defense capacity. The core message is that high-intensity missile warfare is rapidly consuming expensive air defense interceptors with limited production capacity, and replenishment may not keep pace with the rate of use.

Problem

  • Israel informed the United States that it is critically low on key interceptors used to stop ballistic missiles, and continued Iranian attacks are worsening the depletion.
  • This matters because such interceptors are among the most effective defensive tools against long-range missiles, and shortages would directly weaken the protection of territory and military targets.
  • The broader issue is that high-intensity war can quickly drain US and Israeli missile defense inventories, exposing the vulnerability of modern air defense systems in terms of cost, stockpile depth, and production capacity.

Approach

  • This is not a research paper and does not propose a new technical method; instead, it analyzes interceptor stockpile pressure based on statements from US officials and public information.
  • The article’s core mechanism is straightforward: Iran continues launching missiles, Israel relies on interceptors for defense, and when the rate of expenditure exceeds the rate of production and resupply, inventories approach exhaustion.
  • The report also notes that Iran has added cluster munitions to some missiles, which may increase the difficulty of defense and further accelerate interceptor consumption.
  • It also mentions alternative or mitigating measures, including interception by fighter jets, increased US THAAD production, and the possibility of continued military aid.

Results

  • The article does not provide experimental results or reproducible evaluation metrics in the scientific sense; instead, it offers quantitative signals about the conflict situation and stockpile depletion.
  • In June last year, the United States fired more than 150 THAAD interceptors during the 12-day war with Iran, which CSIS said amounted to about one-quarter of the US inventory at the time.
  • According to some reports, in the first 5 days of the current war, the United States had already used about $2.4 billion worth of Patriot interceptors.
  • The White House claimed in the article that Iranian drone attacks fell by 95% and ballistic missile attacks fell by 90%; however, these are government statements, not independently verified research findings in the article.
  • The strongest concrete conclusion in the article is that Israel is currently “critically low” on ballistic missile interceptors, and although the United States says its own stockpile is “sufficient,” it has also begun significantly increasing THAAD production to address the risks of prolonged depletion.
