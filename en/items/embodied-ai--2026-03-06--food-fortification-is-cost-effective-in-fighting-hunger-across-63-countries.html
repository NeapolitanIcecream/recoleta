Summary

This is a systematic review covering 63 countries, 56 studies, and more than 200 economic analyses, concluding that large-scale food fortification is generally highly cost-effective in reducing "hidden hunger." The paper's core value is that it provides updated and cross-country consistent economic evidence for nutrition policy during periods of budget constraint.

Problem

  • The paper addresses the question: whether food fortification is truly worth investing public resources in globally, especially in low- and middle-income countries.
  • This matters because "hidden hunger" (vitamin and mineral deficiencies) has a massive impact: globally, about 56% of children aged 6–59 months and 69% of non-pregnant women aged 15–49 are affected, leading to substantial mortality and disease burden.
  • In the context of cuts to global nutrition aid, policymakers need to prioritize interventions that deliver the greatest health gains per unit cost.

Approach

  • The authors conducted a systematic review: they synthesized and compared existing economic evidence on food fortification rather than carrying out a new single-country experiment.
  • The scope is large: it includes 56 studies, 232 main cost-effectiveness analyses, 47 benefit-cost ratio analyses, and 8 cost-utility analyses, covering 63 countries, of which more than 40 are low- and middle-income economies.
  • The core mechanism is straightforward: add key micronutrients to common staple foods or condiments, such as vitamin A, folic acid/vitamin B9, iron, and iodine, then compare health benefits and costs under fortification versus no fortification.
  • The main evaluation metrics are ICER (the incremental cost required to avert 1 DALY), benefit-cost ratio, and cost-utility analysis, with further assessment of whether interventions are "worth it" using each country's per-capita GDP threshold.

Results

  • Among the 232 main cost-effectiveness analyses, 58% of interventions had ICER < $150/DALY averted; 84% of analyses had ICER < $1000/DALY averted (relative to "no fortification," all converted to 2022 US dollars).
  • Using country-specific income thresholds, 87% of interventions were below 50% of per-capita GDP for that country; among low- and middle-income countries, 84% were below 35% of per-capita GDP; among low-income countries, 71% were below 20% of per-capita GDP.
  • Among the 47 benefit-cost ratio analyses, all were positive, meaning monetized benefits exceeded costs.
  • Of the 8 cost-utility analyses, 6 showed that food fortification was a dominant option compared with no fortification, meaning it was both cheaper and more effective.
  • The study describes itself as the most comprehensive economic review of food fortification to date, and emphasizes that positive results were strongly consistent across countries, methods, and nutrient/food combinations.
  • The article does not report a new SOTA result from a single unified experimental dataset; its strongest claim is that, based on existing evidence across 63 countries, large-scale food fortification is generally a highly cost-effective public health intervention worldwide.
