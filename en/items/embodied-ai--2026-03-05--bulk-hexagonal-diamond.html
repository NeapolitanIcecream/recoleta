Summary

This paper reports the experimental synthesis of millimetre-sized, phase-pure hexagonal diamond (HD), and uses multiple characterization methods and theoretical simulations to demonstrate that it is indeed a distinct carbon phase. It resolves the long-standing controversy over whether hexagonal diamond truly exists, while also showing that this material has slightly higher hardness than cubic diamond (CD) and high thermal stability.

Problem

  • For a long time, there has been a lack of reliable experimental evidence that hexagonal diamond exists as an independent discrete phase, which has hindered research on its physical properties.
  • Previously observed “lonsdaleite/HD” has often been questioned as merely cubic diamond containing stacking faults and twins, leading to persistent debate in the field.
  • If HD does in fact exist and can be prepared stably, it could become a more promising superhard/high-performance semiconductor material than CD, making it important both scientifically and industrially.

Approach

  • Using highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) as the precursor, the authors synthesized millimetre-scale bulk, phase-pure HD under compression along the c-axis combined with high-temperature, high-pressure conditions.
  • Through multiple structural and electronic-state characterization methods including synchrotron XRD, Rietveld refinement, STEM/SAED, EELS, Raman, and XPS, they ruled out the possibility that the sample was a CD mixed phase or could be explained only by stacking faults.
  • Combined with theoretical simulations/machine-learning atomic simulations, they analyzed the transformation pathway from graphite to HD, showing that directional compression and pre-existing defects/interlayer bonding help promote hexagonal stacking.
  • They further measured the mechanical properties and thermal stability of the resulting bulk HD and compared them with CD.

Results

  • The paper’s central result is the successful preparation of millimetre-sized and phase-pure bulk hexagonal diamond, going beyond prior work that mostly involved nanocrystals, transient shock products, or controversial samples.
  • Structural evidence shows that the sample matches the HD crystal structure (P6_3/mmc); the paper reports bond angles of 105.41(16)° and 113.21(13)°, and SAED shows sixfold symmetry distinct from CD.
  • EELS shows typical sp3 bonding features, with a low-loss plasmon peak at 33.75 eV, close to 33.8 eV for CD, supporting its identification as a diamond-like carbon phase rather than graphite or a mixed phase.
  • The authors claim that bulk HD is slightly harder than CD and has high thermal stability; however, the provided excerpt does not include specific hardness values, temperature thresholds, error bars, or percentage improvement.
  • Relative to the skepticism that “HD is merely defective CD,” the paper’s strongest breakthrough claim is that it provides, for the first time, solid experimental evidence supporting HD as a discrete carbon phase, and clarifies the graphite → HD transformation pathway.
