Authors

Summary

This paper proposes a real-time non-verbal human-AI interaction framework based on 2D human keypoints, capable of simultaneously predicting 1 second of future body motion and recognizing emotion. Its core focus is to verify whether lightweight temporal models can enable natural interaction on edge devices, and whether synthetic-data pretraining is genuinely useful.

Problem

  • Robots and embodied AI, under constrained compute, energy, privacy, and connectivity conditions, still need to understand and respond to human body language in order to enable more natural and safer collaboration.
  • Existing generative AI is mostly “disembodied,” excelling at media generation but not at sustained, real-time, purely non-verbal bodily interaction; meanwhile, relevant data is scarce, and subtle emotional movements are difficult to model.
  • There is also a key scientific question: whether AI-generated human motion is statistically truly close to real human motion, or merely remains at the level of surface imitation; this directly affects how well models trained on synthetic data transfer to real interactions.

Approach

  • The task is formulated as dual-task learning: given the past 60 frames (2 seconds) of 17 COCO 2D keypoints, the model outputs a future 30-frame (1 second) motion sequence and a 3-class emotion classification result (enthusiastic, laughing, happy-to-see-you).
  • Keypoints are centered and scale-normalized so the model learns motion patterns independent of a person’s position and size; during training, motion-generation MSE and emotion-classification cross-entropy are jointly minimized, with classification loss weighted at 0.3.
  • Four lightweight architectures are compared: mlp, lstm, cnn-lstm, and transformer, all using a shared encoder plus two task heads to balance real-time motion prediction and emotion recognition.
  • The study uses 437 real human video clips, expanded via sliding windows into 6992 samples; MotionLCM is then used to generate 9k/45k/90k synthetic skeleton sequences for pretraining, followed by fine-tuning on real data.
  • Keypoints are further extracted from videos generated by SORA and VEO for “in-the-wild” testing, measuring the domain gap between training on real/synthetic skeletons and outputs from frontier video generators.

Results

  • In terms of real-time performance, all four models run faster than real time on an NVIDIA Orin Nano: MLP 1.643 ms, LSTM 3.371 ms, CNN-LSTM 4.844 ms, Transformer 9.294 ms; the slowest model is still about 100+ FPS. Parameter counts are 2.91M, 0.35M, 0.59M, and 3.43M, respectively.
  • In terms of data scale, the real dataset contains 437 videos, expanded by sliding windows into 6992 train/test clips; the synthetic pretraining set reaches up to 90k samples (30k per class). The authors report that as pretraining samples increase from 9k→45k→90k, motion-prediction error on the real test set continues to decrease for LSTM/CNN-LSTM/Transformer, while MLP gains little and may even degrade.
  • Emotion recognition is nearly saturated on the human test set: under the Baseline, CNN-LSTM/LSTM/Transformer all reach 100%, while MLP achieves 91.71%; after pretraining, MLP rises to 94.78% (+3.07 percentage points), while the others remain essentially unchanged, indicating a clear ceiling effect in the real dataset.
  • On SORA_Human, post-pretraining accuracies are CNN-LSTM 38.28, LSTM 44.31, MLP 36.72, Transformer 45.98, compared with Baseline values of 45.76, 45.54, 47.99, and 46.65, for changes of -7.48, -1.23, -11.27, and -0.67 percentage points, indicating that SORA’s re-rendering of real human skeletons harms transfer.
  • On SORA_AI, pretraining substantially helps some sequence models: LSTM rises from 32.66 to 50.00 (+17.34), Transformer from 35.74 to 48.15 (+12.41), CNN-LSTM drops from 31.25 to 28.35 (-2.90), and MLP rises from 39.96 to 43.93 (+3.97). This suggests that when the test distribution is closer to MotionLCM, pretraining yields larger benefits.
  • On VEO, results are closer to the real distribution but still show domain gap: CNN-LSTM rises from 39.27 to 51.67 (+12.40), LSTM drops from 61.15 to 59.48 (-1.67), MLP drops from 63.44 to 55.42 (-8.02), and Transformer is roughly flat at 53.12→53.65 (+0.53). The paper also explicitly states that motion-prediction MAE is generally better on VEO than on SORA, indicating that temporal coherence matters more than image fidelity for real interaction performance; however, the paper does not provide a complete MAE table, only trends and figures.
