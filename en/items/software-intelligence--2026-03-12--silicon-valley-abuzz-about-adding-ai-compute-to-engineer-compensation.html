Summary

This article discusses an industry shift that is taking shape: AI inference compute and token budgets are beginning to become part of software engineers' compensation and productivity allocation. Its core point is not to propose a new technology, but to note that after the widespread adoption of generative AI, compute quotas are being managed and negotiated much like salary, bonus, and equity.

Problem

  • Generative AI has been embedded into software development, but inference costs are rising rapidly and becoming a new budget item that companies cannot ignore.
  • Engineers' actual productivity is increasingly affected by the AI compute available to them; if compute allocation is insufficient, individual output and career competitiveness may decline.
  • CFOs and recruiting teams lack a mature framework for assessing how much token/inference budget should be given to employees, whether it is worth it, and whether this cost should be treated as part of “compensation.”

Approach

  • Through industry observation rather than experimental research, the article proposes treating AI inference as a “fourth category of compensation” for engineers: beyond salary, bonus, and equity, adding tokens/compute.
  • Put simply: in the future, companies will not only give employees money, but also consumable AI usage allowances; this allowance directly affects how quickly they can write code and complete work.
  • The article cites comments from people at OpenAI, Levels.fyi, and Theory Ventures to show that candidates have already begun asking about dedicated inference compute, and that the market is also starting to view Copilot subscriptions or token budgets as benefits/quotas.
  • It further proposes a financial evaluation approach: just as companies assess the return on spending for cloud GPUs, they should evaluate how much effective work each dollar of inference cost can buy.

Results

  • The article does not contain paper-style experiments, benchmark datasets, or reproducible experimental results; it mainly provides industry signals and quantitative estimates.
  • The head of OpenAI Codex said that candidates are asking “more and more” during interviews how much dedicated inference compute they will receive; at the same time, “usage per user is growing much faster than overall user growth,” indicating that compute is becoming scarcer, but no specific growth figures are given.
  • Levels.fyi reports that the 75th-percentile salary for software engineers is $375,000; Tomasz Tunguz estimates that adding $100,000 per year in inference costs brings total cost to $475,000, with AI usage accounting for about 20%+ of total compensation cost.
  • Tunguz also gives a personal example: he automates 31 tasks per day, with annual inference costs of about $12,000.
  • For higher-AI-cost scenarios, the article makes a strong claim: if an engineer “burns” $100,000 per year in inference expenses, then their productivity had “better reach 8x,” but this is a judgment-based threshold, not an empirical conclusion.
  • The article’s strongest conclusion is a trend judgment: by 2026, engineers may negotiate not only with dollars and equity, but also with tokens or AI compute budget.
