Summary

This paper proposes “AI phenomenology” as a methodology for studying human-AI interaction experiences, arguing that researchers should not only evaluate whether AI is usable or efficient, but also systematically ask how it feels to live and interact with AI. Based on three empirical studies from 2025, the paper distills reusable research tools, design concepts, and a forward-looking research agenda.

Problem

  • Existing AI evaluation often relies on metrics such as usability, engagement, and productivity, but these flatten users’ subjective feelings, relationship changes, and long-term processes of negotiation with AI.
  • When AI functions both as a tool and as a “social other,” researchers need to understand how users perceive AI agency, value alignment, and effects on professional identity; this is important for human-AI alignment and ethical design.
  • AI capabilities and social expectations vary greatly across eras, and one-off, static evaluations struggle to capture how human-AI relationships co-evolve over time.

Approach

  • The paper proposes an “AI phenomenology” framework that shifts the research focus from “How well did the AI perform?” to “How is AI interaction experienced, interpreted, and renegotiated by people?”
  • The framework is instantiated through three empirical studies: two month-long longitudinal studies centered on the chatbot “Day” (agency and value alignment), and one multi-method study of agentic AI in software engineering.
  • The authors design a reusable toolkit, including progressive transparency interviews, the Value-Alignment Perception Toolkit (VAPT), and task-anchored multi-method elicitation, to progressively reveal AI internal mechanisms, value profiles, and roles within work processes.
  • In the simplest terms, the core mechanism is: first let people interact with AI naturally, then gradually reveal what the AI is “doing behind the scenes,” and observe how users’ understanding of the AI, emotions, and self-perception change.
  • The paper also proposes three design directions: translucent design, agency-aware value alignment, and temporal co-evolution tracking, and advocates treating research findings as “experiential timestamps” specific to a given AI era.

Results

  • In the AI companionship study, the authors propose a 3×4 agency framework; among 22 participants, 11/22 felt that “Day” seemed to have “its own agenda.” Even after its preset strategies were revealed, participants continued to use agentic language to describe the AI.
  • In the value alignment study, the chat-driven personality profile achieved an alignment rate of 77% on personalization questions, while the anti-personality baseline achieved only 25%; this suggests that participants could clearly distinguish which AI profile “felt like them.”
  • Agreement between AI-inferred values and self-reports reached a moderate level: Spearman ρ≈0.58, and 63.6% of predictions fell within ±1 Likert point of self-ratings.
  • Among 20 participants, 5/20 preferred the AI-generated value profile over their own questionnaire-based self-report; 13/20 left the study believing that AI can understand human values.
  • The main contribution of the software engineering study is methodological and design-oriented, such as prompt-and-code reviews (PCRs) for jointly reviewing code and prompt history to preserve accountability, authorship, and learning processes; the excerpt does not provide clear quantitative performance metrics.
  • The paper’s strongest overall claim is that human-AI alignment is not a property that can be measured once at a single moment, but a dynamic process that is experienced, negotiated, and updated over time; therefore, it requires longitudinal, contextualized, and periodically repeatable methods of study.
