Summary

This is an AGI adaptation guide for general readers. Its core argument is to start using AI tools in daily life as soon as possible, identify your truly scarce abilities, and prepare for potential employment and economic shocks.

Problem

  • The article tries to address a practical preparedness question for ordinary non-technical people facing AGI: if AI becomes much more capable very quickly, how can individuals reduce career risk and improve adaptability.
  • This matters because the author believes the next decade will bring stronger economic turbulence and job restructuring, especially for work centered on moving, rewriting, and summarizing information, which is more likely to be replaced.
  • Many people feel anxious about AI while lacking real hands-on experience with it, which means they can neither judge its capability boundaries nor develop effective human-AI collaboration skills.

Approach

  • Use AI tools frequently first: use ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini every day for a month, treating them like a “very fast but error-prone intern,” and build intuition for their strengths and failure modes through practice.
  • Identify and strengthen your "hard and scarce" experiential abilities: not your job title, but the abilities that are difficult to document and come from long-term real-world experience and compressed pattern recognition.
  • Move away from dependence on pure information-shuffling work: if your job is fundamentally about summarizing, rewriting, arranging, or reformatting, you should prepare an alternative path as soon as possible and shift toward roles that “decide what information matters.”
  • Learn to give clear instructions: so-called “prompting skill” is essentially about decomposing problems, expressing constraints, and defining success criteria; the core is not technical knowledge, but clear thinking and communication.
  • Increase real-world resilience: develop a physical-world skill, and make your financial structure more robust (low spending, savings, reducing single-source income risk) to cope with uneven AGI impacts.

Results

  • No experiments, datasets, or formal quantitative evaluation results are provided; this is not an academic paper, but a strategic opinion essay.
  • The clearest time-based claim in the article is that “using AI every day for a month” is enough to help users develop a feel for AI’s strengths and weaknesses and improve their judgment.
  • The author’s strongest employment-related claim is that “junior anything”, copywriting, entry-level analysis, research coordination, content management, and similar information-reorganization roles face higher risk, but no data on unemployment rates, automation shares, or baseline comparisons is given.
  • The author’s strongest qualitative conclusion about economic impact is that disruption over the next decade may be greater than the combined total of the past thirty years, but this is a predictive statement without empirical support.
  • The actionable recommendations include starting to use AI as soon as possible, strengthening non-replaceable abilities, learning to express yourself clearly, preparing a Plan B, and building emergency savings; these are action recommendations, not empirically validated performance improvements.
