Summary

This investigative report reconstructs how the U.S. Navy’s Littoral Combat Ship (LCS) program, under the combined effects of technical defects, inadequate training, and political pressure, evolved into a massively expensive procurement failure. The core conclusion is: although the problems were visible for years and repeatedly exposed, Navy leadership, Congress, and contractors still collectively sustained the program.

Problem

  • The article seeks to answer: why a new warship program promoted as low-cost, multi-mission, and rapidly deployable ultimately became a long-running failure marked by heavy spending, frequent breakdowns, and difficulty accomplishing missions, and what institutional causes lay behind it.
  • This matters because the cost per LCS rose from about $220 million initially to about $500 million, while total lifecycle costs could exceed $100 billion, directly affecting defense budget waste, fleet readiness, and taxpayer interests.
  • More broadly, it reveals a systemic problem in weapons procurement: under political interests, industry lobbying, and bureaucratic incentives, even clearly flawed programs can continue expanding, weakening national security rather than strengthening it.

Approach

  • ProPublica uses an investigative tracing method: reviewing thousands of pages of public records and interviewing insiders from the Navy and shipbuilding industry involved in the ships’ design, construction, testing, deployment, and evaluation.
  • The article builds its argument through multiple key failure cases, especially the engine/propulsion system failures of USS Freedom, USS Milwaukee, and USS Fort Worth, showing a cycle of “sailing despite known problems — breakdown — pressure to restore reputation — breakdown again.”
  • It analyzes technical problems together with institutional mechanisms, including overly aggressive initial requirements (high speed, small crews, rapidly swappable mission modules), insufficient testing, maintenance complexity caused by running two ship variants in parallel, and inadequate personnel and training support.
  • The article further argues that Navy leadership suppressed or ignored criticism, Congress pushed continued procurement based on local jobs, and contractors invested heavily in lobbying, together bypassing the checks and balances originally meant to verify the program’s viability.

Results

  • Cost outcomes: per-ship LCS cost rose from the original promise of no more than $220 million to about $500 million; some early ships were criticized as costing more than $750 million each; a former GAO analyst estimated the class’s total lifetime cost could exceed $100 billion.
  • Quantity and output: the Navy ultimately needed only 32 ships, far below the original plan for more than 50, but Congress still forced the purchase of 3 more; according to interviewed experts, what taxpayers ultimately got was fewer than 30 ships with limited survivability and effectively single-mission roles.
  • Reliability performance: within the 10 months beginning in December 2015, 5 LCS ships suffered breakdowns around the world; USS Milwaukee, about 3 weeks into its maiden voyage, suffered severe damage to its propulsion equipment due to a software fault and was towed about 40 miles for repairs; USS Fort Worth damaged its propulsion system because of operator error and remained out of service for 7 months awaiting parts.
  • Specific maintenance costs: after the 2016 exercise, USS Freedom was found to have engine “galloping corrosion,” then entered two years of repairs at an additional cost of millions of dollars.
  • Capability outcomes: the investigation says the LCS never truly achieved its claimed multi-mission role; the Navy later also acknowledged that the ship was not suitable for fighting a peer adversary such as China, because its “lethality and survivability are not enough to support high-end combat.”
  • Service-life outcomes: ships originally designed for a 25-year service life were in some cases mothballed/retired early after less than 10 years in service.
