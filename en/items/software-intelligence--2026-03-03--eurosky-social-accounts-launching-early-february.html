Summary

This is not a research paper, but a product announcement about the launch of eurosky.social accounts. It proposes a portable social identity based on the AT Protocol, emphasizing European hosting, European governance, and portability of data and social relationships across apps.

Problem

  • Existing social platforms typically bind identity, data, and applications within a single company, causing users to be locked into the platform and lose their network connections and content when migrating.
  • Centralized platform governance can leave accounts and data subject to unilateral decisions by corporations or governments, which is important for digital sovereignty and long-term availability.
  • An open social ecosystem needs a unified identity layer that can be reused across applications; otherwise, different apps struggle to interoperate, compete, and collaborate.

Approach

  • Uses the AT Protocol to provide a “portable social identity,” allowing one eurosky.social account to be used across multiple compatible applications.
  • The account acts as a personal data server (PDS), separating posts, profile data, and social connections from frontend applications, so data is no longer tied to any single app.
  • Users can migrate their identity to other providers, reducing platform lock-in while preserving their network and data.
  • The solution emphasizes management by a European non-profit initiative, hosting on European cloud infrastructure, and governance under European law to highlight regional governance and data sovereignty.
  • The protocol currently mainly targets public data; the announcement notes that private data capabilities are still under development, with protocol-level support expected in 2026.

Results

  • The text does not provide formal experiments, benchmarks, or quantitative performance results, so there are no reportable accuracy numbers, improvement margins, or dataset comparisons.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that one identity can be used across dozens of apps, and it explicitly lists AT Protocol applications that can be accessed, including Bluesky, Tangled, Sill, Streamplace, among others.
  • The announcement claims it enables “Switch apps, keep your network and your data” portability, but provides no figures for migration success rate, latency, or compatibility coverage.
  • The announcement also gives one clear time point: accounts are planned to launch in early February; protocol-level private data support in the AT Protocol is expected in 2026.
