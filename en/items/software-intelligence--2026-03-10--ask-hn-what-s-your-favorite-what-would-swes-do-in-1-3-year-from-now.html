Summary

This is not a research paper, but a Hacker News discussion post about how the role of software engineers may change over the next 1–3 years. It presents several speculative views on AI-driven development, labor reorganization, compensation mechanisms, and industry M&A, but offers no systematic methodology or experimental validation.

Problem

  • The question under discussion is: after the rapid progress of LLM-driven development tools such as Claude Code and Codex, what will software engineers do in the next 1–3 years, and how will roles be reorganized?
  • This matters because if AI significantly changes software production efficiency, it could affect employment structure, compensation models, the competitive landscape of tech companies, as well as migration and investment flows.
  • The text is essentially a solicitation of opinions and scenario forecasting, rather than the solution of a rigorously defined scientific problem.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is not an experimental method, but scenario analysis: assuming that large-model coding capabilities continue to improve rapidly, thereby reducing the bargaining power of traditional SWEs.
  • Put simply, the author believes that the value of future engineers will lie more in collaborating with AI, guiding AI, and fixing problems introduced by AI, rather than purely writing code by hand.
  • The post proposes a market-based idea: in non-tech companies, engineering work may come to resemble a bidding/bounty system, where whoever is better at communicating with AI and solving problems faster gets paid.
  • For large tech companies, the author argues that AI will still require substantial human “steering,” so engineers will shift toward more frequent requirement definition, model guidance, and feature delivery.
  • It further extrapolates to capital markets, predicting that private equity will acquire companies and use AI-driven efficiency gains to compress costs and drive M&A consolidation.

Results

  • It provides no experiments, datasets, baseline models, or reproducible quantitative results.
  • It reports no accuracy, percentage productivity gains, cost reductions, or numerical comparisons with existing software engineering workflows.
  • The strongest concrete claims include that the “pricing power” of traditional SWE roles will decline, and that engineering compensation at non-tech companies may come to resemble sales commissions or bounty-based competition.
  • It also claims that STEM migration to the US/UK may “drop sharply,” but provides no statistical basis or model support.
  • Another explicit prediction is that large IT companies will go through more M&A, industry competition will intensify, and margins will be compressed further.
  • Overall, these are qualitative judgments about the next 1–3 years, not validated research findings.
