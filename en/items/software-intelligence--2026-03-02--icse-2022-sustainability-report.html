Authors

Summary

This is a post-conference survey report on the sustainability of ICSE 2022, with the core aim of evaluating whether the community benefits of in-person attendance are sufficient to offset its carbon footprint. Rather than proposing a new algorithm, the report uses questionnaire results to summarize the trade-offs academic software engineering conferences face among sustainability, inclusivity, and social value.

Problem

  • The paper examines the question: whether the benefits of attending academic conferences in person are sufficient to offset the carbon emissions caused by travel, and how future conferences can become more sustainable.
  • This matters because large international conferences provide networking, recruiting, collaboration, and career development opportunities, while also generating a significant carbon footprint from international travel.
  • For the software engineering community, this directly affects conference organization models, venue selection, hybrid participation design, and whether the format is fair and effective for early-career researchers.

Approach

  • After ICSE 2022, the authors launched an online survey using Google Forms with 16 questions, covering participation mode, estimated carbon footprint, whether in-person benefits offset emissions, and suggestions for future conference formats.
  • The questionnaire included both quantitative and open-ended questions; carbon footprint estimates were self-reported by participants with the help of online calculators.
  • The study collected 53 responses and statistically summarized participant demographics, participation modes, and views on whether “the carbon footprint is offset by the benefits.”
  • For the open-ended responses, the authors grouped answers by theme to summarize the advantages, disadvantages, and recommendations for in-person, online, hybrid, and hub conference models.

Results

  • Sample size and response rate: ICSE 2022 had 2025 total attendees, and the survey received 53 responses, for a response rate of only 2.6%; the authors explicitly note that this limits the reliability of the statistical conclusions.
  • Participant profile: 42/53 were from academia, 1/53 from industry, and 10/53 identified with both categories.
  • Participation mode: Most respondents attended online only, specifically 26 people; the text also notes that only 8 people were “in-person only or did not attend at all.”
  • Carbon footprint estimate: Participants’ average travel-related emissions associated with the conference were approximately 2–5 tons of CO2.
  • Whether benefits offset emissions: Of the 42 people who answered this question, 15 believed that the benefits of in-person attendance could offset the carbon footprint, 8 believed they could not, and 29 were unsure; the abstract also emphasizes that 8/42 held a negative view.
  • Strongest concrete conclusion (non-algorithmic metric): Most respondents believed that the main value of in-person conferences lies in social interaction and career development, especially for early-career researchers; at the same time, the hybrid model was supported by most respondents, and the hub model was proposed as a key future approach for reducing long-distance travel emissions while preserving in-person interaction.
