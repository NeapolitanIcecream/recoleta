This is not a research paper, but a profile piece reviewing how Annette Obrestad won the inaugural WSOP Europe Main Event in 2007 and became the youngest WSOP bracelet winner at the time. The article focuses on her poker talent, aggressive style, and her early rise to fame from online to live play.

Problem

  • It mainly answers: why Annette Obrestad matters, and how she shocked the poker world at a young age.
  • Its significance is more as a sports/poker historical narrative than as methodological innovation or scientific problem-solving.

Approach

  • The article uses a biographical narrative to trace her path from the online player “Annette_15” to WSOPE champion.
  • The core mechanism is not a research method, but a description of how she relied on reading opponents, betting as information signaling, and aggressive strategy to compensate for or even diminish the importance of hole-card information.
  • The article uses a landmark episode to illustrate her poker philosophy: in a $4 buy-in, 180-player Sit & Go, she covered her hole cards with a sticky note, played the entire tournament without looking at them, and ultimately won.
  • The report further links her extensive online volume to her success in live tournaments, emphasizing her ability to judge situations and opponents’ passivity.

Results

  • In 2007, she won the inaugural WSOP Europe Main Event, becoming the youngest WSOP bracelet winner at the time one day before her 19th birthday.
  • The first-place prize in that event was £1,000,000; the article describes it as the most important victory of her career.
  • Before that, she had won a $4 buy-in, 180-player online Sit & Go without looking at her hole cards for the entire tournament, a famous example of her view that “hand strength matters less than information warfare.”
  • The article says she never made a deposit, started through freerolls, and built her bankroll into a high six-figure sum, but does not provide a more precise number.
  • It also mentions that she finished second at EPT Dublin for $429,181, and won the EPT London £5K heads-up event in 2010.
