Summary

This report reveals that France’s vehicle registration system (SIV) was massively abused after partial privatization, resulting in about one million illegally registered vehicles being allowed onto the roads. The problem has not only caused huge losses in taxes and fines, but has also undermined road safety and fueled organized crime.

Problem

  • The problem to be addressed is that France’s vehicle registration system has serious identity and qualification verification loopholes, allowing “ghost dealers” to falsify registration records, making vehicles and owners difficult to trace afterward.
  • This matters because it directly creates road safety risks, allowing unsafe cars and trucks to appear legally registered on the road, while also facilitating car theft, tax evasion, fine evasion, and criminal transport activity.
  • The state audit authority said that in 2022-2024 alone, losses from uncollected registration fees, speeding fines, and parking fines reached €550 million, showing the enormous scale of the problem.

Approach

  • This is not a research paper, but a news investigation / audit summary; its core “mechanism” is explaining how the fraud occurred: starting in 2017, the government opened part of the registration authority to car dealers in an attempt to improve efficiency.
  • The system relied excessively on a “good faith assumption,” allowing dealers direct access to the national vehicle registry (SIV) and enabling them to issue documents for clients.
  • Fraudsters only needed to set up shell companies and pose as dealerships to usually obtain system access smoothly, after which they could tamper with registrations, hide previous owners, alter vehicle attributes, or evade taxes and fees.
  • Citing the Cour des Comptes, police, and media investigations, the report summarizes about 30 types of related fraud and notes that nearly 30,000 dealers authorized to access the system lacked adequate scrutiny.

Results

  • The audit authority says that about 1 million vehicles were illegally registered by nearly 300 “fictitious companies,” operating “totally free of state control.”
  • In 2022-2024 alone, fiscal losses were about €550 million, including uncollected registration fees as well as speeding and parking fines.
  • The fraud covered about 30 forms, including evading environmental taxes on highly polluting vehicles, tampering with inspection results, and erasing the identities of previous owners.
  • Media reports said police noticed a 160% rise in “very high-speed” violations between 2016-2022, and subsequent investigation found that many of those vehicles had fraudulent registrations.
  • The government has begun remedial action: the Interior Ministry said last year’s action plan increased fraud detection and significantly reduced SIV access authorizations, though the article did not provide more detailed quantified results.
  • The article does not propose a new academic model or experimental findings; the strongest specific claims come from the scale figures in the national audit, police leads, and media investigations.
