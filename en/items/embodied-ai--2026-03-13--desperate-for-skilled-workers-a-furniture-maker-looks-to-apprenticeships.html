Summary

This article discusses how, against the backdrop of a skilled-worker shortage in U.S. manufacturing, companies and the government are treating "registered apprenticeships" as a core tool for supplementing labor and developing highly skilled employees. The case focuses on Arkansas furniture manufacturer Virco and the Trump administration's policy goal of expanding the number of apprentices to 1 million.

Problem

  • Manufacturing faces a significant skills gap, especially in roles involving high-precision machining, equipment maintenance, and robot operation, while a wave of retirements is further accelerating the loss of experience.
  • Many companies struggle to directly hire qualified workers; new technologies and automation have raised skill requirements, making it less feasible to place workers directly into "low-skill positions."
  • Although apprenticeships are seen as an effective solution, registered apprenticeship programs in the United States have long been constrained by complex administrative processes, insufficient funding, and weak policy execution.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is "earn while you learn": employees receive mentoring and formal on-the-job training at a company while also attending classes at a college, and their training hours are paid as usual.
  • The article uses Virco as an example: the company selects employees from internal roles for a three-year tool-and-die apprenticeship program, using mentorship and joint company-college instruction to upgrade existing workers into critical technical talent.
  • On the policy side, the Trump administration proposed increasing the number of active apprentices from about 700,000 to 1 million and established the American Manufacturing Apprenticeship Incentive Fund.
  • Arkansas is responsible for managing this nationwide manufacturing incentive fund and provides companies with a $3,500 incentive for each newly registered apprentice who passes a 90-day review.
  • Intermediary organizations such as Apprenticely help companies handle the paperwork, compliance, and program design required for registered apprenticeships, lowering the barrier to adoption.

Results

  • The current total number of active apprentices in the United States is about 700,000, and Trump's executive-order target is to raise it to 1,000,000.
  • The number of registered apprentices in the United States has grown by nearly 80% over the past decade (citing U.S. Department of Labor data in the article).
  • A 2022 study commissioned by the Department of Labor said apprenticeships deliver a 144% ROI, mainly from productivity gains and lower employee turnover.
  • In December 2025, the Trump administration announced its first major investment: a $35.8 million manufacturing apprenticeship incentive fund; in addition, the Department of Labor is also advancing $145 million in competitive grants.
  • The incentive standard for an individual company is $3,500 for each additional registered apprentice; the report also explicitly notes that some in the industry believe this amount is "far from enough."
  • Virco currently has 2 tool-and-die apprentices and plans to expand to 20 apprenticeship positions; company management says it has already lost a cumulative 250 years of employee experience since the beginning of the year.
