Summary

This is not a technical research paper, but a reflective essay on the coexistence of the AI boom, social inequality, and governance failure. Through a trip to San Francisco, the author argues that technological progress is moving quickly, but truly important human problems are often not prioritized.

Problem

  • The article discusses this question: as the current AI and startup ecosystem advances at high speed, are resources being concentrated on problems that are "easy to model and easy to productize," while more fundamental real-world issues such as governance, infrastructure, poverty, pollution, and war are being neglected?
  • This matters because, in the author's view, technological systems are stacking up and reinforcing themselves layer by layer, while many people still lack the most basic public services and means of survival, creating a simultaneous sense of "futurity" and "disorder."
  • The article also points to a mismatch of values: society, capital, and engineers' attention is more easily drawn to AI agents, payment protocols, advertising, and efficiency tools than to the hardest but most critical public problems.

Approach

  • The core "method" is not an experiment or model, but a sociotechnical commentary based on firsthand observation: the author uses what they saw on a trip to San Francisco/the Bay Area as a sample, linking together scenes of robot coffee, AI ads, a YC hackathon, homeless people, whale watching, and international conflict.
  • The author develops the argument through contrast: contrasting San Francisco's AI boom with disorder on the streets, contrasting the United States and India in terms of technological capability and governance systems, and contrasting people's immediate sympathy for whales and balloons with their neglect of long-term suffering such as air pollution and poverty.
  • The author uses the metaphor of layered stacking to explain the current technological path: from payments to digital payments, to banking apps, to AI agents, and then to agent payment protocols, arguing that many new layers solve new problems created by the previous layer, while original human needs become obscured.
  • The essay also cites Peter Singer's ethical framework and the UN SDGs as criteria for judging where individuals and society "ought to direct resources."

Results

  • No quantitative results are provided for experiments, models, datasets, or benchmarks; the article does not claim any breakthrough in technical performance.
  • The most specific numbers given are mainly narrative facts rather than research metrics: the author stayed in the Bay Area for 10 days; the YC hackathon lasted 8 hours; about 40 people joined the whale-watching trip, at roughly $80–100 per person; India still has 1.4 billion adults without bank accounts; the UN's 17 SDGs were set in 2015 with a target of 2030, and the author believes completion remains far off.
  • The strongest central claim is: AI will not automatically fix problems of governance, corruption, infrastructure, and social distribution, and the technology industry's attention may in fact be drifting away from these high-value but difficult issues.
  • Another important conclusion is that the author believes the AI wave will indeed reshape the world, but inequality, war, pollution, and institutional failure will not pause because of AI progress; instead, they make choices about priorities even more urgent.
