Summary

This is not a research paper, but rather a discussion post about “how to build a human-first online community in the LLM age.” The core concern is: without giving up anonymity, how can communities increase confidence that participants are real humans while reducing the erosion caused by LLM-generated content and scraping?

Problem

  • The question raised in the discussion is: how can online communities continue to build trust between people in an era of mass LLM-generated content and content scraping? This matters for community quality, authenticity, and a long-term sense of belonging.
  • The author argues the issue is important because LLMs bring more than traditional spam; they are polluting the online commons at scale, weakening users’ confidence that “the person on the other end is real.”
  • There is also a conflict between goals: the community wants to be resistant to LLM infiltration, without fully sacrificing anonymous/pseudonymous interaction, and ideally also be resistant to scraping.

Approach

  • The text does not propose a fully formed technical method or experimental plan; instead, it solicits ideas in the form of an open question.
  • It clearly lays out a wishlist-style set of design goals:
    • Users should be able to feel relatively confident that the people they interact with are real humans, not LLMs or bots.
    • Some form of identity authority may exist, while users can still remain pseudonymous or partially anonymous in their interactions.
    • The system should reduce, as much as possible, the risk of public content being scraped and repurposed at scale by LLMs.
  • The post also suggests that future community governance may require stricter protective mechanisms than an “honor system”, rather than relying only on voluntary good behavior.

Results

  • No quantitative experimental results, datasets, baselines, or metric comparisons are provided; accordingly, there are no research performance numbers to report.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that the author believes LLM content generation and scraping are already undermining trust in online public spaces at scale, but this is a statement of opinion rather than an empirical finding.
  • The text argues that existing community governance approaches based on self-restraint may be insufficient in the future, and that stronger mechanisms for distinguishing humans from machines and protecting content may be needed, but it does not provide an implementation method or validating evidence.
