Summary

This is not a technical research paper, but a reflective essay on San Francisco, the AI boom, social inequality, and the misalignment of human priorities. Drawing on observations from travel, the author argues that while AI is advancing rapidly, many more basic and important systemic problems remain unresolved.

Problem

  • The article examines whether the allocation of attention and resources between social needs and technological innovation is seriously misaligned, especially in the AI startup boom: are people prioritizing problems that are "modelable" and productizable while neglecting more critical but harder-to-handle issues such as governance, infrastructure, poverty, pollution, and war.
  • This matters because the author believes that AI and the startup ecosystem are accelerating the shaping of the future, but the problems that truly affect the quality of life for hundreds of millions of people will not disappear automatically because of technological progress, and may instead be further marginalized as attention shifts away from them.
  • The piece also implies a deeper question: how should individuals, companies, and society rank values between "building the future" and "responding to present suffering."

Approach

  • The core method of the essay is not experiments or models, but first-person observation plus contrastive argumentation: by juxtaposing what the author sees and hears in San Francisco (robot coffee, AI advertisements, a YC hackathon, homeless people on the street) with real-life experiences in India and Canada, the essay highlights the contradiction between technological prosperity and social imbalance.
  • The author uses one simple thread throughout: the more a problem can be engineered, the more likely technologists are to choose it; the more complex a human systems problem is, the more likely it is to be set aside.
  • To support this view, the essay links multiple examples: the proliferation of AI startup ads, layers of technical stacking in payments and AI agents, the rise of street economies and content creation in India, air pollution, failures of urban governance, and differences in how compassion is distributed between ocean conservation and global poverty.
  • The essay also draws on Peter Singer's ethical framework and the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals to argue that it is not that we lack a list of problems to solve, but that we have not placed the most important problems at the center of resource allocation.

Results

  • This article does not provide formal experiments, datasets, models, or reproducible experimental results, so there are no traditional quantitative research metrics to report.
  • The most concrete numerical fact in the piece is mainly a contextual example rather than a research result: 1.4 billion adults still do not have bank accounts, used to criticize technological stacking that is detached from real needs.
  • The author mentions the United Nations' 17 Sustainable Development Goals, established in 2015 with a target year of 2030, and says that "we are still far from achieving them," as an intuitive judgment of insufficient global progress.
  • Specific numerical details from the trip include: a 10-day stay in the Bay Area, an 8-hour YC hackathon, a whale-watching trip with about 40 participants, and individual ticket prices of about $80–100; these numbers strengthen the essay's sense of reality, but they are not research evidence.
  • The strongest core conclusion is that the AI boom may be accelerating the "solving of problems created by the previous layer of technology," without correspondingly improving human society's capacity to respond to basic governance, fairness, and survival issues.
