Summary

This article reports that Chinese toy company Pop Mart has sued Bambu Lab and its MakerWorld platform over pirated 3D Labubu models uploaded by users, seeking to hold the platform legally responsible for infringing content posted by its users. This is not a research paper, but an industry/legal news report whose core significance is that it could change the boundaries of compliance responsibility for user-generated content on 3D printing platforms.

Problem

  • The problem to be addressed is: when users upload infringing models to a 3D printing file platform, should the platform also bear copyright/intellectual property liability.
  • This matters because if the court supports the plaintiff, 3D model-sharing platforms, generative design communities, and creator ecosystems could all face stricter review, takedown, and compliance obligations.
  • The article also exposes another practical problem: large-scale automated takedowns may mistakenly remove many unrelated models, affecting legitimate creators.

Approach

  • The "mechanism" described in the article is not a technical method, but a legal and platform-governance action: Pop Mart is suing Bambu Lab and its MakerWorld platform, even though the models at issue were not officially created or uploaded by the platform.
  • Bambu Lab has taken platform-governance measures, removing all Labubu imitation-related models and using automation to handle potentially infringing content.
  • Bambu Lab has also launched the Creator Copyright Protection Service, allowing MakerWorld Exclusive Designers to report their works when they are illegally uploaded to other platforms or marketplaces.
  • The article uses historical cases for comparison: in the past, actions were more often DMCA/takedown requests against individual infringing models, whereas this time it is emphasized as the first time a 3D printing platform itself has been named as a defendant.

Results

  • The article does not provide research experiments, benchmark tests, or reproducible quantitative performance results.
  • The clearest event-level result given is: the trial date is set for April 2, and the case will be heard in a Chinese court.
  • The article claims this is the "first time" a 3D printing platform has been dragged into court as a defendant; compared with past takedowns aimed only at individual models, the scope of liability being pursued is broader.
  • Bambu Lab has already removed all Labubu imitation toy, keychain, fidget, and cookie-cutter models, but during the automated takedown process, "dozens" of unrelated files were also mistakenly deleted, after which "most" were restored; the article does not provide exact numbers.
  • The article observes that on several Chinese 3D model sites, Labubu/Lafufu-related content has become difficult to find, while these models can still be found on Western platforms (such as Printables, Thangs, Thingiverse, MyMiniFactory, Cults); this is a qualitative comparison, not a systematic measurement.
