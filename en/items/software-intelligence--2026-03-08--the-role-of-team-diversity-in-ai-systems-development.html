Summary

This is a qualitative study based on real-world enterprise projects that examines how team diversity influences bias identification and fairness practices in AI system development. The paper's core contribution is the identification of six roles that team diversity plays in AI development, and based on these, it emphasizes that social factors are not merely a supplement to technical issues, but a key prerequisite for fair AI.

Problem

  • The paper addresses the question: how team diversity in AI system development helps identify and mitigate bias, and why relying only on technical means such as data, models, and algorithms is insufficient to guarantee fairness.
  • This matters because AI has already been used in high-impact settings such as education, energy, accessibility, and facial recognition; if bias goes undetected, it may amplify discrimination, undermine reliability, and cause legal and social harm.
  • Existing software engineering research places more emphasis on technical fixes, while lacking empirical research on how the social structure, cognitive differences, and collaboration mechanisms of development teams shape AI outcomes.

Approach

  • The authors use Grounded Theory, inductively deriving concepts from frontline practitioners' experiences rather than imposing a theoretical framework in advance.
  • The data come from 4 AI projects within a large software company operating in Brazil and Portugal, covering areas such as sign language translation, digital twins/predictive models, LLMs in educational settings, and facial recognition, while collaborating with global clients.
  • The study conducted 25 interviews over 3 iterative rounds; 3 open-ended questionnaires additionally supplemented the perspectives of key participants. Interviews lasted 23–42 minutes, totaling 4 hours and 49 minutes of audio and producing 504 pages of transcripts.
  • The research analyzed the material through line-by-line coding, focused coding, and theoretical coding, and reached theoretical saturation in the third round. It ultimately identified six roles of team diversity: diversifying perspectives for bias identification, bringing empathy to AI development, addressing systemic discrimination, supporting inclusive and participatory decision-making, serving as a safeguard against bias, and broadening approaches to problem-solving.

Results

  • The paper's main "results" are qualitative findings rather than benchmark improvements; it does not report quantitative performance metrics such as model accuracy, F1, or AUC, nor does it provide numerical comparisons against technical baselines.
  • The strongest empirical basis comes from its study sample: 4 AI teams/projects, 25 software professionals, 3 rounds of data collection, 3 open-ended questionnaires, 504 pages of transcripts, and 4 hours 49 minutes of recordings.
  • The key contribution claimed by the paper is the identification of 6 key roles of team diversity in AI development, showing that more diverse teams are better able to challenge default assumptions, expose hidden issues in data and models, and incorporate fairness/inclusion into decision-making.
  • The authors further argue that diversity not only improves the ability to detect bias, but also helps AI projects better align with social goals such as fairness, equality, and inclusion, thereby reducing the risk of unjust outcomes; however, these claims are mainly based on interview-based synthesis and are not validated through controlled experiments or quantitative causal analysis.
