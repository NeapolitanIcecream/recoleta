Summary

This article reviews the development of Schrödinger's cat paradox from a thought experiment to macroscopic quantum superposition experiments. Its central theme is how large a scale quantum superposition can extend to and how decoherence limits that extension. The article emphasizes that modern experiments have pushed the “cat” from electrons to visible micromechanical objects and nanoclusters containing thousands of atoms.

Problem

  • It discusses the question of whether quantum superposition applies only to microscopic particles, or whether it can also extend to macroscopic objects visible to the naked eye or approaching biological scales.
  • This matters because it touches on the boundary between quantum mechanics and the classical world, directly relating to the Copenhagen interpretation, the measurement problem, and the mechanism of decoherence.
  • If larger quantum superpositions can exist stably, that would also affect the feasibility of quantum information technologies such as quantum computing and quantum communication.

Approach

  • Rather than proposing a new algorithm or theory, the article uses a historical review to explain the physical meaning of Schrödinger's cat paradox: amplifying atomic-scale uncertainty to a macroscopic object to test whether “being in two states at once” is absurd.
  • Put as simply as possible, the core mechanism is to let an object simultaneously take two paths or occupy two vibrational/positional states, and then observe whether interference appears; if interference is present, that indicates the superposition still exists.
  • The article uses decoherence to explain the quantum-to-classical transition: the larger the system and the more channels it has to interact with the environment, the faster the superposition is “washed out” by the environment, making it appear as the classical “either-or.”
  • The article organizes the evidence by increasing experimental scale: single-electron double-slit, ion spatial superposition, C60 molecular interference, 16-microgram mechanical resonator superposition, and matter-wave interference of nanoclusters containing about 7000 sodium atoms.

Results

  • In 1974, the single-electron double-slit experiment showed that sending “one electron at a time” through a double slit still produced interference fringes, proving that a single electron can exist in a superposition of spatial paths.
  • In 1996, the JILA team used a trapped beryllium ion to realize a Schrödinger cat state, coupling the internal electronic state to external motional states and obtaining a spatially separated superposition.
  • In 1999, the Vienna team demonstrated C60 molecular interference; each molecule contains 60 atoms, marking the expansion of the “cat” from fundamental particles to complex molecules.
  • In 2023, ETH Zurich placed a 16-microgram mechanical resonator into a spatial superposition of two mechanical vibrational states. The sample contained about 10^17 atoms, with dimensions of roughly 1 millimeter in diameter and 100 microns thick, and a superposition decoherence time of about 40 microseconds; this is the most striking naked-eye-visible-scale result highlighted in the article.
  • In 2026, a matter-wave interferometer measured interference from metal clusters composed of about 7000 sodium atoms, with cluster sizes around 10 nanometers; the article says that by some “macroscopicity” metrics, this may have been the largest quantum cat at the time.
  • The article does not provide a unified benchmark table or standardized SOTA comparison, but its strongest conclusion is that quantum superposition has expanded from electrons to visible mechanical devices and large nanoclusters, and that the experimental frontier is still advancing, possibly even toward biological macromolecules or bacterial scales.
