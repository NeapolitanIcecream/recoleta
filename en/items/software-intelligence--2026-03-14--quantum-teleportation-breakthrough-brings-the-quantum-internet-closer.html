Summary

This work achieved quantum teleportation for the first time between two different, physically separated quantum dots, transferring the polarization state of a single photon produced by one quantum dot to a correlated system associated with another quantum dot. It is regarded as an important milestone toward scalable quantum relays and the future quantum internet.

Problem

  • The goal was to solve the problem that quantum teleportation/quantum relays could not be reliably realized between independent quantum emitters, which is crucial for building large-scale quantum communication networks.
  • Previous related experiments mostly relied on photons produced by the same emitter; this limited true networked interconnection between different nodes and different devices.
  • This problem matters because quantum entanglement and quantum state transfer are foundational capabilities for secure communication, quantum computing, and distributed quantum networks.

Approach

  • The research team used two different semiconductor quantum dots as independent quantum emitters, rather than taking photons from the same source.
  • They connected the two systems through a 270-meter free-space optical link and performed a quantum teleportation protocol between the separated locations.
  • The core mechanism can be understood simply as follows: by using entanglement and joint measurement, the polarization information of one photon is “transferred” to a correlated quantum system at the other end, rather than physically transporting the photon itself.
  • To enable long-distance, heterogeneous devices to work together, the experiment combined GPS-assisted synchronization, ultrafast single-photon detectors, and stabilization systems used to suppress atmospheric turbulence disturbances.
  • Success also depended on precise cross-institutional capabilities, including the joint optimization of high-precision quantum dot fabrication, nanofabricated resonators, and optical quantum experimental platforms.

Results

  • It reported for the first time successful quantum teleportation between two different quantum dots, which is the most central breakthrough result in the article.
  • The experiment was completed over a 270-meter free-space link, showing that the scheme is not just an in-device demonstration but a validation in a realistic scenario for interconnecting network nodes.
  • The teleportation state fidelity reached up to 82 ± 1%.
  • This fidelity exceeded the classical limit by more than 10 standard deviations, indicating that the result cannot be explained by classical simulation and that the quantum teleportation process is statistically significant.
  • Compared with previous approaches that mainly relied on the same emitter, this work advances the capability to the level of independent emitters/independent nodes, providing direct evidence for scalable quantum relays.
  • The paper also proposes that the next step is to realize entanglement swapping between two quantum dots in order to build the first quantum relay based on two deterministic entangled photon sources; the current excerpt does not provide quantitative results for that next stage.
