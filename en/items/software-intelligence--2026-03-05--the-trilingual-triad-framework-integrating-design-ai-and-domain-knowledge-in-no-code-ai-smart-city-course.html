Summary

This paper proposes the “Trilingual Triad” framework, explaining how students transform generative AI from a tool into a “teammate” in a no-code smart city course, and learn through the synergy of design, AI, and domain knowledge. Based on three student-built GPT cases, the paper emphasizes that this constructionist process can enhance AI literacy, metacognition, and learner agency.

Problem

  • In higher education, students often treat generative AI as a passive output tool rather than an actively designed and collaborative knowledge partner.
  • Merely teaching students to “use AI” is insufficient to cultivate AI literacy, critical understanding, transparency, and learner agency, which is increasingly important as AI enters education and professional practice.
  • There is a lack of a replicable and theoretically grounded teaching framework explaining how students can integrate design, AI, and domain knowledge in a no-code environment to build customized GPT systems for real-world tasks.

Approach

  • Proposes the Trilingual Triad framework: treating Design, AI, Domain Knowledge as three “languages” that must be orchestrated together to support human-AI collaborative learning.
  • Uses a qualitative multi-case study to analyze three student projects in a graduate course at SUTD: Interview Companion GPT, Urban Observer GPT, and Buddy Buddy.
  • Uses the “Tool-to-Teammate” teaching strategy as the main thread, comparing for each case the “Before” state where AI serves as a general tool and the “After” state where it serves as a customized teammate.
  • Uses four theoretical explanatory mechanisms: SDT explains motivation and agency, Constructionism explains “learning by building,” TPACK/AI-TPACK explains knowledge integration, and Distributed Cognition explains why AI is experienced as a cognitive partner.
  • The core mechanism, in the simplest terms, is: students first “teach” their domain knowledge to GPT, then package it into a usable learning interface through interaction design, so AI can help with thinking in specific tasks rather than directly replacing humans.

Results

  • The paper does not provide standardized quantitative metrics, statistically significant results, or numerical comparisons against strong baselines; its conclusions mainly come from qualitative analysis of three cases.
  • Across the three cases, the authors claim to observe the same pattern: domain knowledge determines AI logic, design is responsible for translating AI capabilities into learnable interactions, and AI extends students’ cognitive and reflective capacities.
  • Interview Companion GPT was deployed in a real project: for interview practice in collaboration between SUTD students and the National Archives of Singapore oral history initiative; the authors say it helped identify missed follow-up questions, maintain conversational fluency, and serve as a real-time reflective partner, but no accuracy, learning gain, or efficiency improvement figures are reported.
  • Urban Observer GPT is described as transforming urban observation from unstructured notes into structured “Observation Matrix”-style observation, combining computer vision with Socratic questioning to scaffold learning; however, no task completion rates, observation quality improvement magnitude, or user study figures are provided.
  • Buddy Buddy is described as supporting personalized pre-class preparation in a flipped classroom and generating an “Educator Report” to help teachers adjust classroom strategies; the authors claim it goes beyond traditional RAG and emphasizes the value of “interaction-intensive” dialogue, but no quantified improvements in student performance, engagement, or teacher efficiency are provided.
  • The paper’s strongest concrete claim is that by building no-code GPTs for real domain tasks, students can shift from passive AI users to active AI designers, while strengthening AI literacy, metacognition, agency in the process; these claims are currently supported mainly by case narratives and cross-case synthesis.
