Summary

This is not a research paper, but an encyclopedia entry about the "Diagram Prize" (the award for the oddest book title), introducing the prize's origins, rules, historical changes, and controversies. Its core subject is an annual humorous literary award focused on "unusual titles," rather than any technical method or experimental result.

Problem

  • It does not solve a scientific or engineering problem; the entry mainly records an annual humorous prize that began in 1978 and how it operates.
  • Its social significance lies in providing entertainment and media talking points for book fairs and the publishing industry, while also giving winning books additional exposure.
  • From an information perspective, it also reflects the interaction and controversy among public voting, title aesthetics, and publishing marketing.

Approach

  • The mechanism is very simple: each year, candidate titles are selected from English-language book titles that are considered "sufficiently unusual/odd," and then a winner is chosen.
  • The prize was originally decided by a judging panel, but since 2000 it has been decided by a public vote on The Bookseller website; Horace Bent organizes it and produces the shortlist.
  • The eligibility rules include: you may not nominate your own book, you may not nominate a book you published, and titles written to be intentionally funny are usually rejected.
  • The rules also require that the title must be in English, though the book's main text does not have to be; nominations via Twitter have been accepted since 2009, and self-published works have been eligible since 2014.

Results

  • The entry provides no scientific experiments, models, datasets, or baseline comparisons, so there are no quantitative research metrics to report.
  • One of the clearest quantitative facts is that 90 nominations were received in 2009, 50 of them from Twitter, compared with 32 the previous year, representing nearly 3x growth.
  • In terms of public participation, the 2008 Diagram Prize received 8,500 votes, higher than the 7,800 votes for the Best of Booker.
  • The prize was not awarded twice (1987 and 1991) because the organizers believed no title that year was odd enough to deserve it.
  • In 2008, a related book was published to mark the prize's 30th anniversary; there were also two special commemorative selections called "Diagram of Diagrams," corresponding to the 15th anniversary (1993) and the 30th anniversary (2008).
  • The most recent winner mentioned in the text is: in November 2025, The Pornographic Delicatessen: Midcentury Montréal's Erotic Art, Media, and Spaces won.
