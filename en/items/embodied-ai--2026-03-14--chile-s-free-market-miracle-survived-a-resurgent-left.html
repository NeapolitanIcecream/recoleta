Chile's Free Market Miracle Survived a Resurgent Left

Summary

This article reviews how Chile’s free-market economic framework managed to endure through a left-wing backlash, social protests, and two constitutional rewrite attempts, while also arguing that its survival does not mean political risks have disappeared. The author contends that what truly sustained this model was not ideological slogans, but its long-run growth performance and the institutional resilience built during the democratic era.

Problem

  • The article examines this question: Why did Chile, despite a strong anti-“neoliberal” wave, left-wing rule, and pressure for constitutional reconstruction, still refrain from fully abandoning its free-market system?
  • This matters because Chile has long been seen as Latin America’s most successful market-oriented case, and the fate of its institutions bears on how to balance “growth, distribution, fairness, and legitimacy.”
  • The article also warns that if free markets again become associated with authoritarian nostalgia and nationalist right-wing politics, the future could see a repeat of the political backlash that enabled the left’s rise.

Approach

  • The article uses a historical-political-economy narrative to trace the institutional evolution from the crisis under Allende, to market reforms under Pinochet, to the continuation of market policies by democratic governments after 1990.
  • The core mechanism is simple: although the public is dissatisfied with inequality, inadequate pensions, and corruption, the free-market framework has become embedded in institutions because it delivered long-term growth, poverty reduction, and relative stability, making it difficult to dismantle quickly.
  • The author links the 2019 protests, Boric’s rise to power, the failure of two constitutional processes, and pension reform to show that while the left advanced an agenda of expanding government and social rights, radical restructuring failed to win sustained majority support.
  • The article further argues that the key to the Chilean model’s “survival” lies not only in its economic returns, but also in the fact that voters in the democratic era repeatedly rejected a complete rewriting of the existing order through constitutional revolution.

Results

  • The article says that Chile’s poverty rate “nearly halved” between 1990 and 1998, while the extreme poverty rate fell from about 13% (1990) to about 6%, and that by the early 2000s Chile had become one of the wealthiest countries in Latin America.
  • On pensions, the initial social expectation was a retirement replacement rate of about 70% of wages, but the author says the actual replacement rate for the median worker was below 35%, and for low-contribution, intermittently employed workers it could be as low as 4%; the average pension was about 25% of pre-retirement wages.
  • The article offers a structural explanation: Chile’s mandatory savings rate is only 10%, below the OECD average of 19%; at the same time, life expectancy increased by 11 years from 1981 to 2021, while the retirement age was not adjusted in parallel, diluting pensions.
  • On constitutional politics, the author notes that 78% of voters once supported launching a constitutional convention, but the texts produced in the subsequent two rounds were both rejected in referendums, showing that most people rejected radical transformation of the social order.
  • Regarding the Boric government’s record, the article’s core conclusion is not a quantitative assessment of policy effectiveness, but that there was “a huge gap between promises and implementation”; the clearer institutional changes include raising the minimum wage, expanding cash transfers, and passing pension reform that introduced mandatory employer contributions and gradually increased the total contribution rate.
  • The article does not provide a systematic quantitative evaluation of Boric’s reforms or Kast’s policies; its strongest concrete claim is that Chile’s free-market framework withstood two failed constitutional processes and the shock of one explicitly anti-market left-wing government.
