Authors

Summary

This paper studies how the European public sector can systematically improve its capabilities to adopt, develop, and collaborate on open source software through Open Source Program Offices (OSPOs), and proposes six OSPO archetypes as references for organizational design. Its core value lies in organizing fragmented public-sector open source practices into reusable institutional models to support digital sovereignty, interoperability, and improvements in public service quality.

Problem

  • The public sector relies heavily on open source software, but often lacks systematic capabilities in procurement, compliance, technical capacity, organizational culture, and long-term policy support, making it difficult to use open source effectively.
  • Existing research on OSPOs mostly comes from corporate contexts and lacks design guidance tailored to the public sector, different levels of government, resource constraints, and policy goals.
  • This issue is important because open source software supports more than 90% of digital infrastructure; if the public sector lacks corresponding capabilities, this will affect digital transformation, interoperability, transparency, and digital sovereignty.

Approach

  • The authors use qualitative research: they interviewed 18 OSPO representatives, covering 16 European public-sector OSPO cases across the European Union, Norway, Liechtenstein, and Iceland.
  • Through cross-case analysis, they distilled these practices into 6 OSPO archetypes, comparing their organizational structures, scopes of responsibility, and ways of supporting open source adoption.
  • The study also used 2 follow-up focus groups to validate and supplement the findings, with participants including earlier interviewees and additional experts.
  • The core mechanism can be understood simply as: first systematically observing how multiple public-sector OSPOs operate, then abstracting shared patterns into “archetypes” to help other institutions select and design models according to their own context.

Results

  • The paper’s main contribution is the identification of 6 public-sector OSPO archetypes, along with design insights related to organizational structure, responsibilities, and policy goals.
  • The research sample includes 18 interviewees / 16 cases / 2 focus groups, which is the clearest empirical scale information provided in the text.
  • The paper claims that these archetypes can provide actionable design references for public-sector organizations, helping them configure OSPOs according to level of government, resource conditions, and policy goals.
  • The paper also offers policy recommendations, emphasizing that OSPOs can serve as strategic tools for promoting open source adoption, digital sovereignty, economic growth, interoperability, and the quality of digital services.
  • The excerpted text does not provide quantitative performance metrics, nor does it give numerical comparisons with baseline methods; its strongest specific claim is that this study is the first to derive a practice-oriented six-archetype classification and policy recommendations based on European public-sector cases.
