Summary

This article discusses how OpenAI and Anthropic are competing for distribution, implementation, and customer relationships in the enterprise AI market by allying with large consulting firms. The core signal is that leading AI labs are treating consulting firms as key channels and implementation partners for entering large enterprises.

Problem

  • The problem to solve is how to get enterprise customers to move large-model tools from pilot projects to large-scale deployment, rather than leaving them at the demo or limited experimentation stage.
  • This matters because the enterprise market is the most revenue-rich and hardest-to-crack market for AI companies, involving legacy system integration, process redesign, compliance requirements, and organizational change.
  • For OpenAI and Anthropic, whoever can achieve enterprise implementation faster is more likely to gain long-term revenue, ecosystem control, and industry-standard status.

Approach

  • The core method is to establish multi-year partnerships with large consulting firms, allowing the consultancies to serve as sales channels, implementation partners, and trusted endorsements that help enterprises adopt AI tools.
  • OpenAI is partnering with BCG, McKinsey, Accenture, and Capgemini, with consultants and OpenAI engineers working together to help customers connect AI to existing systems, redesign workflows, and deploy “AI coworkers” at scale.
  • Anthropic has taken a similar approach, partnering with Accenture and Deloitte to move customers from AI pilot to full-scale deployment, while expanding delivery capacity through certification training.
  • Put simply, AI model companies provide the underlying capabilities, while consulting firms package, integrate, and embed those capabilities into enterprises’ day-to-day operations.

Results

  • OpenAI announced multi-year partnerships with 4 major consulting firms this week: BCG, McKinsey, Accenture, Capgemini.
  • Anthropic previously partnered with Accenture to help enterprises move from AI pilots to full-scale deployment; it also partnered with Deloitte to make Claude available across its global network and trained 15,000 Deloitte employees to build AI solutions for clients in regulated industries.
  • McKinsey said that about 40% of its business is now related to analytics or AI, and it is shifting toward generative AI.
  • BCG said that about 90% of its 33,000 employees are using AI; the number of employees internally building custom GPTs is 5 times last year’s level.
  • The article does not provide model performance improvement data on standard academic benchmarks; the strongest concrete conclusion is that leading AI labs are accelerating enterprise distribution, integration, implementation, and scaled adoption through consulting partners in order to compete for the high-value enterprise market.
