Summary

This article is an opinion essay rather than a technical research paper. The author argues that what LLMs change through prompts, role settings, or PERSONALITY.md is only surface behavior; their true capabilities and "nature" can only be changed during training, post-training, or fine-tuning.

Problem

  • The article attempts to correct a common misunderstanding: treating changes in personality, role, or tone in prompts as real changes to a model's "nature" or capabilities.
  • The author believes this confusion matters because it exaggerates current AI's autonomy, morality, and job-replacement risk.
  • The piece also emphasizes that treating imitation as conscience leads people to misunderstand the model's responsibility and reliability in high-risk decisions.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is very simple: the author distinguishes between context-driven surface behavior and real parameter changes formed during training.
  • The article argues that practices like AGENTS.md, PERSONALITY.md, and role prompts only change the output style, wording, and script within the current session.
  • According to the author, a model's "nature" can only be changed through training, post-training, or fine-tuning; prompts cannot give a model real conscience, reasoning, or a stable personality.
  • Using coding agents and chat scenarios as examples, the author explains that so-called "personality" is merely a product of temporary context being passed back and forth through the data center, not a persistent internal state.

Results

  • No quantitative experimental results are provided: the article includes no datasets, metrics, baselines, ablations, or statistical comparisons.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that PERSONALITY.md, role settings, and similar prompt engineering cannot change a model's nature; they can only change session behavior for a few seconds.
  • The author explicitly claims that real changes to a model occur only during training / post-training / fine-tuning, not during ordinary context injection.
  • The article also offers a normative conclusion: current models are more like "Artificial Artificial Intelligence (AAI)"—that is, an imitation of intelligence, emotion, and morality, rather than something that truly possesses those attributes.
