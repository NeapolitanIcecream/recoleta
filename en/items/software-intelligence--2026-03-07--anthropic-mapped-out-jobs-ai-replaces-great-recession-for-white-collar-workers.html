Summary

This study uses Claude usage data from real occupational settings to measure the gap between “what AI can do” and “what AI is actually being used to do right now.” The core conclusion is: the technical exposure of white-collar work is already high, but real-world adoption still lags significantly; however, that gap may shrink in the future and affect highly educated, high-income roles.

Problem

  • The study aims to address this question: how to measure not only which job tasks AI could theoretically replace, but also how many tasks it has already actually taken on in the real labor market.
  • This matters because inferring unemployment risk from technical capability alone can overstate short-term impacts; but if real-world adoption gradually approaches the technological ceiling, white-collar roles could face significant employment pressure.
  • The paper focuses in particular on: which occupations are most exposed, whether job losses or hiring slowdowns have already appeared, and whether this shift could evolve into a “great recession for white-collar workers.”

Approach

  • It introduces a new metric, observed exposure, to compare two things: the theoretical executability of occupational tasks by LLMs, and the frequency with which those tasks are actually performed by Claude in real professional environments.
  • The data comes from Claude interaction usage in occupational/work-related settings, which is then used to estimate actual AI adoption across occupations and tasks.
  • It then compares the “red zone” of actual adoption with the “blue zone” of theoretical capability to quantify the adoption gap, i.e., the shortfall between real-world use and technical possibility.
  • The study also links exposure to demographic and labor-market variables, analyzing gender, education, income, age, and changes in hiring/unemployment.

Results

  • In computer and math occupations, LLMs can theoretically handle 94% of tasks, but in observed professional usage Claude currently covers only 33%, showing a large adoption gap.
  • In office and administrative roles, AI’s theoretical capability is about 90%, but actual adoption is only a small fraction of that; the article does not provide a more granular unified percentage.
  • Compared with the least exposed occupational group, the most exposed group is associated with a 16 percentage point higher probability of being female, 47% higher average income, and a nearly 4x greater likelihood of having a graduate degree, indicating that the risk is concentrated among highly educated, highly paid white-collar workers rather than manual labor roles.
  • 30% of workers have zero AI exposure, such as cooks, mechanics, bartenders, and dishwashers; jobs requiring physical presence are currently difficult for LLMs to directly replace.
  • In the post-ChatGPT era, AI-exposed occupations saw a 14% decline in job-finding rates / success in finding work (relative to 2022); the authors also emphasize that this result is only barely statistically significant.
  • Related external research also shows that employment among 22–25-year-olds in AI-exposed roles fell by 16%. But that study also notes that no systematic rise in unemployment has yet been observed; the “great recession for white-collar workers” remains a risk scenario rather than an established fact.
