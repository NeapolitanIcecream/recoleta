WorldVLM: Combining World Model Forecasting and Vision-Language Reasoning

Authors

Summary

WorldVLM proposes combining the high-level semantic reasoning of vision-language models with the dynamic prediction capabilities of world models for interpretable behavior planning and trajectory generation in autonomous driving. The core idea is to first let the VLM provide “why to drive this way” and “how to drive,” and then use the WM to convert that intent into a physically consistent future trajectory.

Problem

  • Existing VLMs excel at scene understanding and language reasoning, but they are not reliable enough for precise spatial relationships and trajectory prediction, and are easily affected by 2D representations, causal confusion, and distribution shift.
  • Existing World Models are good at learning environment dynamics and future evolution, but they lack high-level semantic decision-making and interpretability, making it difficult for them to directly handle behavior planning in safe driving.
  • Autonomous driving requires high-level contextual decision-making, dynamic prediction, safety, and interpretability at the same time, which is especially important in long-tail scenarios such as pedestrians, construction zones, and lane merging.

Approach

  • A hybrid framework is proposed: the VLM receives front-view images, navigation instructions, and current vehicle speed, and outputs structured JSON including justification, action, and action token; a behavior head then predicts a continuous 2D behavior vector (steering angle + speed).
  • This behavior vector is injected as a conditional input into the existing world model LAW: it is concatenated to waypoint queries, spatial features, and WM decoder inputs, guiding future latent representations and trajectory prediction.
  • Training is conducted in two stages: first, the VLM is fine-tuned to learn “explanation + action + behavior vector,” and then the WM is trained to perform trajectory prediction using conditions generated by the VLM.
  • The authors also extend nuScenes by constructing a new dataset with justification-action JSON annotations, generated through a combination of doScenes, DriveLM, and GPT-OSS-120B.
  • The paper includes multiple ablations: comparing no behavior condition, continuous motion vectors, discrete actions, whether to concatenate navigation instructions, and different token representation methods.

Results

  • On nuScenes validation, compared with the LAW baseline, WorldVLM is basically on par in open-loop trajectory error: L2@1s 0.31 vs 0.31, L2@2s 0.62 vs 0.61, L2@3s 1.03 vs 1.02; collision rates are 0.10/0.14/0.48%, while LAW has 0.10/0.14/0.44%, indicating that semantic conditioning does not clearly harm trajectory accuracy, though long-horizon collision rate is slightly higher.
  • For reasoning text generation, the fine-tuned Qwen1.5-0.5B shows significant improvement over zero-shot: BERTScore F1 0.67 vs 0.54, ROUGE-1 0.47 vs 0.09, BLEU-1 0.36 vs 0.04, BLEU-3 0.15 vs 0.03; the authors claim semantic alignment improves by about 24%.
  • In the conditional structure ablation, using the ground-truth Motion Vector greatly outperforms the baseline: Table III shows L2@3s 0.27~0.28 vs 1.02, with collision rate 0.10~0.11% vs 0.44%; the authors report about a 73% relative improvement in 3-second L2 over the baseline, showing that fine-grained continuous control signals are highly effective for the WM.
  • In the condition type ablation, on the small data split, Motion Vector (without navigation) performs best: L2 0.20/0.27/0.28, Collision 0.06/0.07/0.10%; meanwhile, the discrete Action (Nav x Speed) with navigation is better than LAW on 3-second collision rate: 0.39% vs 1.0%, suggesting that coarse-grained semantic commands combined with navigation can improve long-horizon safety.
  • The ablation on behavior head input representation shows that using the first 16 tokens of the VLM output works best, achieving MAE angle 0.0135, MAE speed 0.1788; using the last 5 tokens performs much worse (for example, MAE speed 0.5768).
  • The paper also reports system efficiency: with long reasoning text, a single VLM inference takes about 1 second, and LAW runs at about 12Hz on an RTX 4090.
