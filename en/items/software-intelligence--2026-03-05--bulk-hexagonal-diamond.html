Summary

This paper reports the experimental synthesis and confirmation of millimeter-scale, phase-pure hexagonal diamond (HD), resolving the long-standing controversy over whether it truly exists as an independent carbon phase. The authors also show that this bulk HD has slightly higher hardness than cubic diamond (CD) and high thermal stability.

Problem

  • For a long time, it has been controversial whether hexagonal diamond is a genuinely existing independent carbon phase; much of the so-called “lonsdaleite” evidence has been considered merely cubic diamond containing stacking faults/twins.
  • If phase-pure HD cannot be stably produced and unambiguously identified, its intrinsic physical properties such as hardness and thermal stability cannot be reliably measured, which also limits its potential applications.
  • This matters because diamond-like materials are candidates for advanced semiconductors and superhard materials; confirming the existence and properties of HD could reshape understanding of the graphite-to-diamond phase transition and expand the materials design space.

Approach

  • Highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) was used as the precursor and compressed along the c-axis under high temperature and high pressure, enabling a direct phase transition from graphite to bulk HD.
  • Multiple advanced characterization methods were used jointly to verify the product identity, including synchrotron XRD/Rietveld refinement, STEM/SAED, Raman, XPS, and EELS, in order to distinguish HD from CD and its stacking-fault variants.
  • By comparing experimental diffraction patterns with simulation results, the authors demonstrate that the sample is more consistent with hexagonally stacked HD rather than pseudo-signals arising from stacking faults mixed into CD.
  • The phase-transition pathway was reconstructed with theoretical simulations, explaining how HOPG transforms into HD under anisotropic pressure and high-temperature conditions.
  • The resulting bulk HD was further subjected to mechanical and thermal-stability testing to evaluate its performance advantages relative to CD.

Results

  • The central breakthrough of the paper is the first report of millimeter-scale, phase-pure bulk HD synthesis, confirmed by multiple structural lines of evidence as an independent hexagonal carbon phase rather than simply stacking-faulted/twinned CD.
  • In the experimental conditions, the supplementary materials explicitly provide a representative transformation pathway: under anisotropic pressures of 35, 35, and 45 GPa and at 1,500 K, HOPG can transform into HD.
  • Structurally, the authors identify HD as belonging to the P6_3/mmc space group and report bond angles of about 105.41(16)° and 113.21(13)°; the low-loss EELS peak is 33.75 eV, close to 33.8 eV for CD, supporting its assignment as sp3-bonded carbon.
  • Morphologically, the obtained HD is a millimeter-scale bulk material with an internal layered texture, with individual layer thicknesses of tens of nanometers; SAED shows consistent diffraction features at 11 random positions, indicating relatively high crystallographic consistency.
  • In terms of performance, the authors claim that bulk HD has hardness slightly higher than CD and high thermal stability; however, the provided excerpt does not give specific hardness values, thermal-stability temperatures, or quantitative comparison data with CD.
  • From the scientific conclusion perspective, the strongest claim is that this work resolves the long-standing controversy over whether HD exists, and provides a new foundation for understanding the graphite-to-diamond phase-transition mechanism and for potential advanced applications of HD.
