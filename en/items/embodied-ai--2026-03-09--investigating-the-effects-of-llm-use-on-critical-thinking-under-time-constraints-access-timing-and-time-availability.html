Authors

Summary

This paper studies at what point in time LLMs help or harm critical thinking, rather than simply asking whether to use an LLM or not. Through a controlled experiment, the authors find that the effect of LLMs strongly interacts with time pressure: when under time pressure, using them earlier is more beneficial, whereas when time is sufficient, thinking independently first is actually better.

Problem

  • The paper addresses the question: Do LLMs ultimately improve or weaken human critical thinking performance, and does this effect depend on time pressure and the timing of LLM access.
  • This matters because real-world reasoning and decision-making tasks often involve both deadlines and increasing integration of LLMs; if time is ignored, the pros and cons of human-AI collaboration may be evaluated incorrectly.
  • Prior research has mostly relied on self-reports or only compared “with vs. without LLM,” lacking systematic experiments on actual performance and the interaction with time conditions.

Approach

  • The authors conducted a 4×2 between-subjects experiment (n=393), jointly manipulating two time-related factors: LLM access timing (early、continuous、late、no-llm) and whether task time was sufficient (10 minutes insufficient vs 30 minutes sufficient).
  • The task used the iPAL critical thinking performance assessment: participants read a set of multi-source documents about a real-world public decision scenario and wrote a reasoned decision essay, rather than answering simple multiple-choice questions.
  • The core mechanism can be summarized simply as: when people are under time pressure or not, does thinking on their own first versus involving an LLM from the start change the final quality of reasoning.
  • The main outcome was essay performance, with additional measures of Recall, Evaluation, Comprehension, and post-task self-assessment to observe the cognitive processes that LLMs might affect.

Results

  • The paper reports a “temporal reversal effect”: under insufficient time, early/continuous LLM access improved critical-thinking essay performance; but under sufficient time, the pattern reversed, and late/no-llm performed better.
  • Under the sufficient time condition, having access to the LLM from the start (early/continuous) also harmed Recall; the authors therefore argue that early reliance on LLMs may interfere with internalizing document content.
  • Comparing different time limits within the same access mode, sufficient time significantly improved Essay and Recall for the late/no-llm groups, but provided little benefit to the early/continuous groups, suggesting that “more time” does not always stack with “earlier AI assistance.”
  • Post-task self-assessment changed little across conditions, indicating that people may not accurately perceive the true effect of LLMs on their own critical thinking.
  • In terms of quantitative information, the abstract and provided excerpt explicitly give sample size n=393, a 4×2 design, 10 minutes vs 30 minutes, and 4 access timings; however, the excerpt does not provide specific effect sizes, means, p-values, or percentage improvements relative to baseline, so more detailed numerical comparisons cannot be reported.
