Authors

Summary

This paper examines how students collaborate with generative AI in design education, and uses the SuperSkillsStack (Agency, Domain Knowledge, Imagination, Taste) to explain which “human capabilities” remain irreplaceable. The core conclusion is that AI is more like a cognitive booster that accelerates early-stage thinking, rather than a creator that replaces design judgment.

Problem

  • The paper addresses the question: in design education, how should students use generative AI without weakening key human capabilities such as observation, empathy, contextual judgment, and creative evaluation.
  • This matters because AI can already rapidly generate ideas, summarize information, and produce design sketches, but if students outsource their thinking to AI, it may undermine genuine design learning and the development of professional competence.
  • Although there is extensive discussion already, there is still a lack of empirical research on how students in real course projects actually balance AI outputs with field observation, user research, and human judgment.

Approach

  • The authors propose using the SuperSkillsStack framework as an analytical lens, focusing on four capabilities: Agency (deciding when and how to use AI), Domain Knowledge (using site and user knowledge to verify AI), Imagination (using AI to expand ideas), and Taste (judging which ideas are worth keeping).
  • Methodologically, they use qualitative thematic analysis to analyze reflective writing from 80 student teams in a human-centered design course.
  • The data were first deductively coded according to the four framework dimensions, and then inductive pattern analysis was conducted to observe how these capabilities appeared across design stages such as site observation, ideation, analysis, evaluation, and refinement.
  • Put simply, the authors are not training a new model, but examining, when students collaborate with existing AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude, etc.), which tasks are handed to AI and which judgments must be made by humans.
  • The paper further summarizes a human-AI collaboration workflow: AI first generates initial ideas/summaries → students interpret and verify them using domain knowledge → teams critically filter them → humans continue refining and finalizing.

Results

  • The dataset consists of course reflections from 80 student teams; this is the clearest sample size reported in the paper.
  • The paper states that Taste (evaluative judgment) appeared in every team reflection, i.e., a coverage rate of 100% (80/80); this indicates that nearly all teams emphasized that final selection and revision must rely on human judgment.
  • The paper states that Domain Knowledge appeared in “most” reflections, Agency also appeared “frequently,” and Imagination was mainly concentrated in early-stage brainstorming; however, the excerpt does not provide a more detailed frequency table, percentages, or statistical tests.
  • A core finding is that students mainly used AI in early stages, such as brainstorming, information synthesis, problem framing, and material organization; but for field observation, interpretation of user interviews, contextual matching, and final polishing of solutions, students tended to limit or stop using AI.
  • The paper’s strongest empirical claim is that AI outputs often “look reasonable but are detached from the specific context,” so students relied on field research and team discussion to identify errors, remove generic suggestions, rewrite text, and redraw visuals.
  • The paper does not report quantitative gains relative to baseline methods, control groups, or downstream learning outcomes; its main contribution is qualitative evidence grounded in a real teaching context, supporting the conclusion that “generative AI is primarily a cognitive accelerator rather than a substitute for human creativity.”
