Summary

This investigative report reviews how the U.S. Navy’s Littoral Combat Ship (LCS) program continued to move forward despite persistent breakdowns, runaway costs, and inadequate combat capability, driven by bureaucratic, political, and industrial interests. The core conclusion is that the LCS was not only a technical failure, but also a classic case of dysfunction in the defense procurement system.

Problem

  • The article seeks to explain: why a ship program repeatedly shown to have serious mechanical, training, and combat deficiencies was still able to retain support from Navy leadership and Congress for nearly 20 years.
  • This matters because the LCS involved public spending in the tens of billions to over one hundred billion dollars, and directly affected Navy readiness, fleet structure, and taxpayer interests.
  • More broadly, it shows how the military-industrial complex, congressional local interests, and internal military incentives can cause investment in a failed program to keep escalating, even after the ships had been shown to “lack the lethality or survivability required for high-end combat.”

Approach

  • This is not an academic paper, but an investigative reporting project; ProPublica says it reviewed thousands of pages of public records and interviewed/tracked Navy and shipbuilding industry insiders involved in different stages of construction.
  • The article uses a timeline + representative accident cases approach to connect the full history of the LCS, from concept development and procurement decisions to parallel construction of two variants, followed by multiple ship failures and early retirements.
  • Its explanation of the core mechanism is straightforward: senior leaders suppressed or ignored warnings, contractors and lawmakers pushed for continued procurement, and the larger the program became, the harder it was to stop.
  • The report highlights several institutional mistakes in particular: buying in large numbers before adequate testing; running two ship variants in parallel, which meant parts and personnel could not be shared; and combining small crews with complex mission requirements, which led to insufficient training and maintenance capacity.
  • Through breakdowns involving ships such as Freedom, Milwaukee, and Fort Worth, the article shows a cycle: deploying while compromised → breakdown and loss of operations → pressure to deploy again to restore reputation → another breakdown.

Results

  • Per-ship cost rose from the originally advertised no more than $220 million to about $500 million; the first few ships were even criticized as reaching more than $750 million per ship.
  • The article cites former GAO analyst John Pendleton’s judgment that the LCS’s full life-cycle cost may exceed $100 billion; yet what taxpayers ultimately received was fewer than 30 ships with limited survivability and single-mission capability.
  • The Navy originally planned to build more than 50 ships, later said it needed only 32, but even so, Congress still forced the purchase of 3 more.
  • The intended design life was 25 years, but some LCS ships were targeted for early retirement or mothballing before reaching 10 years of service.
  • In the 10 months beginning in December 2015, 5 LCS ships suffered breakdowns worldwide; for example, USS Milwaukee experienced a major failure about 3 weeks into its maiden voyage and was towed roughly 40 miles for repairs, USS Fort Worth was sidelined for 7 months because of operator error and mechanical problems, and after one exercise USS Freedom required 2 years and millions of dollars to repair a damaged engine.
  • Qualitatively, the Navy ultimately acknowledged that the LCS “did not have” the lethality and survivability required for high-end combat against peer adversaries such as China; this is one of the strongest concluding judgments in the piece.
