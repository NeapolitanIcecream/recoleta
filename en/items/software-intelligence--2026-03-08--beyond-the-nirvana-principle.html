Summary

This is a comparative literature/intellectual history paper that traces the genealogy of Freud’s “Nirvana Principle” in Schopenhauer and Indian philosophy, and questions the mystical-metaphysical foundations on which psychoanalysis relies while claiming to be scientific. The author’s central claim is that the so-called “Nirvana Principle” is not the direct inheritance of a single concept, but a hybrid produced through translation and reconstruction across languages and traditions.

Problem

  • The paper seeks to address the question of where exactly Freud’s “death drive/Nirvana Principle” comes from, and whether this mystically inflected origin weakens—or instead expands—the explanatory power of modern critical theory and psychoanalysis.
  • This matters because the author regards psychoanalysis as a highly influential theoretical tradition, yet its core drive theory may not rest on a rigorously scientific foundation, but rather on metaphysical and religious-philosophical borrowings.
  • The author also tries to clarify a more specific issue: “nirvana/涅槃” in Freud, Schopenhauer, and Indian philosophy are not the same thing, and cannot simply be treated as a linear transmission of ideas.

Approach

  • The paper uses genealogy and comparative textual analysis: closely reading Freud’s Beyond the Pleasure Principle, Schopenhauer’s The World as Will and Representation, and secondary/translated materials related to Indian philosophy.
  • It first reconstructs Freud’s “life drive–death drive” framework, especially why he used the name “Nirvana Principle” for an impulse tending toward tension reduction, return, and ultimately a reversion to the inorganic state.
  • It then traces Freud’s own acknowledgments of Schopenhauer’s influence, arguing that the “death drive” was not proposed out of nowhere, but is closely related to Schopenhauer’s notions of “will,” “pain/pleasure,” and the “negation of the will.”
  • It further analyzes the multiple meanings of the term “nirvana” in Schopenhauer and in Indian traditions, emphasizing that after passing through Sanskrit, Persian, French/Latin, and European Orientalist interpretation, it had already become a hybrid concept rather than a stable term.
  • On this basis, the author proposes a simplest-mechanism explanation: Freud borrowed Schopenhauer’s structure in which “experience/drive precedes reason,” and then named this dynamic of return, tension reduction, and movement toward termination the “Nirvana Principle.”

Results

  • The article does not provide experiments, datasets, or reproducible quantitative metric results; this is a humanities paper and does not report numerical performance improvements.
  • The strongest concrete conclusion is that the author believes Freud explicitly acknowledged Schopenhauer as his precursor, and that this acknowledgment appears in at least 3 pieces of textual evidence: 1917 One of the Difficulties of Psychoanalysis, 1920 Beyond the Pleasure Principle, and New Introductory Lectures about 12 years later.
  • The author claims that the “Nirvana Principle” was not inherited through a single path from ancient Indian philosophy to psychoanalysis, but was reconstructed through at least 3 layers: Schopenhauer’s philosophical rewriting, the European Orientalist translation chain, and Freud’s psychoanalytic recoding.
  • In terms of structure, the paper proposes 4 main chapters (Liberation, Nirvana, Moksha, Jouissance) plus the conclusion, Rebirth, in order to systematically compare the relationships among Freud, Schopenhauer, the Bhagavad Gita/Samkhya school, and Lacan.
  • The paper’s key breakthrough claim is not new data but an interpretive framework: one of the most central drive theories in psychoanalysis is in fact built on a “scientifically packaged mystical-metaphysical tradition”; the author argues that this is not accidental rhetoric but a theoretical foundation.
