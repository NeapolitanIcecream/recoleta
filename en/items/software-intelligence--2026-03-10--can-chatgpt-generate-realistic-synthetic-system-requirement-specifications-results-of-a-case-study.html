Can ChatGPT Generate Realistic Synthetic System Requirement Specifications? Results of a Case Study

Summary

This paper investigates whether ChatGPT can generate synthetic SyRSs that “look realistic” without access to real system requirement specifications (SyRSs) and without domain experts participating in the generation stage. The conclusion is: yes, to some extent, but relying on LLM self-evaluation alone is not reliable, and expert review is still needed.

Problem

  • Real SyRSs are important for requirements engineering research, test generation, and tool evaluation, but they are often difficult to obtain due to confidentiality, proprietary restrictions, and limited availability.
  • Using black-box LLMs to directly generate synthetic SyRSs is attractive because it does not require real data or additional training, but it faces hallucinations and overconfidence, which are especially risky in specialized domain texts.
  • The core question is: under the conditions of no real samples, no RAG, and no domain experts involved in generation, are the synthetic requirements documents generated by ChatGPT realistic enough to substitute for real SyRSs in research.

Approach

  • The authors designed an iterative generation-evaluation process: using ChatGPT-4o to generate documents from templates, then using prompts to assess “completeness” and “Degree of Realism (DoR),” while also using SBERT to measure semantic similarity among documents in the same domain to avoid exact repetition.
  • Generation covered 10 industry domains and went through 10 iterations, producing a total of 300 SSyRSs; in each round, 3 documents were generated per domain, and the results from the tenth round were selected for focused analysis and expert evaluation.
  • Prompt engineering used a simple but systematic method: zero-shot, template pattern, persona pattern, chain-of-thought; and the authors deliberately used “scenario” rather than “SyRS” to reduce the risk of hallucinations triggered by terminology.
  • Realism evaluation included not only ChatGPT self-assessment but also cross-model checks, comparing GPT-4o, GPT-5.2 (instant / thinking), and Sonnet 4.5 to test context bias and model bias.

Results

  • The final dataset contains 300 synthetic SyRSs; the tenth-round subset contains 30 in total, with 21,478 words, an average of 716 words per document, and a range of 644–811 words.
  • Completeness: the authors state that all SSyRSs passed the template completeness check, meaning the final dataset’s completeness result was all valid.
  • Diversity / similarity: SBERT semantic similarity had an overall mean of 0.66, median of 0.67, and range of 0.50–0.82; the lowest domain mean was government at 0.59, and the highest was healthcare at 0.73. Based on this, the authors considered the data “similar but not redundant.”
  • Expert evaluation: a survey was conducted on 33% of the sample, with n=87 submissions received (elsewhere the paper mentions n=83 experts); 62% of experts considered these synthetic SyRSs realistic and credible.
  • LLM realism scores vary greatly: by average model DoR, GPT-4o (same context) scored 0.90, GPT-4o (new context) 0.86, GPT-5.2 instant 0.85, GPT-5.2 thinking 0.73, and Sonnet 4.5 0.64. This shows that model choice has a much greater effect on scoring than context does.
  • Scoring instability is significant: the same healthcare SSyRS was repeatedly evaluated 10 times with Sonnet 4.5, and DoR ranged from 0.48 to 0.73, a spread of 0.25, with a mean of 0.59 and standard deviation of 0.08. Based on this, the paper explicitly states that quantitative DoR evaluation is highly unreliable; deeper inspection also found contradictory statements and deficiencies in the text.
