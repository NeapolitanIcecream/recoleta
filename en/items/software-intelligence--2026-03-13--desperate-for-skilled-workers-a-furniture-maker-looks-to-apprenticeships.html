Summary

This report discusses how, against the backdrop of skilled-labor shortages in U.S. manufacturing, companies are using registered apprenticeships to develop internal talent, and analyzes the practical challenges of the Trump administration’s goal of expanding apprenticeships to 1 million positions. The core message is: apprenticeships are seen as a viable path to easing manufacturing labor shortages, but there remains a clear gap between policy investment, execution strength, and the pace of expansion.

Problem

  • Manufacturing companies face severe shortages of skilled workers, especially for roles involving new equipment, robotics, and high-precision manufacturing, and external hiring is not sufficient to meet demand.
  • A wave of retirements and intensified regional labor competition have worsened the loss of experience; for example, since the beginning of the year Virco has lost the equivalent of 250 years of experience from departing employees.
  • The U.S. hopes to fill labor gaps in critical industries by expanding registered apprenticeships, but companies are widely concerned about complex processes, high costs, and insufficient speed of expansion, which affects talent supply for manufacturing, infrastructure, semiconductors, data centers, and other sectors.

Approach

  • Adopt a registered apprenticeship model of learning while working: apprentices receive mentorship and on-the-job training at the company while also attending school, and they are paid for both classroom and work hours.
  • Companies select promising workers from lower-skill internal positions and transition them into high-skill trades through 3-year apprenticeships, such as tool and die, equipment maintenance, and robotics repair.
  • At the policy level, federal and state funding is used to drive expansion; for example, the manufacturing apprenticeship incentive fund provides $3,500 in incentives for each newly registered apprentice.
  • Intermediary organizations such as Apprenticely help companies handle the paperwork, compliance, and program setup required for registered apprenticeships, reducing “red tape”-style administrative burdens.

Results

  • The U.S. currently has about 700,000 active apprentices, and the Trump administration aims to raise that to 1 million; over the past decade, the total number of registered apprenticeships has grown by nearly 80%.
  • A 2022 study commissioned by the U.S. Department of Labor said that apprenticeships deliver a 144% return on investment for employers, mainly from productivity gains and reduced employee turnover.
  • In December 2024, the government announced $35.8 million for the American Manufacturing Apprenticeship Incentive Fund; there is also a $145 million competitive funding program for industries including IT, AI, healthcare, shipbuilding, and defense.
  • But experts estimate that even if the full $145 million is implemented effectively, it would add only about 35,000 to 50,000 apprenticeship positions, still far short of the 1 million target.
  • Arkansas-based Apprenticely has helped create about 2,500 registered apprenticeship positions since 2019; Virco also plans to expand its current 2 apprenticeship positions to 20.
  • The article does not provide results from a strict randomized controlled trial or a unified national program evaluation; the strongest empirical claim is that at the firm level, apprenticeships can ease skills gaps, improve retention, and generate a relatively high ROI.
