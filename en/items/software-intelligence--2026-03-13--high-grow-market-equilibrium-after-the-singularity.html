Summary

This article proposes a projection of the post-singularity economy: when all capital is managed by equally powerful AI agents, market competition may not trend toward long-term decentralization, but instead converge into a small number of — or even a single — super conglomerate. The author argues that it is precisely the frictions and limitations of human organizations that currently sustain competition; once those constraints are removed, capital compounding and barriers to market entry will drive extreme concentration.

Problem

  • The article tries to answer: if nearly all investment decisions are handed over to highly capable AI agents, what will market structure ultimately evolve into, and will a long-run “winner-take-all” equilibrium emerge?
  • This question matters because it bears on post-singularity capital allocation, industrial organization, wealth inequality, and whether AI first-mover advantages will be dramatically amplified.
  • The author is especially focused on whether, even if “everyone can use the same equally strong AI,” competition would still self-eliminate because of compounding, similar strategies, and barriers to entry.

Approach

  • The core method is a conceptual economic thought experiment: first assume that everyone simultaneously gains access to equally powerful AI, and that agents optimize only for “the highest investment return.”
  • The author assumes these intelligent agents will continuously search for high-ROI opportunities, but because they are all sufficiently rational and highly capable of processing information, they will form similar judgments about high-quality opportunities, causing leaders to rapidly seize the best market positions.
  • The argument then introduces real-world barriers to entry, such as chip fabs, supply chains, and capital-intensive industries, to show that once leaders establish scale and know-how, new entrants will struggle to catch up; leaders will also deliberately price at a level that is “just enough not to attract new competitors.”
  • Unlike human companies, the author argues that AI-run organizations would not face obvious limits on management bandwidth, the innovator’s dilemma, or diseconomies of scale, so the fastest-compounding agents could keep expanding across industries into giant conglomerates.
  • In a more realistic extended setup, the author adds that differences in starting capital, deployment timing, resource control, and “who gets the strongest AI first” would further intensify path dependence, making early luck and first-mover advantage determine long-term wealth shares.

Results

  • This is not an empirical paper; the text does not provide quantitative experiments, datasets, metrics, or baseline comparison results.
  • The strongest core conclusion is that, in the limit where “all dollars are managed by highly capable AI,” markets may trend toward extreme concentration, with the “fastest-compounding agent/conglomerate” ultimately continuing to acquire or squeeze out other competitors.
  • The article explicitly argues that even if 100% of participants can use strong AI, the result may not be more equal; instead, inequality may be determined mainly by path dependence and luck in the first few minutes or on “day 1.”
  • The author also claims that if a leading lab gains capability improvements earlier than others, that first-mover advantage will continue to amplify through a feedback loop of “AI accelerating R&D for the next generation of AI,” so “who builds AI first” may matter more than initial capital.
  • In the most extreme end-state scenario, the author suggests that market equilibrium could even converge to 1 company owning nearly all high-value assets, while everyone else would hold only tiny indirect shares as shareholders.
