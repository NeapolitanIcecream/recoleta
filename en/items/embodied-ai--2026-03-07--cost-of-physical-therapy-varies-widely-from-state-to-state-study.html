Cost of physical therapy varies widely from state to state: study

Summary

This study analyzed publicly available prices for hospital outpatient physical therapy (PT) services in the United States and found clear differences in commercial negotiated rates, cash prices, and prices across regions. The authors note that this price variation may increase patients’ out-of-pocket burden, thereby affecting PT use and rehabilitation outcomes.

Problem

  • Physical therapy is widely used for acute injuries, postoperative rehabilitation, and chronic disease management, but price opacity and payment differences may cause patients to reduce or discontinue treatment.
  • This matters because PT is generally considered a component of cost-effective care; if patients forgo treatment because of cost, it may lead to worse functional recovery and greater subsequent use of health care services.
  • Previously, it was unclear how large the differences in outpatient PT prices were across hospitals, regions, and payment methods nationwide in the United States.

Approach

  • The researchers used hospital price transparency data aggregated by Turquoise Health to estimate prices for hospital outpatient PT services for commercial insurers and cash-paying patients in the United States.
  • The sample covered 1,666 independent hospitals in the United States and focused on 10 common hospital outpatient PT services, including evaluation/management and supervised exercise interventions.
  • They compared list price, cash price, and commercial negotiated price, and analyzed price variation both across hospitals and within the same hospital.
  • After adjusting for geographic differences in medical prices, the study also compared PT prices between rural and urban facilities.

Results

  • The study found substantial interstate/regional variation in outpatient PT prices in the United States, with large fluctuations in commercial insurance payment prices.
  • For common evaluation services, the median price ranged from about $151 to $215, showing clear differences across locations and institutions.
  • Price variation came more from differences between hospitals than from differences among services within the same hospital.
  • Commercial negotiated prices were usually lower than cash prices, indicating that uninsured or cash-paying patients may face a higher burden.
  • After adjusting for geographic price differences, prices in rural areas were generally lower than in urban areas.
  • The text does not provide quantitative comparisons regarding utilization, patient outcomes, or prior research/baseline models; the strongest specific conclusion is that location, insurance status, and payment method all significantly affect PT costs, which may in turn influence treatment participation and downstream health outcomes.
