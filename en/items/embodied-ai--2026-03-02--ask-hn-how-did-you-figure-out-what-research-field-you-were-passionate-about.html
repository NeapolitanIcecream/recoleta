Summary

This is not a research paper, but a request-for-advice post about how to discover the research direction one is truly passionate about. The core content is that the author, before graduating from high school, is still uncertain about their future research field and hopes to learn how others found long-term interests through courses, projects, labs, or exploratory experiences.

Problem

  • The author's core question is: how to determine which research field suits you and inspires real passion when you have not yet been exposed to enough disciplines.
  • This matters because research usually requires long-term commitment, and choosing a direction affects not only one's university learning path but also future career and research sustainability.
  • The post also explicitly asks how to distinguish between a temporary interest and a genuine research interest that can sustain years of investment.

Approach

  • The text does not propose a research method or technical mechanism; instead, it solicits experiential advice in the form of questions.
  • The background clues provided by the author include: having applied to electrical engineering, finding hardware and computing systems "interesting enough," but not yet having encountered any subject that makes them want to explore it deeply on their own.
  • The questions revolve around several possible paths of exploration: courses, papers, projects, lab experience, cross-disciplinary trial and error, random conversations, summer programs, and self-directed reading.
  • Put simply, the "mechanism" of this post is: by collecting personal paths and decision signals from people with research experience, the author hopes to probe different directions at low cost.

Results

  • There are no experiments, datasets, models, metrics, or quantitative results, because this is not a research paper.
  • The most concrete information is the author's current situation: about to graduate from high school, having applied to electrical engineering, and hoping to explore different disciplines in a low-risk way during undergraduate study.
  • The evaluation criteria explicitly raised in the post include: whether certain questions keep bothering you over the long term, whether you can tolerate the daily routine of experiments/debugging/reading, and whether the interest goes beyond short-term novelty.
  • Therefore, the main "output" of this text is a discussion framework of open questions, rather than validated research conclusions.
