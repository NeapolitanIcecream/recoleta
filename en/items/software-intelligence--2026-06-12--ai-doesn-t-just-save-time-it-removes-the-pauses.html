Summary

The piece argues that AI changes work by removing pauses, not just saving time. That matters because the missing pauses can push people to keep expanding scope and keep working on the wrong thing.

Problem

  • AI removes friction that used to force breaks in coding and other knowledge work.
  • Without those breaks, people can keep prompting, refining, and expanding tasks past the point of usefulness.
  • The article treats this as a continuity problem: work feels productive while losing the chance to stop and reassess.

Approach

  • The author uses a personal comparison with Pomodoro and older coding workflows to show how natural pauses used to appear.
  • The core mechanism is simple: AI answers immediately, so each next step feels easy and the work keeps flowing.
  • The article frames stopping as a deliberate habit, not as a productivity failure.
  • It suggests a fixed pause every 30 minutes: stop prompting, stand up, walk, and review the goal without the model.

Results

  • No quantitative experiment or benchmark is reported.
  • The main claim is qualitative: AI can turn a 20-minute task into 2 hours of continuous iteration.
  • The article claims this can lead to more code, more refactors, and more features before the original problem is understood.
  • It also claims the user may mistake that continuity for progress.
  • The proposed outcome of regular pauses is better task selection and less scope creep.
