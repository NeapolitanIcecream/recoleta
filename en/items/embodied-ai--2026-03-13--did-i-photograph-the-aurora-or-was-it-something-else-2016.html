Summary

This article is not an academic paper, but a practical identification guide for the general public to help users determine whether what they photographed is actually an aurora. Through a set of simple questions, it reduces cases where clouds, fog, and light pollution are mistaken for aurora.

Problem

  • The problem it addresses is that ordinary observers often cannot distinguish real aurora from other similar phenomena, such as cloud reflections, fog, haze, ground lights, and special clouds.
  • This matters because aurora observation is highly dependent on environmental conditions, and misjudgments can lead to incorrect sharing, incorrect reports, and misunderstandings about auroral activity levels.
  • The article is especially aimed at the UK observing context: aurora are rare, often appear low near the northern horizon, and are therefore more easily confused with light pollution.

Approach

  • It proposes a seven-step checklist based on rules of thumb rather than an algorithmic model: first check the AuroraWatch UK alert level, then whether it is dark enough, whether you can see stars, and whether you are near sources of light pollution.
  • It uses observable clues for elimination: under thick cloud, aurora usually cannot be seen; if the sky is bright but no stars are visible, it is more likely that clouds are reflecting ground lights.
  • It uses motion and color characteristics to assist judgment: aurora are commonly green with red/purple bands above, and their brightness may change slowly; orange and yellow are more likely to come from light pollution such as street lamps.
  • It uses geometric position as a final constraint: in the UK, aurora should appear in the north; if the glowing phenomenon appears in other directions, aurora can basically be ruled out.

Results

  • The article does not provide quantitative experimental results, datasets, metrics, or baseline comparisons, so there are no numerical performance gains to report.
  • The strongest concrete conclusion is the proposed 7 diagnostic questions: alert level, whether it is dark enough, whether stars are visible, whether there is light pollution, whether it is moving, whether the colors match, and whether the viewing direction is north.
  • The timing rule of thumb given is that in the UK the most common observing window is about 9 pm to 12 pm, while around dawn and dusk it is usually too bright for observation.
  • The directional rule of thumb given is that in the UK aurora should only appear in the north; if the “aurora” is seen in the south or another direction, then it is “definitely not” aurora.
  • The color rule of thumb given is that common auroral colors are green and red/purple; orange and yellow are usually not aurora, but are more likely caused by ground lighting.
