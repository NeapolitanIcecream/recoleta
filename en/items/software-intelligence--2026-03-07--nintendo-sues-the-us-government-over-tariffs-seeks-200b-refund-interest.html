Summary

This article reports that Nintendo of America has sued the U.S. government, seeking a refund of tariffs collected since 2025 under tariffs later ruled unlawful, plus interest. Its core significance is that this affects not only Nintendo’s pricing and supply chain, but also whether the president has the authority to impose large-scale tariffs without Congress.

Problem

  • The issue discussed in the article is: whether tariffs imposed under the 1977 International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) were lawful, and whether companies should receive refunds plus interest.
  • This matters because these tariffs have already affected Nintendo’s console accessory pricing, Switch 2 preorder timing, and U.S. market pricing, while also impacting more than a thousand companies.
  • More broadly, this concerns the limits of executive power, the pass-through of international trade costs, and consumers ultimately bearing higher prices.

Approach

  • On March 6, 2026, Nintendo filed suit in the U.S. Court of International Trade, seeking a refund of tariffs it paid since February 2025 under the relevant executive orders, as well as interest, attorney fees, and reprocessing of import entry records.
  • The core of its legal argument is simple: these tariffs were imposed under IEEPA, but the U.S. Supreme Court ruled on February 20 that IEEPA did not grant the president authority to impose such tariffs, so these duties were unlawfully collected.
  • Nintendo also cites the government’s statements in the earlier V.O.S. Selections v. Trump case: if the tariffs were ultimately found unlawful, the government would refund the plaintiffs and pay post-judgment interest, using this to emphasize that the government had already acknowledged a refund obligation.
  • The complaint covers 10 executive orders, involving tariffs imposed on goods from countries including Canada, Mexico, China, Brazil, and India; among them, the tariff rate on Chinese goods reportedly rose from 10% to 125%, before falling to 34% in May 2025.

Results

  • This is not a research paper, so there are no experiments, model metrics, or benchmark results; the article provides factual outcomes at the legal and business-impact level.
  • Nintendo is not seeking “$200 billion for Nintendo” itself; the article makes clear that the cumulative total collected under the relevant tariffs since February 2025 exceeds $200 billion, while Nintendo is seeking a refund only of the portion it paid, plus interest and attorney fees.
  • The Supreme Court already ruled on February 20 that IEEPA does not authorize the president to impose these tariffs, and ordered U.S. Customs and Border Protection to issue refunds; however, CBP said on the same day Nintendo filed suit that it was “not able to comply” with that order.
  • On pricing effects, Nintendo had previously delayed Switch 2 preorders from April 9 to April 24 because of the tariff assessment; accessories later became more expensive, for example Joy-Con 2 rose from $90 to $95 and the Pro Controller rose from $79.99 to $84.99.
  • In the U.S. market, affected by a 20% tariff on goods from Vietnam, Nintendo also raised prices for the original Switch lineup in August 2025: the Switch OLED increased from $349.99 to $399.99, and the standard Switch increased from $299.99 to $339.99.
  • The article also states that Nintendo is not an isolated case, but rather one of more than 1,000 companies that have sued, including FedEx, Costco, and Revlon, showing the broad impact of the case, though the execution of refunds and the risk of subsequent new tariffs remain unresolved.
