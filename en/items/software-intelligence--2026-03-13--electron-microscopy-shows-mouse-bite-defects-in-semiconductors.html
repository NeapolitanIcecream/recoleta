Summary

This work used high-resolution 3D electron imaging for the first time to directly visualize atomic-scale interfacial defects in advanced semiconductor transistors, the so-called “mouse bites.” Its significance lies in providing a previously almost irreplaceable direct observation tool for failure analysis, process debugging, and next-generation device optimization during chip development.

Problem

  • Modern transistors have shrunk to the atomic scale, with channel widths of only about 15–18 atoms, so “the position of every single atom” affects performance.
  • Existing failure analysis has long struggled to directly resolve the atomic-scale roughness and defects inside 3D transistors, relying instead on indirect inference from projection images.
  • These defects can disrupt carrier channels, slow device performance, and affect the reliability and performance of everything from phones and automobiles to AI data centers and quantum computing chips.

Approach

  • Used electron ptychography: electrons were passed through the transistor, and changes in electron scattering patterns at different scan positions were recorded.
  • Used the EMPAD pixel-array electron detector to capture scattering data with high precision, then computationally reconstructed high-fidelity 3D atomic structure images.
  • In collaboration with TSMC and ASM, imaged modern semiconductor samples and tracked and analyzed atomic positions.
  • This enabled direct measurement of transistor channel interface roughness and identification of the atomic-scale defects known as “mouse bites” formed during optimized growth processes.

Results

  • For the first time, atomic-scale defects that can damage performance were detected in computer chips; this is the paper’s central breakthrough.
  • Rough defects called “mouse bites” were directly observed at the channel interfaces of modern transistors and attributed to interfacial defects formed during optimized growth processes.
  • The device-scale information provided in the article shows that transistor channels are only about 15–18 atoms wide, indicating that the method can effectively characterize structures at this extreme scale.
  • The article does not provide standard benchmark-style quantitative metrics (such as error rate, throughput, or numerical comparisons with baseline methods); the most specific performance claim is that this detection and imaging system had previously achieved atomic imaging at the level of “the world’s highest-resolution images” and supports direct observation and debugging of structural changes after process steps.
