Summary

This paper presents the first systematic benchmark evaluation of patch overfitting detection (POD) in automated program repair under realistic usage scenarios. The core conclusion is that, under realistic patch distributions, the six existing POD methods are usually worse than a simple random selection baseline, indicating that the practical usefulness of this line of work has been overestimated.

Problem

  • The problem to solve is that patches generated by APR may pass the existing tests while still being incorrect, i.e., they are "overfitting patches"; these need to be identified automatically.
  • This matters because if APR cannot reliably distinguish correct patches from incorrect ones, it will struggle to truly reduce developers' debugging and review costs.
  • Prior POD evaluations were often conducted on unrealistic data distributions or in isolated settings, so they cannot show whether these methods are genuinely valuable in real workflows.

Approach

  • The authors construct and curate two datasets that are closer to practice, requiring that patches come from APR tools run under the same experimental environment and time budget, in order to preserve the real distribution of "correct patches:overfitting patches."
  • Based on these two datasets, they evaluate 6 representative POD tools spanning three categories: static analysis, dynamic analysis, and learning-based methods.
  • The evaluation considers not only classification metrics, but also measures from the developer's perspective, such as how many candidate patches must be inspected to find one correct patch.
  • The authors introduce and emphasize two simple baselines: random selection (RS) and a new weighted probabilistic classifier (WPC); the core idea is straightforward: whether a complex method can at least outperform "making random judgments/samples according to the true class proportions."
  • The dataset sizes include: the classical dataset with 819 patches (129 correct, 690 overfitting, 92 bugs, 10 APR tools) and the repairllama dataset with 170 patches (63 correct, 107 overfitting, 50 bugs, 1 LLM-style APR tool).

Results

  • Strongest conclusion: simple random selection outperforms all existing SOTA POD tools in 71% to 96% of cases, with the exact proportion depending on the POD tool being compared.
  • In the classical dataset, there are 819 uniquely labeled patches, including 129 correct and 690 overfitting; in the repairllama dataset, there are 170 uniquely labeled patches, including 63 correct and 107 overfitting, showing that overfitting is the clear majority in realistic scenarios.
  • The authors evaluate 6 POD tools and compare them with 2 baselines; this is claimed to be the first comprehensive practical evaluation conducted on data with realistic distributions.
  • The paper also cites prior results under realistic settings: at the 3-hour experimental point, for representative bugs, when developers inspect patches at random, they typically need to review only 2 patches to encounter a correct one with reasonable confidence (if one exists), which weakens the practical marginal benefit of complex POD tools.
  • The paper's qualitative finding is that learning-based methods are fast and good at filtering out overfitting patches; dynamic methods are better at identifying correct fixes, but are slower and less precise. However, when practical utility is considered overall, the random baseline still matches or exceeds them on most bugs.
  • The abstract and excerpt do not provide a complete table of classification metric values for each specific POD tool; the clearest quantitative headline result is the above 71%–96% advantage range for the random baseline.
