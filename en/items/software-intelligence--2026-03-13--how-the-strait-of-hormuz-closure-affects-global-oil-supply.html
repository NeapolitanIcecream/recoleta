Summary

This is a news graphic based on public energy and shipping data, explaining how a closure of the Strait of Hormuz could quickly impact global oil and gas supply, prices, and regional economies. Its core value lies in integrating transport bottlenecks, alternative pipeline capacity, refinery damage, and demand-side chain reactions into a clear picture of global energy risk.

Problem

  • The article addresses the question: if the Strait of Hormuz closes, how much global oil and LNG supply would be lost, which regions are most vulnerable, and whether alternative routes are sufficient.
  • This is important because the strait carries about one-fifth of the world’s daily oil and liquefied natural gas supply, and any disruption would directly push up energy prices, worsen inflation, and threaten transportation, industry, power generation, and agriculture.
  • It also asks a practical question: even if the conflict ends quickly, whether the recovery of oilfields, refineries, LNG facilities, and shipping would continue to weigh on supply.

Approach

  • By combining multiple types of visualized data, including shipping chokepoint flows, crude oil/LNG prices, Asian import dependence, regional trade flows, and locations of damaged infrastructure, it constructs the chain of disruption impacts.
  • Put simply, the core mechanism is: once the strait closes, ships cannot pass, oil-producing countries cannot load tankers, so they must stuff oil into storage tanks first; once storage is full, they have to cut production; after output falls, global refineries have less feedstock, prices rise, and inventories are forced to be drawn down.
  • The article further assesses alternative export capacity, such as Saudi Arabia’s East-West pipeline at up to about 5 million barrels/day and the UAE’s Habshan-Fujairah pipeline at about 1.5 million barrels/day, and points out that this capacity is not enough to fully replace shipments through the strait.
  • It also incorporates downstream constraints: refinery shutdowns/damage, slow restarts at LNG plants, lagged recovery after oilfield production cuts, and rising shipping insurance and security risks, showing that the issue is not simply one of “rerouting shipments.”

Results

  • The central quantitative conclusion is: the Strait of Hormuz carries about 20% of the world’s daily oil and LNG supply; its closure is described as a “nightmare scenario” for the global energy system and one of the most serious supply disruptions ever.
  • On the price shock, Brent crude rose to $119 per barrel, the highest since 2022; the article also notes that prices for natural gas, Asia LNG, gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals, electricity, and fertilizer all rose significantly.
  • On supply buffers, the International Energy Agency plans to recommend releasing 400 million barrels of oil reserves, which the article says would be the largest such action in IEA history, to cushion the supply shortfall.
  • On alternative transport capacity, Saudi Arabia normally exports about 6 million barrels/day through Hormuz; although its East-West pipeline can reach up to 5 million barrels/day, historical loading volumes at Yanbu have rarely exceeded 2.5 million barrels/day; the UAE’s bypass pipeline capacity is about 1.5 million barrels/day, showing that substitute capacity is limited.
  • On downstream facilities, the Kuwait Al Zour refinery has capacity of 615,000 barrels/day and cannot export fuel; the Bahrain Sitra refinery has capacity of 380,000 barrels/day and declared force majeure after being attacked; Qatar’s LNG plants may require weeks to ramp back up from a full shutdown.
  • In terms of regional impact, the article clearly states that Asia is the most vulnerable region, and gives policy responses as examples: China ordered refineries to stop fuel exports, South Korea imposed fuel price caps for the first time in 30 years, and Bangladesh closed universities to save electricity and fuel.
