Summary

This article argues for a user-centered future of social networking built around Eurosky accounts / AT Protocol: users own their identity, data, and relationships instead of being locked into a single platform. The focus is not on building yet another app, but on turning the account into a cross-app, portable, and permissionable foundational layer for the web.

Problem

  • Existing social media platforms typically control user accounts, data, social graphs, and rules, which locks users into platforms and makes switching costly.
  • Platforms can change policies, distribution logic, and service terms at any time, or even ban users, while not allowing them to fully take their content and relationship networks with them.
  • If future social networks expand into scenarios like private groups, collaboration, storage, and login, account ownership, hosting providers, and applicable law will become even more important.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is to design the AT Protocol/Atmosphere account as the user’s own “primary web account”: the user owns the account itself, while service providers are only responsible for hosting and maintenance.
  • The account can serve as a cross-app identity layer, allowing users to carry data, friend relationships, content, and preferences across different social/web services instead of rebuilding everything on each platform.
  • Eurosky plans, on the one hand, to provide infrastructure and developer tools to lower the barrier for third parties to build new kinds of social applications; on the other hand, to build foundational services and prototype apps to catalyze ecosystem growth.
  • The article also emphasizes that private data capabilities will be added in the future, so the account can support not only public social use cases but also private groups, professional collaboration, cloud storage, and fine-grained data permissions.
  • An important design goal is to let users switch account hosting providers and gain stronger data and governance protections under a European legal framework.

Results

  • The text does not provide formal experiments, datasets, or quantitative benchmark results, so there are no verifiable performance improvements.
  • The concrete progress so far is that a Eurosky account can already serve today as an entry account for Bluesky/Atmosphere, and is positioned as an early form of a broader future web identity layer.
  • The article claims that “many apps exist and more are being released every week” on Atmosphere, but does not provide a specific application count.
  • The article gives a clear timing point: private data capabilities are expected to enter the protocol “this year”, extending it to scenarios such as private communities, collaboration tools, and storage, but there are no quantified deployment results.
  • Eurosky says it is building multiple prototypes and hopes to release apps later in 2026, but this is a roadmap commitment, not a validated result.
  • The strongest actionable claim is that in the future users will be able to move their “data, friend network, and content” between service providers, and manage cross-app permissions and preferences through a single account.
