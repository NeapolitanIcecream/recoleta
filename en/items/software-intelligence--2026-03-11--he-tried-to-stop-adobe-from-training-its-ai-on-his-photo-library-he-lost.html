Summary

This article is not an academic paper, but a case report about a contract and arbitration dispute triggered by Adobe’s use of a photo library to train generative AI. The core signal is that standard pre-AI-era content licensing contracts may be interpreted by platforms as permitting AI training, thereby reshaping the boundaries of creators’ rights.

Problem

  • The article discusses whether a platform can rely on an older content distribution contract to use creators’ images to train generative AI models without separate licensing or explicit consent.
  • This matters because AI training can turn scarce, expensive, and differentiated content into the platform’s own generative capability, directly weakening the original content provider’s commercial moat.
  • The case also exposes how arbitration clauses and litigation costs may make it practically difficult for small and mid-sized creators to challenge large platforms, thereby influencing industry precedent for similar future disputes.

Approach

  • This is not technical research, but a legal/business case: Diversity Photos founder Gerald Carter publicly describes the process of his dispute with Adobe over authorization for AI training.
  • Carter argues that the 2018 Adobe Stock Contributor Agreement authorized Adobe only to distribute and promote the content, and did not amount to permission to feed the entire photo library into Firefly and Sensei for model training.
  • Adobe’s core mechanism/position is simple: it interprets contract language such as “developing new features and services” as broad enough to cover AI training, even though that use did not exist at the time the contract was signed.
  • Carter then attempted to stop the use through communication, a formal demand letter from counsel, and arbitration, and objected both to Adobe’s symbolic payment offer and to its effort to have the arbitration result recognized as precedent for future cases.

Results

  • The article provides no model performance, dataset benchmark, or research experiment metrics; its “results” are mainly legal and commercial developments.
  • Carter says Adobe used nearly 12,000 of his images to train at least two AI models: Adobe Sensei and Adobe Firefly.
  • The additional payment Adobe offered was $1,173.93, which Carter refused to accept; by his account, this was not a formal licensing fee but a “bonus.”
  • Carter states that when his photo library is licensed for AI evaluation, a single one-year license can reach hundreds of thousands of dollars, underscoring how severely Adobe’s offer mismatched the value of the content.
  • During arbitration, to prove he could not afford the costs and to require Adobe to pay the related expenses under the contract, Carter says that merely having the arbitrator review that issue would require $24,000, which ultimately led him to withdraw his remaining claims.
  • The strongest concrete conclusion in the article is that Adobe largely succeeded in arbitration in blocking Carter’s challenge, and is now attempting to have that arbitration ruling confirmed in court as a reference precedent for future similar AI training disputes."
