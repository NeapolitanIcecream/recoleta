Summary

This article argues that the United States is using procurement contracts, rather than public law and regulation, to govern military AI, but this model of “governance by contract instead of governance through law” is fundamentally inadequate in democratic accountability, enforceability, and real-time constraint. Using the disputes involving Anthropic, OpenAI, and the Pentagon, the author shows that procurement mechanisms cannot reliably handle high-risk issues such as domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons, and intelligence oversight.

Problem

  • The article addresses the question of whether the key boundaries of military AI can be governed through bilateral contracts between the government and vendors, and why this model fails.
  • This matters because the subject being governed involves core public-law issues such as domestic surveillance, lethal strikes, autonomous weapons, and intelligence oversight, affecting national security, civil rights, and democratic accountability.
  • The author argues that contracts bind only the parties that sign them and often depend on vendor technical controls or ex post dispute resolution; once the government unilaterally interprets “lawful use” during real-world operations, contracts struggle to impose timely and effective constraints.

Approach

  • The article’s core analytical mechanism is to characterize current U.S. military AI governance as regulation by contract and examine the structural limits of this model within the federal procurement system.
  • Using Anthropic being designated a supply chain risk and OpenAI quickly reaching an agreement with the Pentagon and then revising terms through social media as cases, the author compares two different modes of constraint: one in which the supplier preserves explicit red lines, and another in which restrictions are tied to “existing law” while interpretation is ceded more heavily to the government.
  • The article further breaks down differences among contractual vehicles, emphasizing that if Other Transaction (OT) is used rather than the FAR/CDA framework, default dispute resolution, remedies, and protective provisions may all be absent, making it difficult even for outsiders to determine whether supposed “red lines” are enforceable.
  • The author also analyzes the so-called safety stack: vendors use cloud access, monitoring, classifiers, and refusal mechanisms to impose real-time technical constraints. Put simply, contractual accountability after the fact is often too late, so the only thing that may actually stop improper use in the moment is technical guardrails; but this directly conflicts with the Pentagon’s demand for “any lawful use.”

Results

  • The article is not an experimental paper and does not provide quantitative results on machine learning benchmarks, datasets, or performance metrics.
  • One of its strongest specific claims is that in July 2025, the Pentagon awarded Anthropic a 2-year prototype OT agreement capped at $200 million, but later designated it a supply chain risk because it refused to remove certain usage red lines, showing how unstable contractual arrangements become under intense political and military pressure.
  • The article notes that the Secretary of Defense’s AI strategy memorandum in January required relevant contracts within 180 days to include standard language permitting “any lawful use,” and required the adoption of models “not constrained by use policies that may limit lawful military uses”; the author sees this as the direct institutional trigger for the current conflict.
  • The OpenAI-Pentagon agreement was reportedly rushed through in a matter of days, and key terms were then revised on March 2 through a social media post by the CEO; on this basis, the author argues that crucial governance boundaries in military AI involving domestic surveillance are being shaped by ad hoc negotiation and public backlash rather than formal rule-of-law processes.
  • The article emphasizes that even in FAR contracts, disputes are usually after the fact and centered mainly on monetary remedies; and for OT agreements, they do not automatically receive CDA protections, so whether any comparable dispute mechanism exists depends entirely on bilateral negotiation, leaving them almost powerless to prevent improper uses in real time.
  • The concluding breakthrough claim is that procurement is not a substitute for public law. When the power to interpret “any lawful use” is held primarily by the government, contractual red lines can be rewritten, narrowed, or enforced only after action has already occurred; therefore, these questions should be answered by Congress and the courts, not by the procurement framework.
