Summary

This is not a research paper, but a discussion post about "how to build a human-first online community in the LLM age." The core goal is to find a new kind of community mechanism that can both verify real human participation, preserve anonymity as much as possible, and reduce LLM scraping.

Problem

  • The author argues that LLMs are infiltrating online communities at scale, weakening trust between people and making "real human interaction" increasingly hard to identify.
  • The central question is: how can a community be built that is more resistant to LLM content mixing in, while not requiring users to give up anonymity or pseudonymous space.
  • A second concern is also raised: publicly shared content may be scraped for model training, which discourages users from contributing, so the community also needs some degree of anti-scraping capability.

Approach

  • The text does not propose an implemented method, but instead presents a requirements list as constraints for future community design.
  • The central mechanism envisioned is some form of "human identity verification + pseudonymous interaction layer": it may rely on an identity authority to prove someone is a real person, while still allowing users to interact under pseudonyms.
  • Another design direction is to improve the ability to detect or block LLM "mixing in / infiltration," rather than relying only on an honor system or user self-restraint.
  • At the same time, the author hopes platform-level defenses against content scraping can be strengthened, reducing the risk of works being ingested by LLMs at scale.

Results

  • No experiments, datasets, metrics, or quantitative results are provided; this is a discussion post about a problem rather than a research result.
  • No verifiable system implementation, baseline comparison, or performance figures are given.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that existing community governance based on "user self-discipline" may be insufficient in the future, and that stricter, more systematic human-first community mechanisms will be needed.
  • The importance of the post mainly lies in its problem framing: it clearly identifies three design goals—trustworthy human participation, preservation of some anonymity, and reduced LLM scraping—but does not yet provide a solution.
