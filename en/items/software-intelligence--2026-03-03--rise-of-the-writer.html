Summary

This is an opinion piece whose central argument is: as AI-generated text surges across the internet and training data becomes increasingly "recursively contaminated," genuine human writing is becoming scarcer and more valuable. The author urges people to do more original writing, because future models and information retrieval systems may place greater importance on uncontaminated human text.

Problem

  • The article focuses on the problem that, after 2022, LLM training corpora are increasingly mixed with AI-generated content, raising the risk of "model collapse / recursive contamination" and causing models to gradually forget the real distribution of human knowledge and expression.
  • This matters because high-quality, native human writing may become a scarce resource, affecting future model training, content discovery, credibility assessment, and knowledge preservation.
  • The author also points out that readers and platforms are developing a pattern-recognition habit of judging "does this look AI-written," making it harder for new content to establish trust.

Approach

  • This is not a paper proposing a new algorithm, but an analytical commentary based on existing "model collapse" research and industry observation.
  • Put as simply as possible, the core mechanism is: if models keep training on text generated by other models, it is like repeatedly photocopying a photocopy—details are gradually lost, and the result ultimately drifts away from the true distribution of human language and knowledge.
  • Using the golden age of blogging as an example—especially technical blogs from 2003–2009—the author explains why structured, context-rich, genuinely human-written public text is especially valuable for model training.
  • The piece further infers that large AI companies may work harder in the future to filter out AI slop and increase the weight of "authenticity" signals, so the marginal value of personal original writing will rise.

Results

  • The text does not provide new experiments, datasets, evaluation metrics, or quantitative results, so there are no paper-style breakthrough numbers to verify.
  • The strongest concrete claim is: after 2022, web-scraped training data is being increasingly "contaminated" by AI-generated content, while the share of human writing is declining and its importance is rising.
  • The empirical example given is that LLMs show unusually strong understanding of technical content from the 2003–2009 blogging era. The author uses the case of Shoes.rb largely fading after 2009 to illustrate that models can still generate relevant content well because there were many human blogs, code-sharing posts, and discussions from that period.
  • The author predicts (explicitly marked as a judgment without citation) that future large-model scraping and filtering systems will more actively downrank or remove AI-generated content and more proactively seek out "authentic" human text.
  • The article also offers a non-quantitative but important judgment: in a context of AI content saturation, the distribution and training value of personally "handwritten" original content may achieve outsized impact.
