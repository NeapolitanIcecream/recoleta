Summary

This is not a robotics or machine learning research paper, but an opinion article about the direction of Windows development. The author argues that modern Windows is undermining users' control over their personal computers and is no longer worth using because of privacy issues, cloud dependence, and system bloat.

Problem

  • The issue discussed in the article is that modern Windows is shifting from a "locally controlled operating system under the user's control" to a platform controlled by vendors and cloud services.
  • The author believes this matters because it brings privacy risks, system instability, forced account binding, increasing ads, and reduced user autonomy.
  • The article also emphasizes that this change could make it easier for governments or large companies to track, censor, or mishandle ordinary users' data and behavior.

Approach

  • This article does not propose a technical method, algorithm, or experimental system; its core mechanism is a critical discussion based on the author's personal observations and product evolution.
  • The author regards Windows 7 as the last "truly excellent" version and summarizes the changes in Windows 8/10/11 as: a more chaotic UI, more testing shifted to end users, and increasing dependence on the cloud and Microsoft accounts.
  • Put in the simplest terms, its central argument is that Windows is no longer just "the operating system on your computer," but more like a terminal connected to and influenced by Microsoft's services.
  • As alternatives, the author suggests that privacy-conscious users consider Linux; those seeking a more complete commercial experience may also consider macOS.

Results

  • No quantitative experimental results, datasets, metrics, or formal comparisons with baselines are provided.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that Windows 10 and 11 updates have "made many PCs unusable," but the article does not provide quantities, sources, or statistical evidence.
  • The author claims that Windows has become an "advertising platform" and that the initial setup more forcefully requires Microsoft account sign-in, but does not provide reproducible experimental measurements.
  • The article also claims that Linux gaming compatibility has "significantly improved" because of Proton, but likewise provides no compatibility rates, performance numbers, or comparative tests.
