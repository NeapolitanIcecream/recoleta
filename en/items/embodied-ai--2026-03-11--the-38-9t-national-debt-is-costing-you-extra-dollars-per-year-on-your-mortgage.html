Summary

This article reports on a policy study by Yale Budget Lab arguing that additional federal debt accumulated since 2015 has significantly increased the cost of mortgages, auto loans, and small-business loans for American households by pushing up interest rates. The core message is that the national debt is not an abstract macroeconomic number, but is transmitted into ordinary people's daily lives through higher borrowing costs.

Problem

  • The article addresses the question: How exactly does rising national debt affect the cost of living for ordinary households, especially for major expenses such as housing.
  • This matters because the public usually only feels that mortgages and prices have become more expensive, but does not easily connect those costs to long-term fiscal deficits and newly added debt.
  • The study attempts to quantify how much less borrowers would have paid if legislation since 2015 had not added to the debt.

Approach

  • The core method is simple: construct a counterfactual scenario comparing borrowing costs with the additional debt since 2015 versus without that additional debt.
  • The study assumes a one-to-one pass-through between 30-year mortgage rates and long-term Treasury yields, meaning that if long-term government bond rates rise, mortgage rates rise by the same amount.
  • The study also uses an empirical rule of thumb: for every 1% increase in national debt, interest rates rise by 0.02%.
  • Based on the estimates given in the article, new legislation since 2015 increased expected debt by 49%, leading to an interest-rate increase of nearly 1 percentage point, which is then used to calculate the cumulative additional costs of mortgages, auto loans, and small-business loans.

Results

  • For the average new homebuyer purchasing a home at a benchmark price of about $426,000 with a 30-year mortgage, the study estimates a cumulative extra cost of $76,014, or about $2,534 per year.
  • Under a more conservative interest-rate sensitivity scenario, the additional mortgage cost is still about $57,347, or about $1,912 per year; under a more sensitive scenario, it rises to $112,640, or about $3,755 per year.
  • The study says that laws enacted since 2015 increased expected national debt by 49%, corresponding to an interest-rate rise of nearly 1 percentage point, which is the key mechanism pushing up housing finance costs.
  • For a 5.75-year auto loan, the cumulative extra cost is about $670, or roughly $120 more per year.
  • For a 10-year small-business loan, the cumulative extra cost is about $7,723, or roughly $770 more per year.
  • The article does not provide the kinds of experimental baselines, dataset leaderboards, or statistical significance results common in academic papers; its strongest quantitative claims come from policy simulation and scenario estimates rather than peer-reviewed model comparison experiments.
