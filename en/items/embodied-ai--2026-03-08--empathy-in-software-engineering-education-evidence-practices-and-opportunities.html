Authors

Summary

This is a systematic review on the teaching of “empathy” in software engineering and engineering education, synthesizing 43 studies from 2001–2025. The paper’s core contribution is to clarify how empathy is defined, cultivated, and assessed, as well as the distinctive instructional pathways of software engineering compared with general engineering education.

Problem

  • Software engineering education has long emphasized technical competence, while key human capabilities such as empathy remain fragmented in curricula and lack systematic integration.
  • There is a lack of synthesized evidence spanning both engineering education and software engineering education, making it difficult for instructors to answer “why teach empathy, how to teach it, and how to assess it.”
  • This matters because empathy is believed to affect collaboration, ethical judgment, inclusive design, requirements understanding, and the quality of user-centered development.

Approach

  • The paper uses a systematic literature review method, following established review guidelines in software engineering, and conducts automated + manual searches across six databases.
  • After the search, 374 studies were identified; 38 duplicates were removed, leaving 336; title and abstract screening excluded 220; after full-text review, 43 primary studies were ultimately included.
  • The authors coded and synthesized the literature around four questions: how empathy is incorporated into curricula, how it is cultivated, what benefits it brings, and what challenges it faces.
  • The analysis combines descriptive statistics and thematic synthesis: the former examines study distribution and trends, while the latter summarizes teaching methods, benefits, barriers, and cross-disciplinary differences.
  • One core conclusion is that general engineering education is more likely to treat empathy as an ethical/reflective capacity, whereas software engineering is more inclined to translate it into structured, design-oriented, and assessable instructional practices, such as personas, scenarios, empathy maps, and design thinking activities.

Results

  • The most direct quantitative result is the review flow: 374 → 336 → 116 → 43, meaning that 43 studies from 2001–2025 were ultimately included, covering both engineering education and software engineering education.
  • The paper claims that cross-study evidence shows empathy teaching can improve collaboration, ethical reasoning, bias awareness, and learning motivation; however, the provided excerpt does not report a unified effect size, statistical significance, or meta-analytic values.
  • The paper’s main comparative finding is that engineering education emphasizes social responsibility and ethical reflection, while software engineering emphasizes design practice, user understanding, and actionable and measurable methods.
  • The paper stresses that empathy is evolving from a “peripheral soft skill” into a teachable and assessable pedagogical construct in software engineering education, and argues that it should be continuously embedded into design and development courses.
  • At the same time, the authors identify three main limitations: low curricular prioritization, measurement difficulties, and resource constraints; these are the main barriers to large-scale implementation at present.
  • In terms of “breakthrough results,” this paper is better understood as a piece of evidence synthesis and a baseline for curriculum framing rather than one proposing a new algorithm or reporting new high-value SOTA results.
