Summary

This is not an academic paper, but a practical article on pricing experience for freelancers and B2B service providers. The core argument is that whether a client buys depends more on priority and friction than on the quote itself.

Problem

  • The problem addressed: freelancers, independent consultants, and small service providers often become overly anxious when quoting clients because they fear either “underpricing and losing out” or “overpricing and losing the deal,” leading them to discount themselves.
  • Why it matters: poor pricing hurts income, negotiating position, and mental burden at the same time, and over the long term leads to being “chronically undervalued.”
  • The article specifically limits these rules to serious, realistic, non-delusional buyers, not people who clearly do not respect market prices.

Approach

  • The author proposes three high-level “pricing theorems”: Everybody has money、Everybody has $5,000 in their front pocket、Nobody cares what you cost。
  • Put simply, this framework argues that clients usually do not “lack money”; rather, they do not see your project as the top current priority. If the priority is high enough, budget will be made available.
  • The “$5,000 front pocket” idea is an experience-based threshold: for many mid-sized and large B2B buyers, about $5,000 is “walking-around money” that does not require deep approval and can be accepted relatively easily.
  • Once you go above this experience-based threshold, closing the deal depends more on whether you tie the project’s value to the client’s specific priorities and prove that it is worth the “sacrifice.”
  • The unifying explanation is that all three rules are really about friction (transaction friction): the higher the project priority, the lower the price friction; the lower the priority, the more likely it is to be dropped even if it is cheap.

Results

  • The article contains no formal experiments, datasets, statistical tests, or reproducible quantitative results, so there is no academic-style benchmark improvement or SOTA claim.
  • The most concrete numerical claim is an experience-based benchmark: for “midmarket and enterprise buyers,” $5,000 can often be treated as a low-decision-friction amount; the author says they have “numerous times” seen B2B negotiations close at around $5,000, and that deals below $5,000 often require little to no intense price haggling.
  • The author also gives a non-rigorous range judgment: once a quote reaches about 2.5x that benchmark, objections become more likely, and progress may require reducing scope or making a stronger value case.
  • The article’s strongest bottom-line claim is not “what you should charge,” but this: do not preemptively discount for the client because you assume they cannot afford it; for serious buyers, whether a deal closes is driven mainly by project priority, not simply by the price tag.
  • The evidence is primarily anecdotal and based on the author’s roughly 20 years of self-employment experience, rather than systematic research.
