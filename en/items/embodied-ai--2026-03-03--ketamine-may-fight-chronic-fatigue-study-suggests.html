Summary

This was a small randomized crossover proof-of-concept trial for chronic fatigue, exploring whether low-dose ketamine can produce a rapid antifatigue effect. The results showed a signal of fatigue reduction reaching the prespecified threshold in the short term after dosing, but it did not show a significant advantage over the active placebo, so it is better viewed as an early clue worth validating further in a larger sample.

Problem

  • The study sought to address the problem of the lack of effective, rapid interventions for chronic fatigue; this symptom is common among cancer survivors and patients with fibromyalgia, lupus, and chronic fatigue syndrome, and it severely affects work, family life, and basic functioning.
  • This matters because rest is usually ineffective, while standard management approaches such as exercise require patients to have enough motivation and physical capacity to stick with them, which many people cannot do in practice.
  • The mechanisms of fatigue have long been difficult to clarify, and it is therefore often overlooked in clinical care; about 3.3 million adults in the United States are affected.

Approach

  • The core idea was simple: researchers had previously found that the glutamate receptor mGluR5 is associated with chronic fatigue, and ketamine blocks glutamate receptors, so they tested whether this pathway could serve as a treatment target.
  • The study used a randomized crossover trial: 10 participants with chronic fatigue each received one low-dose ketamine infusion and, two weeks later, one midazolam injection as an active control.
  • Before the trial began, the researchers prespecified a standard for being “worthy of further study”: a 20% reduction in real-time fatigue scores on day 3 after dosing.
  • The study focused on rapid, short-term energy improvement. The authors proposed that, if effective, ketamine would more likely serve as a “bridge” to help patients begin interventions already known to have more durable benefit, such as exercise.

Results

  • In the ketamine group, real-time fatigue scores on day 3 after dosing fell by an average of 21% from baseline, just exceeding the study's prespecified positive threshold of 20%.
  • The largest effect appeared 24 hours after dosing, with real-time fatigue decreasing by nearly 39%.
  • However, ketamine was not significantly better than the active control midazolam; the latter also showed fatigue reduction, which weakened the strength of the conclusion.
  • The study originally planned to recruit 59 participants but ultimately completed only 10, mainly due to COVID-19 and strict inclusion and exclusion criteria, so its statistical power was limited.
  • The strongest conclusion is that, in a small-sample proof-of-concept trial, low-dose ketamine showed a rapid antifatigue signal, but the current evidence is insufficient to prove clear efficacy relative to the control, and larger clinical trials are still needed for confirmation.
