Summary

This report uses Claude’s real occupational usage data to propose “observed exposure,” measuring the gap between how much work AI can do and how much work it is actually already doing. The core conclusion is: white-collar roles are, in theory, highly coverable by large models, but real-world adoption still lags significantly. However, the researchers believe this gap may converge in the future, creating a larger impact on highly educated, high-income occupations.

Problem

  • The study aims to solve this problem: how to distinguish between AI’s theoretical capability frontier and its actual substitution progress in the real labor market, rather than discussing “whether AI will take jobs” based only on subjective speculation.
  • This matters because looking only at technical demos overestimates the current pace of substitution, while looking only at current usage underestimates the medium- and long-term impact; policymaking, hiring, and career planning all need more precise early signals.
  • The research focuses especially on white-collar and knowledge work, because these roles may be affected earlier than traditional manual jobs as language model capabilities expand.

Approach

  • The core method is straightforward: first estimate which occupational tasks AI can theoretically complete, then use Claude’s actual interaction data in professional settings to measure which tasks it is currently really being used for. Comparing the two yields “observed exposure.”
  • This metric separates the “blue region” (feasible capability) from the “red region” (actual adoption), thereby identifying which occupations are “already widely handled by AI” and which are only “technically automatable but not yet deployed at scale.”
  • The researchers also compare exposure across dimensions such as occupation, education, income, and gender to identify who is most likely to bear the employment shock from AI diffusion.
  • They further connect this framework to labor market data to observe signals of changes in hiring and unemployment for AI-exposed occupations in the post-ChatGPT period.

Results

  • For computer and math occupations, LLMs can theoretically handle 94% of tasks, but in Claude’s observed professional usage, they currently cover only 33%, showing a large gap between capability and adoption.
  • For office and administrative occupations, AI has about 90% theoretical capability, but actual adoption is still only a small fraction of that; the article does not provide a more precise current share.
  • 30% of workers have zero AI exposure, mainly in jobs requiring physical presence, such as cooks, mechanics, bartenders, and dishwashers, indicating that the current LLM impact is heavily skewed toward non-manual white-collar work.
  • Compared with the least exposed occupational group, the most exposed group has a 16 percentage point higher likelihood of being female, 47% higher average income, and an almost 4x higher likelihood of having a graduate degree; representative high-risk occupations include programmer, customer service rep, and data entry keyer.
  • In the post-ChatGPT period, the job finding rate for AI-exposed occupations fell 14% relative to 2022, but the authors note that this result is only barely statistically significant, and no systematic rise in unemployment has yet been observed.
  • Related external research also notes that employment in AI-exposed occupations among 22–25-year-olds fell 16%. The paper also provides a monitoring threshold: if unemployment in high-exposure occupations were to double from 3% to 6%, as in 2007–2009, the framework should detect it clearly; but for now, this “great recession for white-collar workers” scenario has not yet occurred.
