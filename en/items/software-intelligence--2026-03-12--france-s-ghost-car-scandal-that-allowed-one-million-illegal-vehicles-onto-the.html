Summary

This report exposes a large-scale regulatory loophole in France’s vehicle registration system after its partial privatization, giving rise to “ghost car” registration fraud. Its impact has not only caused massive losses in taxes, fees, and fines, but has also allowed untraceable and potentially unsafe vehicles onto the roads.

Problem

  • The problem it identifies/reveals is that France’s national vehicle registration system (SIV) has been widely abused by fake car dealers, resulting in about 1 million illegally registered vehicles on the road, with the vehicles and their owners potentially becoming untraceable afterward.
  • This matters because the loophole creates public safety risks, losses in taxes, fees, and fines, and convenience for organized crime, including laundering stolen cars, evading environmental taxes, and using vehicles for drug transport.
  • The root cause is that after 2017 the registration process was partially privatized, the system effectively assumed “good faith” from entities granted access, and the state conducted seriously inadequate qualification checks on authorized dealers.

Approach

  • This is not a technical paper, but an investigative report based on the audit findings of France’s national audit institution, Cour des Comptes, with the core mechanism being an analysis of how the system was exploited.
  • The most central “method” is simple: wrongdoers first set up shell dealerships/fake dealers, then apply for access to the SIV registration system; because approval is lax, they can usually obtain access.
  • After gaining access, they can, for a fee, tamper with or falsify registration records for others, making vehicle documents appear normal on the surface while concealing the true ownership, history, or identity.
  • The frauds they can carry out include: falsifying owner information, avoiding registration fees/environmental taxes, altering inspection results, hiding previous owners, and re-registering stolen cars.
  • The government’s current remedial measures include increasing fraud detection and drastically reducing the number of entities authorized to access SIV, but the article does not provide complete data on the effectiveness of these measures.

Results

  • The report says that about 1 million cars on the road were registered by nearly 300 fictitious companies “operating totally free of state control.”
  • France’s audit institution said that in 2022-2024 alone, uncollected registration fees, speeding fines, and parking fines caused fiscal losses of €550 million.
  • The audit report lists 30 different types of fraud linked to the scam, ranging from evading environmental taxes on heavily polluting vehicles to tampering with vehicle roadworthiness inspection results.
  • The system granted access to about 30,000 dealers, but the state did not adequately verify their qualifications; the barrier to abuse was so low that “setting up a shell company and applying” was usually enough to get approved.
  • Le Figaro cited police data saying that between 2016 and 2022, “very high-speed” violations increased by 160%, and investigation of registration information found that many were linked to falsified registrations.
  • It does not provide experimental metrics or benchmark comparisons like an academic paper; the strongest concrete conclusion is that flaws in the system’s design systematically amplified problems in road safety, tax enforcement, and crime control over many years.
