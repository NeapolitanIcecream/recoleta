Summary

This article is not a technical research paper, but a commentary on U.S. research governance and the relationship between science and politics. The author argues that criticism of the NIH and the U.S. research system has some merit, but the MAGA-style path of “correcting course through punishment and funding cuts” may not make American science stronger.

Problem

  • The article examines whether the U.S. medical research system, especially the NIH, is simultaneously facing two deep crises: politicization, groupthink, and loss of public trust on the one hand, and bureaucratization, inefficiency, and lack of output orientation on the other.
  • The author further asks: even if these diagnoses are correct, can cutting research funding and disrupting universities and research institutions really repair the system, or will it instead weaken America’s capacity in science and great-power competition.
  • This matters because the NIH funds more than $40 billion in medical research each year, and America’s universities and research system are seen as key assets for national competitiveness, innovation capacity, and biomedical leadership.

Approach

  • The author uses a policy commentary/argumentative analysis approach rather than experimental research; the core mechanism is a critical examination of Jay Bhattacharya and the associated reform idea that “open inquiry can correct scientific failure.”
  • The article connects two kinds of criticism: one concerns closure, censorship, and politicization during Covid; the other concerns the NIH’s long-term structural inefficiency and drift in mission, and it suggests that both may share a common source in herd thinking.
  • The author then tests Bhattacharya’s core assumption: so long as open debate is restored, truth will prevail, and both science and politics will self-correct.
  • To do so, the author uses public opinion and policy disputes during Covid as a counterexample, arguing that “open discussion” did not automatically allow Great Barrington Declaration–style views to prevail at the national level, showing that scientific disputes and political disputes are not equivalent.
  • The author ultimately offers a simpler explanation: scientific openness requires stable institutions and social consensus as a backdrop; if those foundations are damaged by revolutionary-style political shocks, merely “returning to the first principles of science” is not enough to rebuild the research system.

Results

  • The article does not provide original experiments, datasets, or model results; there are no quantitative research results in the conventional sense.
  • The most concrete quantitative background cited includes: the NIH funds more than $40 billion in medical research each year; the Trump administration reportedly plans to cut funding for research institutions by about half, and the author argues that there is no clear causal case connecting this to “making American science stronger.”
  • In the public judgment cited by the author, Bhattacharya noted that 93% of American children receive the MMR vaccine, while only 13% of children are up to date on the latest Covid vaccination; the author uses this to show that the public is not always completely controlled by the scientific establishment.
  • But the author also cites a 2024 Harvard poll saying that 80% of Americans believed that one or more restrictive measures during the pandemic were, overall, a “good idea”; among them, 62% of Republicans and 70% of rural residents also agreed. This is used to rebut the simplistic narrative that “as long as debate is open, anti-establishment truths will broadly prevail.”
  • The article also cites the attractiveness of U.S. higher education as real-world evidence: about 300,000 Chinese students and 1.1 million international students study in the United States, supporting its argument that “American universities remain a strategic asset of historic importance and should be reformed rather than destroyed.”
  • The central concluding claim is that the judgment against the old scientific establishment “may be justified,” but if the reform camp mistakenly assumes that punishment = rebuilding, then what will be damaged is not only the old elite, but potentially the American scientific system itself.
