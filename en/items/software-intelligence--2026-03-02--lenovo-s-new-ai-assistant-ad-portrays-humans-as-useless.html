Summary

This article is not a research paper, but a commentary report criticizing Lenovo's advertisement for its AI assistant Qira for portraying human employees as nearly worthless bystanders. The core argument is that the ad both exaggerates AI's current capabilities and conveys a troubling vision of future working relationships.

Problem

  • The issue discussed in the article is how companies market AI assistants, and whether this marketing depicts humans as "useless" or "replaceable."
  • This matters because it can shape public perceptions of AI, job displacement, and human-AI collaboration, and may lend support to narratives of layoffs and excessive automation.
  • The article also points out a practical issue: the ad promises that AI can flawlessly complete reports, answer budget questions, and support meetings in one shot, which does not match the real-world reliability of AI.

Approach

  • This is not a technical paper and does not propose algorithms or experimental methods; its core "method" is a scene-by-scene interpretation and critique of Lenovo's Qira advertisement.
  • The article uses several specific scenes to illustrate how the ad works: an employee forgets to write a report, and the AI generates one directly from a sketch; when a colleague asks about the Q1 budget, the AI answers fully on the employee's behalf; during meetings, the AI continues to carry the main workload.
  • From this, the author draws a simple conclusion: the ad presents AI as the party doing the real work, while humans merely stand by receiving the output and the credit.
  • At the same time, the article compares the ad's narrative with the actual capabilities of AI, emphasizing that current AI still cannot stably and flawlessly complete all office tasks.

Results

  • It provides no quantitative experimental results, datasets, baseline models, or performance metrics, so there is no paper-style breakthrough result.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that, across the multiple scenes shown in the ad, "not a single truly productive action is performed by a human", while the AI handles report generation, budget responses, and meeting support.
  • The article explicitly claims that the advertised capabilities are "an AI assistant can’t reliably deliver", meaning that in reality AI still cannot reliably achieve the fully automated office workflow shown in the ad, though no numerical evidence is provided.
  • The article uses Amazon and Microsoft as examples to suggest that even large companies face the problem of imperfect AI, but does not include experimental numbers or formal comparisons.
