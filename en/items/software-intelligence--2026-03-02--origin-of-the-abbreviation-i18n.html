Summary

This article explains the origin and formation of the abbreviation "i18n," and clarifies that it is not an acronym but a numeronym. It also briefly reviews the evolution of the terms "internationalization" and "localization" in the software industry from the 1980s to the early 1990s.

Problem

  • The question the article aims to answer is: What exactly does "i18n" mean, where did it come from, and what type of abbreviation is it?
  • This matters because internationalization/localization are foundational concepts in software engineering, and confusion about the terminology can affect cross-team communication and historical understanding.
  • The text also tries to distinguish "internationalization" from alternative terms used early on by some vendors, such as "international-enabling."

Approach

  • The author gives the definition directly: i18n = i + 18 middle letters + n, corresponding to the word "internationalization."
  • The article explains the mechanism in the simplest terms: this is not an acronym, but a numeronym, meaning a number is used to represent the count of omitted letters in the middle.
  • It explains this type of abbreviation through comparative examples, including K9, K7, and 411, helping readers understand the word-formation rule behind "i18n."
  • Drawing on historical materials and industry recollections, the author outlines a timeline of term usage across different companies, such as differences in adoption among Apple, DEC, Unix, IBM, and Lotus.

Results

  • Clear conclusion: "i18n" is a numeronym for "internationalization," not an acronym.
  • Specific numeric structure: initial letter i + 18 letters + final letter n.
  • Historical timeline claim: the term "internationalization" was in use by Apple and DEC by 1985 at the latest; Unix adopted it at least by the late 1980s.
  • Historical comparison claim: before 1990, IBM and Lotus more commonly used "international-enabling," and only began using "internationalization" in the 1990s.
  • Parallel terminology claim: "localization" was already in use in the mid-1980s by IBM, DEC, Apple, Lotus, HP, and the Unix camp.
  • The text provides no experiments, datasets, or quantitative performance metrics; its strongest result is a qualitative clarification of the term’s origin, classification, and history of industry usage.
