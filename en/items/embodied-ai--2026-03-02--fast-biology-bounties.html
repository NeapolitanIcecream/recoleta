Summary

This is not a technical paper, but a public call for submissions: the author is offering a total of $10,000 in prizes for original and feasible technical ideas that could significantly speed up or reduce the cost of wet-lab experiments. The core claim is that wet-lab work is the key bottleneck for scientific progress and for turning AI-driven biological discoveries into reality, and that it urgently needs concrete methodological breakthroughs like those seen in sequencing technology.

Problem

  • Wet-lab biology is slow and expensive, making it a real bottleneck for scientific progress; even if AI proposes good hypotheses, they still must be validated through real experiments.
  • Many common experimental workflows (such as cloning, PCR, protein synthesis, etc.) still take days, hundreds of dollars in reagents, and expensive equipment, creating a sharp contrast with the digital world.
  • The author argues that existing vague ideas about “automation / cloud labs / physical intelligence” are not enough; what really matters is technical solutions that can be implemented and improve specific commonly used methods.

Approach

  • The “method” proposed in the article is essentially a bounty mechanism: using prize money to incentivize external researchers to submit feasible, original ideas for speeding up wet-lab work, rather than the single algorithm or system commonly seen in papers.
  • The judging criteria emphasize two points: originality and technical tractability; submissions only need to be a few paragraphs.
  • It encourages narrow, practical improvements targeting specific experimental steps, rather than grand but vague visions of general-purpose platforms.
  • The article gives example directions, such as a “protein printer” that does not require a DNA template, or using Vibrio natriegens with an in situ mutagenesis system to accelerate mutational scanning.

Results

  • No experimental quantitative results, dataset, or baseline comparison are provided; this is a public call for submissions, not a report of completed research results.
  • The clearest numerical details given are the prize structure: 4 prizes totaling $10,000, specifically $5,000 / $3,000 / $1,000 / $1,000.
  • The deadline is March 15, 11:59pm PT, and winner notifications are planned to be sent on March 22.
  • The historical comparison used to support the claim that “biological experiments can be dramatically accelerated” is that in 1965 Robert Holley spent several years sequencing a 77 nt alanine tRNA, while today an entire human genome (billions of nucleotides) can be sequenced in a few hours.
  • The strongest concrete claim in the article is that time- and cost-related order-of-magnitude improvements like those seen in sequencing should also be achieved in other common biological experimental methods.
