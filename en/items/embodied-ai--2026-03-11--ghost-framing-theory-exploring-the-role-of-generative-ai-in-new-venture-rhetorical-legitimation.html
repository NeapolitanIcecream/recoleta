Summary

This paper proposes Ghost Framing Theory (GFT) to explain how generative AI covertly participates in entrepreneurial narratives and legitimacy construction on both the entrepreneur and investor sides. The core argument is that fundraising discourse is no longer produced solely by humans, but is recursively co-created, filtered, and adjusted by human-machine collaborative collectives.

Problem

  • The paper seeks to address the following problem: when both entrepreneurs and investors begin covertly using generative AI, existing theories of entrepreneurial narratives, rhetorical framing, and new venture legitimacy can no longer adequately explain the processes of persuasion and evaluation.
  • This matters because fundraising, screening, and relationship building depend heavily on whether the presentation is persuasive; if AI significantly changes language fluency, author visibility, and evaluation mechanisms, it will alter resource allocation and legitimacy judgments.
  • Prior research assumes that information is written by humans and that authorship is relatively transparent, but generative AI makes “high-quality expression” cheaper and authorship more concealed, while also enabling evaluators themselves to use AI-assisted judgment.

Approach

  • The paper introduces a new conceptual framework, Ghost Framing Theory, modeling both entrepreneurs and investors as human-genAI ensembles, that is, human-machine collaborative agents rather than standalone human actors.
  • It defines ghost agency: AI-generated rhetorical content is assumed to be attributable to humans during evaluation, while AI’s involvement may be partially or completely invisible.
  • It identifies five generative AI rhetorical affordances: generativeness, extreme combinatorics, tone repertoire, velocity/energy, and shared substratum.
  • It constructs a recursive process model with three stages: ghost pitching, ghost screening, and ghost relationship-building, showing how both sides jointly shape “resonance” and “legitimacy” through interaction.
  • It proposes three theoretical propositions: founders’ use of AI can increase initial resonance; this effect is influenced by whether investors also use AI; and once the interaction enters the long-term relationship stage, interpersonal relationships weaken this conditional difference.

Results

  • This is a theoretical paper, and the excerpt does not report the author’s own experiments, datasets, or formal quantitative evaluation results.
  • External industry evidence cited in the paper includes a blind test of 250 American investors + 250 business owners, in which 80% of respondents judged a GPT-4-generated pitch deck to be more persuasive, and post-reading investment willingness rose to 3x that for human-written copy.
  • Another survey of 140 PE/VC firms reports that 82% of institutions are running generative AI modules in deal sourcing and screening, up from 47% the previous year.
  • The author’s strongest claim is not a new performance SOTA, but a theoretical breakthrough: proposing a process model of entrepreneurial legitimacy for the age of generative AI, explaining how “founder-side AI” and “investor-side AI” jointly change rhetorical resonance and screening outcomes.
  • The paper also claims to extend affordance theory by emphasizing affordance transitivity and visibility in multi-actor settings, and by reincorporating micro-level legitimacy judgments under AI conditions into framing research.
