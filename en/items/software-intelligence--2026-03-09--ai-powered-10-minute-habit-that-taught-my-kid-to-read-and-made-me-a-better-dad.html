Summary

This is a practical experience article describing how the author combined Anki’s spaced repetition with AI-generated entertaining content to help a young child learn to read in 5–10 minutes a day. The core insight is not simply improving memory efficiency, but tuning the system into a learning experience that feels “easy to win, affirming, and warm for the parent-child relationship.”

Problem

  • The problem to solve is: how to get a roughly 4-year-old child, with limited attention and little intrinsic motivation to learn, to keep willingly practicing character recognition and early reading.
  • This matters because early reading ability, self-efficacy, and the belief that “effort leads to progress” affect later learning development; traditional practice can easily trigger frustration, resistance, and parent-child conflict.
  • The author also implicitly raises a broader question: how AI can make personalized, low-cost, sustainable production of children’s educational content feasible.

Approach

  • Use Anki as a “curriculum planner”: based on spaced repetition and the testing effect, it schedules which letters, sounds, and vocabulary to review each day.
  • Use generative AI to create many exaggerated, strange, and fun image variations for the 26 letters, increasing children’s attention and avoiding overfitting to a single example through multiple instances of the same letter.
  • Do 5–10 minutes of parent-child learning every day: look at pictures, name the object, then say the corresponding letter; the parent scores mastery in Anki (1–4), and the system schedules later review.
  • Later, shift the optimization target from “maximizing memory efficiency” to “maximizing the feeling of winning and confidence”: deliberately add many very easy cards so the child gets most answers right and develops the experience of “I can do this.”
  • The author emphasizes that the real mechanism also includes the emotional environment: short duration, a stable habit, hugs, and patience, turning memory training into low-pressure, affirming intimate interaction.

Results

  • The author claims the method “worked”: the child started at about age 4 and, after sustained practice through 2023–2024, can now read fluently, though somewhat mechanically; the article does not provide quantitative metrics such as standardized test scores, reading speed, or error rate.
  • The daily intervention intensity is very low: each session is 5–10 minutes, and it is done every day; the author believes short, frequent sessions help maintain habit and emotional stability.
  • In terms of content design scale, the author generated multiple visual variants for the 26 letters and explicitly says this diversity helped the child avoid relying too heavily on a single illustration.
  • In tuning the system, the author would make 18 out of about 20 cards in a set very easy, keeping only 2 cards as new content, sacrificing per-session learning efficiency in exchange for a sustained “success experience.”
  • Beyond literacy, the author also claims the child “also became very good at arithmetic” and learned the mindset that “investing practice brings rewards”; these are likewise qualitative observations, without formal controls or statistical results.
  • For classroom adoption, the article provides no experimental data, but offers a concrete implementation judgment: the main current barriers are multi-user usability, reliability, and privacy/regulation; the author believes that as smaller local models and voice interaction mature, classroom adoption will become “feasible very soon.”
