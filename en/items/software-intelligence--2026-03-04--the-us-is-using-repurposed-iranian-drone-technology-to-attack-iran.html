Summary

This article analyzes how the United States has reverse-engineered and adopted the logic behind Iran’s low-cost Shahed kamikaze drones, rapidly deploying and using the Lucas drone in combat to change the cost structure of operations against Iran. The core argument is that cheap, expendable, and scalable drones are reshaping the philosophy of modern air warfare.

Problem

  • The article discusses the problem that, although the United States’ traditional high-end air power is powerful, it is expensive, limited in number, slow to replenish, and politically costly, making it ill-suited to modern battlefields that require large-scale attrition.
  • This matters because modern warfare increasingly emphasizes “affordable mass,” especially in the suppression of air defenses, saturation attacks, and penetration of high-risk frontline targets, where low-cost drones can significantly alter the economics of offense and defense.
  • The article also points out that Iran’s Shahed system has already demonstrated that low-cost drones can exhaust and overwhelm enemy air defense systems through mass use, forcing the United States to absorb this approach.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is straightforward: the United States first captured and reverse-engineered the Shahed-136, initially producing the FLM-136, a replica used for counter-drone training, and then further adapting it into the operational Lucas version.
  • Lucas continues the Iranian one-way attack drone concept of low cost and expendability, but adds a nose-mounted gimbal camera and a satellite connectivity module, enabling it not only to fly preset routes but also potentially to be retasked mid-flight or coordinated in strikes.
  • The article argues that this design allows Lucas to be launched in large numbers to saturate radar, suppress air defenses, and open penetration corridors for more expensive US and Israeli weapons.
  • A further potential upgrade path is AI-driven swarm coordination: using satellite links to enable multiple drones to attack cooperatively as a team. However, the author emphasizes that the United States does not yet truly possess the technical, legal, and social conditions necessary to fully deploy an “AI air force.”

Results

  • The article says the Lucas program advanced extremely quickly: within five months of the program’s launch, the Pentagon had already equipped US forces in the Middle East with Lucas.
  • The unit cost of Lucas is about $35,000; by comparison, the Iranian/Russian Shahed-136 costs more than $20,000. The article uses this to show that the United States is embracing the operational logic of low-cost, expendable platforms.
  • The goal of the US “drone dominance” plan is to stockpile 340,000 similar drones by early 2028, indicating an intention to institutionalize and scale low-cost drones.
  • The article provides capability parameters: the Shahed-136 has a range of more than 1,000 miles, a speed of about 115 mph, and a warhead of 40–50 kg; the lighter FLM-136 has a range of about 400 miles and a payload of about 18 kg. These figures are used to show that the US replica system made trade-offs between performance and manufacturability.
  • The article cites experiential data claiming that on the battlefield in Ukraine, about 75% of battlefield casualties were attributed to small drones, supporting the argument that “cheap drones are changing war.”
  • Strictly speaking, the article does not provide formal experimental metrics or controlled evaluation results for Lucas relative to a baseline; the strongest concrete claim is that it completed rapid deployment, completed sea-launch testing, and was confirmed by US Central Command to have been used in combat for the first time on February 28, 2026.
