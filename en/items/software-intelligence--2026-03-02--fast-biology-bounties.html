Summary

This is not a technical paper, but a bounty call: $10,000 in prizes for original and feasible technical ideas that could significantly accelerate wet-lab work or reduce its cost. Its core position is that even if AI can generate good hypotheses, progress in biology is still constrained by the slow execution of real-world experiments.

Problem

  • Wet-lab work is a key bottleneck to scientific progress: ideas in the digital world can iterate quickly, but the “manipulation of atoms” in biological experiments is slow, expensive, and requires costly equipment.
  • Even if AI generates valuable biological hypotheses, those hypotheses still must be validated through real experiments, so experimental throughput directly limits the pace of discovery.
  • The author argues that many common methods (such as PCR, cloning, and protein synthesis) still lack the orders-of-magnitude improvements seen in sequencing; this matters because it determines whether biological R&D can scale up in speed and drive down costs.

Approach

  • It proposes an open bounty mechanism: soliciting concrete technical proposals from anyone for speeding up wet-lab experiments, rather than funding vague automation visions.
  • The evaluation criteria focus on two points: originality and tractability.
  • Submissions are encouraged to target specific improvements to high-frequency experimental workflows, such as PCR, cloning, and protein synthesis, rather than broad concepts like “cloud labs” or “physical intelligence.”
  • The article gives example directions: for instance, a “protein printer” that could manufacture proteins by sequence without a DNA template, or using Vibrio natriegens with an in situ mutagenesis system to accelerate mutational scanning.

Results

  • The text does not provide experimental quantitative results, benchmarks, or model performance comparisons; it is a call for submissions, not a report of a validated method.
  • The strongest concrete numbers given are the prize details: a total of $10,000, with 4 awards planned in the amounts of $5,000 / $3,000 / $1,000 / $1,000.
  • The deadline is March 15, 11:59pm PT, with winners to be notified on March 22; the submission can be just a few paragraphs.
  • Historical comparisons used to illustrate the severity of the problem include: in 1965, sequencing a 77 nt alanine tRNA took several years, whereas today a whole human genome (billions of nt) can be sequenced in a few hours; however, this is only background context, not a result of the project itself.
  • The “breakthrough” claimed by the project is not an existing technical achievement, but the hope of catalyzing the next generation of concrete methods that can dramatically shorten wet-lab cycle times and costs through highly original, executable ideas.
