Summary

This article analyzes how stablecoins, through a mechanism that uses U.S. Treasuries as their primary reserve assets, have rapidly become an important new buyer in the U.S. Treasury market. It emphasizes that stablecoins may both expand the dollar’s influence and introduce new systemic financial risks.

Problem

  • The article examines the question of whether the rapid expansion of stablecoins could transmit volatility and run risk from the crypto market into the U.S. short-term Treasury market and the broader financial system.
  • This matters because stablecoins have evolved from an internal crypto-market tool into a key channel connecting blockchains, banks, payments, and the U.S. Treasury market.
  • The core regulatory challenge is how to encourage financial innovation, dollar internationalization, and improved payment efficiency while limiting systemic risks from runs, liquidity shocks, and low-quality collateral.

Approach

  • The article’s core mechanism is straightforward: stablecoins promise to remain worth approximately 1 U.S. dollar, and issuers typically support that peg by holding reserve assets such as U.S. Treasuries and cash.
  • As stablecoins grow in scale, issuers need to buy more Treasuries, thereby channeling crypto capital into the U.S. Treasury market.
  • If users redeem stablecoins en masse, issuers may need to quickly sell Treasuries or other reserve assets to raise cash, transmitting stress from the crypto market into traditional financial markets.
  • The article also analyzes this mechanism at the policy and institutional level, focusing on the regulatory details after the GENIUS Act: which assets may serve as collateral, which institutions may issue stablecoins, and how anti-money-laundering and redemption rules should be designed.
  • At the same time, the article discusses potential upside mechanisms: stablecoins could improve cross-border payment efficiency, support offshore demand for dollars, and potentially increase demand for U.S. debt, thereby lowering yields.

Results

  • Total stablecoin market capitalization has grown from about $20 billion in 2020 to $300 billion; the Federal Reserve estimates it could reach $3 trillion within 5 years.
  • The article says that major stablecoin issuers now hold more U.S. Treasury debt than major U.S. creditor countries such as Saudi Arabia and South Korea, indicating that they have become significant participants in the Treasury market.
  • Current risks are concentrated in the roughly $7 trillion U.S. Treasury bill (T-bill) market; at the current growth rate, stablecoins could become “a fairly large part” of it.
  • In past stress episodes, USD Coin fell below $0.87, and Tether/USDT fell below $0.94, showing that their 1:1 peg is not absolutely stable, although both ultimately met redemption demand.
  • The article cites November 2024, when S&P downgraded Tether from “constrained” to “weak”, citing reserve assets that were riskier than Treasuries and cash, including Bitcoin, gold, and corporate bonds.
  • It does not provide a single experimental-style “benchmark comparison” result; the strongest concrete conclusion is that stablecoins may on the one hand strengthen the dollar’s reserve-currency status and lower U.S. Treasury yields, while on the other hand they may trigger Treasury sell-offs, bank liquidity pressure, and financial contagion in the next crypto crisis.
