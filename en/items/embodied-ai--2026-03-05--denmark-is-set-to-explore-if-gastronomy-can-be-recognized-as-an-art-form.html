Summary

This report discusses whether Denmark should formally recognize fine dining as an art form. The central context is that the country’s top restaurants are combining food with performance, imagery, and social issues into immersive experiences. This is not a research paper and does not present technical methods or experimental conclusions; rather, it is a public discussion about cultural policy and the definition of art.

Problem

  • The problem to be addressed is: whether fine dining should be reclassified from “craft” to “art”, and why that classification matters, because it would affect social recognition, cultural status, and eligibility for funding.
  • Supporters argue that some top-tier dining is no longer just cooking, but uses food to express ideas and create emotional and narrative experiences.
  • Opponents argue that food’s basic function is to be consumed, which makes it fundamentally different from art created solely for expression, and therefore the two should not be conflated.

Approach

  • In January 2025, Denmark’s culture minister said the government would explore whether to formally recognize gastronomy as an art form; if advanced, it would require a vote in Denmark’s 179-seat parliament to reclassify it.
  • Chefs who support this direction point to immersive dining as an example: combining food, music, projections, spatial storytelling, and social issues, arguing that “food is a medium of expression.”
  • The article uses a case study to illustrate the mechanism: for example, Alchemist uses about 50 impressions, dome projections, and thematic dishes to design dining as a complete experience akin to performance art or installation art.
  • The expected policy effect is that, once recognized as art, chefs could receive state subsidies and private foundation support like writers or musicians.

Results

  • No scientific experiments, models, datasets, or quantitative performance results are provided; the text is a news report, not a research paper.
  • The strongest concrete facts in the article are that Alchemist opened in 2019, was ranked the world’s 5th-best restaurant in 2025, and has 2 Michelin stars.
  • Denmark has a population of about 6 million and 37 Michelin-starred restaurants, and is described as an international dining destination.
  • If the policy is implemented, Denmark could become the first country to place cooking—at least fine dining—on a legal footing close to arts such as painting, but it remains in the exploratory stage for now.
  • The plan also faces political uncertainty; the report notes that its progress could be affected by the March 24 general election.
