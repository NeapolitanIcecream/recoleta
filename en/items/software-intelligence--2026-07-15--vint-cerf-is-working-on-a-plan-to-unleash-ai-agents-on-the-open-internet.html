Summary

Innovation Labs is developing DNSid, a proposed open identity system for AI agents that links each agent to an internet domain and records its registration with cryptographic proofs. The initiative aims to support accountability and interoperability as agents increasingly interact across organizational boundaries, but adoption and technical standards remain unsettled.

Problem

  • AI agents usually operate inside proprietary systems, while cross-internet agent interactions lack a shared way to identify, audit, and assign authority to agents.
  • This matters because organizations need to know who controls an agent, what authority it has, how that authority was derived, and who is accountable for its behavior.
  • Multiple competing standards could prevent agents built by different companies from interoperating.

Approach

  • DNSid gives an AI agent an identity linked to an existing internet domain name.
  • Cryptographic proofs record the agent's registration over time, creating an auditable identity history.
  • Innovation Labs is trialing the proposed standards with several unnamed hyperscalers and identity companies.
  • The proposal is intended to remain an open architecture without Innovation Labs owning registration data or combining the standard with a broader AI business.

Results

  • No quantitative performance results, benchmark scores, or controlled comparisons are reported in the provided text.
  • Innovation Labs says it is conducting trials with several unnamed hyperscalers and identity companies, but provides no trial results or participant names.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that DNSid offers a mechanism for agent identification and historical registration auditing through domain linkage and cryptographic proofs.
  • Vint Cerf argues that broad adoption will depend on practical functionality and interoperability, drawing an analogy to the adoption pressure that supported TCP/IP.
  • Cerf does not regard an agent-based economy as inevitable; he expects organizations to keep pursuing it because delegated actions can make online activity easier.
