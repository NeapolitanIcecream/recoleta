Summary

This article is not an academic paper, but an opinion piece aimed at product managers (PMs). The author argues that PMs should not treat “personally submitting production code” as high-leverage work, but should use coding and prototyping skills to communicate better, understand systems, and run experiments.

Problem

  • The article discusses the question of whether, in a large-company environment, PMs should personally write and ship production code, and whether this practice really creates high value.
  • The author argues this matters because a PM’s core responsibility is to drive prioritization and resource alignment. If they spend time writing code inefficiently, it may create opportunity cost, technical debt, and bypass organizational processes.
  • It also touches on a broader issue: treating the ability to “land prod diffs” as an achievement may create the illusion of “appearing to make progress while actually operating with very low leverage.”

Approach

  • The core view is simple: do not use PM coding for formal production delivery; use it for prototyping, communication, experimentation, and understanding systems instead.
  • The author first argues against PMs directly landing production code from the perspective of organizational efficiency: if a feature is truly important, the prioritization system should be fixed rather than bypassing the process and doing it yourself.
  • Second, the author argues from the perspective of cost and risk: PMs may code more slowly than professional engineers, while costing more in labor, and they can easily introduce technical debt or even affect the stability of internal tools or production environments.
  • The article then gives positive reasons for why PMs should still know how to code: to show ideas more concretely, deepen understanding of how systems are implemented, build more realistic experimental prototypes, and temporarily leverage their own unique domain/API experience.
  • Its mechanism is not a new algorithm, but rather a principle of role division and workflow optimization: use coding as a tool for cognition and experimentation, rather than as the PM’s primary mode of delivery.

Results

  • The article does not provide any quantitative experiments, benchmark data, datasets, or metrics, so there are no verifiable numerical results.
  • The strongest concrete claim is that in large-scale organizations like Meta, PMs personally shipping production features is usually a low-leverage activity that tends to “bypass the prioritization system” rather than fix systemic problems.
  • The author explicitly claims that PMs writing production code brings multiple negative effects: their coding efficiency may be like a “slow E3,” while the company bears opportunity cost closer to an “IC7 salary”; however, this is a rhetorical comparison rather than a formal measurement.
  • The article also makes a specific risk judgment: random PM pet features can easily accumulate technical debt, and even changes to intern tools may affect production systems.
  • On the positive side, the author argues that the most valuable scenarios for PM coding include demonstrating features more effectively, helping communicate with engineering teams, and running more realistic human experiments, rather than directly producing formal production diffs.
