Summary

This article presents the vision for a Eurosky account built on the AT Protocol: turning user accounts from platform-owned attachments into user-owned online identities and data hubs. The core argument is that the future of social media should not be dominated by a single app, but driven by personal accounts that are portable, permissionable, and reusable across apps.

Problem

  • Existing social platforms typically control users' accounts, data, social graphs, and rules. Users find it hard to migrate and are easily affected by platform policy changes.
  • This kind of "platform lock-in" weakens users' control over their online identity, social graph, and content, and also limits the emergence of healthier, more diverse social products.
  • As private data features are added to the protocol in the future, account hosting, legal jurisdiction, and data governance will become more important, creating a need for user-centered account infrastructure.

Approach

  • The article proposes Eurosky account + AT Protocol as the foundational layer for a "personal web account," allowing users to own and control their accounts, data, connections, and preferences.
  • The core mechanism can be understood simply as: separating social identity from any single app and turning it into a personal account that can be taken elsewhere, authorized, and used across services.
  • Eurosky aims both to build infrastructure tools that help developers quickly create various social web apps, and to provide foundational services and applications to catalyze ecosystem growth.
  • The article envisions this account eventually managing data permissions, login, ad preferences, and cross-app social relationships across different applications, while supporting migration between service providers.
  • They also plan to explore new application experiences, such as new forms of social media emphasizing focus and information customization, as well as microblogging variants designed to improve conversational health.

Results

  • The text does not provide experiments, benchmarks, or quantitative evaluation results, so there are no reportable metrics, datasets, or numerical comparisons.
  • The most concrete current progress is that the Eurosky.social account already exists as an initial foundational service, mainly used as an entry point to Bluesky, but positioned as a much broader personal online identity infrastructure.
  • The article claims that AT Protocol/Atmosphere already covers or targets many social web scenarios, including social media, blogging, music discovery, code collaboration, livestreaming, communities, commerce, and search, but gives no scale data.
  • It explicitly states that private data features are expected to enter the protocol "this year", and argues that this will enable new application scenarios such as private groups, local communities, collaboration tools, and cloud storage, but provides no validation results.
  • On the product roadmap, the team says it is building multiple prototypes and hopes to release them later in 2026, which is a time-based commitment rather than an empirical performance result.
