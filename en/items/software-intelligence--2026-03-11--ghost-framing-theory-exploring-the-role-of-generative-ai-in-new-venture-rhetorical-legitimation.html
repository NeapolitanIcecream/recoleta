Summary

This paper proposes Ghost Framing Theory (GFT) to explain how generative AI, through its covert use by both entrepreneurs and investors, jointly shapes resonance and legitimacy judgments in new venture narratives. It is a theoretical paper, and its core contributions are the concepts of “ghost agency,” five rhetorical affordances, and a recursive process model spanning pitching, screening, and relationship-building.

Problem

  • Existing research on entrepreneurial narratives and legitimacy assumes that information is primarily written by humans and that authorship is transparent, but generative AI can now produce highly fluent fundraising materials at low cost while concealing their source, undermining these assumptions.
  • Both entrepreneurs and investors may be using generative AI “behind the scenes”: the former to write pitch decks, the latter to screen, score, and provide feedback; existing theory struggles to explain how this two-sided human-AI collaboration affects resonance and legitimacy.
  • This matters because in the early stages of resource acquisition, the “persuasiveness” of fundraising narratives directly affects investment, screening, and subsequent relationships, while AI-driven linguistic fluency may amplify or distort judgments of competence, credibility, and morality.

Approach

  • Proposes Ghost Framing Theory (GFT): modeling entrepreneurial fundraising communication as interaction between two “human + generative AI” ensembles rather than between one individual and another.
  • Defines ghost agency: humans and generative systems jointly produce rhetorical content, but the AI contribution may remain partly or completely invisible during evaluation, similar to “ghostwriting.”
  • Identifies five generative AI rhetorical affordances: generativeness, extreme combinatorics, tone repertoire, velocity/energy, shared substratum.
  • Develops a three-stage recursive process model: ghost pitching, ghost screening, ghost relationship-building, describing how the entrepreneur side and the investor side respectively use these affordances to generate, screen, and adjust narratives.
  • Advances three theoretical propositions: founders’ use of AI increases initial resonance; this effect is moderated by whether investors also use AI for screening; once interactions move into the relationship-building stage, interpersonal relationships weaken this moderating effect.

Results

  • This paper does not report original experiments, dataset evaluations, or statistically significant results; its main outputs are a conceptual framework, a process model, and theoretical propositions.
  • The paper cites external industry evidence to support the importance of the problem: a blind test of 250 U.S. investors + 250 business owners reportedly found that 80% of respondents considered AI-generated pitch decks more persuasive, and willingness to invest after reading them rose to about 3x (relative to human-written versions).
  • Another survey of 140 PE/VC firms reportedly found that 82% had already deployed generative AI modules in deal sourcing/screening, up from 47% the previous year, indicating rapid growth in investor-side “shadow AI” usage.
  • The paper’s core “results” are theoretical claims: compared with not using AI, entrepreneurs using ghost framing are more likely to achieve resonance in early legitimation after controlling for underlying venture quality; if investors also use AI, this advantage is further amplified.
  • The paper also argues that as interaction moves from initial screening into ongoing relationship-building, the impact of whether each side uses AI declines, because interpersonal relationships and continued interaction partly replace the early reliance on rhetorical fluency.
