Authors
Eric Lacosse; Mariana Duarte; Peter M. Todd; Daniel C. McNamee

Summary

This paper studies semantic memory search in large language models during the "Semantic Fluency Task" and finds that they exhibit human-like "clustering/switching" strategies. The authors further use mechanistic interpretability methods to locate where these behaviors can be read out in the output distribution and intermediate layer representations.

Problem

  • The paper aims to answer: Do LLMs truly exhibit human-like search strategies during semantic memory retrieval, and can these strategies be identified in the model's internal mechanisms?
  • This matters because if the "human-like cognitive behaviors" of LLMs can be mapped to interpretable internal signals, we can more reliably understand, align, or intentionally "misalign" the model's cognitive style for human-AI collaboration and behavior control.
  • The specific setting is the classic Semantic Fluency Task (e.g. "name as many animals as possible"), in which humans generate words in clusters within the same category and switch to a new category at appropriate times.

Approach

  • The authors collected and compared 699 human animal-naming sequences with matching-start-condition sequences generated by the Llama-3 family (1B/3B/8B/70B); after filtering, they analyzed 681 human sequences and 2285 LLM sequences (1B: 606, 3B: 502, 8B: 572, 70B: 605).
  • They used an animal category taxonomy to label adjacent word pairs as non-switch/clustering (same category) or switch (across categories), and constructed category transition probability matrices to compare humans and models at the level of overall search behavior.
  • At the mechanistic level, the authors grouped vocabulary items into words that would continue the current category and words that would switch to other categories, then used the logit lens to trace changes in probability mass for these two word groups across layers, in order to observe when switch signals emerge.
  • They also applied PCA + logistic regression probes to intermediate residual stream representations to test whether switch vs. non-switch events could be distinguished directly from layer representations; in addition, they constructed three contrastive prompt datasets—neutral / convergent / divergent—to amplify the behavioral differences between these two modes.

Results

  • The category transition matrices of humans and LLMs are highly correlated, with Spearman ρ = 0.701, p < 0.001, indicating clear similarity in overall semantic search patterns.
  • However, LLMs switch categories less often than humans: the average switch ratio is 0.55 for humans versus 0.40 for LLMs, a significant difference (Mann-Whitney p < 0.0001); based on this, the authors argue that LLMs are more inclined to more thoroughly "exhaust" a semantic cluster before switching.
  • In the 70B model's output distribution, significant probability changes can be detected around switch events: from relative position -1 to 0, within-category d = -0.158, between-category d = 0.144, actual-sequence d = -0.184, and all p < 0.001.
  • The logit lens shows that after layer 40, the model begins to clearly distinguish switch from non-switch: during non-switch, the average within-category probability is about 0.0035 and between-category 0.0005; during switch, within-category is suppressed to 0.0008, close to between-category 0.0007, though a statistical difference remains (p < 0.001).
  • When using only real human sequences for residual stream probing, switch detection is relatively weak: AUROC = 0.57 (Llama-3.3-70B); by contrast, classification based only on the output distribution reaches AUROC = 0.751.
  • After using the constructed contrastive data, the switch/non-switch signal in the residual stream becomes very strong: on Llama-3.3-70B, neutral AUROC = 0.96, convergent 0.98, divergent 0.97. Based on this, the authors argue that the convergent/divergent mechanisms of semantic foraging are identifiable and potentially controllable internal properties in LLMs.
