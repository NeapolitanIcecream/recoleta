Summary

This article is not a technical research paper but a political-economy commentary. Its core argument is that Chile's free-market system survived a left-wing resurgence, two constitutional rewrite attempts, and the Boric administration, demonstrating strong institutional resilience. At the same time, the article warns that the newly ascendant right-wing populist forces may once again bind economic freedom to authoritarian nostalgia, thereby planting the seeds for a new backlash.

Problem

  • The article addresses the question of why Chile, after a strong anti-neoliberal wave, the 2019 protests, left-wing rule, and the constitution-making movement, still did not abandon its free-market framework.
  • This matters because Chile has long been seen as Latin America's most successful market-oriented case, and the fate of its institutions affects growth, poverty reduction, pension disputes, and democratic legitimacy.
  • The article also emphasizes a deeper problem: if the market order is once again tied to the history of dictatorship or to a new form of right-wing authoritarianism, its political legitimacy will be damaged again.

Approach

  • The article uses a historical-political narrative analysis rather than experiments or models: it traces the story from the economic collapse during the Allende period, to market reforms under Pinochet, to the continuation of those policies by democratic governments after 1990, and then to the 2019 protests and Boric's rise in 2021.
  • Its core mechanism is straightforward: the reason the free market "survived" is not that it has no flaws, but that the relevant institutions were continuously preserved during the democratic era, generated growth benefits, and developed institutional inertia and interest-group foundations that were difficult to dismantle quickly.
  • The article uses the pension system as a representative case of a legitimacy crisis to explain why the public came to resent "neoliberalism": the originally assumed replacement rate of about 70% did not materialize; in practice, the median was below 35%, and for some low-contribution groups it was even only 4%.
  • The author then uses the failure of two constitutional processes to support this judgment: even amid intense social discontent, voters did not accept radical proposals to thoroughly reconstruct the economic and political order.
  • Finally, the article offers a two-sided view of the future: the failure of radical left-wing transformation does not mean right-wing populism is correct; if Kast combines the market economy with nationalism, xenophobia, and Pinochet nostalgia, the old backlash may return.

Results

  • The key historical outcomes presented in the article include that between 1990 and 1998, Chile's poverty rate was "nearly cut in half," while the extreme poverty rate fell from about 13% in 1990 to about 6%.
  • Pension data are used as evidence of institutional failure: the pension replacement rate implied in early official messaging was about 70%, but the actual replacement rate for the median worker was below 35%; for some people with discontinuous work histories, self-financed pensions could be as low as 4% of wages; the average pension was about 25% of wages in the final years before retirement.
  • The article also provides numerical explanations for contributing factors: the mandatory contribution rate was only 10%, while the OECD average was 19%; between 1981 and 2021, life expectancy in Chile increased by 11 years, but the retirement age was not raised accordingly.
  • The quantitative evidence from constitutional politics includes that in the referendum launching a new constitution process, 78% of voters supported beginning the process; but the first draft constitution was later rejected by a "wide margin," and the second constitutional text in 2023 was also rejected.
  • The first draft constitution is described as highly radical: it contained 388 articles, listed 103 social rights, and designed 11 separate judicial systems for different Indigenous groups.
  • The article does not provide strict causal identification, statistical tests, or a new dataset, so its "breakthrough findings" are mainly a synthetic judgment based on public historical data and political events rather than an academic experimental conclusion.
