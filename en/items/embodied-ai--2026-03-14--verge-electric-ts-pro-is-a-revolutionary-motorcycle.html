Summary

This article introduces the core innovation of the Verge TS Pro/Ultra electric motorcycle: making the motor into the hubless rear wheel itself, thereby freeing up frame space, reducing system complexity, and claiming to deliver high torque and more practical range. It reads more like a product review/technical report than an academic paper.

Problem

  • Traditional electric motorcycles usually place the motor within the frame, taking up battery space and limiting range and overall vehicle packaging.
  • Placing the motor close to the battery and other heat sources increases cooling-system complexity, such as requiring liquid cooling, pumps, and radiators.
  • Traditional chain/shaft drive and conventional motor layouts add weight, cost, and mechanical complexity, and may affect center of gravity and low-speed handling.

Approach

  • The core mechanism is to design the rear wheel as a kind of "reverse electromagnetic motor": the stationary part is on the inside, the rotating part is on the outside, and the rear wheel itself is the motor, rather than stuffing a motor into the hub.
  • This allows more of the 20.8-kWh battery to be placed in the lower half of the frame, freeing internal space and lowering the center of gravity.
  • Because power is applied directly to the rear wheel, components such as chains and driveshafts are eliminated, which the article says can reduce weight, cost, and complexity.
  • The article also claims that the motor requires no additional cooling system and that torque can be controlled on a millisecond timescale.

Results

  • The TS Pro is claimed to produce 137 hp and 738 lb-ft of torque; the TS Ultra is claimed at 201 hp and 885 lb-ft.
  • The TS Pro's claimed curb weight is 450 lb, lower than the Energica Ego+ at 573 lb and the LiveWire One at 562 lb mentioned in the article.
  • The TS Pro's claimed range is 217 miles, and it supports Level 3 charging.
  • The TS Pro's claimed 0-60 mph acceleration is 3.5 s.
  • The article gives product pricing starting at $30,000 for the TS Pro and $45,000 for the TS Ultra, with deliveries possibly beginning as early as the end of the year.
  • There are no formal benchmark tests or peer review data; it is mainly manufacturer-claimed specifications plus the author's subjective riding impressions.
