Summary

This paper is an experience report discussing how to integrate requirements engineering courses more flexibly into software engineering curricula for working professionals. Based on three real-world projects, the authors summarize the characteristics of such curricula and propose a lightweight integration method centered on content item mapping.

Problem

  • The problem the paper addresses is how to systematically and effectively integrate requirements engineering (RE) courses into dynamic, modular software engineering curricula for professionals.
  • This matters because traditional university curricula often treat RE as an “after-the-fact addition,” emphasizing breadth of knowledge over depth and industry readiness, which creates a persistent gap between teaching content and industry needs.
  • Curricula for professionals (CfP) are more dynamic and loosely coupled than traditional degree programs, give instructors greater autonomy, and lack a suitable framework for continuous alignment and collaborative development.

Approach

  • Based on three cases (PROMIS, Software4KMU, TASTE), the authors derive key characteristics of CfP: high modularity, high dynamism, practice orientation, less centralized upfront alignment, strong instructor autonomy, and a better fit for organization around learning paths rather than a globally unified course structure.
  • The core method is curriculum alignment based on content items (CI): courses are broken down into fine-grained content units of about 10–15 minutes each, with each unit recording a title and the topics/concepts it covers.
  • RE course instructors then collaborate with instructors of related courses to identify overlaps, dependencies, and appropriate sequencing among content items from different courses, and reorder them into a unified sequence.
  • On top of this unified sequence, they further create modules and learning paths, add teaching materials, and use these to define or validate learning objectives.
  • Simply put, the mechanism is: first split multiple courses into small knowledge blocks, then rearrange the related blocks like puzzle pieces to form learning paths that better match professional roles and practical needs.

Results

  • The authors claim that the method successfully supported RE course integration in 3 projects/cases: PROMIS, Software4KMU, and TASTE.
  • In PROMIS, the first version of the RE course developed with this method has been taught for 3 years and received positive feedback from students; however, the paper does not provide quantitative scores, sample size, or baseline comparisons.
  • In TASTE, the authors built a learning path containing 35 content items (CIs): 11 CIs from the RE course (4 mandatory + 7 optional), 13 CIs from the MBSE course (7 mandatory + 6 optional), and 11 CIs from the QA course (8 mandatory + 3 optional).
  • This TASTE learning path contains 3 mandatory modules and 5 optional modules; among them, 2 mandatory modules combine content from all three courses, while 1 mandatory module comes from a single course.
  • In TASTE, the learning path iterated to 4 versions, indicating that the method supports incremental, collaborative adjustment.
  • The paper does not provide a rigorous quantitative evaluation of effectiveness (such as improvements in learning outcomes, completion rates, or experimental comparisons with other curriculum design methods); the strongest concrete evidence is mainly successful implementation across cases, long-term teaching use, and positive subjective feedback from project managers and students.
