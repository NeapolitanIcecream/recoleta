Summary

This paper presents a generative AI-assisted music psychotherapy tool for Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing (DHH) individuals, combining conversational agents, music generative AI, and visualization to support emotional expression and self-understanding through “collaborative songwriting.” The study emphasizes that music therapy should not only serve auditory training, but can also provide accessible psychological support for DHH users through multimodal interaction.

Problem

  • Traditional music psychotherapy relies heavily on hearing, which has often excluded DHH individuals from therapeutic musical activities such as songwriting.
  • Existing music interventions for DHH populations mostly focus on auditory perception training rather than emotional expression, reflection, and healing; this matters because this group often experiences psychological stress such as social isolation, low self-esteem, anxiety, and depression.
  • There is a lack of digital/remote music psychotherapy tools that simultaneously account for sensory accessibility, communication styles, and therapeutic safety.

Approach

  • The study used a co-design approach with therapists: first, it interviewed 6 music therapists with DHH clinical experience to identify current practices, pain points, and needs.
  • It then conducted co-design workshops with 4 therapists to design a generative AI-assisted songwriting tool based on therapeutic workflows and risk considerations.
  • The tool’s core mechanism is that an LLM conversational agent guides users to describe their experiences through supportive empathy, example response options, and visual metaphor prompts, and then uses music generative AI and music visualization to transform these narratives into a songwriting process.
  • It subsequently carried out a usage study with 23 participants who use cochlear implants and identify as Deaf, observing the effects of collaborative songwriting on emotional expression and therapeutic engagement.

Results

  • The research included a usage study with 23 DHH participants, interviews with 6 therapists, and co-design workshops with 4 therapists, indicating that the system underwent an initial empirical evaluation with the real target population.
  • The paper does not report standardized scale scores, significance tests, or quantitative comparisons with baseline systems in the provided excerpt.
  • The strongest empirical conclusion is that collaborating on songwriting with the CA helped participants achieve emotional release, reinterpretation of experiences, and deeper self-understanding.
  • The authors explicitly claim that the CA’s three strategies—supportive empathy, example response options, and visual-based metaphors—effectively facilitated “musical dialogue” for DHH users.
  • Among these, visually based prompts (imagining music as images or scenes) were identified as playing a key role in enhancing musical expression.
  • The study also claims that the tool has the potential to support therapists, expand access to emotional care, and advance inclusive AI design, but the excerpt does not provide numerical improvements relative to existing tools.
