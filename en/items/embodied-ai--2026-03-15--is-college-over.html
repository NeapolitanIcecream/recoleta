Summary

This is a commentary-style overview on whether "college is losing its status as the default path," with the central argument that AI and skills-based hiring are weakening the employment monopoly of degrees, but the value of universities in peer networks, judgment, and civic education has not disappeared.

Problem

  • The article examines whether college is still worthwhile as the default life and career path in a context where generative AI is automating knowledge work, companies are loosening degree requirements, and tuition and debt continue to rise.
  • This matters because it affects returns on individual education investment, youth entry into the workforce, social mobility, and the public role of universities in cultivating judgment, collaboration, and civic literacy.
  • The piece especially emphasizes that computer science, once seen as the "safest" major, is also being hit, indicating that the change is not a marginal phenomenon but a structural shift in the labor market.

Approach

  • This is not a technical paper, but an argumentative article based on secondary sources and cases, synthesizing news reports, surveys, academic research, and public statistics to support its claims.
  • The core mechanism can be understood most simply as: AI is automating the "entry-level cognitive skills" that universities primarily used to sell, and companies are therefore becoming more willing to hire based on skills rather than credentials.
  • The article splits its argument into two sides: one side presents "anti-college" evidence, such as declining degree requirements, shrinking entry-level roles, and tuition and debt pressure; the other presents evidence that "college still has value," such as peer effects, mentor relationships, social networks, and the cultivation of cross-disciplinary judgment.
  • Its final synthesis is not that "college is dead," but that "the degree itself is no longer sufficient"; going forward, what matters more is combining higher education with AI literacy, demonstrable skills, and faster-updating curricula.

Results

  • Degree requirements are falling: 45% of companies planned to eliminate degree requirements in 2024, and another 25% planned to do so by the end of 2025.
  • Entry-level tech roles have shrunk significantly: the article says entry-level tech jobs are down 67% in the US and 46% in the UK; a Harvard study (covering 62 million workers and 285,000 firms) says companies adopting generative AI reduced entry-level employment by 7.7% within 6 quarters, and hired 3.7 fewer entry-level employees per quarter than non-adopters.
  • Computer science education and employment are weakening: a CRA survey says 62% of institutions reported declining CS enrollment in fall 2025, with graduate programs at -15% and undergraduate at -6%; the article also says the unemployment rate for CS graduates has reached 6.1%.
  • Overall demand for college is under pressure but has not lost all returns: enrollment has fallen 15% since 2010; but the article also says college graduates still earn significantly more over their lifetimes, and 70% can recoup their educational investment within 10 years.
  • Alternative pathways are growing: the US has 680,000 active apprentices, nearly double the 2014 figure; starting pay is about $18/hour, rising to about $32/hour upon completion.
  • The student-side tension is strong: 83% of Gen Z say college is important, but only 53% think they can afford it; average student debt in 2025 is $39,375, and borrowers in 2024-25 pay $136 more per month and $16,266 more over a lifetime than borrowers in 2020-21. The article does not present new original experimental results; its strongest "result" is a synthetic judgment based on existing statistics and research.
