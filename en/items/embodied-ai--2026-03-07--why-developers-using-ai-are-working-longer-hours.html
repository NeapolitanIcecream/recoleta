Summary

This article synthesizes multiple industry reports and studies to argue that, although AI coding tools are often seen as improving developer efficiency, in reality they also often come with longer working hours, greater delivery instability, and potential skill degradation. The core conclusion is: AI has not automatically reduced the workload of software engineering; instead, under organizational pressure, it may turn "higher productivity" into "greater exhaustion."

Problem

  • The article seeks to answer the question: Do AI coding tools really make developers more efficient and less burdened, or do they turn efficiency gains into longer hours and greater pressure? This matters because software engineering is seen as one of the easiest areas for AI to be applied.
  • Looking only at "faster code generation" is not enough. Developers still have to verify, debug, roll back, and maintain code; if AI increases output while reducing stability, companies and engineering teams will pay higher hidden costs.
  • It also involves long-term effects: if developers increasingly rely on AI to complete tasks, it may weaken their debugging skills, ability to understand new libraries, and professional growth, while worsening burnout and attrition risk.

Approach

  • This is not a technical paper proposing a new algorithm, but rather a synthesis based on multiple surveys, company studies, and behavioral data discussing AI’s real-world impact on software engineering work.
  • The article compiles Google DORA’s survey of nearly 5,000 technology professionals, focusing on the relationship among AI adoption, perceived productivity, and software delivery stability.
  • It also cites engineering-practice data from Multitudes covering more than 500 developers, observing changes in pull request merges and out-of-hours commits in the AI era.
  • At the same time, it incorporates related research from UC Berkeley, Anthropic, and Harvard Business School to analyze AI’s effects on work pace, cognitive effort, learning outcomes, debugging ability, and collaboration patterns.
  • Put simply, the article’s mechanism is: AI makes writing code faster, but also makes it easier for organizations to demand "more"; and the faster code is generated, the more time humans need to spend checking it, patching it, and dealing with the consequences.

Results

  • Google DORA’s report shows that among nearly 5,000 technology professionals, 90% said they use AI at work, and more than 80% believed AI improved productivity. At the same time, however, the more AI was used, the higher software delivery instability became, with a greater likelihood of rollbacks or patch fixes after release.
  • A report from Multitudes covering more than 500 developers found that, on average, engineers’ merged pull requests increased by 27.2%, while out-of-hour commits increased by 19.6%, indicating that higher output came with longer working hours and potential burnout risk.
  • Researchers at UC Berkeley Haas observed at a U.S. technology company that after employees adopted AI, they took on more tasks, worked at a faster pace, and worked longer hours. The article does not provide specific numbers, but it clearly notes that even when the company did not require AI use, employees still used AI during lunch breaks, rest periods, and meetings.
  • Anthropic found in a study involving tasks with a new software library that engineers using AI assistance showed only a small and statistically insignificant improvement in completion speed; however, their post-task test scores were 17% lower than those of the non-AI group, suggesting that AI may weaken knowledge retention.
  • Anthropic also noted that the largest performance gap appeared in debugging-related questions. This implies that junior developers who rely too heavily on AI may not only find it harder to write code independently, but may also struggle more to understand and fix code they generated with AI assistance.
  • The article’s strongest overall claim is: AI in software engineering has created a situation where "more output" coexists with "more rework/longer hours." Therefore, what truly determines the outcome is not AI itself, but whether organizations also manage quality, workload, training, and burnout risk.
