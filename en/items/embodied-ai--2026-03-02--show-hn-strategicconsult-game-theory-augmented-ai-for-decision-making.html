Summary

This is a decision-support tool that combines large language models with game theory to analyze interactions among multiple stakeholders and provide more robust strategic recommendations. Its core selling point is using interpretable mathematical modeling instead of purely intuitive judgment, targeting scenarios such as negotiation, organizational change, and consensus building.

Problem

  • It aims to solve the problem of complex multi-party decision-making/negotiation, where participants have conflicting objectives, information is incomplete, and opponents' reactions are difficult to predict.
  • This matters because in business negotiations, internal project execution, organizational change, or community decision-making, misjudging others' positions can lead to high costs, deadlock, or low adoption rates.
  • Existing human judgment often relies on experience and intuition, lacking a repeatable and transparent analytical framework.

Approach

  • Use LLM to extract the situation, stakeholders, positions, and possible strategies from natural-language descriptions, turning vague problems into structured “games.”
  • Model multi-party interactions based on game theory, emphasizing the ability to handle incomplete information scenarios, and compute Nash equilibria and stable outcomes where “no one can improve unilaterally.”
  • By simulating the likely reactions of different participants under given positions, compare the resistance of different proposals/action paths and find the “path of least resistance” or solutions more likely to reach consensus.
  • After calibrating each party’s parameters through human-machine collaboration, run advanced simulations and optimization to output actionable recommendations and potential partners.
  • The system emphasizes that the analytical process is based on established mathematical principles, is reproducible, and is paired with data encryption and automatic deletion to meet confidentiality requirements.

Results

  • The text does not provide formal paper experiments, benchmark datasets, or quantitative metrics, so the magnitude of performance improvement cannot be verified.
  • It explicitly claims to compute Nash equilibria and “optimal strategies,” and to simulate outcomes under conditions of multiple stakeholders and incomplete information, but does not provide figures for problem scale, speed, or success rate.
  • The strongest concrete claim given is that it applies to multiple scenarios: business negotiations, internal conflicts of interest, major change rollouts, community consensus building, and even trips with friends/personal choices.
  • The only clear product-level numerical information is that pricing is based on complexity, and starts “starting from the initial consultation,” but no specific amount or baseline comparison of effectiveness is disclosed.
