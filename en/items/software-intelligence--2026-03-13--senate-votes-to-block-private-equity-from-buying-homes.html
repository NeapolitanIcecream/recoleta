Summary

This article discusses the progress of U.S. housing legislation passed by the Senate in an 89-10 vote, centered on restricting large institutional investors from purchasing single-family homes in order to curb Wall Street’s financialization of housing. The author describes it as a major policy shift around whether housing should be treated as a family asset or a financial asset.

Problem

  • The article argues that the worsening of U.S. housing affordability stems not only from supply constraints, but also from the financialization of housing and market control by large institutional investors and major homebuilders.
  • The concentrated acquisition of single-family homes by institutional buyers is said to crowd out ordinary homebuyers, drive up rents, increase fees, and weaken homeownership rates, which are important for social stability and household wealth accumulation.
  • The key policy dispute is whether the problem should mainly be addressed by loosening zoning/increasing supply, or by directly restricting capital concentration and institutional ownership.

Approach

  • The Senate version of the bill combines the two approaches: on the one hand, it advances supply-side measures such as manufactured housing, faster zoning approvals, and financing for multifamily housing; on the other hand, it adds restrictions on homes held by institutional investors.
  • The core mechanism is to ban large institutional investors from continuing to acquire additional single-family homes, and to set a cap of no more than 350 homes.
  • For the “build-to-rent” model, the bill allows institutions to build new homes, but requires them to sell after 7 years; if tenants wish to continue living there, this can be extended by 3 more years.
  • The article also cites regulatory and research materials to support this mechanism: the FTC, congressional investigations, and Federal Reserve research all indicate that institutional ownership is associated with higher rents, more fees, and a worse rental experience.

Results

  • The clearest result is political progress: the bill passed the U.S. Senate by a wide 89-10 margin, showing that restricting institutional home purchases has gained bipartisan support.
  • The article says that the number of U.S. homebuilders fell by 60% after the financial crisis, which is used to support the argument that the housing problem has structural financial roots.
  • The article states that the “build-to-rent” sector’s market share doubled during 2021-2024, which is viewed as an important signal of the expansion of institutional housing ownership.
  • The demographic change given in the article is that the average/typical first-time homebuying age rose from 39 to 59 over the past 15 years, presented as one piece of evidence that it has become harder for ordinary families to buy homes.
  • The cited research by Federal Reserve economists says that after institutional investors first acquire properties, rent growth is 60% higher than the average increase; congressional materials also say that institutionally owned single-family rentals are often associated with higher rent increases, more miscellaneous fees, and worsening housing quality.
  • The article does not provide a strict causal identification study or a complete academic evaluation; its main “breakthrough” is the bill’s overwhelming passage at the legislative level and the incorporation of restrictions on institutional home purchases into a mainstream housing bill.
